Chapter 02 · Part I — Foundations

The Chemistry of Life
Biomolecules and Enzymes

Every structure you meet in this book — the membrane from Chapter 1, the DNA you’ll read in Chapter 4, a muscle sarcomere fifteen chapters from now — is built from four families of molecules, and all of them are doing chemistry that is, at bottom, one glass of water at a time. Learn what those four families are made of, and learn the one trick (a catalyst called an enzyme) that makes their reactions fast enough to run a living cell, and you have the vocabulary for most of the rest of NEET biology.

Six elements, three kinds of bond

Weigh a cell, strip out the water, and look at what’s left: over 95% of that dry mass is built from just six elements — carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur (CHNOPS). Carbon does the heavy lifting: it forms four stable covalent bonds at once, which lets it build the long chains, rings, and branches that no other common element can. Every biomolecule you’ll meet in this chapter is, underneath the label, a carbon skeleton decorated with functional groups built from the other five.

Two kinds of bond then do two very different jobs. Strong covalent bonds (C–C, C–H, C–N, C–O) hold a molecule’s backbone together reliably enough to survive being knocked around by thermal motion for years. Weak bonds — hydrogen bonds, ionic attractions, van der Waals forces — are individually fragile but form by the thousands inside one folded protein or one DNA double helix, and it’s exactly that combination of “strong enough to hold a shape, weak enough to bend and unfold on demand” that makes biology’s molecules functional rather than just stable. A diamond, held together entirely by strong covalent bonds, cannot fold into an active site. A protein can, because most of its 3-D shape is held by weak bonds that can flex.

All of this happens suspended in water, which is not a passive backdrop. Water is polar, so it hydrogen-bonds to itself and to any polar or charged group on a biomolecule — that’s why sugars, amino acids, and ions dissolve in it. It is exactly this same polarity that excludes nonpolar (hydrophobic) groups, forcing them to cluster away from water and toward each other — the hydrophobic effect. That single effect is why phospholipids spontaneously form the membrane you read about in Chapter 1, and why a protein’s oily amino acids bury themselves inside the folded structure while its charged ones face outward into the cytoplasm. You will see “because water forces it to” as the real answer behind a surprising number of NEET diagrams.

Macromolecules are functional polymers

Three of the four biomolecule families follow the same construction rule: small repeating units, called monomers, are strung together into long chains called polymers. The chemistry that links one monomer to the next is also the same reaction, reused everywhere in biology:

Core concept

Condensation (dehydration synthesis) joins two monomers by removing a water molecule to form the bond between them; hydrolysis is the exact reverse — adding a water molecule back in to break that bond apart. Digestion (Chapter 13) is hydrolysis of the food you eat back down to monomers; biosynthesis, growth, and storage are condensation, building monomers back up into polymers. Once you see this one reversible reaction underneath carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, you stop needing to memorize three separate bond-forming mechanisms.

The fourth family, lipids, doesn’t fit this monomer→polymer pattern as neatly — more on that when we get there. Here’s the map before the detail:

Carbohydrates: one sugar ring, two very different jobs

Every carbohydrate reduces to some number of monosaccharides — simple sugars with the general formula (CH2O)n, the most important being six-carbon glucose. Two monosaccharides condense to a disaccharide via a glycosidic bond: sucrose (glucose + fructose, table sugar), lactose (glucose + galactose, milk sugar), and maltose (glucose + glucose, malt sugar) are the three NEET actually asks about by name. String hundreds to thousands of monosaccharides together and you get a polysaccharide — and here the exact same building block, glucose, produces radically different materials depending on how the glycosidic bonds are angled.

NEET trap

Starch, glycogen, and cellulose are all pure glucose polymers — the trap is assuming different monomers explain their different behavior. The real difference is the glycosidic bond’s geometry: starch (plant storage) and glycogen (animal storage, more heavily branched) use α-1,4 linkages, which kink the chain into a helix that packs compactly for storage. Cellulose (plant cell walls) uses β-1,4 linkages, which keep the chain straight, letting thousands of parallel chains hydrogen-bond into rigid fibers. Humans have enzymes (amylases) that hydrolyze α bonds but not β bonds — which is exactly why you can digest a potato but not the paper it’s wrapped in, despite both being made of glucose.

Chitin is carbohydrate’s other structural polymer — a nitrogen-containing glucose derivative that forms the exoskeleton of arthropods and the cell walls of fungi, and a favorite “identify the polysaccharide” option precisely because it isn’t pure glucose.

Proteins: one sequence, one shape, one function

A protein is a polymer of amino acids — twenty standard types, each built from a central (α) carbon bonded to an amino group (–NH2), a carboxyl group (–COOH), a hydrogen, and one variable R group that gives each amino acid its personality: some R groups are nonpolar and oily, some are charged and water-loving, a few carry sulfur. Two amino acids condense via a peptide bond (between the carboxyl of one and the amino of the next) to form a dipeptide; many in a row form a polypeptide.

A polypeptide only becomes a functioning protein once it folds, and biology organizes that folding into four levels, each built on the last:

  1. Primary structure — the linear sequence of amino acids, held together purely by peptide bonds. Change one amino acid here (a mutation) and you can change everything downstream — sickle-cell hemoglobin differs from normal hemoglobin by exactly one amino acid substitution.
  2. Secondary structure — local, repeating folds (the α-helix and β-pleated sheet) held by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms, not the R groups.
  3. Tertiary structure — the complete 3-D folding of one polypeptide chain, driven by interactions between R groups: hydrophobic R groups bury inward (the same hydrophobic effect from earlier in this chapter), while hydrogen bonds, ionic attractions, and covalent disulfide bridges (between two cysteine sulfurs) lock the shape in place. This is the level at which an active site is created.
  4. Quaternary structure — two or more separately folded polypeptide chains assembling into one functional unit. Hemoglobin is the textbook example: four separate globin chains (two α, two β), each already tertiary-folded, come together around four heme groups.
NEET trap

“Which of the following is not a protein” is one of the most reliable question stems in the Biomolecules chapter. Insulin, hemoglobin, keratin, collagen, trypsin, and most enzymes are proteins. The classic wrong-answer bait that looks biological but is not a protein: chlorophyll (a magnesium-porphyrin pigment), thyroxine (an iodinated amino acid derivative, not a peptide hormone), cellulose and starch (carbohydrates), and steroid hormones like estrogen and testosterone (lipids). If a molecule is a hormone or pigment, don’t assume protein — check what it’s actually built from.

Heat, extreme pH, or heavy metal ions can break the weak bonds holding tertiary/quaternary structure without touching the peptide bonds of the primary sequence — the protein unfolds and loses function, a process called denaturation (frying an egg denatures its proteins irreversibly). This is also exactly why enzymes, which are almost all proteins, have a narrow working temperature and pH range, covered below.

Lipids: the family that doesn’t quite polymerize

Lipids are grouped by a shared physical property — they don’t dissolve in water — rather than by one shared building-block chemistry, which is why they don’t fit the monomer/polymer story as cleanly as the other three families. The core unit is the fatty acid: a long hydrocarbon tail ending in a carboxyl group. A saturated fatty acid has no carbon=carbon double bonds, so its tail is straight and packs tightly (solid at room temperature — most animal fats); an unsaturated fatty acid has one or more double bonds that kink the chain, preventing tight packing (liquid at room temperature — most plant oils).

Three fatty acids condensed onto one glycerol backbone (via ester bonds, releasing three waters) make a triglyceride — the body’s long-term energy reserve, storing roughly twice the energy per gram that carbohydrate does. Swap one fatty acid for a phosphate-containing head group and you get a phospholipid: a hydrophilic head and two hydrophobic tails in one molecule (amphipathic), which is precisely the property that drives phospholipids to self-assemble into the bilayer membrane from Chapter 1 — no enzyme required, just the hydrophobic effect doing its job on a large scale. Steroids (cholesterol, and the sex hormones estrogen and testosterone built from it) are structurally unrelated to fatty acids at all — four fused carbon rings, not a chain — which is exactly why steroid hormones are the other reliable “this looks important but isn’t a protein” NEET option.

Nucleic acids: the fourth family, previewed

The monomer here is the nucleotide: a five-carbon (pentose) sugar, a nitrogen-containing base, and one or more phosphate groups, linked into long chains by phosphodiester bonds between the sugar of one nucleotide and the phosphate of the next. DNA (deoxyribose sugar, bases A/T/G/C, almost always double-stranded) stores genetic information long-term; RNA (ribose sugar, bases A/U/G/C, almost always single-stranded) reads and executes it. That’s deliberately all you need here — Chapter 4, The Genetic Code, is the whole rest of this story.

Enzymes: catalysts that make biology fast enough to live

Here is a fact that surprises most students: the reaction that breaks down glucose for energy is thermodynamically favorable — it releases energy, exactly the kind of reaction that “should” happen. Left alone at body temperature, though, it would take an impractically long time, because reactants first have to climb over an energy hump called the activation energy (Ea) before they can react at all — existing bonds have to strain and partly break before new ones can form. Being thermodynamically favorable tells you a reaction will eventually happen; it says nothing about how fast. That gap between “favorable” and “fast enough to run a cell” is exactly the gap an enzyme closes.

Core concept

An enzyme is a biological catalyst — almost always a protein — that speeds up a reaction by lowering its activation energy, without being consumed and without changing the reaction’s overall free-energy change (ΔG). It doesn’t make an unfavorable reaction favorable; it makes an already-favorable reaction fast enough to matter on a biological timescale. Because it changes kinetics, not thermodynamics, one enzyme molecule can catalyze the same reaction over and over, essentially unchanged at the end of each cycle.

The active site and the specificity problem

An enzyme’s catalytic power comes from a small pocket on its folded (tertiary) structure called the active site, shaped by exactly the R-group interactions from the Proteins section above. The substrate binds there, forming an enzyme-substrate (ES) complex; the enzyme then stabilizes the high-energy transition state, and the products are released, leaving the enzyme unchanged and free to bind the next substrate molecule. Because the active site’s shape and chemistry are so specific, most enzymes act on only one substrate or a very narrow family of substrates — sucrase hydrolyzes sucrose and essentially nothing else.

Both models explain specificity; only induced fit explains why enzymes can also stress and destabilize the substrate’s bonds as they bind, which is a large part of how the activation-energy barrier actually gets lowered. If a question just says “enzyme specificity,” either model applies; if it names the model or the scientist, induced fit is the one modern biology (and NEET) treats as correct.

What controls how fast an enzyme works

Four variables govern enzyme rate, and NEET tests the shape of each relationship as much as the fact of it:

  • Temperature — rate rises with temperature (more kinetic energy, more successful collisions) up to an optimum (≈37–40°C for most human enzymes), then falls sharply as heat denatures the protein and destroys the active site’s shape.
  • pH — each enzyme has its own optimum pH, set by which ionizable R groups its active site needs in which charge state. Pepsin (stomach) works best around pH 2; trypsin (small intestine) works best around pH 7.5–8.5 — the same protein chemistry, tuned to two very different environments.
  • Substrate concentration — rate rises roughly linearly at low substrate levels, then plateaus at Vmax once every active site is occupied continuously; adding still more substrate can’t speed up a reaction that’s already saturated.
  • Enzyme concentration — with substrate in excess, rate rises roughly linearly with enzyme concentration, since more active sites are available at once.

Many enzymes are inactive proteins (apoenzymes) alone and only become catalytically active (a holoenzyme) once bound to a non-protein helper called a cofactor. Cofactors split into two kinds: inorganic ions such as Zn2+, Mg2+, or Fe2+; and organic cofactors, which are themselves split by how tightly they bind — a coenzyme (e.g. NAD+, FAD, coenzyme A — many built from vitamins) is loosely, reversibly attached and can dissociate after each reaction, while a prosthetic group (e.g. the heme in catalase and the cytochromes) is bound tightly, often covalently, and stays put permanently.

Cells put this to routine use as feedback (end-product) inhibition: the final product of a multi-step pathway loops back and inhibits (often non-competitively, at an allosteric site) an early enzyme in its own synthesis pathway — a self-limiting circuit that stops the cell from wastefully overproducing something it already has enough of.

Think first: insulin is a protein hormone, but thyroxine (also a hormone) is not. Doesn’t that break “living things use proteins for everything important”?

It only breaks an over-generalization, not the biology. Proteins are the cell’s general-purpose tool because twenty different R groups let them build almost any shape or chemistry needed — but “important signaling molecule” was never a synonym for “protein” in the first place. Insulin and glucagon are short proteins (peptide hormones), built the normal way on ribosomes from an amino-acid sequence. Thyroxine, by contrast, is a single modified amino acid (a tyrosine with iodine atoms attached) — far too small to be called a protein, and steroid hormones like estrogen and testosterone aren’t even built from amino acids at all, but from cholesterol. The lesson NEET is really testing: check what a molecule is built from before assuming “biologically important” means “protein.” That’s precisely the reasoning the “which of these is not a protein” trap above is built to catch you skipping.

ATP: the currency every one of these reactions gets paid in

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is a nucleotide — the base adenine, the sugar ribose, and, unusually, three phosphate groups chained together instead of one. Those three phosphates repel each other (all negatively charged, crowded close together), which stores strain energy in the bonds between them. Hydrolyzing the outermost phosphoanhydride bond — ATP + H2O → ADP + Pi — releases roughly 7.3 kcal (≈30.5 kJ) per mole under standard conditions, and cells everywhere from bacteria to your own muscle fibers spend that released energy to drive reactions that wouldn’t run on their own.

Calling ATP a “currency” is precise, not just a metaphor: it is earned in energy-releasing (catabolic) reactions like respiration and spent almost immediately in energy-requiring (anabolic, transport, or mechanical) reactions elsewhere in the cell — the same coupling logic Chapter 3, Energy and Matter, builds into a general framework for how life pays its energetic bills without ever violating thermodynamics.

Where this leaves you

Four families of molecules, one reversible bond-forming reaction (condensation/hydrolysis), and one class of catalyst (the enzyme) that makes the whole system run fast enough to be alive — that’s the chemistry underneath every organelle from Chapter 1 and every process still ahead.
