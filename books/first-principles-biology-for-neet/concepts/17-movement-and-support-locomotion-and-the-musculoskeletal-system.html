Chapter 17 · Part IV — Human Structure & Function

Movement and Support
Locomotion and the Musculoskeletal System

Chapter 16 left the body with its internal chemistry stable — blood cleaned, water and ions rebalanced — but stability alone doesn’t get you anywhere. Every visible thing an animal does — walk, chew, breathe, blink, run from a predator — comes down to the same two-part engineering problem: you need something that can pull (a contractile protein machine), and you need something rigid for it to pull against (a lever). Muscle without bone is a contraction with nothing to show for it; bone without muscle is a rigid, immobile scaffold. This chapter is about how those two halves are built, and about the one molecular mechanism — the sliding filament theory — that explains almost everything NEET will ever ask you about how a muscle actually shortens.

Two ideas that carry the whole chapter

The first idea is that movement always reduces to the same molecular trick — a contractile protein pulling on a track — whether it’s an amoeba oozing across a slide, a sperm lashing its flagellum, or your biceps lifting a cup. Muscular movement is simply that trick scaled up, organized, and wired to nerves for precise, on-demand control. The second idea is that a muscle can only pull, never push — so every joint that needs to move in two directions needs at least two muscles working in opposition (an antagonistic pair), anchored to a rigid skeleton that turns that one-way pull into rotation at a joint, the way a lever turns a pull into a swing.

Core concept

The sliding filament theory says a muscle shortens not because its protein filaments themselves get shorter, but because thin (actin) filaments slide past stationary thick (myosin) filaments, ratcheted along by repeated myosin “cross-bridge” strokes — pulling the sarcomere’s two ends closer together. Every fact in this chapter’s muscle-contraction sections is really just filling in the mechanics of that one sentence.

Three kinds of muscle, one contractile machinery

All muscle contracts by the same actin–myosin sliding mechanism, but three tissue types deploy it very differently depending on the job: fast, precise, voluntary movement of the skeleton; slow, sustained, involuntary work in hollow organs; and rhythmic, tireless, self-triggered pumping in the heart.

NEET trap

Don’t treat “striated = voluntary” as an absolute rule — cardiac muscle is striated but involuntary, and it’s myogenic (it triggers its own rhythmic contraction without needing a nerve impulse first, unlike skeletal muscle).

Skeletal muscle, zoomed in: muscle → fibre → sarcomere

A skeletal muscle is a bundle of fascicles (bundles of muscle fibres), each fascicle wrapped in connective tissue (perimysium). Each individual muscle fibre is a single elongated cell enclosed by a plasma membrane called the sarcolemma, with cytoplasm called sarcoplasm — and because each fibre forms by fusion of many embryonic myoblasts, it ends up as a multinucleate syncytium, with nuclei pushed to the periphery. The sarcoplasm houses a specialized, extensive smooth endoplasmic reticulum called the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) — essentially a dedicated Ca2+ reservoir — whose expanded terminal sacs hug narrow tubular infoldings of the sarcolemma called T-tubules (transverse tubules). That T-tubule/SR contact is the physical link that lets an electrical signal at the cell surface trigger Ca2+ release deep inside the fibre, all the way at its core myofibrils.

Each fibre is packed with hundreds of thread-like myofibrils running its full length, and each myofibril is built from thousands of repeating units called sarcomeres — the actual contractile unit, defined as the stretch of myofibril between two successive Z-lines. A sarcomere contains an interleaved array of thick filaments (myosin) and thin filaments (actin), and it is the alternating pattern of these arrays, sarcomere after sarcomere, that gives skeletal (and cardiac) muscle its striped, striated look under a microscope.

The thick filament is a bundle of roughly 300 myosin molecules; each myosin is a six-part (hexameric) protein with two heavy chains twisted into a rod-like tail, and four light chains at the globular head. Splitting myosin experimentally yields two fragments that map neatly onto its two jobs: light meromyosin (LMM) is the straight tail — purely structural, forming the thick filament’s backbone — while heavy meromyosin (HMM) is the globular head plus its short connecting neck, and it is HMM’s head that carries both the actin-binding site and the ATPase activity that hydrolyzes ATP. That head is the cross-bridge — the one part of the whole thick filament that actually touches actin and does mechanical work.

The thin filament is built around F-actin, a helical polymer of globular G-actin subunits, each carrying a myosin-binding site. Two accessory proteins gate access to those sites: tropomyosin, a long filament lying in the groove of the actin helix that physically covers the binding sites at rest, and troponin, a three-subunit complex (TnC, TnI, TnT) spaced along the tropomyosin that acts as the Ca2+ sensor — TnC is what actually binds calcium, TnT anchors the complex to tropomyosin, and TnI holds the whole assembly in its inhibitory, binding-site-blocked position until calcium arrives.

NEET trap

HMM (the head) does the binding and the ATP hydrolysis; LMM (the tail) is purely structural — don’t swap them. And keep troponin and tropomyosin separated by role: troponin senses Ca2+ (via its TnC subunit); tropomyosin physically blocks or exposes the myosin-binding site. Troponin doesn’t block anything directly — it moves tropomyosin.

The sliding filament theory: the full sequence, in order

Contraction begins outside the muscle fibre entirely, at a nerve ending, and ends with a mechanical pull — the whole chain is an electrochemical signal converted, step by step, into force.

  1. Neuromuscular junction: a nerve impulse reaches the motor neuron’s axon terminal, releasing acetylcholine (ACh) into the synaptic cleft.
  2. Sarcolemma depolarizes: ACh binds receptors on the motor end plate, generating an end-plate potential that fires an action potential across the sarcolemma.
  3. T-tubule conduction: the action potential is carried rapidly into the fibre’s interior along the T-tubules.
  4. Ca2+ release: the signal triggers the sarcoplasmic reticulum to dump Ca2+ into the sarcoplasm.
  5. Ca2+ binds troponin (TnC): this reshapes the troponin–tropomyosin complex.
  6. Binding sites exposed: tropomyosin shifts off the actin groove, uncovering the myosin-binding sites.
  7. Cross-bridge forms: an energized myosin head (already primed with ADP + Pi from a prior cycle) binds actin.
  8. Power stroke: releasing Pi swings the myosin head, pulling the thin filament toward the sarcomere’s centre; ADP is released next.
  9. Detachment: a fresh ATP molecule binds the myosin head, and the head lets go of actin.
  10. Re-cocking: myosin’s own ATPase hydrolyzes that ATP to ADP + Pi, resetting the head to its high-energy, ready-to-bind state — and as long as Ca2+ and ATP keep coming, the cycle repeats, ratcheting the filaments further past each other.
  11. Relaxation: once nervous stimulation stops, Ca2+ is actively pumped back into the SR; falling Ca2+ lets tropomyosin re-cover the binding sites, cross-bridges can no longer form, and the sarcomere lengthens back out.
Core concept

ATP is needed twice in this cycle, for two opposite jobs: once to break the actin–myosin cross-bridge (detachment, step 9), and once — via hydrolysis — to re-cock the head for the next stroke (step 10). It is not spent on the power stroke itself, which runs on the energy already stored in the myosin head from the previous cycle’s hydrolysis.

NEET trap

Rigor mortis is the direct, testable consequence of that last point. After death, respiration stops, so ATP production stops — but with no ATP available, myosin heads that are already attached to actin cannot detach (step 9 needs ATP), while leaking Ca2+ keeps triggering cross-bridge formation. The result is sustained, generalized muscle stiffening, not relaxation — a useful reminder that ATP is required for a muscle to let go, not just to contract.

Red vs. white fibres: sprinting muscle vs. marathon muscle

Not every skeletal muscle fibre is tuned the same way. Red fibres are rich in myoglobin (an oxygen-storing pigment, the source of the colour) and mitochondria, run mostly on aerobic respiration, and are slow but highly fatigue-resistant — built for sustained activity like posture and endurance work. White fibres carry little myoglobin, rely on anaerobic (glycolytic) metabolism, and contract fast and forcefully but fatigue quickly — built for short bursts of power. Most real muscles are a mix of both, in proportions that shift with training.

The skeleton: 206 bones, two divisions

The adult human skeleton has 206 bones, split into an axial skeleton (80 bones — the body’s central axis: skull, vertebral column, ribs, sternum, plus the tiny ear ossicles and the hyoid) and an appendicular skeleton (126 bones — the limbs and their girdles, the parts that actually do most of the moving).

The 12 rib pairs split into three groups by how they attach to the sternum: true ribs (pairs 1–7) attach directly via their own costal cartilage; false ribs (pairs 8–10) attach indirectly, via the cartilage of the rib above; floating ribs (pairs 11–12) don’t attach to the sternum at all.

NEET trap

Two counting mistakes account for most wrong answers here. Forgetting the ear ossicles (6) and hyoid (1) belong to the axial skeleton drops the axial total from 80 to 73. Forgetting the patella is one of the 30 bones counted in each hindlimb undercounts the appendicular total by 2 (124 instead of 126). Both errors still add up to the same final total of 206 only if you make neither — check both counts, not just the headline number.

Joints: where two bones meet, and how much they’re allowed to move

Joints are classified first by how much movement they permit. Fibrous joints allow no movement — bones are locked together by fibrous tissue, as in the skull’s sutures. Cartilaginous joints allow slight movement, cushioned by cartilage — the pubic symphysis and the discs between vertebrae are examples. Synovial joints are freely movable, built around a fluid-filled synovial cavity enclosed by a joint capsule, with a synovial membrane secreting lubricating synovial fluid and the bone ends capped in smooth articular cartilage — this is the design that gives limbs their range of motion, and it comes in several distinct geometries depending on what kind of motion a particular joint needs.

NEET trap

The atlas–axis joint (the joint that lets you shake your head “no”) is a pivot joint — rotation only — and is frequently mis-labelled a hinge or gliding joint. Keep pivot (rotation) and hinge (flexion/extension in one plane) mentally separate.

When force generation or leverage fails

Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disease in which antibodies attack the ACh receptors at the neuromuscular junction itself, blunting the very first step of the contraction sequence — the result is progressive muscle weakness and fatigability, distinct from disorders of the contractile apparatus. Muscular dystrophy is progressive, usually genetic degeneration of skeletal muscle tissue. Tetany is uncontrolled, sustained muscle spasm caused by low blood Ca2+ (hypocalcemia) — note this is the opposite direction of rigor mortis, but both ultimately trace back to disrupted Ca2+ handling in the contraction cycle.

On the skeletal side, arthritis covers several distinct joint diseases: osteoarthritis is degeneration of articular cartilage from wear and age; rheumatoid arthritis is autoimmune inflammation of the synovial membrane that damages cartilage and bone; gout is joint inflammation from deposited uric acid crystals. Osteoporosis is age-related loss of bone mass and density, raising fracture risk — it’s especially common in postmenopausal women because of falling oestrogen levels, and is worsened by inadequate calcium and vitamin D.

Think first: hypocalcemia (low blood Ca²♠) causes tetany — uncontrolled muscle spasm — while rigor mortis after death also leaves muscle locked in contraction. If tetany is caused by too much Ca²♠ signalling and rigor mortis is caused by too little ATP, why do both end up looking like the “same” symptom — a muscle that won’t relax?

Because relaxation, in the sliding filament model, isn’t one event — it depends on two separate conditions both being satisfied: Ca2+ must be low enough that tropomyosin re-covers the myosin-binding sites, and ATP must be available so that any already-formed cross-bridges can actually detach. Tetany breaks the first condition — low blood Ca2+ paradoxically triggers abnormal, uncontrolled nerve/muscle excitability that keeps forcing Ca2+ release and binding sites exposed, so cross-bridges keep re-forming faster than the muscle can relax. Rigor mortis breaks the second condition from the opposite direction — Ca2+ is present (leaking freely with no ATP-driven pump to remove it), but with zero ATP, cross-bridges that do form can never let go of actin. Two different upstream failures, converging on the same downstream symptom, because both routes deny the muscle at least one of the two things it needs to unlock a cross-bridge: low Ca2+ and available ATP.

Where this leaves you

Every contraction in this chapter started the same way: a nerve impulse arriving at a neuromuscular junction and releasing acetylcholine. That’s only one small, local instance of a much larger system — the network of neurons that generates, conducts, and integrates signals across the entire body, and the hormonal system that works alongside it on a slower timescale. Chapter 18 opens that system up: how a nerve impulse is actually generated and conducted, how one neuron hands a signal to the next, and how neural and chemical control together keep the body coordinated.
