Chapter 28 · Part VII — Ecology

Biodiversity, Conservation, and Environmental Issues

Chapter 27 ended by zooming out to a single ecosystem — one functioning unit of energy flow and nutrient cycling. This final chapter zooms out once more, to every ecosystem's worth of variation happening on the planet at once: the sheer number of different life-forms doing that flowing and cycling, in every conceivable way. That variety has a name — biodiversity — and it is not decorative. It is what keeps ecosystems stable, productive, and able to absorb a shock without collapsing. This chapter covers three things NCERT treats as one continuous story: what biodiversity is and why losing it matters, what conservation actually does about that loss, and the environmental pressures — pollution, warming, ozone depletion, deforestation — that put biodiversity under threat in the first place.

Three different things people mean by "biodiversity"

The term itself — coined by Edward Wilson — is often used loosely to mean "lots of species," but NCERT is precise about it: biodiversity operates at three distinct, nested levels, and a question can be probing any one of them without saying so explicitly.

Core concept — genetic, species, ecosystem

Genetic diversity is variation within a single species — the reason a rice field in India can carry thousands of genetically distinct varieties, or Rauwolfia vomitoria populations across the Himalayan range vary in the potency of the alkaloid reserpine they produce. Species diversity is the variety of species within a region — the count and evenness that Chapter 26 first introduced (recall species richness and the Shannon index). Ecosystem diversity is variety at the largest scale — the range of distinct habitats and ecological processes present, from a desert to a rainforest to a coral reef within one country. Losing genetic diversity within a crop and losing an entire ecosystem type are both "loss of biodiversity," but they are not the same failure, and NEET tests the distinction directly.

Two patterns: richer toward the equator, richer with more area

Species richness is not spread evenly across the globe, and it is not spread evenly across area either — both patterns are remarkably consistent, and both are classic NEET material.

The latitudinal gradient: species richness generally increases as you move from the poles toward the equator. A hectare of tropical rainforest in Ecuador or the Amazon holds vastly more plant and insect species than a hectare of temperate or boreal forest at the same size. Several explanations are offered together rather than as competing alternatives — tropical regions have had a longer, more stable evolutionary history undisturbed by glaciation, giving more time for speciation; a more constant, predictable climate supports finer niche specialization; and higher solar energy input drives greater overall productivity, supporting more consumers per unit area.

The species-area relationship, first described by Alexander von Humboldt: within a region, species richness increases with explored area, but not in a straight line — it rises steeply at first and then flattens into a curve approaching a saturation point (a rectangular hyperbola). log S = log C + Z (log A)
NEET trap

The Z value quoted as "0.1 to 0.2" only applies when comparing areas within one region. When ecologists instead compare species richness across entire continents against one another, the slope steepens sharply to roughly 0.6 to 1.2. A question that gives a Z value near 1 is describing an inter-continental comparison, not a within-region one — don't default to "0.1–0.2" for every species-area question you see.

Why biodiversity matters: three value systems, and a scale problem

NCERT frames the case for caring about biodiversity as three overlapping justifications rather than one:

ValueWhat it meansExample
Narrowly utilitarianDirect, tangible economic benefit humans extractFood, fuel, fibre, timber, and drugs derived directly from wild or cultivated species
Broadly utilitarianIndirect ecological services biodiversity performs, which have no simple price tag but are essentialOxygen production and carbon fixation by photosynthesis, pollination, climate regulation, aesthetic and cultural value
EthicalEvery species has intrinsic value and a right to exist, independent of any use to humansThe argument for conserving a species with no known economic use at all

The scale of what's actually at stake is itself a NEET-relevant number worth anchoring: taxonomists have formally described somewhere around 1.5 to 1.8 million species so far, but that is understood to be a small fraction of what actually exists — estimates for the true global total run from roughly 7 million (Robert May's commonly cited figure) up past 8.7 million in more recent broad counts, meaning the majority of species alive today have never been formally recorded. Among the species that have been assessed for conservation status, amphibians consistently come out as the most threatened vertebrate group — their permeable skin and dual aquatic/terrestrial life cycle make them unusually sensitive to habitat and water-quality change.

Losing it: the "Evil Quartet"

NCERT names four causes of biodiversity loss as a set, nicknamed the Evil Quartet — and NEET likes to test whether you can match a described scenario to the right one of the four:

CauseWhat it isExample
Habitat loss and fragmentationThe single most important cause — destroying or breaking up a habitat into disconnected patches too small to sustain a populationClearing tropical rainforest for agriculture, roads, and settlements (Amazon rainforest loss); fragmentation of the Western Ghats
Over-exploitationHarvesting a species faster than it can reproduce and replace itselfThe extinction of Steller's sea cow within 27 years of its discovery; the passenger pigeon's extinction from overhunting
Alien species invasionAn introduced species outcompetes, preys on, or otherwise displaces native speciesNile perch introduced into Lake Victoria driving over 200 native cichlid fish species to extinction; Parthenium, Lantana, and water hyacinth (Eichhornia) invading Indian habitats
Co-extinctionWhen one species goes extinct, an obligately dependent species (a specialist parasite, pollinator, or symbiont) goes extinct with itA host-specific parasitic insect disappearing along with the sole host species it depended on
NEET trap

Of the four, habitat loss and fragmentation is explicitly the most significant cause of biodiversity loss according to NCERT — if a question asks which single factor contributes most, that's the answer, not over-exploitation or invasive species, even though those two get more memorable individual examples (Steller's sea cow, Nile perch).

Tracking the loss: IUCN categories and the Red Data Book

The IUCN Red List sorts assessed species into categories of extinction risk, and the Red Data Book is the maintained record of that status for a country or the world. Reading the categories in order of increasing risk (with the least concerning first):

CategoryMeaning
Least ConcernWidespread and abundant; not currently at risk
Near ThreatenedClose to qualifying for a threatened category soon
VulnerableFaces a high risk of extinction in the wild in the medium term
EndangeredFaces a very high risk of extinction in the near future
Critically EndangeredFaces an extremely high risk of extinction in the immediate future
Extinct in the WildSurvives only in cultivation or captivity, or as a population well outside its historic range
ExtinctNo reasonable doubt that the last individual has died

India has already lost species within recorded history — the Asiatic cheetah, the pink-headed duck, and the mountain quail are the standard NCERT examples, none of which have been confirmed alive in India for decades. Extinction is not a hypothetical future concern in this chapter's framing — it is documented, ongoing, and the whole reason conservation strategy exists as a discipline.

Conservation: protect it where it lives, or back it up where it doesn't

NCERT splits conservation strategy into two complementary approaches, and a NEET question naming a specific institution is really just asking which category it belongs to:

In-situEx-situ
MeaningConserving species within their natural habitatConserving species outside their natural habitat, under human care
ExamplesNational parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves, sacred grovesZoological parks, botanical gardens, wildlife safari parks, seed banks, in-vitro fertilisation, tissue culture, cryopreservation of gametes
Trade-offPreserves the species and the ecological relationships and processes it participates in, but demands protecting a whole functioning landscapeCan rescue a species on the very edge of extinction with intensive, targeted intervention, but the species is removed from its natural ecological context

Within in-situ conservation, NCERT singles out hotspots — regions with exceptionally high species richness and a high degree of endemism (species found nowhere else) that are simultaneously under the most severe threat of habitat loss. NCERT's original count put the number of global hotspots at 34; more recent international reassessments (Conservation International) have refined that figure up to 36. India sits within four of them — the Western Ghats & Sri Lanka, the Himalaya, Indo-Burma, and Sundaland (the Nicobar Islands) — and NCERT makes a specific, striking claim about the payoff of protecting them: strict protection of hotspots alone, which together cover barely 2–2.5% of Earth's land surface, could reduce the ongoing rate of species extinction by roughly 30%.

India also has a home-grown, pre-modern form of in-situ conservation: sacred groves — patches of forest around a temple or shrine, traditionally left completely undisturbed by local communities on religious grounds. They survive today in the Western Ghats regions of Karnataka, Maharashtra, and Kerala, in Meghalaya, and in parts of Rajasthan, and often harbour rare or locally endemic species that have vanished from the surrounding, exploited landscape entirely on the strength of cultural protection alone.

Environmental issues I: pollution

Air pollution in an urban Indian context is dominated by vehicular and industrial emissions — particulate matter, oxides of sulphur and nitrogen, carbon monoxide, and unburnt hydrocarbons. NCERT's standard case study is Delhi's shift of its public transport fleet to Compressed Natural Gas (CNG) starting in the early 2000s, following a Supreme Court directive, which reduced particulate and CO emissions substantially compared to diesel. Control measures more generally include catalytic converters (which use platinum-palladium catalysts to convert unburnt hydrocarbons and CO into CO₂ and water, and NOx into N₂), electrostatic precipitators removing particulate matter from industrial exhaust, and scrubbers for gaseous pollutants.

Water pollution is measured practically through Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD) — the amount of oxygen microorganisms consume decomposing organic matter in a water sample. High BOD signals heavy organic pollution (sewage, industrial effluent), because it means most of the dissolved oxygen is being used up by decomposers rather than staying available for aquatic life.

Core concept — eutrophication

Excess nutrients (especially nitrates and phosphates from fertiliser run-off and sewage) trigger explosive algal growth — an algal bloom. This is cultural (accelerated) eutrophication when human activity is the nutrient source, distinct from the slow, natural eutrophication a lake undergoes over geological time as it ages and fills with sediment. When the bloom eventually dies, decomposers consuming the dead algae consume dissolved oxygen faster than it's replenished, crashing oxygen levels and causing large-scale fish kills — an ecosystem effectively suffocating itself on its own excess nutrient supply.

A second, distinct water-pollution mechanism is biomagnification: a chemical that isn't broken down (a non-biodegradable pollutant like DDT) becomes more concentrated at each successive trophic level, because each level eats a large mass of the level below and retains the pollutant in its tissue rather than excreting it. NCERT's standard example is DDT in an aquatic food chain: a small starting concentration in water rises step by step through phytoplankton, zooplankton, small fish, and large fish, until concentrations in birds at the top of the chain are high enough to interfere with calcium metabolism and thin their eggshells — a mechanism with no analogue in the energy-flow picture from Chapter 27, where each transfer loses material rather than concentrating it.

Municipal solid waste and increasingly e-waste (discarded electronics, containing heavy metals like lead, cadmium, and mercury) present a separate disposal problem entirely, and NCERT's broad prescription is the 3Rs: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle. Agrochemicals — synthetic fertilisers and pesticides — boost yield but contribute to both eutrophication (fertiliser run-off) and biomagnification (persistent pesticides), tying this section back to the same two mechanisms above.

Environmental issues II: global warming and ozone depletion

These two are frequently confused because both involve the atmosphere and both are commonly (and wrongly) blamed on "pollution" in general terms — but they are different gases, different layers of the atmosphere, and different mechanisms entirely.

Greenhouse gasApproximate share of warming effect
Carbon dioxide (CO₂)~60%
Methane (CH₄)~20%
Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)~14%
Nitrous oxide (N₂O)~6%

The greenhouse effect itself is not inherently harmful — it's the reason Earth's surface is warm enough to support life at all: these gases trap outgoing infrared radiation in the lower atmosphere (the troposphere) rather than letting it all escape to space. The problem NCERT flags is the rate of increase in their concentration since industrialisation, chiefly from fossil fuel combustion and deforestation (fewer photosynthesising producers to draw CO₂ back down), which is intensifying the natural effect faster than ecosystems can adapt — raising average global temperature, melting glaciers and polar ice, and driving up sea level.

Core concept — ozone depletion is a different layer, a different gas, a different mechanism

Stratospheric ozone (O₃) — not tropospheric greenhouse gas — forms a protective layer high in the atmosphere that absorbs incoming harmful UV-B radiation before it reaches the surface. CFCs (used historically as refrigerants and aerosol propellants) are chemically stable enough to drift, undegraded, all the way up into the stratosphere, where intense UV radiation finally breaks them apart, releasing chlorine free radicals. Each chlorine radical then catalytically destroys thousands of ozone molecules without being consumed itself, thinning the layer — most dramatically over Antarctica, where the seasonal "ozone hole" was first detected. Less stratospheric ozone means more UV-B reaching the surface, increasing skin cancer and cataract risk and damaging crop yields. The international response, the Montreal Protocol (1987), phased out CFC production worldwide and is the most-cited example of a successful global environmental treaty in the NCERT syllabus.

NEET trap

CFCs appear in both tables above — they are a greenhouse gas and the primary cause of ozone depletion — but the two effects happen in different atmospheric layers by different mechanisms, and a question about "the cause of the ozone hole" wants the stratospheric UV-triggered chlorine radical mechanism specifically, not a generic "greenhouse gas" answer. Don't answer an ozone-depletion question with a greenhouse-effect explanation, or vice versa.

Deforestation and the case studies NCERT names by name

Deforestation compounds nearly everything above at once: it is the leading driver of habitat loss (the Evil Quartet's top cause), it removes a major CO₂ sink (worsening the greenhouse effect), and it degrades soil and watershed function. NCERT anchors this with a specific Indian environmental movement rather than leaving it abstract:

Core concept — the Chipko Movement

In 1974, in Reni village in the Garhwal Himalaya (Uttarakhand), local villagers — women prominently among them, led by figures including Gaura Devi, with Sunderlal Bahuguna as a leading voice for the wider movement — physically embraced ("chipko" means "to stick to" or "to hug") standing trees to block a logging contractor from felling them. The movement succeeded in halting the felling and grew into a broader grassroots forest-conservation campaign across the Himalayan region, and it's NCERT's standard example of community-driven, non-violent environmental protection preceding formal government policy. It also fed directly into Joint Forest Management (JFM), the policy approach that formally involves local communities in protecting and managing forest land in partnership with the state forest department, on the logic that people who depend on a forest for their livelihood have the strongest incentive to conserve it.

Think first: DDT sprayed on a field ends up in a lake at a tiny, barely measurable concentration. How does a fish-eating bird at the top of that lake's food chain end up with a dangerously high body burden of the exact same chemical?

Because DDT is non-biodegradable and fat-soluble — each organism that eats it stores it in tissue rather than excreting or breaking it down, so almost none of it is lost at each step, unlike energy, which Chapter 27 established is mostly lost as heat at every trophic transfer. A phytoplankton cell absorbs a small amount of DDT from the water; a zooplankton eats thousands of phytoplankton and retains the DDT from all of them in its own tissue; a small fish eats thousands of zooplankton and retains their accumulated DDT; a large fish eats many small fish, and a fish-eating bird eats many large fish over its lifetime. At every step, biomass shrinks (the normal energy-pyramid pattern) but the DDT doesn't shrink with it — it rides along, concentrating into an ever-smaller total mass of tissue. The bird at the top ends up carrying the accumulated, undiluted burden of every organism beneath it in the chain, which is exactly why a top predator is the place you'd look first for evidence of a persistent pollutant nobody sprayed anywhere near it directly.

Where this leaves you — and where the book leaves you

This chapter's shape mirrors the whole book's, one more time at the largest scale it reaches: biodiversity is worth protecting for reasons ranging from the directly economic to the purely ethical; it is being lost through a specific, nameable set of human-driven causes rather than vaguely "human impact"; and both conservation strategy and environmental policy respond to those specific causes with specific, matched tools — a protected core zone for a habitat under pressure, a seed bank for a crop losing genetic diversity, an international treaty for a chemical drifting into the stratosphere. None of it works by accident, and none of it is memorization for its own sake — every mechanism here follows the same kind of first-principles reasoning that opened this book at the level of a single cell.

That is really the whole arc of this book, start to finish: Chapter 1 asked what makes a cell alive; this chapter asks what makes a planet's worth of those cells' descendants worth keeping alive, together. The biology underneath both questions is the same — energy has to flow, matter has to cycle, and the system has to stay diverse enough to keep doing both under changing conditions. Go back to NCERT with that reasoning in hand, and then go solve the questions that test it.
