Chapter 03 · Part I — Foundations

Energy and Matter
Thermodynamics for Biologists

Chapter 2 left you with ATP as a spendable currency and one loose thread: where does the energy in that currency actually come from, and what does physics let a cell do with it? The answer runs through two laws that govern every reaction in the universe, alive or not — and a cell breaks neither of them. It just plays a subtler game than “build more order, full stop.” It trades energy captured from outside for order built inside, one exergonic–endergonic pair at a time, and this chapter is the accounting system behind every energy-related fact you’ll meet for the rest of the book.

Class 11 — Photosynthesis in Higher Plants, Respiration in Plants (bioenergetics context, Unit: Plant Physiology); Class 12 — Ecosystem (energy flow, forward reference)
NEET angle
Photosynthesis-vs-respiration comparison questions, the “doesn’t life violate the second law” paradox, and energy-pyramid / 10% law numericals.

The first law: organisms transform energy, they never create it

The first law of thermodynamics says energy can be neither created nor destroyed, only converted from one form into another. Nothing alive gets an exception. A leaf doesn’t manufacture energy from nothing when it photosynthesizes — it converts light energy arriving from the sun into the chemical bond energy of glucose. A mitochondrion doesn’t manufacture energy either — it converts that same chemical bond energy into the phosphate-bond energy of ATP, releasing the rest as heat. A contracting muscle converts ATP’s chemical energy into mechanical work, again releasing some as heat. At every step in this book, from here to the last chapter, “where does the energy come from” has one answer: somewhere else, in a different form — never nowhere.

Core concept

Living organisms are energy transducers, not energy generators. Trace almost any biological energy transformation back far enough and it terminates at sunlight captured by a photosynthetic producer — which is exactly why Chapter 10 opens with photosynthesis rather than respiration. (The one well-known exception NEET occasionally tests: chemosynthetic bacteria, which oxidize inorganic compounds like H2S instead of capturing light, support life around deep-sea hydrothermal vents with no sunlight involved at all.)

Notice something else buried in those three examples: heat shows up at every conversion. No biological energy transfer is ever 100% efficient — some fraction always degrades to heat and escapes the system. That’s not sloppy engineering on life’s part. It’s a second, independent law forcing its hand, and it’s the same law that explains why a food chain can only run so many trophic levels deep.

The second law, and biology’s favorite “paradox”

The second law of thermodynamics says that in an isolated system (one that exchanges neither matter nor energy with its surroundings), entropy — a measure of disorder, or more precisely how spread out and unusable a system’s energy has become — only ever increases. Left alone, things drift toward disorder, not away from it: a hot cup of coffee cools to room temperature and never spontaneously reheats itself; a drop of ink diffuses through water and never spontaneously regathers into a drop.

Now look at a cell. It is extraordinarily ordered — folded proteins, organized membranes, a genome copied letter-for-letter. It looks like a standing violation of the second law, and this exact observation is a favorite NEET-style “paradox” setup. The resolution is that a cell is never an isolated system — it is an open system, constantly exchanging both matter and energy with its surroundings. It imports usable, low-entropy energy (sunlight, or the chemical energy in food) and exports high-entropy waste (heat, CO2, metabolic byproducts). The cell’s own entropy can fall exactly because it’s paying for that drop by raising the entropy of everything around it by more — the physicist Erwin Schrödinger, writing about exactly this puzzle in 1944, described a living organism as feeding on this steady export of disorder to its surroundings. Total entropy, cell plus surroundings, still only ever goes up. The second law survives untouched; it just never applied to the cell in isolation to begin with.

NEET trap

“Photosynthesis builds an ordered six-carbon sugar out of loose CO2 and H2O — doesn’t that violate the second law?” No, for the same reason a refrigerator doesn’t: it isn’t a spontaneous, energy-free process. It runs only with continuous light energy pouring in, and the entropy bill is paid many times over by the sun and by heat lost at every step of the light and dark reactions. The second law governs an isolated system plus its surroundings — never an organism considered alone. Whenever a question dresses up “order from disorder” as a contradiction, check whether energy is flowing in from outside; if it is, there’s no paradox.

Free energy: the one number that says whether a reaction runs on its own

The first law tells you energy is conserved; the second law tells you disorder wins in an isolated system. Put them together in one quantity and you get Gibbs free energy (G) — specifically its change, ΔG, which tells you whether a reaction can proceed on its own, without any outside energy push:

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

where ΔH is the change in the system’s total bond energy (enthalpy), T is absolute temperature, and ΔS is the change in entropy. You don’t need to compute this for NEET — you need the sign of the answer:

  • Exergonic reaction (ΔG < 0) — products hold less free energy than reactants; energy is released; the reaction is spontaneous and can, in principle, run without outside energy input. Catabolic reactions — respiration breaking down glucose, digestion breaking down food, ATP hydrolysis — are exergonic.
  • Endergonic reaction (ΔG > 0) — products hold more free energy than reactants; energy must be invested; the reaction is non-spontaneous and cannot run on its own. Anabolic reactions — photosynthesis building glucose, protein synthesis, DNA replication, ATP synthesis itself — are endergonic.
Core concept

Keep ΔG (thermodynamics: will it happen, and which way) and activation energy (kinetics: how fast) firmly separate — NEET tests the difference directly. “Spontaneous” does not mean “instant.” Diamond converting to graphite is spontaneous (ΔG < 0) yet immeasurably slow at room temperature, because the activation-energy barrier is enormous. An enzyme (Chapter 2) can make an exergonic biological reaction fast enough to matter on a cellular timescale, but it can never make an endergonic reaction spontaneous — it changes the barrier’s height, never ΔG itself.

Coupling: how a cell pays for an uphill reaction

An endergonic reaction cannot run alone — but it can run if it’s paired, step for step, with a strongly exergonic reaction that supplies more free energy than the endergonic step needs. This pairing is called energy coupling, and in a cell the intermediary is almost always ATP. Catabolic, exergonic reactions (glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, the electron transport chain — all of Chapter 10’s respiration half) drive the endergonic synthesis of ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate. That ATP is then hydrolyzed — itself exergonic — to drive endergonic reactions elsewhere: building a polypeptide, pumping an ion against its gradient, sliding a muscle filament. The coupling mechanism is usually direct, not just proximity: an enzyme transfers ATP’s terminal phosphate group onto a substrate (phosphorylation), and that phosphorylated substrate is now higher-energy and reactive enough to complete the endergonic step it couldn’t manage unmodified.

This is worth stating precisely, because “ATP stores energy in its bonds” is a phrase that misleads as often as it helps.

NEET trap

“High-energy phosphate bond” sounds like the bond itself is a tiny battery storing energy the way a compressed spring does. It isn’t. ATP hydrolysis is exergonic (ΔG°′ ≈ −7.3 kcal/mol, Chapter 2) because the products — ADP and Pi — are far more stable than ATP, once they’re relieved of the electrostatic repulsion between three crowded negative phosphate groups and once the free phosphate is resonance-stabilized. Read “ATP is a high-energy molecule” as shorthand for “ATP hydrolysis has a large negative ΔG,” not as “the bond itself is a store of energy” — the second reading is the one that produces wrong answers about where the energy actually comes from.

Matter cycles. Energy doesn’t.

Zoom out from one cell to a whole ecosystem and the first and second laws produce one more consequence worth holding onto as a single sentence: matter is reused; energy is not. A carbon atom fixed into glucose by a plant today might be respired back to CO2 tomorrow, fixed by another plant next year, built into an animal’s protein the year after, and released again on that animal’s death and decomposition — around and around, indefinitely, through the biogeochemical cycles (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water) that later chapters on ecosystems return to. The same atoms genuinely get reused.

Energy cannot make that same round trip. Once concentrated chemical or light energy has been transformed and some of it degraded to heat — which the second law guarantees happens at every transfer — that heat disperses and is lost from the biosphere as infrared radiation into space. No organism can gather scattered heat back up and reconcentrate it into usable chemical energy. That is exactly why every ecosystem needs a continuous energy input (almost always sunlight) to keep functioning at all, while it needs no equivalent continuous matter input — matter just keeps circulating through the same closed loop.

Core concept

This is the chapter’s title, compressed to one line: energy flows through an ecosystem in one direction and is progressively lost as heat; matter cycles through it repeatedly and is conserved. Every “why does an ecosystem need sunlight but not a constant supply of new carbon atoms” question is really just this distinction, asked from a different angle.

Where the energy actually goes: trophic transfer and the 10% law

Producers (green plants, algae, cyanobacteria) capture light energy and fix it into organic matter at a rate called gross primary productivity (GPP) — the total energy a producer captures via photosynthesis. Some of that is immediately spent on the producer’s own respiration (R); what’s left over, available to build new biomass or be eaten, is net primary productivity: NPP = GPP − R. NPP is the energy actually on offer to the next trophic level — the primary consumers.

And most of that offer goes unclaimed. Raymond Lindeman’s ten percent law (1942) states that, on average, only about 10% of the energy present at one trophic level is transferred to and stored as biomass at the next; the remaining ≈90% is lost as heat through respiration, spent on movement and maintenance, or left unconsumed and undigested. Run that 10% loss through a few levels — producers 100%, primary consumers ≈10%, secondary consumers ≈1%, tertiary consumers ≈0.1% — and you get both the answer to “why do food chains rarely exceed four or five trophic levels” (there simply isn’t enough energy left to support one more level) and the shape of every pyramid of energy ever drawn: wide at the base, narrowing sharply toward the apex, always upright, in every ecosystem, without exception.

NEET trap

“Ecological pyramids are always upright” is true for only one of the three kinds. A pyramid of numbers can invert (thousands of insects feeding on one large tree). A pyramid of biomass can invert too (in a pond, the standing crop of phytoplankton at any instant is tiny compared to the fish biomass it supports, because phytoplankton turn over — reproduce and get eaten — extremely fast). Only the pyramid of energy is upright without exception in every ecosystem, because it plots a rate of energy flow over time, not a snapshot of standing mass or headcount, and the second law guarantees that rate only shrinks moving up the chain. If a question says “always upright, no exceptions,” the safe answer is energy.

Two energy transformations that mirror each other

Photosynthesis and respiration are the two biological reactions this whole chapter has been building toward, and NEET compares them constantly — so it’s worth previewing them here as a matched pair of opposites before Chapter 10 opens up their internal machinery (light reactions, the Calvin cycle, glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, the electron transport chain) in full.

Read that equation row as literal mirror images — respiration’s reactants are photosynthesis’s products, and vice versa — and most of the “compare photosynthesis and respiration” question stems NEET asks become pattern recognition instead of a memorized table.

Think first: respiration releases energy (it’s exergonic), but making ATP from ADP + Pi is itself endergonic. So is respiration exergonic or endergonic, overall?

Exergonic overall — and the apparent contradiction is exactly what “coupling” resolves. Fully oxidizing one mole of glucose to CO2 and H2O releases roughly 686 kcal of free energy. Only a fraction of that is captured in the endergonic step of stitching phosphate onto ADP to make ATP; the rest is released directly as heat along the way (which, incidentally, is a large part of how endothermic animals like you maintain body temperature). So respiration is really two processes running together: a very large exergonic reaction (glucose oxidation) driving a smaller endergonic one (ATP synthesis) via coupling, with heat making up the difference. The net ΔG for the whole system is still strongly negative — it has to be, or respiration couldn’t run spontaneously at all — even though one sub-step inside it is locally endergonic. This is precisely what “energy coupling” means in practice, not just in definition.

Where this leaves you

Two laws, one accounting rule: energy is conserved but never fully usable twice, so every biological transfer leaks some of it as heat, no exceptions. A cell stays ordered only by exporting that disorder outward; an endergonic reaction runs only when coupled to a sufficiently exergonic one, usually through ATP; matter cycles through the biosphere while energy passes through it once and is gone. Hold onto that last sentence especially — it’s the thread that ties this chapter to photosynthesis and respiration’s full machinery in Chapter 10, and to the trophic pyramids and productivity numbers waiting in Ecosystems near the end of this book. Next, though, the story turns from energy to information: Chapter 4 asks how a cell stores, copies, and reads the instructions that tell all of this machinery what to build in the first place.
