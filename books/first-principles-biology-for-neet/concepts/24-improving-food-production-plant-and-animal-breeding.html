Chapter 24 · Part VI — Applications: Health, Food & Biotechnology

Improving Food Production – Plant and Animal Breeding

Chapter 23 ended on a hand-off: microbes had done their part — fermenting, digesting, fixing nitrogen — and the lever we hadn't touched yet was the organism actually being grown or raised. This chapter pulls that lever directly. Every technique here — plant breeding, tissue culture, single-cell protein, animal breeding — is a variation on one move: find or create genetic variation, then select the individuals that combine the traits you want, generation after generation. None of it is new biology either. A wheat plant already varies in yield, height, and disease resistance from one individual to the next; a breeder's whole job is choosing which of those individuals gets to become next year's parent.

NCERT mapping
Class 12 — Strategies for Enhancement in Food Production (plant breeding, single cell proteins, tissue culture and micropropagation, animal husbandry and animal breeding).
NEET angle
Steps of plant breeding in order, crop–variety–resistance matching (Himgiri, Pusa Sawani, Parbhani Kranti…), biofortification vs. SCP vs. tissue culture — which problem each one actually solves, inbreeding vs. the three types of outbreeding, the sequence of steps in MOET, and totipotency as the principle behind micropropagation.

One organizing idea: variation is raw material, selection is the tool

Every method in this chapter answers one of two questions. Where do I get genetic variation I don't already have? — cross two different parents, induce a mutation, cross two different species, or simply go and collect wild relatives that already carry a useful gene. And once I have a population with variation in it, how do I keep only the individuals I want? — test them, breed the best ones to each other, and repeat over generations. Plant breeding and animal breeding are the same logic applied to two different kinds of organisms with two very different generation times and reproductive biology, which is exactly why their toolkits look different on the surface but rhyme underneath.

Core concept

Read every technique in this chapter as an answer to "where's the variation from?" and "how do I fix the good combination?" — hybridization crosses two parents to combine their variation; mutation breeding and wide (interspecific) crosses reach outside a species' normal gene pool for variation it doesn't already have; tissue culture and MOET don't create new variation at all — they multiply a genotype you've already selected, faster and in greater numbers than normal reproduction would allow.

Plant breeding: a four-step cycle, run over years

Classical plant breeding follows the same four steps for almost any crop and almost any goal — higher yield, disease resistance, drought tolerance, better quality. First, collection of variability: assemble every available strain, land race, and wild relative of the crop — its germplasm collection — because you can't select for a trait that isn't present anywhere in your starting material. Second, evaluation and selection of parents: screen that germplasm to identify individuals with the specific desirable traits you're after, and choose the best of them as parents. Third, cross hybridization: deliberately cross the selected parents so their desirable traits end up combined in the same offspring — this is the step where the plant's own biology has to be overridden, since a normally self-pollinating flower will fertilize itself before a breeder can get pollen from a different parent onto it. Fourth, selection and testing of superior recombinants: among the hybrid progeny, identify the individuals that actually inherited the best combination of traits, and evaluate them rigorously — including multi-location field trials — before a new variety is released to farmers.

Step 3 is where the biology fights back. Most crop flowers are bisexual and happily self-pollinate before a breeder gets near them, so a controlled cross needs two extra techniques: emasculation — removing the anthers from a flower before they shed pollen, so it physically cannot fertilize itself — followed by bagging, covering the emasculated flower so no unwanted pollen from elsewhere lands on it either, until the breeder manually dusts on pollen collected from the chosen father plant. Skip either step and you can't be sure whose pollen actually fertilized the flower, which means you can't trust what the offspring's genotype should be.

NEET trap

Get the order right: emasculation happens before bagging, and both happen before the flower's own anthers would have dehisced (burst open and shed pollen) — do it too late and the flower has already self-pollinated, and the whole cross is compromised. Also don't confuse this with tissue culture's sterile technique later in the chapter — emasculation/bagging is about controlling who fertilizes whom, not about preventing contamination in a culture medium.

One classic Indian example shows exactly what combining traits from different parents buys you: cultivated sugarcane in North India was historically Saccharum barberi — adapted to the region's climate but thin-stemmed with low sugar content, while the tropical Saccharum officinarum grown further south had thick juicy stems and high sugar content but couldn't tolerate North Indian conditions well. Breeders hybridized the two, and the resulting canes combine thick stems, high sugar yield, and tolerance for North Indian growing conditions — a hybrid genuinely better than either parent, exactly what step 4 is designed to find.

High-yielding and resistant varieties: the payoff, crop by crop

Applied at national scale, this four-step cycle is what produced India's Green Revolution of the 1960s. Semi-dwarf, high-yielding, disease-resistant wheat varieties — Sonalika and Kalyan Sona — were bred using dwarfing genetics from Mexican semi-dwarf wheats and released across India's wheat belt from 1963 onward, a program closely associated with M. S. Swaminathan's work in India building on Norman Borlaug's breeding at CIMMYT in Mexico. Rice breeding followed the same short-stemmed, lodging-resistant, fertilizer-responsive logic, producing high-yielding semi-dwarf varieties such as Jaya and Ratna. Alongside pure yield, breeders target disease and pest resistance directly — the table below is a representative, exam-relevant sample.

Parbhani Kranti is worth a second look: its YVMV resistance was bred in through interspecific hybridization with a wild Abelmoschus relative of cultivated okra — the resistance gene simply didn't exist anywhere inside the cultivated species' own gene pool, so breeders had to reach outside it. That's the plant-side mirror of interspecific hybridization in animal breeding (coming up later in this chapter) — same principle, same reason: go find variation in a related species when your own species doesn't carry the gene you need.

Biofortification: breeding for nutrition, not just yield

Everything above optimizes yield, height, or resistance — but a bumper harvest of a nutrient-poor staple still leaves people malnourished. Biofortification is plant breeding aimed specifically at raising the micronutrient and protein content of the food itself: iron- and zinc-enriched staples such as pearl millet, wheat, and rice; vitamin-A–enriched maize; and protein-quality–improved maize hybrids with higher lysine and tryptophan content — two amino acids ordinary maize protein is naturally poor in. Historic wheat variety Atlas 66, notable for unusually high grain protein content, has been used worldwide as a donor parent in breeding programs aimed at raising the protein content of wheat.

NEET trap

Biofortification is not a synonym for "high-yielding variety." A HYV like Sonalika is bred to produce more grain; a biofortified variety is bred so the same grain contains more nutrients per unit — the two goals aren't mutually exclusive, but they're testing different traits, and a question naming a nutrient (iron, zinc, vitamin A, lysine) is pointing at biofortification specifically, not yield.

Tissue culture: multiplying a genotype you've already chosen

Everything so far has been about creating or combining new genetic variation. Tissue culture does the opposite job: once you have one plant with exactly the genotype you want, how do you turn it into thousands of identical plants, fast, without waiting for seed-grown generations to shuffle the genes again? The principle underneath it is totipotency — the fact that a single living plant cell retains the full genetic instructions to regenerate an entire plant, given the right nutrients, hormones, and sterile conditions. Micropropagation exploits this directly: a small piece of tissue (an explant) is grown on a sterile nutrient medium containing sugars, minerals, and a carefully balanced mix of the plant hormones auxin and cytokinin, first proliferating into an undifferentiated mass of cells called a callus, then induced to differentiate into shoots, then roots, before the resulting plantlets are hardened off and transferred to soil.

Two more applications lean on the same sterile-culture toolkit for different reasons. Meristem culture takes tissue specifically from the shoot tip's actively dividing meristem, which viruses infecting the rest of the plant typically haven't reached (they spread mainly through the plant's vascular tissue, which the meristem itself hasn't yet differentiated) — growing a new plant from just that tip yields a virus-free clone, valuable for crops like banana and sugarcane that are otherwise propagated vegetatively and would carry any viral infection forward indefinitely. Embryo rescue solves a different problem: in a wide cross between distantly related species (an interspecific plant cross, like the Parbhani Kranti example above), the hybrid embryo often forms but then aborts because the endosperm supporting it fails — rescuing that embryo onto artificial culture medium before it dies lets the hybrid plant develop anyway, turning an otherwise-doomed cross into a viable new variety.

Tissue culture also has one built-in irony: cells grown for many generations in culture sometimes accumulate genetic and chromosomal changes on their own, producing somaclonal variation — plantlets regenerated from the same starting explant that are, despite the whole process being designed to produce identical clones, not quite genetically identical after all. Breeders don't just tolerate this; they use it as a cheap secondary source of novel variation to screen for useful new traits.

NEET trap

Don't let "tissue culture creates variation" and "tissue culture multiplies without variation" collide in your head — both are true, for different reasons. The intended output of micropropagation is identical clones (that's the whole point — multiply a chosen genotype). Somaclonal variation is an unintended side-effect that shows up despite that intention, not the goal of the technique.

Single cell protein: growing protein instead of growing crops

Every method so far still needs arable land, a growing season, and reasonable weather. Single cell protein (SCP) sidesteps all three: it's protein-rich biomass harvested directly from the rapid growth of microorganisms — algae, bacteria, or fungi/yeast — grown in bioreactors or open ponds, often on waste substrates such as molasses, whey, or industrial wastewater. Spirulina, a cyanobacterium, can be cultured on the waste generated from potato-processing plants, straw, animal manure, and even sewage, simultaneously producing a protein- and vitamin-rich food source and reducing the pollution load of the waste it consumed — a double win over dumping that waste untreated. The bacterium Methylophilus methylotrophus makes the case for speed: grown on methanol, it has a generation time of only about two hours, and can be harvested for a protein-rich product used mainly in animal feed.

Core concept

SCP is fast for the same reason microbial fermentation in Chapter 23 was fast: microbial doubling times are measured in hours, not the months a crop needs to mature. A single cow adds protein to the world at the pace of cattle growth; a vat of Methylophilus methylotrophus doubles its protein output roughly every two hours — the same "microbes as rented, fast metabolism" idea from the last chapter, just aimed at protein supply instead of curd or antibiotics.

NEET trap

Keep SCP, biofortification, and tissue culture straight — they solve three different problems that examiners love to swap into each other's questions. SCP = an entirely new, non-agricultural protein source (microbial biomass). Biofortification = breeding a conventional crop to contain more nutrients. Tissue culture / micropropagation = multiplying an already-selected plant genotype, not creating nutrition or new genetic combinations at all.

Animal breeding: the same logic, applied to livestock

Animal breeding runs on the identical two-question logic as plant breeding — get variation, then fix the good combinations — but livestock reproduce slowly, sexually, and can't be bagged and hand-pollinated, so the vocabulary is different even where the underlying genetics rhymes.

Inbreeding mates closely related animals of the same breed for several (typically four to six) generations. Because related animals share more alleles, this steadily raises homozygosity, which does two useful things at once: it exposes harmful recessive alleles as homozygous individuals (so they can be identified and culled from the breeding population) and it locks in a uniform, predictable set of desirable traits, producing a genetically stable pure line. Push it too far, though, and you get inbreeding depression — reduced fertility and productivity as homozygosity for deleterious recessives outpaces the benefit of uniformity. The fix is a controlled dose of outbreeding: mating with an unrelated individual, done once, to restore lost vigor without abandoning the line entirely.

Outbreeding itself comes in three flavors, and NEET tests the differences between them directly. Outcrossing mates two animals of the same breed that share no common ancestor on either side for the past four to six generations — the mildest form, used specifically to correct inbreeding depression while staying within the breed. Cross-breeding mates a superior male of one breed with a superior female of a different breed of the same species, combining desirable traits from both breeds into new offspring — India's Hisardale sheep, developed in Punjab by crossing Bikaneri ewes with Marino rams to combine hardiness with improved wool quality, is the textbook example. Interspecific hybridization goes furthest, mating male and female animals of two different species entirely — a mule, the cross of a male donkey and a female horse, is the classic case, prized for traits (strength, endurance) neither parent species has alone.

NEET trap

The three outbreeding types are ranked by genetic distance between the parents: same breed (outcrossing) < different breed, same species (cross-breeding) < different species (interspecific hybridization). Hisardale is cross-breeding, not outcrossing or interspecific hybridization — Bikaneri and Marino are different breeds of the same species, Ovis aries (sheep); it's a frequently swapped MCQ option precisely because all three terms sound similar.

Beyond choosing which animals to mate, breeders also engineer how many offspring a genetically superior female can produce. Multiple Ovulation Embryo Transfer (MOET) starts by giving a donor cow an FSH-like hormone to induce superovulation — six to eight eggs maturing in one cycle instead of the usual one. The cow is then artificially inseminated with semen from a genetically elite bull, and the resulting embryos are non-surgically recovered at the 8–32 cell stage and transferred into surrogate (recipient) mother cows, who carry them to term as if they were their own. One elite female, instead of producing roughly one calf a year the ordinary way, can contribute genetically to several calves per cycle — multiplying the reproductive output of the best animals in a herd far faster than natural breeding allows. The same underlying technique — superovulation, controlled fertilization, embryo transfer — is also used successfully in sheep, goats, buffaloes, and horses.

Crossbreeding shows up in poultry too, on the same combine-two-strengths principle: Aseel, an Indian breed prized for hardiness and disease resistance, has been crossbred with White Leghorn, an Italian breed bred for very high egg production, aiming for offspring that are both productive and well-adapted to Indian conditions — the same logic as Hisardale sheep, just in a different species.

Think first: inbreeding increases homozygosity; outbreeding (especially interspecific hybridization) increases heterozygosity. These pull in opposite genetic directions. Why does a breeder need both tools instead of just using whichever one sounds "better"?

Because they solve two different problems, and neither one solves the other's. Inbreeding's job is purification and predictability: by raising homozygosity, it exposes harmful recessive alleles so they can be culled, and it locks a herd or line into a uniform, reliably-inherited set of traits — you can't build a stable pure line, or know for certain what a mating will produce, without pushing homozygosity up first. But homozygosity has a cost that compounds with each generation: as harmful recessives accumulate in homozygous form faster than they're removed, fertility and productivity fall — inbreeding depression. Outbreeding's job is the opposite: restoring the heterozygosity that masks mildly deleterious recessive alleles behind a dominant, functional counterpart, which is a large part of why crossbred or hybrid offspring often show hybrid vigor — noticeably better performance than either purebred parent. The catch is that outbred offspring are genetically less predictable; the good combination you got this generation isn't guaranteed to breed true in the next one. So breeders alternate, deliberately: inbreed for several generations to fix and purify a desirable line, then outcross once to correct the depression that inbreeding inevitably causes, then resume inbreeding within the now-refreshed line. It's the same tension plant breeders navigate between a stable pure line and a vigorous F1 hybrid — uniformity and predictability on one side, vigor and novel combinations on the other, and no single technique gets you both at once.

Where this leaves you

Strip away the crop names and breed names, and this chapter is one argument, applied six times: find variation (germplasm collection, wide crosses, mutation, a different species entirely), then keep only the combination you want (selection and testing, pure lines, MOET's controlled fertilization) — or, once you've found a genotype worth keeping, multiply it faithfully instead of reshuffling it again (micropropagation). Chapter 23 recruited microbes as tools; this chapter improved the organisms being grown and raised directly. Chapter 25 takes the same underlying goal — better organisms, more useful products — and drops classical crossing and selection entirely, editing the genetic material itself with recombinant DNA technology instead of waiting for the right cross to happen.
