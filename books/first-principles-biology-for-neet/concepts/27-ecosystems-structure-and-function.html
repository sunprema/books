Chapter 27 · Part VII — Ecology

Ecosystems – Structure and Function

Chapter 26 stopped at two populations locked together — a predator and its prey, a parasite and its host. This chapter zooms out one more time, from a pair of species to everything sharing a place at once: every population, every physical factor around them, and every exchange running between the two. That whole functioning unit — organisms plus their physical environment, interacting as a system — is an ecosystem. NCERT's chapter title splits it cleanly in two, and this chapter keeps that split: structure is what an ecosystem is made of; function is what it does with what it's made of — specifically, how energy flows through it and how matter cycles within it. Energy and matter behave completely differently at this scale, and almost every NEET question in this chapter turns on knowing exactly how they differ.

NCERT mapping
Class 12 — Ecosystem (ecosystem structure and components, productivity, decomposition, energy flow, ecological pyramids, ecological succession, nutrient cycling).
NEET angle
Identifying which pyramid (numbers/biomass/energy) can invert and which never can, GPP–R–NPP productivity arithmetic, matching succession type to starting substrate (hydrarch vs. xerarch, primary vs. secondary), and correctly sorting the carbon vs. phosphorus cycle by whether it has an atmospheric phase.

One system, two questions: what's in it, and what does it do

An ecosystem can be as large as an ocean or as small as a puddle or a crumbling log — size isn't the defining feature. What makes it an ecosystem is that living organisms and their non-living surroundings are bound together into one functional unit through continuous interaction. NCERT organizes everything that follows under two headings, and it's worth holding onto that split as a map for the rest of this chapter: structure asks what the ecosystem is built from — its biotic and abiotic components, arranged into trophic levels; function asks what the system actually does — productivity, decomposition, energy flow, and nutrient cycling.

Core concept — energy flows, matter cycles

This one sentence is the spine of the whole chapter. Energy enters an ecosystem from the sun, passes through it once in a single direction — producers to herbivores to carnivores — losing a large fraction as heat at every step, and is never recycled back into a usable form; the sun must keep supplying more. Matter (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water) is not lost the same way: it's drawn from the physical environment, passed through the food chain, and returned to the environment by decomposers to be used again. Energy flow is one-way and non-recyclable; nutrient cycling is closed-loop and repeatable. Nearly every "why" question in this chapter reduces to that asymmetry.

Structure: biotic and abiotic components

The abiotic component is everything physical and chemical: climatic factors like temperature, light, and rainfall, and edaphic (soil-related) factors like soil pH, mineral content, and topography. These set the operating conditions every organism in the ecosystem has to work within — the same physical-tolerance logic from Chapter 26, now scaled up to a whole community.

The biotic component is organized by function, not just by species identity, into three roles:

RoleWhat it doesExamples
Producers (autotrophs)Fix radiant (or chemical) energy into organic matter — the sole entry point of energy into the systemGreen plants and cyanobacteria (terrestrial); phytoplankton, algae (aquatic)
Consumers (heterotrophs)Depend directly or indirectly on producers for foodHerbivores (primary consumers), carnivores (secondary/tertiary), omnivores
Decomposers (saprotrophs)Break down complex organic matter in dead tissue, releasing simple inorganic nutrients back to the abiotic poolBacteria, fungi

Decomposers deserve their own category rather than being lumped in with consumers, because they perform a function nothing else in the system does: they are the only route by which matter locked up in dead organic material re-enters the cycle as usable inorganic nutrients. Without them, nutrient cycling stops.

Productivity: how fast the system makes new organic matter

Primary productivity is the rate at which producers capture energy as organic matter, per unit area, per unit time — expressed as either the mass of new organic matter produced (g m−2 yr−1) or the energy it represents (kcal m−2 yr−1). It splits into two figures that are constantly confused on NEET, so anchor them by what each one represents:

Core concept — GPP, respiration, and NPP

Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) is the total rate of organic matter production by photosynthesis — everything the producers fix, before they spend any of it on their own upkeep. Producers then use a portion of that in their own respiration (R) to stay alive. What's left over — the organic matter actually available for storage, growth, and for herbivores to eat — is Net Primary Productivity (NPP): NPP = GPP − R. Secondary productivity is the rate at which consumers convert the food they eat into their own new biomass — the same idea, one trophic level up.

Worked the way NEET tends to phrase it: if a patch of forest fixes 15,000 kcal m−2 yr−1 as GPP, and the producers themselves respire away 5,000 kcal m−2 yr−1 keeping their own cells running, the NPP available to the rest of the food chain is 15,000 − 5,000 = 10,000 kcal m−2 yr−1. That NPP figure — not GPP — is what actually gets passed on to herbivores; GPP already had a slice removed before anything else could touch it.

Primary productivity depends on the plant species occupying an area, and varies enormously across ecosystem types — it is much higher in a tropical rainforest or an estuary than in open ocean or desert, largely tracking available light, water, and nutrients. Summed across the whole biosphere, annual NPP comes to roughly 170 billion tonnes (dry weight) of organic matter a year — and despite oceans covering close to 70% of Earth's surface, they contribute only about 55 billion tonnes of that total, with the remaining ~115 billion tonnes coming from land. Ocean area is large, but ocean productivity per unit area is comparatively low.

Two more terms distinguish a snapshot from a flow. Standing crop is the mass of living organisms present in a trophic level at one point in time (measured as biomass or as a number, per unit area). Standing state is the analogous snapshot for a nutrient — the amount of a given mineral nutrient present in the soil at any given time. Both describe a stock, not a rate; productivity describes the rate at which that stock is being generated.

Food chains, food webs, and trophic levels

A food chain is the sequence along which food energy moves from producers through a series of organisms, each eating the one before it and being eaten by the one after — and each step in that sequence is a trophic level: producers occupy the first trophic level, primary consumers (herbivores) the second, secondary consumers the third, and so on. An organism's trophic level is defined strictly by its position in the food chain being described, not by a fixed identity — the same species can sit at different trophic levels in different chains depending on what it's eating in that particular chain.

NCERT names two starting points for a food chain, and they behave very differently in terms of energy source and typical organism size:

Chain typeStarts fromTypical pattern
Grazing food chain (GFC)Living green plantsProducer → herbivore → carnivore; organism size generally increases up the chain, number generally decreases
Detritus food chain (DFC)Dead organic matter (detritus)Detritivores and decomposers feed on dead plant/animal material rather than on a living producer

In most natural ecosystems, the DFC actually processes more energy than the GFC — especially in ecosystems like a forest floor, where most primary production isn't grazed directly but dies and gets broken down instead. In reality, no ecosystem runs on a single straight-line food chain: chains interconnect at every level (a hawk eats snakes, frogs, and rats, each of which has its own separate prey), producing a food web — the actual, branching, more accurate picture of who-eats-whom in an ecosystem.

Ecological pyramids: three ways to stack a food chain, one that never inverts

An ecological pyramid plots each trophic level as a horizontal bar stacked on the one below it — producers at the base, successive consumer levels above — sized by whichever quantity you're measuring. NCERT names three, and the whole point of studying all three together is that they don't always agree with each other about whether a pyramid is "upright."

Pyramid of numbers counts individual organisms per trophic level. It's usually upright in a grassland (many grasses, fewer herbivores, fewer carnivores still), but a single large tree supporting a swarm of insects, which in turn support only a few insectivorous birds, gives a spindle-shaped pyramid — narrow at the very base, wide in the middle, narrow again at the top. Pyramid of biomass uses total dry weight per trophic level instead of raw counts. It's typically upright on land (plant biomass comfortably exceeds herbivore biomass), but in many aquatic ecosystems it inverts: the standing crop of phytoplankton at any instant is small because they're consumed and replaced so rapidly, even though their productivity over time is high, so the biomass sitting above them (zooplankton, small fish) can outweigh it.

NEET trap

Of the three pyramids, only energy is guaranteed to be upright in every ecosystem, without exception — because energy transfer between trophic levels is never 100% efficient, a fixed fraction is always lost as heat at each step, so each successive level must always represent less energy than the one below it. Numbers and biomass are only usually upright and can each invert under the right conditions (single-tree numbers, aquatic biomass) — don't generalize "pyramids are upright" past the energy pyramid.

All three pyramid types share the same two structural blind spots, and NCERT tests both directly: they don't accommodate a species that feeds at more than one trophic level at once (an omnivore eating both plants and herbivores doesn't fit cleanly into a single bar), and none of them include the decomposers, even though decomposers handle a very large share of the ecosystem's energy flow.

Decomposition: where dead matter becomes usable nutrients again

Decomposition is the breakdown of complex organic matter in dead plant and animal remains (detritus) into simpler inorganic substances, and it's the step that makes nutrient cycling possible at all — without it, carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus locked in dead tissue would simply stay locked up. It proceeds through several processes acting together rather than in strict sequence:

ProcessWhat happens
FragmentationDetritivores (e.g. earthworms) physically break detritus into smaller particles, increasing the surface area available for microbial attack
LeachingWater-soluble inorganic nutrients trickle down through the soil and are lost to the horizon below as run-off
CatabolismBacterial and fungal enzymes chemically degrade detritus into simpler inorganic substances
HumificationProduces humus — a dark, amorphous, colloidal substance that decomposes very slowly and acts as a nutrient reservoir
MineralizationMicrobes further degrade humus, releasing inorganic nutrients back into the soil

The rate of decomposition depends on two things: the chemical composition of the detritus, and the local climate. Detritus rich in lignin and chitin decomposes slowly; detritus rich in nitrogen and water-soluble sugars decomposes fast. Warm, moist conditions favor rapid decomposition; decomposition slows sharply when it's either cold or anaerobic (waterlogged, low oxygen).

Nutrient cycling: gaseous cycles versus sedimentary cycles

A biogeochemical cycle is the circulation of a nutrient element between its reservoir (usually in the abiotic world) and the living organisms of the ecosystem. NCERT splits these into two kinds based on where the reservoir physically sits:

Gaseous cycleSedimentary cycle
ReservoirAtmosphere / hydrosphereEarth's crust (rock, sediment)
ExamplesCarbon, nitrogen, oxygenPhosphorus, sulphur
Atmospheric phasePresent — nutrient moves through the air readilyEffectively absent — movement between organism and environment is comparatively slow

The carbon cycle is the standard gaseous-cycle example: producers fix atmospheric CO₂ by photosynthesis into organic carbon; that carbon passes up the food chain; all organisms return a portion of it to the atmosphere as CO₂ through respiration; decomposers release the rest from dead matter; and combustion of biomass or fossil fuel returns carbon that had been locked away for very long timescales. About 4 × 1013 kg of carbon is fixed by producers globally each year, and a substantial share of Earth's carbon reserve sits dissolved in the oceans rather than in the atmosphere at any given moment.

The phosphorus cycle is the standard sedimentary-cycle example, and its behavior is a direct consequence of having no atmospheric phase. Phosphorus's reservoir is phosphate-bearing rock; weathering releases phosphate ions into the soil, where plant roots absorb them; phosphorus then moves up the food chain from producers to consumers exactly as carbon does; and decomposers return it to the soil once again as available phosphate. But because there is no gaseous phosphorus phase, there is no route by which phosphorus is lost to the atmosphere the way carbon is lost via respiration — the entire cycle stays local to soil, water, and living tissue.

NEET trap

A question that describes a nutrient cycle with "no respiratory loss" and "no gaseous exchange with the atmosphere" is describing the phosphorus cycle specifically, not a general property of all nutrient cycles — the carbon cycle relies on exactly the respiratory CO₂ release that phosphorus lacks. If the cycle's reservoir is described as rock or sediment rather than air, it's sedimentary; if it's described as air, it's gaseous.

Ecological succession: how a community changes over time

Ecological succession is the gradual, fairly predictable change in the species composition of a given area over time, driven by the community itself modifying its own environment until conditions favor a different set of species — ending, if left undisturbed, in a relatively stable climax community in equilibrium with the local climate. The whole sequence of communities that occupies a site along the way, from the very first colonizers to the climax, is called a sere, and each intermediate stage is a seral stage.

NCERT distinguishes succession by two independent axes: how the site started, and what it started as.

Core concept — primary vs. secondary, hydrarch vs. xerarch

Primary succession starts on a completely bare, lifeless substrate that never previously supported life — newly cooled lava, exposed rock, a new sand dune — so soil has to form before anything much can grow, making it slow. Secondary succession starts where an existing community was disturbed or destroyed (a flood, fire, or abandoned cultivated field) but the soil is already present, so it proceeds much faster than primary succession because the slowest step — soil formation — has already happened. Independently, succession is also classed by starting substrate: hydrarch succession begins in water (a pond, for instance) and proceeds from hydric toward mesic conditions as the water body fills in with sediment and organic matter; xerarch succession begins on dry, bare substrate (bare rock) and proceeds from xeric toward mesic conditions as weathering and pioneer organisms build up soil and moisture-holding capacity. Both directions converge on the same kind of endpoint — a mesic (moderately moist) climax community — from opposite starting extremes.

The identity of the pioneer species — the very first colonizers of the bare area — depends on the substrate. On bare rock (xerarch), the pioneers are typically lichens, which secrete acids that chemically weather the rock surface, beginning soil formation; they're followed by mosses, then herbaceous plants, shrubs, and eventually trees, as each stage's activity makes the site more hospitable to the next. In a pond or lake (hydrarch), the pioneers are phytoplankton, followed by submerged and then floating hydrophytes, then marsh plants, as sediment and organic matter fill the basin, drying it toward mesic land conditions that eventually support forest.

NEET trap

Don't swap the pioneer identity between the two succession types: xerarch pioneers on bare rock are lichens (a fungus-alga partnership that can survive with no soil at all); hydrarch pioneers in open water are phytoplankton. And "primary vs. secondary" is a completely separate axis from "hydrarch vs. xerarch" — a question can combine either starting-condition label with either substrate label, so read both halves of the description before answering.

Think first: a pond's pyramid of biomass is inverted, but its pyramid of energy is still upright. How can both be true of the exact same pond at the exact same time?

Because biomass is a snapshot (a standing crop, measured at one instant) while energy flow is a rate (measured over a span of time) — and a small standing crop can still push a large amount of energy through it if it turns over fast enough. Phytoplankton in a pond are eaten and replaced so quickly that the mass of phytoplankton present at any single instant is small — smaller than the biomass of the zooplankton and fish it supports, which live longer and accumulate biomass over time. But phytoplankton's productivity — the total energy they fix summed over, say, a year — is still large enough to support every level above them, and each of those levels still only receives a fraction of the energy the level below it fixed, exactly as the 10% rule of thumb for energy transfer predicts. A small, fast-turnover stock (low standing biomass) is perfectly compatible with a large, sustained flow (high productivity, upright energy pyramid) — it's precisely why biomass and energy pyramids are allowed to disagree with each other even when they're describing the very same trophic levels.

Where this leaves you

Everything in this chapter followed from the split in the overview: energy enters once, flows one way through producers, consumers, and decomposers, and leaks away as heat at every transfer — which is exactly why the energy pyramid can never invert even when numbers or biomass do. Matter takes the opposite path: drawn from a reservoir, passed through the same food chain, and returned by decomposition to be used again, whether the reservoir sits in the air (carbon) or in rock (phosphorus). Succession is what happens when you let that whole system run forward across time instead of studying it at one instant — species replacing species, seral stage by seral stage, until the community and its physical setting settle into a climax equilibrium. This chapter treated a single ecosystem as the unit of study; the next and final chapter zooms out one last time, to the sum of every ecosystem's variety at once — biodiversity itself, what threatens it, and what conserving it actually requires.
