Chapter 13 · Part IV — Human Structure & Function

Fuel and Building Blocks
Digestion and Absorption

Chapter 12 closed on a promise: every cell in you runs on a tightly held internal environment, and every organ system from here on is one of the tools that holds it there. This chapter opens the most literal of those tools. The alimentary canal doesn’t regulate a variable so much as manufacture the raw material every other system in this book will spend the rest of its chapters moving, using, and regulating. A chapati, a lentil, and a glass of milk are, to a single cell, enormous, insoluble, unusable polymers on the way in; glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, and glycerol are small, soluble, and directly usable on the way out. Digestion is the purely mechanical and purely enzymatic machinery that gets you from one state to the other, and absorption is how those small molecules actually cross from the gut into the bloodstream that will carry them to every cell in the body.

Two ideas that explain almost the whole chapter

Nearly everything in this chapter is one of two ideas, repeated at a different scale. The first is hydrolysis: every macromolecule in food — starch, protein, fat — is a polymer held together by bonds formed when its building blocks were joined with the loss of a water molecule. Digestion runs that reaction backward, adding water back across each bond to release the monomers. Every digestive enzyme in this chapter, without exception, is a hydrolase: amylases hydrolyse starch, proteases hydrolyse peptide bonds, lipases hydrolyse the bonds in a triglyceride. The second idea is surface area. An enzyme can only act on a substrate molecule it can physically touch, so the body spends real effort maximizing exposed surface at both ends of the process: chewing and gastric churning physically shred food into smaller particles, bile emulsifies fat into microscopic droplets, and on the absorption side, the small intestine folds its own lining into villi and microvilli specifically to pack more absorptive surface into a fixed length of tube.

Core concept

Chemical digestion is hydrolysis, end to end. Mechanical digestion — chewing, churning, emulsification — exists only to keep surface area high enough for that hydrolysis to happen fast enough to matter. Read every enzyme and every anatomical fold in this chapter as serving one of these two jobs.

The alimentary canal: one tube, six specialized regions

The buccal (oral) cavity starts the job mechanically and chemically at once. Teeth are heterodont (four different shapes doing four different jobs: incisors cut, canines tear, premolars and molars grind), thecodont (each tooth is anchored in its own socket in the jawbone), and diphyodont (a milk set is replaced once by a permanent set). The permanent dental formula is 2123/2123 per half-jaw — 2 incisors, 1 canine, 2 premolars, 3 molars, top and bottom, left and right — giving 32 teeth in total; the milk set drops the premolars (2102/2102) for 20. The muscular tongue mixes food with saliva and shapes it into a swallowable bolus, and saliva itself — mostly water and mucus, but carrying the enzyme salivary amylase (ptyalin) — begins breaking cooked starch down to maltose right there in the mouth.

The pharynx is a short shared crossroads for food and air; swallowing (deglutition) routes the bolus past it and into the oesophagus, a long muscular tube whose only job is transport, which it does by peristalsis — a travelling wave of muscular contraction that pushes the bolus onward regardless of which way gravity happens to be pointing.

The stomach is a J-shaped muscular bag with three regions — cardiac (nearest the oesophagus), fundic (the main body), and pyloric (opening into the duodenum through the pyloric sphincter) — and it is where mechanical churning and chemical digestion of protein both begin in earnest. Its lining is packed with gastric glands built from three distinct cell types, each with exactly one job: mucous neck cells secrete protective mucus, chief (peptic) cells secrete the inactive proenzymes pepsinogen and prorennin, and parietal (oxyntic) cells secrete hydrochloric acid and intrinsic factor (needed later for vitamin B12 absorption in the ileum). Churning plus acid plus enzyme together convert a bolus into a thick, semi-liquid mixture called chyme, released in small amounts through the pyloric sphincter into the duodenum.

NEET trap

Match the cell to its single secretion and nothing else: mucous neck cells → mucus only; chief/peptic cells → pepsinogen and prorennin (inactive proenzymes); parietal/oxyntic cells → HCl and intrinsic factor. A question naming “oxyntic cells” is testing HCl and intrinsic factor together — missing the intrinsic-factor half is the usual slip.

The small intestine is the longest part of the canal and the site where digestion of all three macromolecules is finished and almost all absorption happens. It has three regions: the short, C-shaped duodenum, which receives both bile and pancreatic juice and is where most enzymatic digestion actually occurs; the jejunum; and the long ileum, which joins the large intestine at the ileo-caecal valve (a one-way valve preventing backflow). Its inner lining is thrown into permanent folds, and those folds carry finger-like projections called villi, whose own surface cells bear a fringe of microvilli forming a “brush border” — folds within folds within folds, the surface-area idea from the last section taken to its structural limit.

The large intestine begins at the caecum (which bears the vermiform appendix, a narrow blind-ended tube treated at NEET level as a vestigial organ), continues through the ascending, transverse, descending, and sigmoid colon, and ends at the rectum and anal canal. It does essentially no enzymatic digestion; its job is reabsorbing water and some minerals from the remaining material and compacting what’s left into faeces.

Accessory glands: enzymes and helpers that don’t sit in the tube itself

Three pairs of salivary glands — parotid (largest, in the cheek), sub-maxillary/submandibular, and sublingual — supply the saliva that moistens food, lubricates swallowing, and carries salivary amylase.

The liver, the body’s largest gland, continuously secretes bile: a mix of bile pigments (bilirubin and biliverdin, waste products of haemoglobin breakdown), bile salts, cholesterol, and the phospholipid lecithin, all in an alkaline fluid. Bile is stored and concentrated in the gallbladder and released into the duodenum on demand. Its job is entirely physical — emulsifying large fat globules into a fine suspension of microscopic droplets, which multiplies the surface area lipase has to work with, and helping absorb the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, and K.

NEET trap

Bile contains zero digestive enzymes. It never breaks a chemical bond in fat — it only emulsifies it, and the actual hydrolysis is done afterward by pancreatic and intestinal lipase. “Bile digests fat” is the single most common wrong phrasing NEET tests around this gland.

The pancreas is heterocrine — part endocrine (insulin and glucagon, covered again in Chapter 18), part exocrine. Its exocrine, acinar tissue secretes pancreatic juice into the duodenum: the inactive proenzymes trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, and procarboxypeptidase; the already-active pancreatic amylase and pancreatic lipase; nucleases (DNase and RNase); and a load of bicarbonate ions that neutralize the acidic chyme arriving from the stomach so the intestinal enzymes, which need a near-neutral pH, can actually work.

The enzyme cascade: who activates whom

Run each macromolecule through the whole canal and the hydrolysis idea becomes concrete. Carbohydrates: starch → (salivary amylase, mouth) → maltose, paused in the acidic stomach, then → (pancreatic amylase, duodenum) → maltose → (brush-border maltase/sucrase/lactase) → glucose, fructose, galactose. Proteins: protein → (pepsin, stomach) → proteoses and peptones (large fragments) → (trypsin and chymotrypsin, duodenum) → smaller peptides → (carboxypeptidase and brush-border dipeptidases) → free amino acids. Fats: triglycerides → (bile salts) → emulsified fat droplets → (pancreatic and intestinal lipase) → fatty acids, monoglycerides, and glycerol, packaged with bile salts into transportable micelles.

NEET trap

HCl does not digest protein. It (1) converts pepsinogen/prorennin to their active forms, (2) gives pepsin the acidic pH it needs to work, and (3) kills swallowed microbes. The actual peptide-bond hydrolysis is pepsin’s job alone. A related trap: salivary amylase is inactivated by stomach acid, which is exactly why starch digestion pauses in the stomach and only resumes once pancreatic amylase reaches the duodenum.

NEET trap

The first cut in the duodenal cascade is made by enterokinase (from the intestinal mucosa), not by trypsin itself — trypsin doesn’t exist yet to do anything. Only after enterokinase produces the first trypsin does trypsin take over and autocatalytically activate the rest of the cascade (more trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, procarboxypeptidase). “Trypsin activates trypsinogen” is true only as the second step, never the first.

Absorption: three different exits for three different molecules

Every villus is itself a small transport hub: its epithelial cells (bearing the brush-border microvilli) sit over a network of blood capillaries and one central lacteal — a lymphatic capillary. Which of the two a digested nutrient enters depends entirely on how it crosses the epithelial cell, and that in turn depends on the molecule.

Glucose and amino acids cross by active transport, riding a Na+ gradient (secondary active transport) that lets the cell absorb them even against their own concentration gradient, then exit into the blood capillaries. Fructose instead crosses by facilitated diffusion — carrier-assisted, but not energy-spending — and joins glucose and amino acids in the same capillary network, which drains into the hepatic portal vein and delivers everything straight to the liver before it reaches the rest of the body.

Fat takes a genuinely different route. Fatty acids, monoglycerides, and glycerol arrive at the epithelial cell packaged inside bile-salt micelles and cross the membrane by simple diffusion. Once inside the cell, they are immediately re-esterified back into triglycerides, then packaged with cholesterol and protein into droplets called chylomicrons, which are too large to enter a blood capillary and are instead exocytosed into the central lacteal. From there they travel as a milky fluid called chyle through the lymphatic system, entering the bloodstream only later, via the thoracic duct — bypassing the liver’s first pass entirely, unlike glucose and amino acids.

Core concept

Same villus, two exits. Water-soluble nutrients (glucose, amino acids, fructose) go active or facilitated into the blood capillary and hit the liver first via the hepatic portal vein. Fat-derived products go simple-diffusion into the cell, get repackaged as chylomicrons, and leave via the lacteal into lymph — reaching the general circulation only later, downstream of the liver.

Regulation: hormones that time the pipeline

Secretion isn’t constant — it’s triggered by what’s actually in the canal at each moment, coordinated by gut hormones (working alongside direct vagal/neural stimulation, which generally boosts secretion from every gland in the canal).

Keep the trio separate by what each one delivers: gastrin → more acid; secretin → watery, bicarbonate-rich pancreatic juice (neutralizes acid); CCK → enzyme-rich pancreatic juice and gallbladder contraction. A question that mentions bile release specifically is pointing at CCK, not secretin.

When the pipeline breaks

Jaundice: the liver is affected and bile pigments accumulate, turning skin and eyes yellow. Vomiting is a reflex, driven by a vomit centre in the medulla, that forcibly ejects stomach contents. Diarrhoea (frequent watery stools) reduces the time available for absorption; constipation is the opposite failure — faeces retained too long in the rectum. Indigestion covers incomplete digestion generally — bloating, acidity, nausea — from causes as varied as enzyme shortfall, overeating, or anxiety. PEM (protein-energy malnutrition) splits into marasmus (both protein and total calories deficient, typically in infants) and kwashiorkor (protein specifically deficient, typically after weaning). Peptic ulcers are erosions of the stomach or duodenal lining by HCl and pepsin, strongly associated with Helicobacter pylori infection. GERD is acidic stomach content refluxing back up into the oesophagus, causing heartburn and inflammation.

Think first: a rare inherited condition leaves someone unable to produce enterokinase at all. Their pancreas still secretes trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, and procarboxypeptidase into the duodenum completely normally. Would you expect their protein digestion to fail completely, or only partly — and why?

Only partly, and tracing the cascade explains exactly how much is lost. Gastric digestion doesn’t depend on enterokinase at all — pepsin, activated by HCl, still breaks proteins down into proteoses and peptones in the stomach as normal. What’s lost is everything downstream of enterokinase: with no trypsin ever produced, chymotrypsinogen and procarboxypeptidase also stay locked in their inactive forms (both are normally activated by trypsin, not directly by enterokinase), so essentially none of the duodenal protein-digesting machinery ever switches on. The person absorbs some amino acids from what pepsin managed to produce, but loses the vast majority of protein digestion and, as a result, a large fraction of their amino acid and often fat absorption too — enterokinase deficiency is a real, documented condition, and it presents exactly this way: failure to thrive, diarrhoea, and low blood protein, from one missing activating enzyme collapsing an entire downstream cascade.

Where this leaves you

You now have glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, and glycerol crossing from gut lumen into blood and lymph — the fuel and building blocks this chapter’s title promised, ready to be delivered anywhere in the body that needs them. But arrival in the blood isn’t enough on its own: those molecules still need a supply of oxygen to be burned for ATP, and a way to get rid of the carbon dioxide that burning produces. Chapter 14 opens the next organ system on Chapter 12’s table — the respiratory system — which does for O2 and CO2 exactly what this chapter’s digestive system did for food: moves a molecule the body can’t make for itself across a specialized, thin, moist surface and into the blood that carries it everywhere else.
