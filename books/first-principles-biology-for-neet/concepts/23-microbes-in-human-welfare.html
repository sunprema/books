Chapter 23 · Part VI — Applications: Health, Food & Biotechnology

Microbes in Human Welfare

Chapter 22 spent its whole length on microbes as threats — pathogens to keep out, immune systems built to fight them off. This chapter turns that lens around completely. The same microscopic organisms — bacteria, fungi, and their relatives — that cause disease when they get into the wrong place are, in the right place, chemical factories humans have been quietly renting for millennia: they turn milk into curd, grape juice into wine, mould into medicine, sewage into clean water and fuel, and soil nitrogen gas into fertilizer no factory needs to synthesize. None of this required inventing new biology. Every application in this chapter is a microbe doing what it already does in nature — fermenting a sugar, secreting a digestive enzyme, fixing nitrogen, decomposing organic matter — with a human simply building a process around it and collecting the output.

One organizing idea: microbes as rented metabolism

Every example in this chapter fits a single pattern: a microbe's ordinary metabolic activity — something it would do with or without a human watching — happens to produce a compound, or perform a transformation, that people find useful. Humans didn't engineer these abilities from scratch; they noticed them and scaled them up. That's the thread connecting curd in a kitchen, a fermentation vat producing an antibiotic, an aeration tank cleaning sewage, and a legume root nodule fixing nitrogen — four settings that look unrelated until you ask "what is the microbe actually doing, and who benefits?"

Core concept

Sort every microbe in this chapter by the metabolic trick being harvested, and the whole chapter collapses into four categories: fermentation (sugars → acids, alcohol, or gas — curd, bread, beverages, organic acids), secreted chemical warfare or catalysis (antibiotics that kill rival microbes, enzymes that break down substrates — both are compounds the microbe already makes for its own purposes, extracted and purified), decomposition (breaking complex organic matter down to simpler, often gaseous, end-products — sewage treatment, biogas), and nutrient acquisition (pulling scarce nutrients like nitrogen or phosphorus out of air or soil in a form a plant can use — biofertilizers). Biocontrol agents are the one category that doesn't fit this list directly; they work by the microbe attacking or out-competing a pest or pathogen, which is really just decomposition and chemical warfare redirected at an agricultural problem instead of a fermentation vat.

Household products: fermentation on the smallest scale

The most familiar microbial products never leave the kitchen. Curd forms when lactic acid bacteria (LAB) — chiefly Lactobacillus — are introduced into milk as a starter culture and allowed to multiply; they ferment lactose to lactic acid, and the drop in pH coagulates the milk proteins into the familiar set curd. LAB also improve the milk's nutritional quality: they increase vitamin B12 content, and by partially digesting milk proteins they make curd easier to digest than the milk it came from. Bread dough rises for an entirely different reason: baker's yeast, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, ferments the sugars in dough and releases CO2, and it's that trapped gas — not any chemical change in the flour — that puffs the dough up. Toddy, a traditional fermented beverage from parts of India, comes from fermenting the sap of palms. Cheese ripening adds a second layer of microbial work on top of curdling: Roquefort cheese gets its characteristic blue veining and sharp flavour from being ripened with the mould Penicillium roqueforti, while the large holes in Swiss cheese are punched out by CO2 gas released during propionic acid fermentation carried out by the bacterium NCERT names Propionibacterium sharmanii.

NEET trap

Curd and bread are the pair students mix up most: curd is made by a bacterium (Lactobacillus) fermenting lactose to acid, which coagulates protein; bread rises because a fungus/yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) ferments sugar to gas (CO2), which is trapped, not because of any acid. Different kingdom, different product of fermentation, different mechanism of the visible effect — don't let "both are fermentation" collapse the distinction.

Industrial-scale fermentation: from beverages to antibiotics

Scale up the same fermenting yeast from a kitchen to a vat, and you get fermented beverages: wine and beer are both produced by Saccharomyces cerevisiae acting on natural sugars, but on different substrates — wine ferments fruit (grape) juice directly, beer ferments malted cereals. Distilled beverages such as whisky, brandy, and rum start the same way but add a step: the fermented product is distilled afterward, concentrating the alcohol far beyond what fermentation alone produces. The distinction NEET tests is exactly that extra step, not a different organism.

The single most consequential industrial discovery in this category is the antibiotic — a chemical substance, produced by one microbe, that can kill or inhibit the growth of other, disease-causing microbes. Alexander Fleming discovered the first one entirely by accident in 1928: a mould, Penicillium notatum, had contaminated a culture plate of Staphylococcus bacteria he'd left uncovered, and he noticed the bacteria couldn't grow anywhere near the mould's colony. He hadn't yet named the active compound or purified it — that work, which turned the observation into a usable drug, was done later by Ernst Chain and Howard Florey, and large-scale industrial production shifted to a related, higher-yielding species, Penicillium chrysogenum.

Core concept

An antibiotic isn't a compound humans designed — it's chemical warfare a microbe already wages against its neighbours, extracted and purified into a drug. Penicillium secretes penicillin to suppress competing bacteria in its own environment; humans simply found the compound already did, in a human body, exactly what it was doing on that culture plate — killing bacteria — and built an industry around producing it in bulk.

Sewage treatment: microbes as the city's water filter

Municipal sewage — used water carrying human waste and organic matter — can't be released untreated into a river or lake; the organic load would let bacteria multiply explosively there instead, consuming dissolved oxygen and killing off aquatic life. Treatment plants solve this by doing, in a controlled facility, exactly what decomposer microbes would eventually do in the environment — just faster, and with the resulting sludge and gas captured rather than left to pollute.

The number that tells you how well a stage is working is biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) — the amount of oxygen microbes would need to break down the organic matter present in a water sample over a fixed period. A high BOD means a lot of organic matter is still there for microbes to consume, so treatment isn't finished yet; a low BOD means most of that organic load has already been removed, which is exactly what the aeration tank's aerobic microbes are doing. Once BOD drops enough, the water passes to the settling tank, where the bacterial flocs (now called activated sludge) sediment out: a small fraction is recycled back into the aeration tank as a fresh inoculum, and the larger remaining fraction is pumped into anaerobic sludge digesters, where a different community of anaerobic bacteria — including methane-producing methanogens — digest it further, releasing a mixture of gases (methane, CO2, H2S, H2) collectively called biogas.

NEET trap

Low BOD = clean water, not dirty water. Students commonly flip this: a high BOD number sounds "more" and gets read as "more purified," but it actually means more unconsumed organic matter remains, i.e. more pollution. Also keep the two tanks straight: the aeration tank is aerobic and reduces BOD; the sludge digester is anaerobic and produces biogas — swapping which one is aerobic is a recurring distractor.

Biogas plants: the same digestion, purpose-built for fuel

A dedicated biogas plant runs the anaerobic-digester half of the sewage process on its own, feeding it a purpose-chosen raw material: cattle dung slurry, which is why biogas is also called gobar gas in India. The dung is rich in exactly the right microbes to begin with — methanogens such as Methanobacterium already live in the rumen of cattle, where they help the animal digest cellulose from grass, and they carry on that same digestion once the dung is fed into a sealed tank. In India, this technology for village-scale biogas plants was developed jointly by the Indian Agricultural Research Institute (IARI) and the Khadi and Village Industries Commission (KVIC). The spent slurry left after digestion is not wasted either — since digestion mainly converts carbon compounds into gas, the nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium in the original dung remain in the residue, so it's used directly as an excellent field manure.

Core concept

A biogas plant and a cow's rumen are running the same biology for the same reason: both are sealed, oxygen-free environments where methanogens break cellulose-rich organic matter down to methane. The plant doesn't need any exotic microbiology — it just relocates a digestion process cattle were already doing, into a tank sized to capture the gas instead of letting the cow simply belch it out.

Microbes as biocontrol agents: pest control without synthetic chemicals

Biological control uses living organisms — instead of synthetic chemical pesticides — to keep pest and pathogen populations down, and several microbes do this job well. Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) produces a protein toxin that, when ingested by specific insect larvae (notably butterfly and moth caterpillars), kills them by disrupting their gut; this same toxin gene is what's engineered directly into crop plants to make Bt cotton and Bt brinjal, a link to genetically modified crops covered later in this Part. Trichoderma species are free-living fungi found abundantly in the root ecosystem (rhizosphere) of plants, and they're effective biocontrol agents against several plant pathogens. Baculoviruses (genus Nucleopolyhedrovirus) are pathogens that specifically attack insects and other arthropods; most species in this genus infect only their target species, or close relatives of it, which makes them narrow-spectrum, species-specific insecticidal agents with no documented negative impact on plants, mammals, birds, fish, or non-target insects — a sharp contrast with a broad-spectrum chemical pesticide.

NEET trap

The defining advantage baculoviruses get tested on is narrow host range: because most species infect only one target insect (or close relatives), they're ideal for Integrated Pest Management (IPM) precisely because they spare everything else in the ecosystem — the opposite trade-off from a broad-spectrum chemical insecticide, which kills the target pest but also non-target and beneficial insects.

Microbes as biofertilizers: pulling nitrogen and phosphorus out of thin air and soil

Plants can't use atmospheric nitrogen gas (N2) directly — the triple bond holding it together is too strong for a plant's own enzymes to break. Biofertilizers are organisms that solve this (and the related problem of scarce soil phosphorus) biologically, enriching soil nutrient availability without a synthetic fertilizer factory. Rhizobium is the best-known example: it lives symbiotically inside root nodules of leguminous plants, where it fixes atmospheric N2 into a form the plant can use, in exchange for a steady supply of sugars from the plant's own photosynthesis. Azospirillum and Azotobacter fix nitrogen too, but as free-living organisms in the soil, with no host plant and no nodule required. Cyanobacteria — Anabaena, Nostoc, Oscillatoria — are autotrophic, nitrogen-fixing microbes important in both aquatic and terrestrial environments; the symbiotic association between Azolla (a small floating fern) and Anabaena living in its leaf cavities is a widely used biofertilizer system in paddy fields. Mycorrhiza is a different kind of partnership entirely: it's a symbiotic association between a fungus, most commonly Glomus, and the roots of a vascular plant, in which the fungal partner absorbs phosphorus from the soil and passes it to the plant. This isn't limited to legumes — it's found broadly across crop plants, and plants with a mycorrhizal association also show increased resistance to root-borne pathogens and to drought and salinity stress.

NEET trap

Keep symbiotic nitrogen fixers (Rhizobium, needs a legume host and forms a nodule) separate from free-living ones (Azotobacter, Azospirillum, no host or nodule needed) — a very common "which of these needs a host plant" question. Also don't fold mycorrhiza into the nitrogen story: Glomus and other mycorrhizal fungi supply the plant with phosphorus, not nitrogen, and the association isn't restricted to legumes the way Rhizobium is.

Think first: both Rhizobium (symbiotic, needs a legume host) and Azotobacter (free-living, no host needed) fix atmospheric nitrogen into a usable form. Free-living sounds strictly better — no dependency on a host plant. So why hasn't free-living nitrogen fixation simply replaced the more complicated root-nodule partnership?

Because the enzyme that actually fixes nitrogen, nitrogenase, is destroyed by oxygen — and a nodule solves that problem in a way open soil never can. Inside the nodule, the plant produces leghemoglobin, an oxygen-binding pigment that keeps the interior reliably low in free oxygen, protecting nitrogenase's active site. A free-living soil bacterium has no such protected chamber; it has to fix nitrogen more cautiously, or only in the anaerobic micro-pockets it happens to find, which caps how much it can fix. The nodule also gives Rhizobium something free-living fixers don't get: a direct, continuous supply of photosynthetic sugars piped straight from the plant, instead of whatever organic matter happens to be scavengeable in the surrounding soil. The trade being made is the same one that shows up whenever biology chooses symbiosis over independence — a more complicated, host-dependent partnership buys a protected environment and a richer, guaranteed energy supply, and in exchange delivers a far higher rate of nitrogen fixation than a free-living organism working the open soil on its own can sustain. Free-living fixation isn't obsolete — it's just the lower-yield, lower-commitment option on the same spectrum.

Where this leaves you

Read end to end, this chapter is really one argument made six different ways: nothing here required humans to design new biology, only to notice metabolism microbes were already running and build a process around it — Lactobacillus souring milk, yeast releasing CO2, Penicillium waging chemical warfare on a rival bacterium, aerobic flocs consuming organic waste, methanogens finishing the job anaerobically, and Rhizobium trading fixed nitrogen for sugar inside a root nodule. Chapter 24 continues this same "improving food production" thread, but shifts the lever from microbial tools to the organisms being fed and grown themselves — selecting and breeding better crop plants and livestock directly, rather than recruiting a microbe to do the work.
