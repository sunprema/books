Chapter 07 · Part II — Information Flow & Genetics

Evolution
Change in Populations Over Time

Chapter 6 closed on a question: if mutation keeps introducing new alleles into a gene pool — a new haemoglobin variant, a repressor that binds slightly differently — and selection keeps favouring or disfavouring the phenotypes those alleles produce, what happens to that allele's frequency across many generations? This chapter answers it, and in doing so pins down what “evolution” actually means at the level biology can measure. It's tempting to picture evolution as something an individual does — a giraffe stretching its neck, a bacterium toughening up over its lifetime. It isn't. An individual's genotype is fixed the moment its zygote forms; what can change, generation after generation, is a population's mix of alleles. Evolution is a property of populations, not individuals — and this chapter is about the forces that shift that mix, the independent lines of evidence that it has been shifting for billions of years, and the point at which enough shift stops being variation within a species and becomes a new one.

Before selection can act, there has to be something alive to select

Earth is roughly 4.5 billion years old, and its earliest atmosphere looked nothing like today's: little to no free oxygen, and rich instead in gases like methane, ammonia, hydrogen, and water vapour. The Oparin–Haldane hypothesis — proposed independently by Alexander Oparin (1924) and J. B. S. Haldane (1929) — argued that under those conditions, energy from lightning, UV radiation, and volcanic heat could drive simple inorganic molecules to combine into organic ones: amino acids, sugars, simple nucleotides. Life, on this view, didn't begin with a cell — it began with chemical evolution, non-living chemistry slowly assembling the building blocks a living system would eventually need.

That hypothesis went from speculation to experiment in 1953, when Stanley Miller and Harold Urey built a closed apparatus circulating methane, ammonia, hydrogen, and water vapour past a continuous electric spark discharge, standing in for lightning striking the early atmosphere. Within about a week, the flask contained amino acids — formed from nothing but inorganic starting material and an energy source, with no living cell anywhere in the apparatus. The Miller–Urey experiment is the standard textbook evidence that the Oparin–Haldane picture is chemically plausible, not just a story.

Getting from a soup of organic monomers to something that can replicate itself is a much bigger, still actively researched gap — candidate steps include self-assembling boundary structures (protobionts, or coacervates) that could concentrate and protect chemistry inside a membrane-like sac, and the “RNA world” hypothesis, which proposes RNA as the earliest self-replicating genetic material, since RNA (unlike DNA) can also fold up and act as a catalyst for its own reactions. NEET doesn't expect you to resolve that gap — it expects you to know that chemical evolution came first, that Miller–Urey experimentally supports it, and that everything the rest of this chapter describes only starts once some self-replicating, heritable chemistry exists for selection to work on.

Core concept

Chemical evolution explains how life's building blocks could arise from non-living matter. Biological evolution — the subject of the rest of this chapter — explains how, once self-replicating systems existed, populations of them changed over time. Question-writers sometimes fold both into one “origin of life” bucket; keep the two ideas conceptually distinct even while treating them as one continuous story.

Darwin's mechanism, and what the “modern synthesis” added to it

Charles Darwin's On the Origin of Species (1859) proposed a simple, four-part logic he called natural selection: individuals in a population vary in their traits; more offspring are produced each generation than the environment can support, so there is a constant struggle for survival; individuals whose variation happens to suit the environment tend to survive and reproduce more successfully than others; and if that trait is heritable, it becomes more common in the next generation. Darwin called the outcome “descent with modification.” Note carefully what selection does not do: it doesn't generate variation to order in response to need. A giraffe stretching its neck during its lifetime doesn't produce longer-necked offspring — that was Jean-Baptiste Lamarck's earlier idea of inheritance of acquired characters, and it was directly tested and rejected (August Weismann famously cut the tails off mice for many successive generations; every generation was still born with a full-length tail). Selection can only act on heritable variation that already exists in the population before the environment favours one version over another.

Darwin didn't know what generated that heritable variation in the first place — genetics wasn't yet part of biology's toolkit; Gregor Mendel's 1866 paper went essentially unnoticed until 1900. The modern synthesis (built through the 1930s–40s by population geneticists including R. A. Fisher, J. B. S. Haldane, Sewall Wright, and Theodosius Dobzhansky) fused Darwin's natural selection with Mendelian genetics: mutation and recombination, the same processes Chapters 4–6 already covered, turned out to be exactly the source of heritable variation Darwin's theory needed. That fusion also gave evolution a precise, measurable definition, replacing the vague “species change over time”:

Core concept

A population's gene pool is the sum of all alleles, at every locus, carried by every individual in it. Evolution, defined operationally, is a change in allele frequency in a population's gene pool from one generation to the next. This is the definition the rest of this chapter — and every Hardy–Weinberg question — is built on: not “organisms getting better,” just measurable movement in what fraction of a population's alleles are which version.

The clearest case of natural selection caught happening within a human research lifetime, rather than inferred from fossils, is industrial melanism in the peppered moth, Biston betularia, in nineteenth- and twentieth-century England. The species has two colour morphs, light (typica) and dark (carbonaria), controlled largely by variation at a single gene — both forms existed in the population well before industrialisation. Before heavy coal-burning industry, lichen-covered pale tree bark camouflaged the light form well against bird predators, and light moths dominated. As soot from industrial pollution blackened tree trunks and killed the lichen, the dark form became the better-camouflaged one, and its frequency in polluted regions rose sharply over just a few decades, reported above 90% in the most heavily polluted areas by the mid-twentieth century. After clean-air legislation cut soot pollution from the 1960s onward and bark lightened again, the frequency of the dark form fell back down over the following decades. That's a documented, reversible shift in allele frequency tracking a measurable change in a single selective pressure — predation risk, mediated by camouflage.

NEET trap

Pollution did not cause the dark-moth mutation to appear on demand — both colour morphs were already standing genetic variation in the population beforehand. What pollution changed was which morph had the survival advantage. An option describing industrial melanism as pollution “creating” the dark mutation is the same Lamarckian trap as the giraffe's neck, dressed in a different example.

Independent lines of evidence, all pointing the same way

Descent with modification implies every living thing is related, however distantly. Several kinds of evidence converge on that same family tree — and part of why they're historically so persuasive together is that none of them individually depends on the others being true.

  • Paleontological (fossil) evidence. Sedimentary rock is laid down in layers over time, and deeper, older strata generally preserve simpler forms while more recent strata preserve forms closer to today's. “Connecting link” fossils are especially prized: Archaeopteryx shows feathers and wings like a bird alongside teeth, a long bony tail, and clawed fingers like a reptile — a genuine mix of traits, not a clean modern body plan, making it hard to explain except as a transitional form between reptiles and birds.
  • Comparative anatomy — homologous organs. The forelimbs of a human, a cat, a whale's flipper, and a bat's wing all share the same underlying bone arrangement (humerus → radius/ulna → carpals → digits), just reshaped for walking, swimming, or flying. Structures that share the same basic origin but have diverged to serve different current functions are homologous, and they point to a shared ancestor followed by divergent evolution.
  • Comparative anatomy — analogous organs. The wings of a butterfly and the wings of a bird look and function alike (both fly) but arise from completely different structural origins — an insect exoskeleton extension versus a modified forelimb. Structures that converge on a similar current function from different origins are analogous, the signature of convergent evolution solving the same problem independently, not shared ancestry.
  • Adaptive radiation. When one ancestral form disperses into an area with several vacant ecological niches and diversifies to fill them, all without leaving that region, the result is adaptive radiation. Darwin's finches on the Galapagos — roughly a dozen species descended from one common ancestral finch, each with a beak shaped for a different diet (thick seed-crushing beaks, thin insect-probing beaks, cactus-flower feeders) after colonising separate islands — are the textbook case. Australia's marsupials, radiating in relative isolation into grazing, burrowing, and predatory forms that parallel placental mammals elsewhere, are the other one NCERT pairs with it.
  • Biochemical evidence. The genetic code is essentially universal across all known life, and the degree of amino-acid difference in essential proteins shared across species (cytochrome c is the standard example) scales with how evolutionarily distant those species are — close relatives differ in fewer amino acids than distant ones, exactly as expected if they diverged from a common ancestor at different points in the past.
NEET trap

NCERT's own plant example is the one to have ready: the thorn of Bougainvillea and the tendril of Cucurbita are both modified axillary buds — same origin, different function (defense vs. climbing support) — making them homologous, the product of divergent evolution. Meanwhile a potato (a modified underground stem) and a sweet potato (a modified root) both store food underground — same current function, different origin — making them analogous, the product of convergent evolution. Same-origin-different-function = homologous = divergent; same-function-different-origin = analogous = convergent. Options are built specifically to swap these two.

Hardy–Weinberg: measuring whether a population is evolving at all

If evolution is a change in allele frequency, the natural next question is: what would a population's allele frequencies look like if nothing were pushing them to change? That's exactly what the Hardy–Weinberg principle (G. H. Hardy and Wilhelm Weinberg, independently, 1908) works out. For a single gene with two alleles, A (frequency p) and a (frequency q), where p + q = 1, random union of gametes predicts genotype frequencies of:

Core concept

p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1 — where p2 is the frequency of AA, 2pq is the frequency of Aa, and q2 is the frequency of aa. The principle states that, generation after generation, these allele and genotype frequencies stay exactly constant provided a population meets five conditions simultaneously: no new mutation, no gene flow (migration in or out), no genetic drift (the population is effectively infinite/very large), random mating, and no natural selection (no genotype has a survival or reproductive advantage). Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium is therefore the theoretical “null hypothesis” of no evolution — and each of its five conditions, read backwards, names one of the five real forces that actually do drive evolution when a real population violates it.

A worked numerical, in the style NEET actually asks it: suppose a recessive disorder shows up in 1 of every 10,000 newborns in a large, randomly mating population, and the population otherwise satisfies Hardy–Weinberg's conditions. Affected individuals are aa, so q2 = 1/10,000 = 0.0001, which gives q = 0.01 and p = 1 − 0.01 = 0.99. The frequency of unaffected carriers, Aa, is 2pq = 2 × 0.99 × 0.01 ≈ 0.0198 — roughly 1 in 50 people. Notice what that means: a condition rare enough to affect only 1 in 10,000 people has a hidden carrier frequency 200× higher. That gap between “how often the recessive phenotype shows up” and “how common the recessive allele actually is, mostly masked in heterozygotes” is precisely why Hardy–Weinberg math matters beyond the exam: it's how population geneticists estimate an allele's real prevalence from the only number you can actually observe directly, the phenotype frequency.

NEET trap

Always take the square root from the homozygous recessive frequency (q2), never from the total percentage showing any version of the trait, and never assume the given percentage is already an allele frequency rather than a genotype (phenotype) frequency. A second common trap: after finding q, forgetting p = 1 − q before plugging into 2pq — a wrong carrier-frequency answer almost always traces back to one of these two slips.

Speciation: when variation stops being “within a species”

Biologically, a species is usually defined as a group of populations whose members can interbreed and produce fertile offspring, and who are reproductively isolated from other such groups — an imperfect definition (it doesn't cleanly cover organisms that reproduce asexually, for instance) but the working one NEET uses. Speciation is the process by which one such group becomes two.

The most common route is allopatric speciation: a physical barrier — a mountain range rising, a river changing course, a population colonising a separate island — splits one population into two that can no longer exchange genes. Cut off from each other, the two subpopulations accumulate different mutations and face different local selection pressures independently, gradually diverging. Darwin's finches are the case already introduced under adaptive radiation, looked at again through this lens: an ancestral mainland finch population reached the geographically isolated Galapagos islands, and separate island populations — isolated from each other by open ocean, not just from the mainland — diverged under different food-resource pressures into species that, brought back into contact today, no longer interbreed.

Core concept

Geographic isolation and reproductive isolation are not the same thing, and NEET tests the difference directly. Geographic isolation is the trigger — it stops gene flow and lets two populations start diverging. Reproductive isolation is the outcome that actually defines a new species — the point at which the two populations could no longer interbreed successfully even if physically reunited. A population separated by a mountain range that hasn't yet accumulated enough difference to lose interbreeding compatibility is geographically isolated but not yet a separate species.

Speciation without any geographic barrier at all — sympatric speciation — is rarer in animals but common in plants, most directly through polyploidy: an error in meiosis (or a hybridisation between two species followed by chromosome doubling) produces offspring with a different chromosome number from either parent population. That offspring generally can't produce fertile hybrids by backcrossing to either original population, so reproductive isolation is achieved in a single generation rather than accumulated gradually over many. Many cultivated crop species, bread wheat among the best-known, arose this way.

Think first: a different recessive condition, in a large randomly mating population at Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium, affects 1 in 2,500 people. What fraction of the population are unaffected carriers?

q2 = 1/2,500 = 0.0004, so q = 0.02 and p = 1 − 0.02 = 0.98. Carrier frequency is 2pq = 2 × 0.98 × 0.02 = 0.0392, or just under 1 in 25 people — roughly the real-world carrier frequency reported for several human autosomal recessive disorders with an affected-birth rate in this range. Same two-step method as the worked example above: square root the affected frequency to get q, subtract from 1 to get p, then compute 2pq — the only thing that changed is the starting number.

Human evolution: one branch of the same tree

Everything above is general machinery — it applies exactly the same way to the lineage that produced Homo sapiens. NCERT sketches that lineage as a rough sequence of fossil stages; treat the timeframes below as approximate, useful for getting the order and the trend right (steadily increasing brain capacity and upright posture) rather than as precise dates — new fossil finds regularly revise the exact numbers, and several of these stages likely overlapped rather than replacing one another in a clean line.

NEET trap

A recurring question format lists several of these stages and asks which had the smallest brain capacity. Keep the ascending order straight: Australopithecus (~450–500 cc) < Homo habilis (~650–800 cc) < Homo erectus (~900 cc) < Homo sapiens (~1,350 cc) < Homo neanderthalensis (~1,400 cc). Among the stages most commonly offered as answer options (habilis, erectus, sapiens, neanderthalensis), the smallest is Homo habilis — a detail worth more than any single memorised cc figure.

Where this leaves you

Chapters 1 through 7 have been about what a living thing is: a compartmentalised cell running on chemistry and thermodynamics, storing and expressing information as DNA, RNA, and protein, passing variation to offspring, and — this chapter's addition — changing at the level of the population as mutation, selection, and isolation reshape a gene pool's allele frequencies over time, closely enough tracked that Hardy–Weinberg can tell you exactly how much a real population has drifted from doing nothing at all. From Chapter 8 onward, the lens changes from what to how built and how run: starting with the body plan every land plant is constructed on, and why that plan is shaped, tissue by tissue, around the same non-negotiable jobs — capturing light, moving water, and holding itself up — that any land-dwelling organism ultimately has to solve.
