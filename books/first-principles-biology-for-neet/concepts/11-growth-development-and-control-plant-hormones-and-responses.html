Chapter 11 · Part III — Plant Structure & Function

Growth, Development, and Control
Plant Hormones and Responses

Chapter 10 left the plant holding freshly fixed carbon and freshly made ATP with nowhere obvious to spend either — sugar and energy aren’t growth any more than bricks are a building. Something still has to decide when a cell stops dividing and starts elongating, when a bud stays shut and when it breaks, which way a stem bends and a root doesn’t, and when the whole plant commits to flowering instead of just getting taller. A plant has no nervous system to make any of these calls quickly, so it makes them chemically and slowly: five small classes of signalling molecules — plant growth regulators — that almost any cell can make and almost any cell can respond to, in concentrations and ratios that shift with light, gravity, touch, temperature, and the calendar. This chapter is about how growth stops being automatic and becomes controlled.

NEET angle
Hormone-function matching and the discovery experiments behind each one, the auxin:cytokinin ratio in tissue culture, the ABA:GA antagonism in dormancy, ethylene’s triple response and its use to promote female flowers in cucurbits, short-day vs. long-day plants defined by critical night length (not day length), and growth-curve phase identification.

Growth is not “getting bigger” — it’s irreversible, measurable, and never quite finished

Growth is a permanent, irreversible increase in size — in cell number, cell size, or both — and biologists measure it in whatever unit is convenient for the organ in question: length, area, volume, fresh weight, or dry weight. That word “irreversible” is doing real work: a seed swelling as it soaks up water is a size increase, but it’s reversible (dry the seed out and it shrinks back), so imbibition isn’t growth. A cell that has actually divided or actually elongated doesn’t un-divide or un-elongate.

Unlike most animals, a plant never really stops growing. That’s because growth in plants is open (indeterminate): it stays localised for life at specific growing points — the apical meristems Chapter 8 introduced — rather than being distributed evenly and then switched off at maturity. New cells appear continuously at the shoot and root tips (and, in woody plants, at lateral meristems too), which is also why a plant’s final shape is so much more a product of environment and hormonal decision-making than an animal’s largely pre-set body plan.

Core concept

“Growth” and “development” are not synonyms. Growth is purely quantitative — more cells, bigger cells — and it can only go up. Development is the entire, larger programme: growth plus differentiation (cells maturing into specialised, functional forms), and it can include events that look like the opposite of growth — a leaf senescing, a fruit abscising — as fully coordinated, hormonally scheduled steps. When a question asks about “development,” it’s asking about the whole programme, not just the size increase.

Plot growth against time for almost any growing organ or population of cells and the same shape appears: a slow start, a rapid middle, and a levelling-off — the sigmoid (S-shaped) growth curve. A lag phase comes first, while relatively few cells are actively dividing and metabolic machinery is being set up; a log (exponential) phase follows, where growth is fastest because resources (nutrients, water, hormonal signal) are still abundant relative to demand; and finally a stationary phase, where growth slows and plateaus as some factor — nutrient supply, space, an internal developmental cue — becomes limiting. You’ll meet this exact curve shape again, applied to whole populations rather than single organs, in Chapter 26.

Two different growth rates can sit underneath that curve, and NEET likes to test the difference directly. In arithmetic growth, after each division only one of the two daughter cells keeps dividing while the other differentiates — so the growing region adds a roughly constant increment each unit of time, producing a straight-line increase (the classic textbook example is a root elongating at a near-constant rate). In geometric growth, both daughter cells keep dividing, so the number of actively growing cells itself doubles each round — growth starts slowly, then accelerates into a steep, J-shaped curve. The initial lag-then-rapid-rise of the sigmoid curve above is exactly geometric growth in its early stretch, before a limiting factor flattens it into the stationary phase.

NEET trap

A straight diagonal line is arithmetic growth; a curve that gets steeper over time (J-shaped) is geometric growth; a curve that rises and then flattens (S-shaped) is the overall sigmoid pattern most organs actually follow once a limiting factor kicks in. Don’t confuse “geometric” (rate itself increasing) with the sigmoid curve’s log phase (rate is high but the curve as a whole isn’t unlimited) — geometric growth is what the log phase would look like if nothing ever became limiting.

As a meristematic cell stops dividing and takes on a permanent, specialised structure — becoming a xylem vessel element, a sieve tube, a fibre — it undergoes differentiation. That process isn’t always a one-way trip. A mature, differentiated, non-dividing cell can, under the right conditions, regain the ability to divide — dedifferentiation — which is exactly how a plant produces new meristems where it needs them: mature parenchyma cells dedifferentiate to form the interfascicular cambium and the cork cambium that drive secondary growth. The new cells that dedifferentiated tissue produces can then mature again into specialised, non-dividing cells — redifferentiation — such as the cork cells a newly formed cork cambium produces. Underlying all of it is the same fact Chapter 1’s cell theory promised: every living plant cell retains a complete, functional genome and, in principle, the capacity to regenerate an entire plant — totipotency — which is the entire physiological basis of the plant tissue culture techniques Chapters 24 and 25 will put to commercial use.

Five signals, no nervous system: plant growth regulators as the coordinating layer

Chapter 18 will show you an animal solving the coordination problem with two systems working together: fast, wired nerve impulses and slower, blood-borne hormones. A plant only ever gets the second option. Plant growth regulators (PGRs) — small, simple organic molecules, not proteins — are made in tiny amounts almost anywhere in the plant body and diffuse or are actively transported to wherever they’re needed, promoting, inhibiting, or qualitatively redirecting growth depending on their concentration, the tissue receiving them, and — critically — their ratio to other PGRs present at the same time. Naturally produced PGRs are called phytohormones; chemically similar synthetic versions (2,4-D, NAA, ethephon) are used commercially precisely because they mimic or block a natural hormone’s action.

Five classes cover essentially everything on the NEET syllabus: auxins, gibberellins, and cytokinins are broadly growth promoters; abscisic acid (ABA) is broadly a growth inhibitor and the plant’s stress signal; ethylene is the odd one out — a gas, generally associated with senescence and ripening, but capable of promoting growth-like responses (flowering, femaleness) in specific contexts. None of the five acts alone; nearly every developmental decision in this chapter comes down to a ratio between two of them.

Core concept

Read this whole chapter looking for ratios, not absolute amounts: auxin relative to cytokinin decides root vs. shoot in tissue culture, ABA relative to gibberellin decides seed dormancy vs. germination, and auxin relative to ethylene decides whether a leaf stays attached or abscises. A hormone’s effect is defined by what it’s balanced against as much as by what it does on its own.

Auxin: the first hormone found, discovered by watching a seedling bend toward light

Auxin’s discovery is a chain of four classic experiments worth knowing in sequence, because NEET tests the attribution directly. Charles and Francis Darwin (1880) noticed that canary-grass (Phalaris) coleoptiles bend toward unilateral light only if the tip is exposed — covering just the tip with an opaque cap blocked the bend entirely, even though the bending itself happens lower down, implying the tip perceives the light and sends a signal downward. Boysen-Jensen (1913) showed that signal is chemical, not electrical or mechanical: it could still pass through a permeable gelatin block inserted below a cut tip, but not through an impermeable mica sheet. Paal (1919) showed a decapitated tip placed off-centre on the cut stump — even in complete darkness — caused the stump to bend away from the side carrying the tip, meaning the signal alone, with no light involved at all, was sufficient to cause curvature. F. W. Went (1928) finally isolated the substance itself from oat (Avena) coleoptile tips, named it auxin, and built the Avena curvature bioassay — measuring the bend an agar block containing extracted auxin produces on a decapitated coleoptile — to quantify it.

The major natural auxin is indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), synthesised mainly in the shoot apex, young leaves, and developing seeds, and moved through the stem almost entirely in one direction — tip to base — a pattern called polar transport. Its best-known cellular action is cell elongation: auxin activates proton pumps in the plasma membrane, the wall acidifies and loosens, and the cell takes up water and expands (the “acid growth” mechanism — the same wall-loosening logic gibberellin uses too, just triggered differently). Beyond elongation, auxin drives apical dominance — the growing shoot tip suppresses the outgrowth of lateral buds below it, which is exactly why pruning or pinching off a shoot tip releases the lateral buds to grow (the horticultural basis of “pinching” a plant to make it bushier); promotes root initiation in stem cuttings, which is why synthetic auxins (IBA, NAA) are sold as rooting hormones; can induce parthenocarpic (seedless) fruit development when applied to an unpollinated ovary; and, depending on concentration, can either prevent or promote leaf and fruit abscission.

NEET trap

2,4-D is a synthetic auxin used as a selective herbicide — sprayed on cereal fields, it kills broad-leaved dicot weeds (which absorb and are overwhelmed by it) while leaving the monocot crop essentially unaffected. Don’t confuse it with the rooting auxins (IBA, NAA) used in vegetative propagation and tissue culture — same hormone class, opposite practical use.

Auxin also explains directional growth. Under a one-sided stimulus — light hitting one side of a shoot tip, or gravity acting on a horizontally placed root or stem — auxin is redistributed laterally so that one side of the organ ends up with more of it than the other. The side with more auxin elongates faster, and the organ curves as a result — the Cholodny–Went hypothesis. In a shoot under unilateral light, auxin accumulates on the shaded side, that side elongates more, and the tip curves toward the light (positive phototropism). In a horizontal root, auxin accumulates on the lower side, and — because root cells are far more auxin-sensitive than shoot cells — that same higher auxin concentration actually inhibits elongation there, so the upper side grows faster and the root curves downward (positive gravitropism), while a shoot’s lower, auxin-rich side elongates more and the shoot curves upward (negative gravitropism). One mechanism, opposite curvature, purely because root and shoot cells respond to the same auxin concentration differently.

Gibberellin: discovered from a disease that made rice seedlings grow too tall

Gibberellin’s discovery ran in the opposite direction from auxin’s — it started with a plant disease, not a bending seedling. In 1926, Japanese scientist Eiichi Kurosawa was studying “bakanae” (“foolish seedling”) disease of rice, in which infected seedlings grew abnormally tall, spindly, and pale, and often fell over and died before setting seed. The disease turned out to be caused by a fungus, Gibberella fujikuroi, and the chemical it secreted that was actually responsible for the excess elongation was later purified and named gibberellic acid. Well over a hundred distinct gibberellins (GA1, GA2, GA3…, numbered roughly in order of discovery) are now known across fungi and plants; GA3 remains the most extensively studied and the one referred to by default.

Gibberellin’s signature effect is stem and internode elongation, and it shows up in three related NEET-favourite contexts: it can restore normal height to genetically dwarf varieties of pea and maize when applied externally (the plant already has the machinery to elongate — it’s just missing the hormone that switches it on); it triggers bolting — the sudden, rapid elongation of the internode just before flowering — in naturally low, rosette-forming biennials like cabbage and beet; and, in several long-day plants and cold-requiring biennials, applying GA3 can substitute for the correct day length or the required cold exposure and trigger flowering anyway (a link this chapter will pick back up in the vernalisation section). Gibberellin is also essential to seed germination: in barley, the embryo secretes GA3, which diffuses to the surrounding aleurone layer and switches on synthesis of hydrolytic enzymes — above all α-amylase — that break down the starchy endosperm into sugars feeding the germinating embryo. That single reaction is the physiological basis of the malting step in brewing. Commercially, GA3 is also used to elongate the stalks of seedless-grape bunches and increase fruit size, and to speed cane elongation and raise sugarcane yield.

NEET trap

The barley aleurone/α-amylase story is a named, specific experiment — don’t generalise it into “GA breaks down starch.” GA3 induces the enzyme that then breaks down starch; the hormone itself has no catalytic role. Also don’t swap GA with auxin for bolting/flowering — internode elongation before flowering and substituting for photoperiod/cold requirements are gibberellin’s effects, not auxin’s.

Think first: you spray a genetically dwarf pea plant with GA3 once, and it grows to normal height. If you stop spraying, does it stay tall, or revert to dwarf?

It reverts to dwarf. The plant’s genotype (Chapter 6) hasn’t changed — it’s still unable to make its own gibberellin — you’ve only supplied the missing signal from outside, and only for as long as you keep supplying it. This is the general rule for hormone treatments: they switch existing genetic machinery on or off, they don’t rewrite the genotype behind it. A permanently tall dwarf pea would need a change at the gene level, not a spray bottle.

Cytokinin: found while trying to grow tissue in a dish

Cytokinin turned up as a side effect of a tissue-culture problem. Trying to induce cell division in cultured tobacco pith tissue, Folke Skoog and co-workers (including Carlos O. Miller) found that adding old, autoclaved herring-sperm DNA stimulated the tissue to divide; the active compound purified from it was named kinetin — itself a breakdown product, not a hormone plants actually make. The search that success triggered found the real, naturally occurring cytokinins in plants, the first being zeatin, isolated from corn (Zea mays) kernels; coconut milk (liquid endosperm), rich in natural cytokinins, was another early staple of tissue-culture media.

Cytokinin’s central role is promoting cytokinesis — cell division — but it typically needs auxin present to do it, which is the first hint that this hormone is defined mostly by its ratio to auxin. It also delays leaf senescence, keeping a treated leaf region green and metabolically active by drawing nutrients toward itself while surrounding untreated tissue ages normally (the Richmond–Lang effect); and, most usefully for horticulture, it overcomes apical dominance — promoting lateral bud outgrowth — which is the exact opposite of what auxin does to the same buds. In tissue culture, that auxin–cytokinin antagonism becomes the actual dial researchers turn: a high cytokinin:auxin ratio pushes cultured cells to form shoot buds, a low ratio pushes them to form roots, and roughly equal amounts maintain an undifferentiated mass of dividing cells called a callus. This is the physiological foundation of the micropropagation techniques Chapters 24 and 25 build on.

Ethylene: the only gas on the list

Every other hormone in this chapter is a solid, dissolved and transported in solution. Ethylene (C2H4) is a simple gas, synthesised from the amino acid methionine, that diffuses freely through air and tissue and is active at trace concentrations. It is also famously autocatalytic in ripening fruit — a ripening fruit makes ethylene, which triggers more ripening, which makes more ethylene — and diffuses easily enough between fruit that a single ripe apple genuinely can speed up ripening in the rest of the basket.

Ethylene drives fruit ripening in climacteric fruits (banana, mango, tomato, apple) — softening, colour change, starch-to-sugar conversion, aroma production — and promotes senescence and abscission of leaves, flowers, and fruit, often acting alongside ABA and against the protective effect of auxin and cytokinin. In etiolated (dark-grown) pea seedlings, exposure to ethylene produces the classic triple response: reduced elongation, increased radial (lateral) swelling of the stem, and horizontal growth — a reaction distinctive enough that it was historically used as ethylene’s standard bioassay. Ethylene is also the hormone behind two specific commercial applications NEET likes to ask about directly: it promotes the formation of female flowers in cucurbits (cucumber, squash, muskmelon), which growers exploit by spraying to increase fruit yield; and, applied commercially as the ethylene-releasing compound ethephon, it is used to synchronise flowering and fruit-set in pineapple, speed the ripening of mango and tomato, and hasten the degreening of citrus.

NEET trap

Ethylene is usually grouped with the growth inhibitors (senescence, abscission, the triple response’s reduced elongation) — but it is the hormone NCERT credits with promoting female flowers in cucurbits, a promoting effect. Don’t default to “auxin promotes, ethylene inhibits” as a blanket rule; check which specific response the question is asking about.

Abscisic acid: the general-purpose “stop” signal, and the stress hormone

If the first four hormones are mostly about starting or redirecting growth, abscisic acid (ABA) is mostly about stopping it. It is a general growth inhibitor, frequently antagonistic to gibberellin’s effects, and its levels rise sharply under drought, salinity, and other adverse conditions — earning it the nickname the stress hormone.

ABA accumulates in maturing seeds and induces seed dormancy, preventing premature (“vivipary”) germination while the seed is still attached to the parent plant and helping it tolerate desiccation once shed. It works directly against gibberellin’s germination-promoting, amylase-inducing signal, so a seed’s actual decision to stay dormant or germinate comes down to the ABA:GA ratio at that moment, not either hormone in isolation. Under water stress, ABA triggers stomatal closure by causing guard cells to lose K+ and water and collapse their turgor — the same stomatal mechanism Chapter 9 described, now under active hormonal control — which is why ABA is also called an antitranspirant. It additionally promotes bud dormancy heading into unfavourable seasons and cooperates with ethylene in leaf senescence and abscission.

Core concept

The ABA:GA ratio is the dormancy switch, and it’s worth remembering as a ratio rather than as two separate facts. High ABA relative to GA → dormant, closed, stress-protected. High GA relative to ABA → germinating, elongating, growth-committed. Nearly every “why does this seed/bud stay dormant” NEET question resolves to this one balance.

Directional growth vs. directionless movement: tropisms and nastic responses

A tropism is a growth movement of a plant organ toward (positive) or away from (negative) a directional external stimulus, produced — as the phototropism diagram above showed — by unequal, auxin-mediated elongation on the two sides of the organ. Phototropism is the response to light (shoots positive, roots typically negative or indifferent); geotropism (gravitropism) is the response to gravity (roots positive, shoots negative), sensed via dense starch grains (statoliths) settling toward the lower side of specialised cells; hydrotropism bends roots toward higher moisture, and can even override negative geotropism when a root must grow sideways to reach water; thigmotropism is the coiling response of a tendril to physical contact, growing faster on the side away from the touched surface so the tendril wraps around whatever it touched; and chemotropism is growth directed by a chemical gradient — the pollen tube’s growth toward the embryo sac, guided by a chemical cue from the synergids, is the standard NEET example, and Chapter 20 picks it up in full.

Nastic movements are a genuinely different mechanism, not just a different name. A nastic response is not directional — its direction is fixed by the plant’s own anatomy regardless of which side the stimulus comes from — and it is driven not by growth at all but by fast, reversible changes in turgor pressure within a specialised swelling called a pulvinus. Seismonasty is the touch-triggered, rapid leaf-folding of Mimosa pudica (“touch-me-not”): pulvinar cells rapidly lose water and turgor, the leaflets collapse within seconds, and they slowly re-open as turgor is restored. Nyctinasty is the daily “sleep movement” — leaflets of many legumes opening by day and folding by night — driven by a circadian rhythm in the same kind of pulvinar turgor change.

NEET trap

Mimosa’s touch response is a common wrong-answer target: it is seismonasty, a turgor-based nastic movement, not a “thigmotropism.” The distinguishing test is simple — does the response direction depend on which side the stimulus came from (tropism, growth-based, slow, irreversible) or is it fixed regardless of stimulus direction (nasty, turgor-based, fast, reversible)?

Photoperiodism: flowering on a schedule set by night length

Photoperiodism is a plant’s flowering response to the relative lengths of day and night. Plants fall into three groups relative to a species-specific critical day length: short-day plants (SDPs) flower only when the day length is below that critical value (chrysanthemum, cocklebur, tobacco); long-day plants (LDPs) flower only when day length is above it (spinach, radish, many spring cereals); and day-neutral plants (DNPs) flower on developmental schedule regardless of day length (tomato, cotton, sunflower, cucumber).

NEET trap

The name “short-day plant” is a historical misnomer that NEET exploits directly. Night-break experiments proved what the plant actually measures is the length of continuous, uninterrupted darkness: interrupting a long night with even a brief flash of light stops an SDP from flowering — as if the night had never been long — but does not stop (and can even help) an LDP. A “short-day plant” is more accurately a long-night plant; day length is just the inverse of the variable that’s actually being sensed.

The photoperiodic stimulus is perceived not at the shoot apex where flowering happens, but in the leaves — shown by defoliation experiments (a leafless plant fails to respond to a correct photoperiod at all) and by grafting experiments (a single leaf from a photoperiodically induced plant, grafted onto an uninduced one, can transmit the flowering stimulus across the graft union). That implies a mobile signal, made in the leaf and carried to the apex, historically referred to as florigen. The actual light-sensing molecule behind all of this is phytochrome, a pigment existing in two interconvertible forms: Pr (absorbs red light, ~660 nm) and Pfr (absorbs far-red light, ~730 nm, the physiologically active form). Red light converts Pr → Pfr; far-red light converts Pfr → Pr; and in prolonged darkness Pfr slowly reverts to Pr on its own — which is precisely the reversible clock a plant uses to time an uninterrupted dark period. The same Pr/Pfr switch also governs germination in photoblastic seeds such as lettuce, where a final flash of red light promotes germination and a final flash of far-red light inhibits it.

Vernalisation: flowering that needs a cold winter first, not a particular day length

Vernalisation is the induction or acceleration of flowering by exposure to low temperature — a distinct cue from photoperiodism (cold vs. light) even though both ultimately time flowering to a favourable season. Its classic use is in biennial crops such as winter wheat, beet, and cabbage, which naturally need one growing season of vegetative growth, an intervening winter, and then a second season to flower; artificially cold-treating the seed or seedling can compress that into a single growing season, which is exploited agriculturally to shorten breeding cycles.

The cold stimulus is perceived in actively dividing meristematic tissue (shoot apex, root apex, embryo), and that “memory” of having been cold-treated is retained through the subsequent cell divisions of the growing plant — though it can be lost again through devernalisation if a sufficiently high temperature follows too soon after the cold treatment. Tie this back to the gibberellin section: in several vernalisation-requiring species, a dose of GA3 can substitute for the cold requirement entirely and trigger flowering without it ever having been cold-treated — another case of one hormone standing in for what looks, on the surface, like an entirely different kind of signal.

Where this leaves you

Strip away the individual hormone names and this chapter is really one idea, applied five ways: a plant has no nerves to make fast local decisions, so it makes slow, distributed, chemical ones instead — and nearly every decision comes down to a ratio (auxin:cytokinin, ABA:GA, Pr:Pfr) rather than any single hormone acting alone. Meristems keep growth open-ended; hormones decide where that growth goes, how fast, and whether it happens at all; and light, gravity, touch, and cold are simply the inputs those ratios respond to. What this chapter hasn’t touched yet is what happens once you swap a plant’s open, modular, hormone-driven body plan for an animal’s closed, largely fixed one — where coordination has to run through both a fast nervous system and a hormonal one, and homeostasis becomes the central organising problem instead of growth. That’s Chapter 12.
