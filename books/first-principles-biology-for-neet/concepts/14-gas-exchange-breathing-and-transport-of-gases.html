Chapter 14 · Part IV — Human Structure & Function

Gas Exchange
Breathing and Transport of Gases

Chapter 13 left you with glucose and amino acids crossing into the blood, ready to be burned for ATP — but burning fuel needs an oxidizer, and burning produces exhaust. This chapter is the system that supplies the oxidizer and removes the exhaust: it moves O2 from the atmosphere to every mitochondrion in the body, and CO2 from every mitochondrion back out. Nothing in this chapter is more complicated than diffusion along a partial pressure gradient across a thin, moist surface, repeated at three different scales — atmosphere to alveolus, alveolus to blood, blood to tissue — with one clever carrier molecule, haemoglobin, doing almost all of the heavy lifting for the long trip in between.

NCERT mapping
Class 11 — Breathing and Exchange of Gases, in full: respiratory organs and tract, mechanism of breathing, respiratory volumes and capacities, exchange and transport of O2 and CO2, regulation of respiration, and respiratory disorders.
NEET angle
Lung volume and capacity definitions with their arithmetic, partial pressure values at each stage, the Bohr and Haldane effects, chloride shift direction, and which structure actually drives the rate of breathing (it isn’t O2).

Two ideas that carry the whole chapter

The first idea is diffusion, not pumping. No muscle pushes O2 out of an alveolus into blood, or CO2 out of a tissue into blood — every gas exchange step in this chapter happens passively, down its own partial pressure gradient, across a membrane thin enough and moist enough to let it pass. Breathing itself (the mechanical in-and-out of air) exists only to keep those gradients steep: it constantly refreshes alveolar air with high-O2, low-CO2 atmosphere so diffusion always has something to work with. The second idea is that blood plasma alone is a poor transport medium for O2 — O2 barely dissolves in water — so the body carries it almost entirely bound to haemoglobin, a molecule built specifically to pick O2 up where it’s abundant and let it go where it’s scarce. CO2, being far more soluble and chemically reactive, travels by three different routes at once, and haemoglobin is central to two of those three as well.

Core concept

Every step in this chapter is either “a gradient moving a gas passively” or “a mechanism that keeps a gradient steep enough to matter.” If you can’t place a fact into one of those two buckets, you probably haven’t connected it to the chapter’s logic yet.

The respiratory system: one tube, built to keep opening

Air enters through the external nostrils into the nasal passage, lined with mucus and fine hairs that warm, moisten, and filter it, then crosses the pharynx — a shared crossroads with the digestive tract, exactly as in Chapter 13 — and the larynx (voice box), a cartilaginous chamber that also houses the vocal cords. A flap called the epiglottis folds down over the larynx during swallowing specifically to keep food out of the airway. Below the larynx, the trachea runs down the neck, held permanently open by C-shaped rings of cartilage — incomplete at the back where the tract runs against the oesophagus, so that a swallowed bolus still has room to pass.

The trachea splits into two primary bronchi, one per lung, which branch repeatedly into secondary and tertiary bronchi, then into progressively narrower, cartilage-free bronchioles, terminating in clusters of thin-walled sacs called alveoli. This is the actual gas-exchange surface: each alveolus is one cell layer of squamous epithelium wrapped in a dense capillary network, and the two together form the respiratory membrane — thin enough for a gas molecule to cross in a fraction of a second, and repeated across hundreds of millions of alveoli to give the lungs a total exchange surface roughly the size of a tennis court packed into your chest. Each lung sits inside its own pleural cavity, wrapped in a double pleural membrane with a thin film of pleural fluid between the layers that lets the lung expand and slide against the chest wall with almost no friction. The right lung has three lobes, the left has two (making room for the heart).

NEET trap

The trachea, bronchi, and larger bronchioles are conducting zone only — they move air but exchange nothing. Only the alveoli (and the very last generation of respiratory bronchioles/alveolar ducts leading into them) are gas-exchange surface. A question describing a structure as having “cartilage rings” or being “C-shaped” is pointing at the trachea, never at a bronchiole — bronchioles have no cartilage at all.

Mechanism of breathing: Boyle’s law with muscles

Air moves in and out of the lungs for exactly one reason: pressure differences, created by changing the volume of the thoracic cavity. Boyle’s law says pressure and volume are inversely related at constant temperature — enlarge the chest and the air inside expands, dropping its pressure below atmospheric, so outside air flows in to equalize it; shrink the chest and the reverse happens. The lungs themselves have no muscle to do this — they are pulled along passively by the pleural membrane sticking them to the moving chest wall.

Inspiration is active: the dome-shaped diaphragm contracts and flattens, and the external intercostal muscles contract, lifting the ribs and sternum up and outward. Both movements enlarge the thoracic cavity, pulmonary pressure drops to roughly 1–2 mmHg below atmospheric, and air flows in. Expiration at rest is passive — the diaphragm and external intercostals simply relax, elastic recoil of the lungs and chest wall shrinks the cavity back down, pulmonary pressure rises 1–2 mmHg above atmospheric, and air flows out with no muscular effort at all. Only forced expiration (as in a hard cough, or the extra squeeze that empties the expiratory reserve volume below) recruits muscle actively — the internal intercostals pull the ribs down and in, and the abdominal muscles push the diaphragm back up.

NEET trap

“Quiet/normal expiration is passive” is one of the most-repeated true statements on this topic — and one of the most-repeated wrong distractors flips it, claiming expiration always needs muscular contraction. It only does during forced expiration.

Respiratory volumes and capacities: four measurements, four sums

A spirometer traces how much air moves with each breath, and NCERT breaks that trace into four non-overlapping volumes, which combine pairwise and all together into four capacities. Get the four base volumes solid and every capacity is just addition.

VolumeApprox. valueDefinition
Tidal volume (TV)~500 mLAir breathed in or out in one normal, quiet breath
Inspiratory reserve volume (IRV)~2500–3000 mLExtra air you can force in above a normal inspiration
Expiratory reserve volume (ERV)~1000–1100 mLExtra air you can force out after a normal expiration
Residual volume (RV)~1100–1200 mLAir left in the lungs even after the most forceful expiration — the lungs never fully empty

Two more capacities combine adjacent pairs: inspiratory capacity (IC) = TV + IRV (everything you can pull in starting from a normal resting breath), and functional residual capacity (FRC) = ERV + RV (everything left in the lungs after a normal, quiet expiration).

NEET trap

Any capacity built from RV — that’s FRC and TLC — cannot be measured by a simple spirometer, because RV is air that, by definition, never leaves the lung to be traced. Only VC and IC (no RV in either) are directly spirometric. This is also why the exam sometimes states RV needs a different technique (e.g. helium dilution) to measure at all — that statement is testing exactly this distinction, not a random extra fact.

Exchange of gases: the same gradient, twice

Gas exchange happens at two separate interfaces, and both are governed purely by partial pressure — the pressure a single gas in a mixture would exert on its own, written pO2 and pCO2. A gas always diffuses from where its partial pressure is higher to where it’s lower, with no input of energy.

Read the table as two gradients running in opposite directions at each interface. At the alveolus ↔ blood interface, O2 is higher in alveolar air (104) than in the arriving deoxygenated blood (40), so O2 diffuses into the blood; CO2 is higher in that same arriving blood (45) than in alveolar air (40), so CO2 diffuses out of the blood into the alveolus, to be exhaled. At the blood ↔ tissue interface, the pattern repeats in the same two directions for the same reason: oxygenated blood (pO2 95) meets actively respiring tissue (pO2 ~40, constantly using up O2), so O2 leaves the blood; tissue pCO2 (~45, constantly producing CO2) is higher than the arriving blood’s (40), so CO2 enters the blood. Solubility matters too: CO2 is roughly 20× more soluble in the respiratory membrane than O2, so it diffuses efficiently even down a much smaller pressure difference than O2 needs.

Core concept

You never need to memorize which way a gas moves at a given interface — just read the two partial pressure numbers and let the gradient tell you. High pO2 pulls O2 toward it; high pCO2 pulls CO2 toward it. Every exchange step in this chapter reduces to that one comparison.

Transport of oxygen: haemoglobin as a reversible sponge

Only about 3% of the O2 in blood travels dissolved directly in plasma — O2 is simply too poorly soluble in water for plasma alone to carry enough of it. The remaining 97% binds reversibly to the iron in haemoglobin (Hb), each molecule of which carries up to four O2 molecules as oxyhaemoglobin. The binding is cooperative: once the first O2 binds, the other three sites bind more easily, which is why the graph of Hb saturation against pO2 — the oxygen dissociation curve — is S-shaped (sigmoid) rather than a straight line: saturation rises slowly at first, climbs steeply through the middle range, then flattens out near full saturation.

Where Hb sits on that curve is not fixed — it shifts with conditions, and the shift is exactly what makes the loading/unloading useful. High pO2, low pCO2, low H+, and lower temperature (the alveolar environment) all favour loading: Hb grabs O2 greedily. Low pO2, high pCO2, high H+, and higher temperature (an actively respiring tissue) all favour unloading: Hb lets O2 go. The specific effect of rising CO2/H+ pushing the curve rightward — lowering Hb’s affinity for O2 at any given pO2 — is named the Bohr effect, and it is precisely the mechanism that makes Hb dump extra O2 exactly where tissue is working hardest and generating the most CO2.

NEET trap

The Bohr effect shifts the curve right (lower O2 affinity, more unloading) with rising CO2/H+/temperature — conditions of active tissue, not of the lung. Don’t confuse direction: a left shift (higher affinity, e.g. foetal haemoglobin relative to adult, or lower CO2/temperature) means Hb holds on to O2 more tightly, the opposite behaviour.

Transport of carbon dioxide: three routes at once

CO2 is more soluble and more chemically reactive than O2, so the blood carries it three different ways simultaneously. About 7% simply dissolves in plasma. About 20–23% binds directly to the globin (protein) part of Hb — not the haem/iron site O2 uses, so this doesn’t compete with O2 binding — forming carbaminohaemoglobin. The remaining, dominant share, roughly 70%, travels as bicarbonate (HCO3−): inside red blood cells, the enzyme carbonic anhydrase rapidly catalyses CO2 + H2O → H2CO3 → H+ + HCO3−. This reaction runs forward at the tissues (high pCO2 pushes CO2 into the RBC to be converted) and reverses at the lungs (low alveolar pCO2 pulls the equilibrium back toward free CO2, which then diffuses out to be exhaled).

As HCO3− builds up inside the RBC at the tissue level, it diffuses out into the plasma — and to keep the cell electrically neutral, a Cl− ion moves in to replace it. This exchange is the chloride shift (Hamburger phenomenon); at the lungs, as HCO3− is converted back to CO2 and leaves, Cl− moves back out — a reverse chloride shift. The H+ released by carbonic acid’s dissociation, meanwhile, is largely mopped up by binding to deoxygenated Hb, which is exactly why low-O2 (deoxygenated) Hb carries CO2 and H+ better than oxygenated Hb does — the Haldane effect, the CO2-transport mirror of the O2-transport Bohr effect. The two reinforce each other perfectly at both ends of the circuit: at the tissue, Hb is losing O2 (Bohr effect: because CO2/H+ is high) at the very moment it becomes better at picking up CO2/H+ (Haldane effect: because it’s losing O2); at the lung, the reverse happens on both counts at once.

NEET trap

Keep the three CO2 routes and their approximate shares fixed: dissolved in plasma (~7%) < carbaminohaemoglobin (~20–23%) < bicarbonate (~70%, the majority). A common wrong option swaps bicarbonate and carbaminohaemoglobin’s shares, or claims CO2 binds the same haem site O2 uses — it binds globin, a different part of the molecule entirely.

Regulation of respiration: CO2, not O2, sets the pace

Breathing rate and depth are set primarily by a respiratory rhythm centre in the medulla oblongata, which generates the basic inspiration/expiration cycle on its own. A pneumotaxic centre in the pons fine-tunes that rhythm, able to shorten inspiration and so adjust the breathing rate. The rhythm centre itself is steered by a nearby chemosensitive area, plus peripheral chemoreceptors in the aortic arch and carotid arteries — and what all of these actually monitor, first and foremost, is CO2 and H+ concentration, not O2. A rise in blood CO2 (hence a fall in pH) is a strong, fast, direct stimulus that drives the rhythm centre to increase both the rate and depth of breathing; O2 levels have to fall quite far before they meaningfully add to the drive to breathe.

NEET trap

The single most-tested fact in this section: the body’s breathing drive is regulated primarily by CO2/H+, not by O2 levels. “We breathe faster because O2 is low” is the intuitive-sounding wrong answer; the correct trigger, in essentially every normal physiological scenario, is rising CO2.

When the system breaks

Asthma is difficulty in breathing caused by inflammation and narrowing of the bronchi and bronchioles, producing wheezing. Emphysema is a chronic disorder, most often driven by long-term cigarette smoking, in which alveolar walls are progressively damaged and merge into fewer, larger air spaces — the effective gas-exchange surface area shrinks directly as a result. Occupational respiratory disorders arise in industries with heavy exposure to fine dust — grinding, stone-breaking, mining — where years of inhaling particulate matter provoke chronic inflammation that leads to fibrosis (proliferation of fibrous connective tissue in the lung), permanently reducing lung function; this is exactly why such workplaces mandate protective masks.

Think first: a person breathing pure, 100% oxygen still has essentially normal-looking blood pO2 but is now hyperventilating heavily because their blood CO2 has risen (say, from a faulty rebreathing mask). Which change — the high O2 or the high CO2 — is driving the hyperventilation, and why does this match what you’d predict from how regulation actually works?

The high CO2, not the high O2, is driving it — and this is exactly what the regulation section predicts rather than an exception to it. The chemosensitive area and the peripheral chemoreceptors are tuned to detect rising CO2/H+ as the primary trigger for increasing ventilation; abundant O2 does essentially nothing to suppress that drive, because O2 was never the main signal in the first place. This is also why pure-O2 breathing doesn’t “turn off” the urge to breathe even though oxygen delivery is more than adequate: the rhythm centre is responding to the CO2 gauge, and that gauge is reading high regardless of how saturated Hb already is with O2. (It’s also the reason CO2-retaining conditions feel like suffocating even in oxygen-rich air — the sensation of breathlessness tracks CO2, not O2.)

Where this leaves you

Oxygen is now reliably arriving in blood as oxyhaemoglobin, and CO2 is reliably leaving it as bicarbonate, carbaminohaemoglobin, and dissolved gas — but none of that matters unless the blood carrying them actually reaches every tissue and returns, on schedule, under enough pressure to keep the traffic moving. Chapter 15 opens the pump and the plumbing that make that possible: the heart, the vessels, and the closed circulatory system that carries not just O2 and CO2 but everything Chapter 13’s digestion produced, to every cell that needs it.
