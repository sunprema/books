Human Reproduction
and Reproductive Health

Chapter 20 closed with a promise: the same underlying logic that builds a seed inside a flower — gametogenesis, delivery, fertilization, post-fertilization development — also builds a human being, just rewired for a body that can't rely on wind or an insect to make the introduction. In humans, a continuously signalling hormonal axis (hypothalamus → pituitary → gonad) replaces the pollinator as the coordinating system, and internal fertilization inside the female's own body replaces a pollen tube growing through a style. This chapter follows that rewiring end to end — how a testis and an ovary each build a gamete, how the two meet and implant, how a placenta and a birth canal carry a zygote through to a newborn — and then, because getting this machinery right (and protecting it) is a matter of public health as much as biology, how contraception, screening, and assisted reproduction manage it deliberately.

NCERT mapping
Class 12 — Human Reproduction (male and female reproductive systems; gametogenesis; menstrual cycle; fertilisation and embryo development; pregnancy and embryonic development; parturition and lactation) and Reproductive Health (reproductive health and its importance; population stabilisation and contraception; medical termination of pregnancy; sexually transmitted infections; infertility and assisted reproductive technologies).
NEET angle
Hormone–target matching (GnRH/LH/FSH/testosterone/estrogen/progesterone/hCG/oxytocin/prolactin), labelled diagrams of the male and female tracts, menstrual-cycle phase-and-hormone-level questions, site-of-fertilization vs. site-of-implantation traps, contraceptive-method classification, ART acronym matching (IVF/ZIFT/GIFT/ICSI/IUI/IUT).

One hormonal axis, two gonads, two very different jobs

Everything in this chapter's first half runs off the same control loop: the hypothalamus secretes GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone), which drives the anterior pituitary to secrete two gonadotropins — LH (luteinizing hormone) and FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) — which then act on the testis or the ovary. This is the hypothalamus–pituitary–gonadal (HPG) axis, and it switches on at puberty in both sexes. What each gonad does with that same pair of signals, though, is almost opposite in shape.

Core concept

The testis runs the axis continuously and non-cyclically: from puberty into old age, LH and FSH stay on and sperm production never really stops, matching a strategy of producing enormous numbers of small, cheap gametes. The ovary runs the same axis cyclically, in a roughly month-long loop that rises and falls in a fixed order — matching a strategy of releasing one large, maximally provisioned gamete at a time, from a supply that was actually fixed before birth. Keep that one contrast in view and the rest of the chapter's hormone facts stop being a list to memorize and start being consequences of it.

The male reproductive system: a factory, a maturation line, and a delivery duct

The primary male reproductive organs are a pair of testes, held outside the abdominal cavity in a sac called the scrotum. That external position is functional, not incidental: spermatogenesis requires a temperature about 2–2.5°C below core body temperature, and the scrotum's muscles adjust its position to hold the testes there.

Each testis is divided into roughly 250 testicular lobules, each containing one to three tightly coiled seminiferous tubules — the actual site of sperm production. A seminiferous tubule's wall holds two functionally distinct cell types: the dividing male germ cells that will become sperm, and non-dividing Sertoli cells, which nourish the developing germ cells and respond to FSH. In the loose connective tissue between the tubules sit clusters of Leydig (interstitial) cells, which respond to LH by synthesizing and secreting testosterone, the principal male sex hormone (androgen).

Sperm leaving the seminiferous tubules pass through the rete testis and vasa efferentia into the highly coiled epididymis, where they are stored and complete their functional maturation. From there the vas deferens ascends, loops over the urinary bladder, and joins the duct of a seminal vesicle to form a short ejaculatory duct, which opens into the urethra. The urethra, now a shared passage for urine and semen, runs through the prostate gland and then the penis — along the way receiving secretions from the seminal vesicles, the prostate, and the paired bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands, which together make up the fluid part of semen: fructose- and calcium-rich, nourishing and activating the sperm, and, from the bulbourethral glands, lubricating.

NEET trap

Don't mix up the two accessory-gland jobs: the seminal vesicles and prostate contribute the bulk of seminal plasma (fructose, citrate, enzymes, prostaglandins) that nourishes and activates sperm, while the bulbourethral glands mainly secrete mucus that lubricates the urethra before ejaculation — a small-volume contribution, not the main fluid. And rete testis and vasa efferentia come before the epididymis in sperm's route out, not after.

Spermatogenesis: a continuous conveyor belt from puberty onward

At puberty (roughly age 13–14 in humans), a sharp rise in GnRH kicks the HPG axis into action: LH stimulates Leydig cells to secrete testosterone, and testosterone in turn stimulates spermatogenesis itself; FSH acts on Sertoli cells, prompting them to secrete factors that assist spermiogenesis, the final differentiation step. Along the wall of each seminiferous tubule, diploid spermatogonia multiply by mitosis; some commit to the meiotic pathway and enlarge into diploid primary spermatocytes, which complete meiosis I to give haploid secondary spermatocytes, which complete meiosis II to give haploid spermatids. Spermatids then undergo spermiogenesis — a reshaping into a streamlined, motile spermatozoon with no further division — and are finally released into the tubule lumen by spermiation.

A mature sperm has three regions built for one job — deliver a haploid nucleus and enough energy to reach the egg. The head carries an elongated, condensed haploid nucleus capped by the acrosome, a membrane-bound sac of hydrolytic enzymes that digests a path through the egg's coverings at fertilization. The midpiece is packed with spirally arranged mitochondria, generating the ATP that powers the tail (flagellum), whose whip-like beating drives motility.

NEET trap

Fertility questions quote specific NCERT thresholds — a normal ejaculate carries roughly 200–300 million sperm, of which at least 60% must be of normal shape and size, and at least 40% must show vigorous motility, for the semen to be considered fertile. Falling short on any of these is a recognized cause of male infertility, distinct from a hormonal or ductal problem.

The female reproductive system: a fixed egg supply and a rebuildable nursery

The primary female reproductive organs are a pair of ovaries, almond-shaped and 2–4 cm long, each producing both ova and steroid hormones. Alongside them sit the accessory ducts: a pair of fallopian tubes (oviducts), a single uterus, a cervix, and a vagina, plus external genitalia collectively called the vulva.

The fallopian tube's infundibulum, fringed with finger-like fimbriae, sweeps close to the ovary and helps collect the ovum at ovulation; its wider ampulla is where fertilization normally happens, and its narrower isthmus joins the uterus. The pear-shaped uterus has three layers: an outer perimetrium, a thick middle layer of smooth muscle, the myometrium (the layer that contracts powerfully during labour), and an inner glandular endometrium, which thickens and sheds in a monthly cycle and is the tissue a blastocyst implants into. The uterus narrows into the cervix, which opens into the vagina; together the cervical canal and vaginal canal form the birth canal.

Core concept

Notice the division of labour baked into this anatomy before any hormone acts on it: the ampulla is built to be the meeting point (fertilization), while the endometrium is built to be the settling point (implantation) — two different locations, days apart, connected only by the zygote's own onward movement through the isthmus. Site-of-event questions in this chapter are almost always testing whether you've kept those two locations straight.

Oogenesis: a supply fixed before birth, released one at a time

Oogenesis begins in the fetal ovary, long before puberty. Millions of oogonia form by mitosis, and by mid-fetal life every one of them has already entered meiosis and stalled in prophase I — each is now a primary oocyte, arrested at that exact stage until puberty finally allows it to resume, cycle by cycle, decades later. No new oogonia are added after birth: a female's entire lifetime egg supply is set before she is born, and most of it is lost to degeneration long before it is ever used — only 60,000–80,000 primary follicles remain per ovary by puberty, out of the original millions.

Each primary oocyte, surrounded by a layer of granulosa cells, is a primary follicle. Around it, additional granulosa layers plus a surrounding theca layer build a secondary follicle, which develops a fluid-filled cavity (the antrum) to become a tertiary follicle. Within that tertiary follicle, the primary oocyte finally completes its long-stalled meiosis I — but asymmetrically: one division produces a large haploid secondary oocyte, which keeps almost all the cytoplasm, nutrients, and organelles, and a tiny, largely discarded first polar body. The follicle matures into a Graafian follicle; the secondary oocyte develops a surrounding zona pellucida; and the Graafian follicle ruptures at ovulation, releasing the secondary oocyte from the ovary. That secondary oocyte is arrested again — this time at metaphase II — and will only complete meiosis II, producing a second polar body and a true mature ovum, if a sperm actually penetrates it.

NEET trap

What is released at ovulation is technically a secondary oocyte arrested at metaphase II, not a finished "ovum" — meiosis II only completes after sperm entry. Also keep the follicle sequence in strict order for diagram-labelling questions: primary follicle → secondary follicle → tertiary (antral) follicle → Graafian follicle, each stage marked by the same kind of feature name (extra granulosa layers, then an antrum) building on the last.

The menstrual cycle: the female HPG axis running as a fixed-order loop

The first menstruation at puberty is menarche; the cycle then repeats roughly every 28–29 days until menopause (around age 50), except during pregnancy and lactation. One cycle has four phases, driven by the rise and fall of four hormones — FSH and LH from the pituitary, estrogen and progesterone from the ovary.

Core concept

The LH surge is a rare case of positive feedback inside an otherwise negative-feedback-dominated endocrine system (the theme Chapter 12 built around homeostasis): rising estrogen from the maturing follicle, instead of suppressing the pituitary the way it normally would, briefly flips to stimulate a burst of LH release. That surge is the single event that converts "a follicle growing" into "an egg released" — which is exactly why NEET questions treat day 14 as the hinge the whole cycle turns on.

Fertilization, cleavage, and implantation: from two cells to one embedded embryo

Sperm deposited in the vagina during coitus swim up through the cervix, uterus, and isthmus to reach the ampulla, where a released secondary oocyte typically is. Only one sperm actually fertilizes the oocyte: its acrosome releases enzymes that digest a path through the surrounding zona pellucida, and once that first sperm's membrane fuses with the oocyte's, the zona pellucida rapidly becomes impenetrable to any other sperm — the block to polyspermy. Sperm entry also triggers the oocyte to finally complete meiosis II, and the two haploid nuclei — sperm and egg — fuse to form a diploid zygote. Because the sperm alone carries either an X or a Y chromosome (the egg always carries X), it is the father's sperm, not the mother, that genetically determines the child's sex.

The zygote begins mitotic cleavage divisions while being swept along the isthmus toward the uterus — 2, then 4, then 8, then 16 cells (blastomeres), forming a solid ball called the morula. Continued division and reorganization produces a blastocyst: an outer layer of cells, the trophoblast, surrounding a fluid cavity and an inner cell mass that will become the embryo proper. Around day 6–7, the trophoblast attaches to the endometrium — implantation — and pregnancy formally begins.

NEET trap

Fertilization happens in the ampulla; implantation happens in the endometrium of the uterus, roughly a week later — a frequently swapped pair of facts. And "the sperm determines the baby's sex" is worth stating precisely: it's the presence of a Y-bearing versus X-bearing sperm that decides it, since every egg contributes an X regardless.

After implantation, finger-like chorionic villi grow from the trophoblast into the surrounding uterine tissue; villi plus uterine tissue together form the placenta, connected to the embryo by the umbilical cord. The placenta is both a physiological interface — supplying oxygen and nutrients, removing carbon dioxide and waste, without the mother's and fetus's blood actually mixing — and an endocrine gland, secreting human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), human placental lactogen (hPL), estrogens, progestogens, and, later, relaxin. Rising hCG early in pregnancy is what a home pregnancy test detects in urine.

Core concept

Just as Chapter 20 found flowering plants delaying endosperm production until a fertilization event confirmed it was worth the investment, the human body delays its heaviest maternal commitment — sustained high progesterone, an expanded blood supply, an entire new endocrine organ — until implantation confirms a pregnancy is actually underway. hCG's whole job in early pregnancy is to rescue the corpus luteum from its scheduled degeneration, keeping progesterone high, because until the placenta itself is mature enough to take over hormone production, the corpus luteum is still the only thing maintaining the endometrium the embryo is embedded in.

Pregnancy, parturition, and lactation: sustaining, then ending, then feeding

Human gestation lasts about nine months (38–40 weeks). Throughout, placental and maternal hormones — estrogens, progestogens, cortisol, prolactin, hCG, hPL — rise to support fetal growth and prepare the mother's body, with progesterone in particular essential for maintaining the pregnancy by keeping the myometrium quiescent.

Parturition (childbirth) is triggered by signals from the fully developed fetus and placenta, which together produce mild uterine contractions — the foetal ejection reflex. This reflex stimulates the mother's posterior pituitary to release oxytocin, which acts on the myometrium to intensify contractions; stronger contractions stimulate still more oxytocin release — a self-reinforcing positive feedback loop that escalates until the baby, and then the placenta, is expelled through the birth canal.

After birth, lactation begins: prolactin from the anterior pituitary drives milk production in the mammary glands' alveoli, while oxytocin triggers milk ejection (the "let-down reflex") in response to the infant's suckling. The first milk, colostrum, is especially rich in antibodies that give the newborn passive immune protection.

NEET trap

Don't swap the two lactation hormones: prolactin makes milk; oxytocin releases it. Also note the parturition loop is positive feedback (contraction → more oxytocin → more contraction), a deliberate exception to the negative-feedback homeostasis Chapter 12 built as the default — positive feedback is used precisely because childbirth needs a process that escalates to completion rather than one that self-corrects back to a set point.

Reproductive health: from biology to public policy

NCERT defines reproductive health broadly — total well-being in the physical, emotional, behavioural, and social aspects of reproduction, not merely the absence of disease. India's Reproductive and Child Health Care (RCH) programmes exist to spread awareness of reproductive biology, contraception, safe practices, and available medical care; comprehensive sex education in schools is promoted for the same reason — correct information reduces myths and unsafe practices far more reliably than silence does. A specific legal measure worth knowing by name: a statutory ban on prenatal sex determination (the PCPNDT Act), enacted to curb female foeticide and the resulting decline in the female sex ratio.

Medical termination of pregnancy (MTP), legalized in India in 1971 with restrictions, is relatively safe up to the first trimester (12 weeks); second-trimester termination carries substantially higher risk. MTP is a legitimate option for unwanted or medically necessary pregnancies (including after rape), but the same medical technology used to sex-determine a fetus (e.g. amniocentesis) has also been illegally misused to enable sex-selective abortion — the specific harm the statutory ban above targets.

NEET trap

Saheli's two defining facts are both commonly tested together: developed at the Central Drug Research Institute (CDRI), Lucknow, and non-steroidal (unlike ordinary combined pills), taken once a week rather than daily. For IUDs, sort by mechanism, not just name: Lippes loop is non-medicated; CuT/Cu7/Multiload are copper-releasing; Progestasert/LNG-20 are hormone-releasing.

Sexually transmitted infections, infertility, and assisted reproduction

Sexually transmitted infections (STIs), also called sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) or reproductive tract infections (RTIs), spread through sexual contact. NCERT's list: gonorrhoea, syphilis, genital herpes, chlamydiasis, genital warts, trichomoniasis, hepatitis-B, and HIV infection (leading to AIDS).

NEET trap

Except for hepatitis-B and HIV infection, every STI on that list is completely curable if detected early and treated properly. Early symptoms are often mild (itching, discharge, mild pain or swelling) and, especially in women, can be asymptomatic — which is precisely why delayed diagnosis, not the disease's inherent severity, is what allows STIs to badly damage fertility. Consistent condom use, avoiding multiple/unknown partners, and prompt medical consultation are the standard preventive triad.

Infertility is the inability to conceive despite unprotected intercourse for about two years, and can stem from a physical, congenital, hormonal, immunological, or functional problem in either partner. Where specialized treatment can't restore natural conception, assisted reproductive technology (ART) offers alternative routes to pregnancy:

NEET trap

Only ZIFT and IUT transfer something already fertilized (a zygote/embryo) — GIFT transfers unfertilized gametes into the tube, and ICSI and IUI don't involve fertilization happening in the lab's culture dish at all (ICSI injects one sperm directly into one egg; IUI simply places semen closer to where natural fertilization can occur). "How many of these ARTs involve in-vitro fertilization" is a recurring NEET question format — work it out from what each acronym actually does rather than memorizing a count.

Think first: Chapter 19 framed sexual reproduction's whole payoff as genetic variation, generated fresh each generation by meiosis and random fertilization. Yet a human female is born with her entire lifetime egg supply already present, each primary oocyte frozen in prophase I — sometimes for 40+ years before one finally completes meiosis I. Why would the female side "pre-load" its gamete supply before birth instead of manufacturing fresh oocytes continuously, the way a testis manufactures sperm right through adulthood?

The genetic-variation payoff doesn't actually require making new oocytes throughout life — that payoff comes from crossing over and independent assortment, and both already happened once, back when each primary oocyte entered meiosis I during fetal development. What differs between the sexes isn't how much variation gets generated; it's how each gamete is provisioned. An egg has to carry a huge reserve of cytoplasm, mitochondria, and stored mRNA — everything the future zygote needs before its own genes switch on — and building that reserve for millions of oocytes is only feasible during a protected fetal window with abundant maternal resources on tap, not one cell at a time across decades of adult life. A sperm, by contrast, is stripped down to almost nothing but a nucleus, a motor, and a fuel supply, and testosterone-driven continuous production can keep manufacturing billions of them cheaply for as long as the testis functions. That's the isogamy–anisogamy–oogamy spectrum from Chapter 19 again, just scaled up to explain timing as well as size: oogamy's whole logic is "invest heavily in one gamete," and pre-loading the entire supply before birth is simply where that investment gets front-loaded in humans, rather than in a female continuously synthesizing eggs the way a male continuously synthesizes sperm.

Where this leaves you

One control loop, the HPG axis, runs everything in this chapter's first half — continuously in the testis, cyclically in the ovary — and everything after fertilization is that same logic of "confirm, then invest": a block to polyspermy confirms one sperm won, implantation confirms a pregnancy is underway before the placenta commits its resources, and a positive-feedback loop of oxytocin confirms labour has genuinely started before it escalates to delivery. The second half of the chapter is that same biology meeting policy: contraception and MTP manage fertility deliberately, screening and treatment manage the infections and infertility that can disrupt it, and ART rebuilds fertilization itself when the ordinary route fails. Chapter 22 turns from "is this system working as designed" to a broader question — what happens when the body is invaded or malfunctions at all, and how immunity and medicine respond.
