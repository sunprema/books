Sexual Reproduction
in Flowering Plants

Chapter 19 built the general machinery of sexual reproduction — two gametes, one fusion, a genetically new zygote. Flowering plants (angiosperms) run that same machinery, but with a problem animals don't have: a plant cannot walk toward a mate. So it built a structure that does the walking for it — the flower — and evolved a delivery system (pollen, carried by wind, water, or an animal) that gets a male gamete to a female gamete without either individual moving at all. On top of that, angiosperms do something no other group of organisms does: they fertilize twice in a single event, using both sperm cells a pollen tube delivers. This chapter follows that whole choreography — from a flower bud to a fertilized seed — end to end.

The flower is a reproductive organ, not just an ornament

Strip away the petals and a flower is, functionally, two factories arranged around a central axis: the androecium (the stamens, collectively — the male whorl) manufactures pollen, and the gynoecium (the pistil/carpel(s), collectively — the female whorl) manufactures ovules and, later, receives pollen and houses the developing seed. Everything else — sepals, petals, colour, scent, nectar — exists to protect or promote that one transaction: getting a male gamete from a stamen into an ovule inside a carpel.

Core concept

A stamen has an anther (usually bilobed, each lobe with two microsporangia — so four microsporangia per anther, called a tetragonal/dithecous anther) sitting atop a filament. A pistil has a stigma (receives pollen), a style (the pollen tube's path), and an ovary containing one or more ovules attached at the placenta. Every event in this chapter happens inside one of these four structures: microsporangium → pollen; ovule → embryo sac; stigma/style → pollen–pistil interaction; ovary → fruit.

Microsporogenesis: building the male gametophyte

Inside each microsporangium, a mass of diploid sporogenous tissue differentiates into microspore mother cells (pollen mother cells, PMCs). Each PMC undergoes meiosis — the process is called microsporogenesis — to yield four haploid microspores held together as a microspore tetrad. As the anther matures the microspores separate and develop a tough wall to become individual pollen grains: the anther's four microsporangia thereby produce enormous numbers of pollen simultaneously.

A mature pollen grain has a thick, sculpted, chemically tough outer wall (the exine, built largely of sporopollenin — one of the most resistant organic materials known, which is why pollen fossilizes so well and survives almost any chemical or physical attack) and a thin inner wall (the intine). At shedding, the pollen grain's single haploid nucleus has already divided mitotically once, giving a larger vegetative (tube) cell and a smaller generative cell — the pollen grain is thus 2-celled at release in most species. In roughly a third of angiosperm families the generative cell divides again before shedding, giving a 3-celled pollen grain (vegetative cell + two sperm cells) already at the time of release.

NEET trap

Sporopollenin is so chemically inert that no known enzyme, acid, or base degrades it — a favourite "which biomolecule survives fossilization" fact. Don't confuse the vegetative cell (large, irregular nucleus, food-rich, and the one that grows into the pollen tube) with the generative cell (small, spindle-shaped, and the one that divides to give the two male gametes/sperm cells). Whichever way the count goes, every pollen grain always ends up delivering exactly two sperm cells to the embryo sac — the only question is whether that final division happens before shedding (3-celled pollen) or after, inside the growing pollen tube (2-celled pollen).

Megasporogenesis: building the female gametophyte

Inside the ovary, each ovule is a small structure attached to the placenta by a stalk (the funicle), enclosed by one or two protective integuments that leave a small opening — the micropyle — and surrounding a nutritive tissue, the nucellus. In most angiosperms the ovule is anatropous: it is inverted through 180° during development so the micropyle ends up lying right next to the funicle.

Within the nucellus, one cell differentiates as the megaspore mother cell (MMC) — large, dense cytoplasm, a large nucleus. It undergoes meiosis (megasporogenesis) to give four haploid megaspores, usually arranged in a row; in most angiosperms only one of the four survives (the chalazal-most one, typically), and the other three degenerate.

That single surviving functional megaspore now develops into the embryo sac (female gametophyte) without any further cell division walls forming — only nuclear divisions. Its nucleus divides mitotically to 2, then 4, then 8 nuclei, all free within one cell; cell walls then form around six of them, leaving the seventh cell (the central cell) with two nuclei. This is the type most angiosperms use, called the Polygonum type of embryo sac, and it is why a mature embryo sac is described as 7-celled but 8-nucleate:

Micropylar end — egg apparatus1 egg cell + 2 synergids3

NEET trap

The two synergids flanking the egg are not decorative — one of them has a specialised thickened wall structure at its micropylar tip, the filiform apparatus, which guides the pollen tube in and is the actual site where the tube bursts to release its two sperm cells. "Egg cell" and "egg apparatus" are not the same thing on a diagram-labelling question: the apparatus is egg + 2 synergids, three cells total.

Pollination: getting pollen from anther to stigma

Pollination is simply the transfer of pollen grains from an anther to a stigma. What varies is whose anther and whose stigma — and that single variable is the whole story of self- versus cross-pollination.

TypePollen moves from…toGenetic consequence
AutogamyAnther → stigma of the same flowerSelf-pollination; offspring genetically like the parent
GeitonogamyAnther → stigma of another flower, same plantGenetically self-pollination (same genotype), though it needs a pollinator like cross-pollination does
XenogamyAnther → stigma of a flower on a different plantTrue cross-pollination; the only type that introduces genetically different pollen
Core concept

Self-pollination is cheap and reliable but, exactly like asexual reproduction in Chapter 19, produces offspring with little new variation and can concentrate harmful recessive alleles (inbreeding depression). So many bisexual flowers evolved outbreeding devices that force or favour cross-pollination even though both sexes sit in the same flower: dichogamy (anther and stigma mature at different times), herkogamy (anther and stigma are physically separated or positioned so self-pollen can't reach the stigma), and genetic self-incompatibility (a stigma biochemically recognises and rejects its own pollen, as in many species of Brassicaceae and Solanaceae) — a plant-specific parallel to the genetic-variation argument Chapter 19 built for sexual reproduction generally.

Pollen needs an agent to travel. Abiotic agents are wind (anemophily — grasses, maize: pollen is light, non-sticky, and produced in huge excess since so much is wasted) and water (hydrophily — rare, e.g. Vallisneria, Zostera, where pollen itself may be released and float to the stigma). Biotic agents — insects, birds, bats — pollinate the large majority of flowering plants; these flowers invest in colour, scent, nectar, and pollen as a reward, since the visit isn't guaranteed the way wind exposure is.

NEET trap

Anemophilous flowers are a classic "opposite of showy" trap: small, dull-coloured, no nectar, often unisexual, with exposed feathery stigmas and enormous pollen output (to compensate for how much is lost to the wind) — the opposite design logic from an insect-pollinated flower.

Pollen–pistil interaction: the stigma checks the pollen before letting it in

Landing on a stigma isn't automatic entry. The stigma surface recognises whether a pollen grain is of the correct species and genetically compatible — the pollen–pistil interaction — before allowing it to germinate. Once accepted, the vegetative cell absorbs water and nutrients from the stigma and grows out as a pollen tube, which carries the (by-now two) sperm cells at its tip as it grows down through the style toward the ovary.

The pollen tube typically reaches the ovule through the micropyle (porogamy, the common route), enters the embryo sac by penetrating the specialised synergid at the filiform apparatus, and there releases its two non-motile sperm cells into the synergid's cytoplasm — setting up everything needed for double fertilization.

Double fertilization: angiosperms fertilize twice at once

This is the single event that most sharply distinguishes flowering plants from every other group of organisms in this book. Both sperm cells delivered by the pollen tube are used, and each fuses with a different target inside the embryo sac, simultaneously:

FusionParticipantsProductPloidyBecomes
SyngamySperm cell 1 + egg cellZygote2n (diploid)Embryo
Triple fusionSperm cell 2 + 2 polar nucleiPrimary endosperm nucleus (PEN)3n (triploid)Endosperm
Core concept

Why bother building a nutritive tissue (endosperm) only after confirming fertilization actually happened, instead of stocking every ovule with food in advance? Because pre-provisioning every ovule regardless of outcome would waste enormous resources on ovules that never get fertilized. Triple fusion ties endosperm production directly to a successful fertilization event, so the plant only pays the metabolic cost of nursing a seed once there's an embryo worth nursing — the same "don't invest until it's confirmed" logic you'll see again when Chapter 21 covers implantation.

NEET trap

Both fusions use one sperm cell each — it is not "one sperm fertilizes the egg, and a separate leftover cell fertilizes the endosperm on its own": triple fusion itself directly involves the second sperm cell plus both polar nuclei, three haploid nuclei fusing together in one step. And ploidy is the giveaway on a "identify the tissue" question: 2n = embryo lineage, 3n = endosperm lineage, nothing else in a seed is triploid.

Post-fertilization: from a fertilized ovule to a seed inside a fruit

Fertilization triggers a coordinated set of transformations, each renamed as it changes role:

Before fertilizationAfter fertilization becomes…
ZygoteEmbryo (grows by repeated mitosis into radicle, plumule, cotyledon(s))
Primary endosperm nucleusEndosperm (a nutritive tissue; in many dicots it is consumed by the growing embryo before the seed matures, so the mature seed appears non-endospermous, e.g. gram, pea)
IntegumentsSeed coat (testa + tegmen)
OvuleSeed
OvaryFruit (ovary wall → pericarp)

The endosperm typically develops ahead of the embryo — it needs to be in place as food before there's an embryo to feed — usually by free-nuclear division first and cellularisation later, exactly mirroring how the embryo sac itself formed nuclei before cell walls.

NEET trap

A fruit that develops without fertilization at all is a parthenocarpic fruit (e.g. seedless banana) — don't confuse this with apomixis below, which still involves seed formation, just without the normal sexual fusion steps.

Two special phenomena worth knowing by name

Polyembryony is the presence of more than one embryo in a single seed — it can arise from fertilization of more than one egg-like cell (e.g. a synergid also getting fertilized), or from nucellus/integument cells dividing to mimic embryos, and is common and commercially useful in citrus and some mango varieties (it gives genetically uniform "nucellar" seedlings). Apomixis is seed formation without the normal fertilization steps — a diploid cell of the ovule develops directly into an embryo — producing offspring genetically identical to the parent despite still going through the seed/fruit route; it is exploited in hybrid-seed programmes (e.g. some Asteraceae, grasses) because it lets a hybrid's exact genotype be propagated by seed, generation after generation, without it segregating apart the way sexual reproduction normally would.

Think first: hybrid seed companies want a hybrid's exact genotype to breed true from seed, generation after generation — but ordinary sexual reproduction guarantees the opposite: Chapter 19 built meiosis and random fertilization specifically to generate new combinations every generation. How does apomixis resolve this conflict?

Apomixis sidesteps the two steps that generate variation — meiosis (which shuffles alleles via independent assortment and crossing over) and syngamy (which combines two different parental genomes) — while still producing a seed the normal way a farmer plants and a plant disperses. Because the embryo forms directly from a diploid ovule cell without any meiotic reduction or gamete fusion, the offspring is a genetic clone of the parent, exactly like the asexual reproduction Chapter 19 opened with, just packaged inside a seed rather than a bud or a fragment. That is precisely why it's valuable commercially: a plant breeder can spend years combining two parents to get one exceptional hybrid genotype, and apomixis then lets that exact genotype be multiplied indefinitely by seed, instead of losing the combination to genetic reshuffling the moment the hybrid itself reproduces sexually.

Where this leaves you

Trace the whole chapter as one pipeline and every fact here becomes a waypoint rather than a separate thing to memorize: microsporogenesis and megasporogenesis build two haploid gametophytes inside one diploid flower → pollination physically bridges them → pollen–pistil recognition screens the match → a pollen tube delivers two sperm cells → double fertilization spends them both at once, on the egg and on the polar nuclei → and the whole ovule reorganizes into a seed inside a fruit. Chapter 21 now takes this exact same underlying logic — gametogenesis, delivery, fertilization, post-fertilization development — and rebuilds it for humans, where hormones replace pollinators as the coordinating signal and internal fertilization replaces a pollen tube.
