Chapter 04 · Part II — Information Flow & Genetics

The Genetic Code
DNA, RNA, and the Central Dogma

Chapter 3 closed on a promise: the story would turn from energy to information. Here it is. Every trait the rest of this book explains — a plant bending toward light, a red blood cell’s shape, a species drifting slowly into something new — ultimately traces back to one molecule storing an instruction, copying that instruction with almost no errors, and being read out to build a protein. Chapter 2 previewed the players (a nucleotide, a phosphodiester bond, DNA versus RNA) and promised this chapter would be “the whole rest of the story.” This is that chapter: how DNA holds information, how it’s copied without losing it, and how a cell converts stored text into working molecules through the two-step relay Francis Crick named the central dogma.

A molecule built to be copied: DNA structure

Chapter 2 already gave you the monomer: a nucleotide is a pentose sugar, a nitrogenous base, and a phosphate group, strung into a chain by phosphodiester bonds between one nucleotide’s sugar and the next one’s phosphate. In DNA, that sugar is deoxyribose, and the bases come in two chemical families: the two-ringed purines — adenine (A) and guanine (G) — and the single-ringed pyrimidines — cytosine (C) and thymine (T). What Chapter 2 didn’t tell you is what happens when you take two of these chains and put them together — and that structural decision is the reason every mechanism in this chapter works at all.

In 1953, James Watson and Francis Crick proposed a model — built on Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins’ X-ray diffraction data — that DNA is a double helix: two nucleotide chains wound around a common axis, sugar-phosphate backbones on the outside, bases pointing inward and pairing across the middle. Two rules govern how the strands fit together, and both are structural necessities, not arbitrary chemistry:

  • Complementary base pairing. A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine across the helix — specifically A with T (joined by 2 hydrogen bonds) and G with C (joined by 3 hydrogen bonds). This isn’t a rule imposed from outside; it’s the only pairing geometry that keeps the helix a uniform width all the way along its length, since a purine–purine pair would be too wide and a pyrimidine–pyrimidine pair too narrow.
  • Antiparallel orientation. The two strands run in opposite directions — one strand’s sugar-phosphate backbone reads 5′→3′ top to bottom, its partner reads 3′→5′ top to bottom. Every polymerizing enzyme you meet in this chapter (DNA polymerase, RNA polymerase) can only add nucleotides in the 5′→3′ direction, so this antiparallel arrangement is what forces replication and transcription into the specific, slightly asymmetric mechanics covered below.
Core concept

Complementary base pairing means each strand of the helix already specifies its partner. If you know one strand’s sequence, you can write out the other from memory — A always demands a T opposite it, G always demands a C. That single structural fact is the entire reason DNA can be copied at all: separate the strands, and each one is a ready-made template for rebuilding its partner. Everything in the “Replication” section below is really just this one idea, executed by enzymes.

NEET trap

Don’t confuse a nucleoside (sugar + base only, no phosphate — e.g. adenosine) with a nucleotide (sugar + base + one or more phosphates — e.g. adenosine monophosphate, or with three phosphates, ATP). And when a question asks which base pair is more thermally stable, the safe answer is always G–C — three hydrogen bonds beat two, every time, regardless of which organism’s DNA it is.

RNA: three working copies of the same idea

RNA is built the same way as DNA — nucleotides linked by phosphodiester bonds — with three deliberate differences: the sugar is ribose instead of deoxyribose (an extra –OH group at the 2′ carbon), the pyrimidine uracil (U) replaces thymine, and RNA is almost always single-stranded rather than a fixed double helix (though it frequently folds back on itself into local double-stranded loops — you’ll see exactly that in the tRNA structure below). If DNA is the permanent, vault-stored master copy, RNA is the working photocopy the cell actually uses — and it comes in three specialized forms, each doing a different job in getting a protein made:

NEET trap

Intuition says mRNA, the “famous” one, should dominate — it doesn’t. rRNA is the most abundant RNA in any cell, simply because every one of the thousands of ribosomes a cell runs needs several rRNA molecules built into its structure, while any single mRNA is used, translated, and degraded relatively quickly.

The central dogma: how the message flows

In 1958, Francis Crick proposed a simple rule for how biological information moves, later called the central dogma of molecular biology: information flows from DNA to RNA to protein, and, critically, never spontaneously back from protein to nucleic acid. DNA is copied into more DNA (replication), DNA is copied into RNA (transcription), and RNA is decoded into protein (translation). Notice the vocabulary is doing real work here: “transcription” is copying within the same language (nucleotide to nucleotide, like handwriting a document into another script), while “translation” genuinely changes language — a sequence of nucleotide triplets becomes a sequence of amino acids.

NEET trap

Reverse transcriptase, discovered independently by Howard Temin and David Baltimore in 1970 (shared Nobel Prize, 1975), synthesizes DNA from an RNA template — exactly the arrow retroviruses like HIV need to insert their RNA genome into a host cell’s DNA. A question phrasing this as “violates the central dogma” is testing whether you know the dogma’s actual, narrower claim: information never flows from protein back into a nucleic acid. RNA→DNA was always a permitted direction in Crick’s own formulation — it’s just rare enough outside retroviruses that early textbooks glossed over it.

Copying the archive: semiconservative replication

Once you accept that each DNA strand specifies its partner, three models of replication become logically possible: conservative (the original double helix stays fully intact, an entirely new copy is built alongside it), dispersive (both daughter molecules are patchworks of old and new DNA), and semiconservative (each daughter molecule keeps exactly one original strand and gains one newly synthesized partner). Watson and Crick’s own 1953 paper already guessed semiconservative was correct — but it took Matthew Meselson and Franklin Stahl’s 1958 experiment to prove it.

Meselson and Stahl grew E. coli in a medium containing only the heavy nitrogen isotope 15N, so every bacterium’s DNA became uniformly heavy. They then switched the bacteria to normal, light 14N medium and let them divide, extracting DNA after each generation and spinning it in a cesium chloride density gradient (DNA settles at a height in the gradient set by its density). After one generation, all the DNA formed a single band at exactly the density expected of a 15N/14N hybrid — ruling out the conservative model, which would have produced two separate bands (pure heavy and pure light). After two generations, DNA split into two bands, one hybrid and one pure light, in a 1:1 ratio — exactly what semiconservative replication predicts, and incompatible with the dispersive model’s single, progressively-lighter smear.

Core concept

Semiconservative replication means every daughter DNA molecule is half old, half new — one parental strand serving as a direct template, one freshly synthesized strand built by matching a new nucleotide to each base on that template. This is why replication is inherently high-fidelity: the new strand isn’t guessed at, it’s read off an existing, chemically verified strand, base by complementary base.

Executing that idea takes a small assembly line of enzymes, opening the helix at a specific site called the origin of replication and working outward in both directions (a bidirectional replication fork):

  • Helicase unwinds and separates the two parental strands ahead of the fork, breaking the hydrogen bonds between base pairs.
  • Topoisomerase (DNA gyrase in bacteria) relieves the over-winding stress that builds up in the helix further ahead of the unwinding fork.
  • Single-strand binding proteins (SSBs) coat the separated strands to stop them re-annealing or being degraded before they’re copied.
  • Primase lays down a short RNA primer, because DNA polymerase cannot start a new strand from nothing — it can only extend an existing 3′ end.
  • DNA polymerase extends each primer by adding nucleotides exclusively in the 5′→3′ direction, selecting each one by testing it against the template strand for a correct complementary pair.
  • DNA ligase seals the remaining gaps — most importantly, joining the lagging strand’s fragments (below) into one continuous strand.

Because both template strands are antiparallel but DNA polymerase only ever moves 5′→3′, the two new strands can’t be built the same way. One template strand lets its new partner (the leading strand) be synthesized continuously, running straight toward the fork as it opens. The other template strand runs the wrong way for continuous synthesis, so its new partner (the lagging strand) is built backward, away from the fork, in short discontinuous pieces called Okazaki fragments — each one needing its own RNA primer, later removed and filled in with DNA, then stitched to its neighbor by ligase.

NEET trap

Two facts examiners love to test together: DNA polymerase can only extend an existing 3′–OH end, never start a brand-new strand — which is precisely why every fragment, leading or lagging, needs an RNA primer first; and DNA polymerase only ever synthesizes 5′→3′, which is the single fact that explains why the lagging strand has to be discontinuous at all. If a question asks “why are Okazaki fragments necessary,” the answer is this directionality constraint, not anything about the enzyme being “slower” on one strand.

Reading the template: transcription

Transcription copies one gene’s worth of information from DNA into RNA. Only one of the two DNA strands is actually read for any given gene — called the template strand (read 3′→5′ by the enzyme) — while the other, the coding strand, is never touched but happens to carry the same sequence as the resulting RNA (with U standing in for T). The enzyme doing the reading, RNA polymerase, binds a specific DNA sequence called a promoter to begin, synthesizes RNA 5′→3′ through initiation, elongation, and termination, and stops at a terminator sequence — in bacteria, sometimes read directly by the polymerase (rho-independent) and sometimes requiring a helper protein called the rho (ρ) factor to pull the RNA transcript loose (rho-dependent).

RNA polymerase(s)One, transcribes everythingThree specialists — Pol I (rRNA), Pol II (hnRNA/mRNA), Pol III (tRNA, 5S rRNA)

A eukaryotic gene’s primary transcript, made by Pol II, is called hnRNA (heterogeneous nuclear RNA) — and it isn’t yet functional mRNA. Three processing steps convert it:

  • Capping — a modified guanine nucleotide (7-methylguanosine) is added to the 5′ end, protecting the transcript and later helping the ribosome find the start codon.
  • Tailing — roughly 200 adenine nucleotides are added to the 3′ end (the poly-A tail), improving the transcript’s stability and export from the nucleus.
  • Splicing — eukaryotic genes are typically split into coding segments (exons) interrupted by non-coding segments (introns); a large RNA–protein machine called the spliceosome cuts the introns out and joins the exons into one continuous coding sequence.
NEET trap

“Capping, tailing, and splicing occur in prokaryotes” is a classic false statement to spot — all three are eukaryotic-only, precisely because prokaryotic genes generally have no introns to remove and no nucleus to protect the transcript from before it reaches ribosomes that are, in fact, already translating it. Also watch for reversed matching: Pol I → rRNA, Pol II → mRNA/hnRNA, Pol III → tRNA is the one worth memorizing cold, since questions routinely swap two of the three.

The genetic code: the rules of translation

mRNA is read three nucleotides at a time — each triplet a codon — and each codon specifies exactly one amino acid (or a stop). With 4 possible bases and 3 positions per codon, there are 43 = 64 possible codons: 61 sense codons, each coding for one of the 20 standard amino acids, and 3 stop (nonsense) codons — UAA, UAG, and UGA — that code for no amino acid at all and simply end translation. One sense codon does double duty: AUG is both the codon for methionine and, positioned at the right spot, the universal start codon that marks where translation begins.

The code itself was cracked experimentally, not guessed from theory. In 1961, Marshall Nirenberg and Heinrich Matthaei synthesized an artificial mRNA made of nothing but uracil (poly-U) and added it to a cell-free translation system; the only protein produced was a chain of a single amino acid, phenylalanine — proving UUU codes for phenylalanine, the first codon ever deciphered. Har Gobind Khorana then developed methods to chemically synthesize RNAs of known, repeating sequence, systematically filling in the rest of the table. By 1966 the full code was solved; Nirenberg, Khorana, and Robert Holley (who worked out tRNA’s structure) shared the 1968 Nobel Prize for it.

  • Triplet. Every codon is exactly three bases — the minimum length that can distinguish 20 amino acids (42 = 16 would fall short; 43 = 64 comfortably covers them, with room to spare for redundancy and stops).
  • Degenerate (redundant). Most amino acids are specified by more than one codon — e.g. leucine has six. Only methionine (AUG) and tryptophan (UGG) have a single codon each.
  • Unambiguous. Degeneracy runs only one way — a single codon never codes for more than one amino acid, even though one amino acid can have several codons.
  • Non-overlapping and comma-less. The reading frame is fixed once translation starts: each base belongs to exactly one codon, and codons are read back to back with no punctuation between them.
  • (Near-)universal. The same codon specifies the same amino acid across nearly all known organisms, from bacteria to humans — strong evidence for a shared evolutionary origin. The well-known exceptions live in mitochondrial genomes, where, for example, UGA can code for tryptophan instead of acting as a stop signal.
Core concept

Keep degenerate and ambiguous firmly apart — NEET tests the difference directly. Degenerate means “one amino acid, several possible codons” (redundancy in the amino-acid-to-codon direction). Unambiguous means “one codon, exactly one amino acid” (no uncertainty in the codon-to-amino-acid direction). The code is degenerate and unambiguous at the same time — those two properties describe the mapping read in opposite directions, so they don’t contradict each other.

Degeneracy isn’t random slack in the system — it has a mechanical explanation. Francis Crick’s 1966 wobble hypothesis observed that a tRNA’s anticodon pairs strictly with a codon’s first two bases, but pairing at the third (“wobble”) position is looser, tolerating some non-standard base pairs. That flexibility lets a single tRNA species recognize more than one codon differing only in that third position, which is why a cell can get by with roughly 30–32 distinct tRNAs instead of needing a separate one for all 61 sense codons.

Translation: building the protein

Translation happens on the ribosome — 70S (30S + 50S subunits) in prokaryotes, 80S (40S + 60S) in eukaryotes — built from rRNA and protein, with three tRNA-binding sites: A (aminoacyl), P (peptidyl), and E (exit). Before any of that can run, though, each tRNA has to be “charged” with the correct amino acid, and that step is where translation’s accuracy is actually enforced.

Core concept

The enzyme aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase attaches each amino acid to its correct tRNA, spending ATP to do it — and this, not the ribosome, is where translation’s fidelity is actually guaranteed. The ribosome only ever checks whether a codon and an anticodon match; it has no way to check whether the amino acid riding on that tRNA is the right one. If a synthetase mischarges a tRNA with the wrong amino acid, the ribosome will faithfully insert that wrong amino acid anyway, because as far as the ribosome can tell, the codon-anticodon pairing looks perfectly correct.

With charged tRNAs available, translation runs in three stages:

  • Initiation. The small ribosomal subunit binds mRNA at the start codon (AUG); an initiator tRNA, carrying methionine (formylmethionine, fMet, in bacteria) sits in the P site; the large subunit then joins to complete the ribosome.
  • Elongation. A charged tRNA enters the empty A site, its anticodon matching the next codon. The rRNA of the large subunit — acting as a ribozyme, an RNA with catalytic activity, via its peptidyl transferase function — forms a peptide bond between the growing chain (on the P-site tRNA) and the new amino acid (on the A-site tRNA). The ribosome then translocates one codon forward: the growing peptidyl-tRNA shifts A→P, the now-empty tRNA shifts P→E and is released, and the cycle repeats — the polypeptide grows from its N-terminus toward its C-terminus while the ribosome reads mRNA 5′→3′.
  • Termination. When a stop codon reaches the A site, no tRNA matches it; a release factor binds instead, triggering release of the finished polypeptide and dissociation of the ribosome’s subunits, ready to be recycled onto another mRNA.
NEET trap

In bacteria, the initiator tRNA carries N-formylmethionine (fMet), not plain methionine — a detail examiners like to test by asking what distinguishes prokaryotic initiation from eukaryotic initiation (which starts with plain Met). Also remember peptidyl transferase is ribosomal RNA acting as a catalyst, not a protein enzyme — a favorite one-line fact, and a hint (revisited in the Evolution chapter) toward the RNA-world idea that RNA, not protein, may have been life’s original catalyst.

When the message changes: mutations

Every mechanism above assumes the message being copied and read is correct — but occasionally it isn’t. A mutation is simply a change to the DNA sequence itself, and its consequences depend entirely on what kind of change it is, because the genetic code's structure (triplet, non-overlapping, comma-less) makes some changes far more damaging than others.

A point mutation substitutes one base for another, altering at most a single codon. The textbook NEET example is sickle-cell anaemia: a single DNA base change alters the sixth codon of the β-globin gene from one coding for glutamic acid to one coding for valine (GAG → GUG at the mRNA level). One amino acid, out of the roughly 146 in that protein chain, changes — yet that single substitution is enough to make hemoglobin molecules polymerize into rigid fibers under low oxygen, distorting red blood cells into the characteristic sickle shape. A point substitution can also be silent (thanks to the code’s degeneracy, the new codon still specifies the same amino acid) or nonsense (the new codon becomes a premature stop codon, truncating the protein).

An insertion or deletion of bases behaves very differently. Because the ribosome reads the message in fixed, non-overlapping triplets with no punctuation to resynchronize on, adding or removing bases in any number not a multiple of three shifts every single codon downstream of that point — a frameshift mutation. The reading frame doesn’t just wobble briefly; it stays shifted for the rest of the message, typically scrambling every subsequent amino acid and often running straight into an accidental premature stop codon.

Think first: why is inserting a single base almost always more damaging than substituting a single base?

A substitution changes exactly one codon — the rest of the message is read in the same frame as before, untouched. Worst case, that one codon now specifies the wrong amino acid (missense) or an early stop (nonsense); best case, degeneracy absorbs it entirely (silent). An insertion of one base, by contrast, doesn’t just change one codon — it shifts the boundary between every codon from that point onward, because the ribosome has no way to know a base was added and keeps grouping bases into triplets exactly as before, just one base later than it should. Every downstream amino acid is now, in effect, randomized, and the protein almost always ends up either nonfunctional or truncated by a stop codon that appears by accident somewhere in the scrambled sequence. This is precisely why the code being “comma-less” and read in a fixed frame, which makes translation mechanically simple, also makes it fragile to exactly this one class of error.

Where this leaves you

One structural fact — complementary base pairing on an antiparallel double helix — explains almost everything in this chapter at once: why DNA can be copied semiconservatively, why replication needs a leading and a lagging strand, why a codon can be read off unambiguously, and why a single wrong or missing base can ripple into a completely different protein. Hold onto the central dogma’s shape — DNA copied, DNA transcribed to RNA, RNA translated to protein — as the skeleton the next two chapters hang new material on: Chapter 5 asks how these DNA sequences (as genes and alleles) actually get passed from parent to offspring, and Chapter 6 opens the question this chapter deliberately left closed — not every gene is transcribed all the time, and the machinery that decides which genes switch on, using the lac operon as its worked example, is next.
