Chapter 05 · Part II — Information Flow & Genetics

Inheritance
How Traits Pass Across Generations

Chapter 4 closed by asking how a sequence of DNA — a gene — actually gets from one generation to the next, and stayed carefully quiet about the answer. This chapter answers it, but notice the order events actually happened in: an Augustinian friar in Brno worked out the rules of inheritance in the 1860s by counting pea plants, decades before anyone knew DNA existed, let alone that a gene was a stretch of it. Gregor Mendel discovered that heredity obeys precise, countable ratios — and only much later did chromosomes, and then DNA, turn out to be the physical objects making those ratios true. This chapter follows that same order: the rules first, then the physical machinery (chromosomes, meiosis, linkage) that explains why the rules hold.

Why a pea plant, and why it worked

Mendel didn't stumble into clean 3:1 ratios by luck — he engineered the conditions that would let a ratio be visible at all, which is precisely why his experiment succeeded where earlier, vaguer attempts at studying heredity hadn't. He chose the garden pea (Pisum sativum) for reasons that still matter to how you should think about any genetics cross: it has a short generation time, produces large numbers of offspring per cross, and — critically — several of its traits come in only two sharply distinct, easily scored forms with no confusing intermediate states. He also had a decisive technical advantage: pea flowers self-pollinate by default but can be easily cross-pollinated by hand, so Mendel could first establish true-breeding lines (self-pollinated for generations until every offspring matched the parent exactly) and then perform controlled crosses between them.

He studied seven such traits, each with one dominant and one recessive form: seed shape (round/wrinkled), seed color (yellow/green), flower color (violet/white), pod shape (inflated/constricted), pod color (green/yellow), flower position (axial/terminal), and stem height (tall/dwarf). Restricting himself to sharply either/or traits — rather than continuously varying ones like human height — is what made the underlying ratios countable instead of a blur.

Core concept

Fix the vocabulary before the math, because every cross problem hinges on keeping these separate: a gene is a heritable unit of information (Mendel called it a “factor”) that occupies a specific location, or locus, on a chromosome. An allele is one particular version of that gene — the round-seed gene has a round allele and a wrinkled allele. An organism's genotype is which alleles it carries (e.g. Tt); its phenotype is what you actually observe (tall). Homozygous means both alleles at a locus are identical (TT or tt); heterozygous means they differ (Tt).

One trait at a time: the monohybrid cross

Mendel crossed a true-breeding tall plant (TT) with a true-breeding dwarf plant (tt). Every single F1 offspring was tall — the dwarf trait had seemingly vanished. But when he let the F1 plants self-pollinate, dwarf reappeared in the F2 generation, in a strikingly consistent proportion: out of 1064 F2 plants, 787 were tall and 277 were dwarf — a ratio of 2.84:1, close enough to a clean 3:1 that Mendel recognized it as the signature of an underlying rule rather than noise.

Two rules explain this pattern completely. The law of dominance: when two different alleles are both present (heterozygous), only one — the dominant allele (T, tall) — is expressed in the phenotype, while the other — the recessive allele (t, dwarf) — is present but silent, masked rather than destroyed. The law of segregation: the two alleles an organism carries for a gene separate (segregate) from each other during gamete formation, so each gamete carries only one allele, chosen at random. That's why the recessive allele didn't disappear in F1 — every F1 plant was still genotypically Tt, carrying one copy of each allele, just masked by dominance — and why it reappeared once F1 plants self-crossed and some offspring by chance received t from both parents.

Notice the F2 generation actually carries two ratios at once, and NEET regularly tests whether you can tell them apart: the phenotypic ratio is 3 tall : 1 dwarf, but the genotypic ratio underneath it is 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt — two-thirds of the tall-looking plants are secretly heterozygous carriers, indistinguishable from the true-breeding TT tall plants just by looking at them.

NEET trap

“Dominant” does not mean “more common in the population,” “normal,” or “stronger” — it means only that one copy of that allele is enough to determine the phenotype in a heterozygote. Achondroplasia and Huntington's disease are both caused by dominant alleles that are rare in human populations; the recessive allele for a trait can easily be the more common one. Also don't confuse segregation (one gene's two alleles separating) with independent assortment (different genes' alleles distributing independently) — they're two different laws, tested separately below.

The physical basis of segregation isn't a rule imposed on cells from outside — it's a direct consequence of meiosis. An organism's two alleles for a gene sit on homologous chromosomes (one inherited from each parent), and meiosis I is precisely the division that separates homologous chromosomes into different daughter cells. Segregation of alleles is the segregation of homologous chromosomes, just described at the gene level instead of the chromosome level — a connection this chapter returns to explicitly once chromosomal theory is on the table.

Two traits at once: the dihybrid cross

A single trait's 3:1 ratio only tells you how one gene behaves. To ask whether two genes behave independently of each other, Mendel crossed a true-breeding round-yellow plant (RRYY) with a true-breeding wrinkled-green plant (rryy). Every F1 plant was round and yellow (RrYy) — dominance again — but self-pollinating the F1 produced four phenotypic classes in F2, including two combinations — round-green and wrinkled-yellow — that neither original parent had ever shown. Out of 556 F2 seeds: 315 round-yellow, 108 round-green, 101 wrinkled-yellow, 32 wrinkled-green — a ratio of roughly 9:3:3:1.

Sort those 16 boxes by phenotype and the 9:3:3:1 falls straight out: 9 round-yellow (any R_Y_), 3 round-green (R_yy), 3 wrinkled-yellow (rrY_), 1 wrinkled-green (rryy). The law of independent assortment is exactly this observation stated as a rule: the allele pair for one gene segregates into gametes independently of how the allele pair for a different gene segregates, so a gamete's chance of getting R versus r has no bearing at all on its chance of getting Y versus y. That independence is also why the 9:3:3:1 ratio is really just the product of two separate 3:1 ratios: (3 round : 1 wrinkled) × (3 yellow : 1 green) multiplies out to 9:3:3:1, because the two traits genuinely don't influence each other's inheritance.

Core concept

Independent assortment holds only when the two genes sit on different chromosome pairs (or are very far apart on the same long chromosome) — because it's the random orientation of different homologous pairs at metaphase I that produces the independence. Genes sitting close together on the same chromosome don't get this random-orientation freedom; they tend to travel together into the same gamete. That single exception is exactly what the Linkage section below is about, and it's a favorite place for NEET to test whether a deviation from 9:3:3:1 was noticed and correctly explained.

A tool for hidden genotypes: the test cross

Look back at the monohybrid table: a tall plant could be TT or Tt — dominance hides the difference. Mendel's way around this, still the standard tool today, is the test cross: cross the individual of unknown genotype with a homozygous recessive individual (tt). A homozygous recessive parent can only ever contribute a recessive allele, so it can't mask anything — whatever appears in the offspring directly reveals what the unknown parent was carrying. If the unknown parent is TT, every offspring is Tt (all tall, no dwarf offspring at all); if it's Tt, offspring split 1 tall (Tt) : 1 dwarf (tt).

NEET trap

Keep test cross (unknown × homozygous recessive, done specifically to determine an unknown genotype) distinct from a back cross (an F1 individual crossed with either of its own parents, for any purpose — test crosses are one particular kind of back cross, not a synonym for it). A dihybrid test cross (RrYy × rryy) is also the cleanest way to detect linkage: independent assortment predicts a 1:1:1:1 offspring ratio, so any skew away from that in real data is itself evidence the two genes aren't assorting independently.

When dominance isn't complete or exclusive

All seven of Mendel's pea traits happened to show clean, complete dominance — convenient for discovering the underlying laws, but not the only way two alleles can interact. Two other patterns matter for NEET, and the fastest way to tell them apart is to look at the F1 heterozygote's phenotype.

Core concept

The question that separates incomplete dominance from co-dominance is simple: does the heterozygote look like a blend (pink, neither fully red nor fully white — incomplete dominance), or does it show both original phenotypes at once, each fully expressed (a red cell simultaneously carrying both the A and the B antigen, not some averaged antigen — co-dominance)? In both patterns, notice dominance has stopped hiding information: the heterozygote's phenotype is now diagnostic of its genotype, unlike complete dominance where Tt and TT look the same.

ABO blood groups also illustrate a third idea, multiple alleles: a gene can have more than two alleles circulating in a population, even though any one diploid individual still carries only two. The ABO locus has three alleles — IA, IB, and i — with IA and IB co-dominant with each other and both fully dominant over i. That gives four phenotypes (A, B, AB, O) from six possible genotypes: IAIA or IAi (type A), IBIB or IBi (type B), IAIB (type AB), and ii (type O).

NEET trap

A frequent wrong-option setup: “type O blood shows codominance because it's a common blood type.” Type O is simply homozygous recessive (ii) — there's no codominance involved at all, since only one allele type is present. Codominance in the ABO system applies specifically and only to the IAIB genotype.

Giving the rules a physical address: the chromosomal theory

Mendel's factors were a purely abstract bookkeeping device in 1866 — he had no idea what they were made of or where they lived, and his work went largely unnoticed until three botanists (Hugo de Vries, Carl Correns, and Erich von Tschermak) independently rediscovered the same laws around 1900. Once chromosomes had been observed under the microscope, Walter Sutton (working with grasshoppers) and Theodor Boveri (working with sea urchins) independently noticed, around 1902, that chromosomes behave exactly the way Mendel's factors were required to behave: they exist in pairs, one member inherited from each parent; they segregate during meiosis so each gamete gets one member of each pair; and different pairs assort independently of each other. Sutton and Boveri proposed the chromosomal theory of inheritance: genes are physically located on chromosomes, and chromosome behavior during meiosis is the mechanical explanation for Mendel's two laws.

The theory was elegant, but still indirect evidence — a parallel, not a proof. Thomas Hunt Morgan supplied the proof, working with the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster (short generation time, easily bred by the thousands, giant salivary-gland chromosomes visible under a microscope). In 1910, Morgan found a single male fly with white eyes in an otherwise red-eyed population, and by crossing it through several generations, showed that the white-eye trait was inherited exactly along with the fly's sex — specifically, that the gene behaved as though it were physically located on the X chromosome. This was the first gene ever assigned to a specific chromosome, and it converted the chromosomal theory from a compelling analogy into a demonstrated physical fact.

Core concept

This is the moment the story from Chapter 4 and the story from this chapter fuse into one: a gene is a stretch of DNA (Chapter 4), sitting at a specific locus on a specific chromosome (this chapter), and it is the chromosome's mechanical behavior during meiosis — homologous pairs separating, different pairs orienting independently — that produces the segregation and independent-assortment ratios Mendel counted in pea plants forty years before anyone knew chromosomes existed.

The exception that proves the rule: linkage and recombination

Independent assortment assumes each gene's chromosome orients at metaphase I with no reference to any other gene's chromosome — true when two genes sit on different chromosome pairs, but obviously not guaranteed when two genes sit on the same chromosome, since they're then physically tethered together and travel to the same pole by default. William Bateson and Reginald Punnett noticed a deviation from the expected 9:3:3:1 ratio in sweet peas as early as 1905, but it was Morgan's Drosophila work that supplied the mechanism: genes located close together on the same chromosome are inherited together far more often than chance would predict, a phenomenon he named linkage.

Linkage isn't absolute, though, because of crossing over — the exchange of segments between homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis (introduced structurally in earlier biology coursework as chiasma formation). Crossing over occasionally separates two genes that started out on the same chromosome, producing recombinant gamete types alongside the more common parental combinations. The key insight, worked out by Morgan's student Alfred Sturtevant in 1913: genes sitting closer together on a chromosome are separated by crossing over less often, simply because there's less physical distance between them for a crossover event to land in. That gives a direct, measurable relationship between recombination frequency and physical distance:

  • Tightly linked genes (very close together) show a low recombination frequency — parental combinations dominate offspring, and the observed ratio deviates sharply from 9:3:3:1 or 1:1:1:1.
  • Loosely linked genes (farther apart, but still on the same chromosome) show a higher recombination frequency, approaching 50% as distance increases — at which point they behave almost exactly like genes on separate chromosomes, even though they technically aren't.
  • Sturtevant used recombination frequency to build the first genetic map: 1% recombination frequency defines one map unit, later renamed the centimorgan (cM) in Morgan's honor — letting genes be ordered and spaced along a chromosome purely from cross data, with no way yet to see the chromosome directly.
NEET trap

A dihybrid test cross ratio that comes out skewed — parental-type offspring far outnumbering recombinant-type offspring, instead of a clean 1:1:1:1 — is the standard NEET signal for linkage, not for a "mistake" in the cross. Genes are called linked if they're on the same chromosome at all (recombination frequency < 50%) but syntenic and effectively unlinked once they're far enough apart that crossing over separates them roughly half the time — recombination frequency approaching, but never quite reaching, 50% (true independent assortment, from different chromosomes, is the only way to get an actual, exact 50%).

Reading heredity in humans: pedigree analysis

You can't set up controlled crosses in humans the way Mendel did with peas, so human genetics relies on a different tool: the pedigree, a standardized family tree that records who is and isn't affected by a trait across generations, using a fixed set of symbols so any geneticist can read it the same way.

Reading a pedigree is really just running Mendel's laws in reverse — instead of predicting offspring from known parental genotypes, you infer the unknown genotypes (and the underlying mode of inheritance) from the observed pattern of who is affected:

  • Autosomal recessive: can skip generations (two unaffected carrier parents produce an affected child); affects males and females roughly equally; two affected parents always produce only affected children.
  • Autosomal dominant: typically appears in every generation (an affected individual usually has at least one affected parent); affects males and females roughly equally; two unaffected parents essentially never produce an affected child.
  • X-linked recessive: affects males far more often than females (a male needs only one copy, on his single X, to be affected; a female needs two); can skip a generation through unaffected carrier mothers; never passes father-to-son (a father gives his son a Y, not an X).
Think first: in a pedigree, two unaffected parents have an affected daughter. Can this trait be X-linked recessive?

No — and working out why is the whole exercise. For a daughter to be affected by an X-linked recessive trait, she needs the recessive allele on both of her X chromosomes: one from her mother, and one from her father (a daughter always receives her father's one and only X chromosome). But a male has only that single X, with no second allele to mask it — so if her father is carrying the recessive allele on his X at all, he must already be affected by it himself, not just a silent carrier. The scenario states both parents are unaffected, which means the father cannot be supplying that recessive allele — ruling out X-linked recessive immediately for an affected daughter. The pattern is perfectly consistent with autosomal recessive inheritance instead: both unaffected parents are simply heterozygous carriers, and the daughter is homozygous recessive. This is exactly the kind of one-line elimination NEET pedigree questions are built around — know the sex-specific requirement for each inheritance mode, and half the answer choices eliminate themselves immediately.

Where this leaves you

Everything in this chapter reduces to one physical fact and its consequences: genes sit at specific loci on chromosomes, and meiosis separates homologous chromosomes (segregation) while orienting different chromosome pairs independently (independent assortment) — which is why Mendel could discover both laws from pea-counting alone, decades before anyone had seen a chromosome do either thing. Everything else here is a refinement of that one picture: dominance can be complete, incomplete, or co-dominant depending on how two alleles' products interact; genes on the same chromosome bend independent assortment through linkage, recoverable only through crossing over and measurable as recombination frequency; and pedigrees let you run the whole apparatus in reverse on real human families, where controlled crosses aren't an option. Two threads lead out from here. Chapter 6 asks a question this chapter deliberately set aside: having inherited a genotype, how does a cell decide which of its genes to actually switch on, and how much — using the same blood-group and hemoglobin examples this chapter touched only as genotype bookkeeping. Chapter 7 asks the population-level version of the same question: if inheritance is this precise from parent to individual offspring, how do the allele frequencies of an entire population drift and change across generations, and what does that have to do with new species arising at all?
