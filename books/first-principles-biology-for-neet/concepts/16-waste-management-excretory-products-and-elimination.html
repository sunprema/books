Chapter 16 · Part IV — Human Structure & Function

Waste Management
Excretory Products and Elimination

Chapter 15 finished with blood dutifully returning from every tissue carrying CO2 and metabolic waste in tow — but a circulatory system that only collects waste is half a solution. Something has to actually remove it, and remove it selectively: throw away the nitrogenous poison, keep the water, keep the ions, keep the glucose, keep everything the body spent effort building. That something is the kidney, and it does this one job through three deceptively simple operations repeated across roughly a million microscopic filtering units per kidney — filter everything, take back what’s valuable, dump what isn’t. This chapter is that filter, and the hormonal dials that fine-tune exactly how much water and salt it lets go.

Two ideas that carry the whole chapter

The first idea is that excretion is fundamentally a nitrogen-disposal problem: breaking down proteins and nucleic acids for energy or turnover releases amino groups, and free ammonia is toxic enough that it can’t simply accumulate — every organism has to convert it into some exportable form, and which form it chooses is a direct trade-off between toxicity and the water/energy cost of detoxifying it. The second idea is that the kidney does far more than remove waste — it is the body’s primary osmoregulator, continuously adjusting exactly how much water and how many ions leave with the urine so that blood volume, blood pressure, and blood osmolarity all stay inside a narrow tolerable range. Every structure and hormone in this chapter serves one of these two jobs, and often both at once.

Core concept

The nephron does exactly three things to make urine, in this order: filter blood plasma wholesale at the glomerulus, reabsorb back almost everything valuable as the filtrate travels the tubule, and secrete a few extra unwanted ions directly into the tubule to fine-tune the final composition. Urine is what’s left after all three steps — not what was filtered, and not what was excreted; it’s filtration minus reabsorption plus secretion.

Nitrogenous waste: pick your poison, or pay to detoxify it

Protein and nucleic acid catabolism releases nitrogen as ammonia (NH3) — highly toxic, but also highly water-soluble and cheap to make, requiring no extra metabolic energy to produce. Organisms that excrete nitrogen directly as ammonia are ammonotelic (most bony fishes, aquatic amphibians, aquatic invertebrates): viable only because unlimited surrounding water constantly dilutes and flushes the toxin away as fast as it’s made.

Terrestrial animals don’t have that luxury of unlimited water, so most convert ammonia into urea — far less toxic, so it can be safely concentrated in blood and stored before excretion, at the metabolic cost of energy spent in the liver's ornithine cycle to make it. Humans and most other mammals, along with terrestrial amphibians and marine cartilaginous fishes (sharks), are ureotelic. A third strategy pushes the trade-off further: birds, reptiles, and insects excrete nitrogen as semi-solid uric acid — the least toxic of the three, and because it’s poorly soluble it can be excreted as a paste with almost no water loss at all, the most expensive option in energy but by far the cheapest in water. These organisms are uricotelic.

NEET trap

Nitrogenous strategy can switch across a life stage — the classic example is a tadpole, which is aquatic and ammonotelic, metamorphosing into a frog, which is terrestrial and ureotelic. Don’t assume one organism is locked to one strategy for life; the strategy tracks the environment (water availability), not the species identity alone.

The human excretory system: two kidneys and their plumbing

The human excretory system is a pair of bean-shaped kidneys (retroperitoneal, against the dorsal abdominal wall, the left slightly higher than the right), each draining through a ureter into a single muscular urinary bladder, which stores urine until it exits through the urethra. Each kidney has a convex outer border and a concave inner border with a notch, the hilum, through which the renal artery enters, the renal vein and ureter exit, and nerves pass. Internally the kidney has an outer cortex and inner medulla; the medulla is organized into 8–10 conical medullary pyramids, each draining urine at its tip (papilla) into a cup-like calyx, and the calyces converge into the renal pelvis, which narrows into the ureter.

The functional and structural unit of the kidney is the nephron — roughly a million per kidney — and nephrons come in two types by how deep their loop dips into the medulla. About 85% are cortical nephrons, with a short loop of Henle barely entering the medulla; the remaining 15% are juxtamedullary nephrons, with a long loop that plunges deep into the inner medulla. That 15% matters far more than its share suggests — it’s the long loops of these juxtamedullary nephrons that build the steep osmotic gradient the whole medulla relies on to concentrate urine (see Counter-current mechanism, below).

The nephron: one tube, five specialized zones

Each nephron starts with a filtering cup, the Bowman’s capsule, cradling a tuft of capillaries called the glomerulus. Blood enters the glomerulus via a relatively wide afferent arteriole and leaves via a narrower efferent arteriole — that width difference keeps pressure inside the glomerulus high, which is precisely what drives filtration. Past the efferent arteriole, the blood doesn’t head straight back to the vena cava; it flows into a second capillary network, the peritubular capillaries, that wraps around the rest of the tubule (and, in juxtamedullary nephrons, sends long hairpin branches called the vasa recta alongside the loop of Henle) — this second capillary bed is what picks up everything the tubule reabsorbs.

From Bowman’s capsule, filtrate flows in strict sequence through the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT), the loop of Henle (a descending limb dipping toward the medulla and an ascending limb climbing back to the cortex), the distal convoluted tubule (DCT), and finally a shared collecting duct that receives filtrate from many nephrons and carries it down through the medulla’s gradient to a calyx.

Urine formation: filtration, reabsorption, secretion — in that order

Glomerular filtration is passive, pressure-driven ultrafiltration: high hydrostatic pressure inside the glomerular capillaries forces plasma (minus large proteins and cells) through three filtering layers — the capillary endothelium, the basement membrane, and specialized epithelial cells called podocytes, whose foot processes leave narrow filtration slits that act as the final size-selective sieve. Both kidneys together produce roughly 125 mL of filtrate per minute — about 180 litres a day, this rate is the glomerular filtration rate (GFR) — which is a staggering figure given that total blood volume is only about 5–5.5 L: the entire plasma volume is filtered dozens of times over each day.

If 180 L were excreted daily, you’d be dead of dehydration within hours — which is exactly why tubular reabsorption exists: roughly 99% of the filtrate is taken back into the peritubular capillaries as it travels the tubule, leaving only about 1–1.5 L to actually become urine. The PCT does the bulk of this work — reabsorbing roughly 70–80% of the filtrate, and all of the filtered glucose and amino acids by active transport, plus most of the Na+, K+, Cl−, and HCO3−, with water following osmotically. Because this happens no matter what the body’s hydration status is, PCT reabsorption is called obligatory reabsorption. Further down, the DCT and collecting duct perform facultative reabsorption — reabsorption whose amount is dialled up or down by hormones (ADH, aldosterone) depending on the body’s actual water and salt needs at that moment.

The final step, tubular secretion, runs in the opposite direction: the tubule cells (mainly at the DCT) actively move a few extra unwanted substances — H+, K+, NH3, and some drugs — out of peritubular blood and into the filtrate. This is the kidney’s main mechanism for maintaining blood pH and ionic balance minute to minute, independent of how much water is being reabsorbed alongside it.

Core concept

Urine composition = (what was filtered) − (what was reabsorbed) + (what was secreted). Almost every “why does urine contain X” or “why doesn’t urine contain Y” NEET question is really just asking you to apply this one balance — glucose is filtered but fully reabsorbed (so healthy urine has none), urea is filtered and only partly reabsorbed (so it’s the main nitrogenous waste actually excreted), K+ is filtered, reabsorbed, and then re-secreted to fine-tune the final amount.

NEET trap

Glucose in urine (glucosuria) does not mean the PCT has stopped reabsorbing glucose — the PCT has a fixed transport capacity (renal threshold), and in uncontrolled diabetes mellitus the filtered load of glucose exceeds that capacity, so the excess simply isn’t reabsorbed. The defect is upstream (too much filtered glucose), not a failure of the reabsorption machinery itself.

The counter-current mechanism: how urine gets concentrated

Producing urine more concentrated than blood plasma requires a standing osmotic gradient in the medullary tissue — roughly 300 mOsm/L in the cortex rising to about 1,200 mOsm/L deep in the inner medulla — and that gradient is built and maintained by two counter-current arrangements running in parallel: fluid flowing in opposite directions through adjacent limbs of a hairpin loop. The first is the counter-current multiplier, the loop of Henle itself. The descending limb is freely permeable to water but not to solutes, so as filtrate descends into the increasingly salty medulla, water is drawn out osmotically and the filtrate becomes progressively more concentrated. The ascending limb does the opposite: it is impermeable to water but actively pumps NaCl out into the surrounding interstitium, so as filtrate ascends it becomes progressively more dilute — and, crucially, that pumped-out salt is exactly what makes the surrounding medullary tissue salty in the first place, which is what concentrated the descending limb’s filtrate to begin with. The two limbs sustain each other’s gradient.

The second arrangement is the counter-current exchanger: the vasa recta, the hairpin capillary loops that run alongside the loop of Henle in juxtamedullary nephrons. Blood descending into the medulla picks up salt and loses water (matching the gradient), then blood ascending back out does the reverse — picks up water, loses salt back to the tissue. Net effect: the vasa recta supplies the medulla with nutrients and removes the water reabsorbed from the loop and collecting duct, without washing away the salt gradient the loop worked to build. This is exactly why only juxtamedullary nephrons, with their long loops and companion vasa recta, can support a steep medullary gradient — cortical nephrons, with their short loops, simply don’t reach deep enough to use it.

NEET trap

Get the permeabilities exactly right and don’t swap them: the descending limb is water-permeable/solute-impermeable (passive concentration), the ascending limb is water-impermeable/solute-pumping (active dilution). It is the ascending limb, not the descending one, that does the active transport work.

Regulation: ADH, RAAS, and ANF — three dials, opposite defaults

ADH (antidiuretic hormone / vasopressin) is made in the hypothalamus and released from the posterior pituitary when osmoreceptors detect rising blood osmolarity (body getting dehydrated/concentrated) or blood volume/pressure receptors detect a drop. ADH increases the permeability of the DCT and collecting duct to water, pulling more water back into blood and producing small volumes of concentrated urine — and because it also causes mild vasoconstriction, raising blood pressure, it earns its other name, vasopressin. Falling blood osmolarity (overhydration) suppresses ADH, producing large volumes of dilute urine instead.

The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) responds to a fall in glomerular blood flow/pressure (or blood volume), sensed by the juxtaglomerular apparatus (JGA) — a specialized contact zone between the macula densa (modified cells of the DCT) and juxtaglomerular (JG) cells (modified smooth muscle cells of the afferent arteriole). A fall in GFR triggers JG cells to release renin, which converts the plasma protein angiotensinogen (made in the liver) into angiotensin I; this is converted to angiotensin II by angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), mainly in the lungs. Angiotensin II does two things at once: it is a powerful vasoconstrictor, directly raising glomerular blood pressure and GFR back up, and it stimulates the adrenal cortex to release aldosterone, which increases Na+ (and, following it osmotically, water) reabsorption at the DCT — raising blood volume and pressure by a second route.

Atrial natriuretic factor (ANF) runs in the opposite direction: when increased blood volume stretches the atrial wall of the heart, the atria release ANF, which causes vasodilation and suppresses the renin-aldosterone axis — the net effect is more Na+ and water lost in urine (natriuresis), lowering blood volume and pressure back down. ADH/RAAS raise blood pressure and conserve fluid; ANF lowers blood pressure and sheds it — the kidney runs both a gas pedal and a brake on blood volume, chosen by which signal currently dominates.

NEET trap

Keep ADH and aldosterone separated by what they primarily move: ADH acts on water permeability directly; aldosterone acts on Na+ reabsorption, with water following osmotically as a secondary consequence. Also, the JGA sits at the contact between the afferent arteriole and the DCT (via the macula densa) — not the efferent arteriole, and not the PCT.

Micturition: an involuntary reflex you can voluntarily override

Urine formed continuously in the nephrons drains via the collecting ducts, calyces, and renal pelvis into the ureters, which carry it by peristalsis to the urinary bladder for storage. As the bladder fills and its wall stretches, stretch receptors trigger the micturition reflex: signals reach a spinal reflex centre, causing the bladder’s smooth muscle (detrusor) to contract and the internal urethral sphincter (involuntary, smooth muscle) to relax, producing the urge to void. The external urethral sphincter, made of skeletal muscle, is under voluntary control from the brain — which is what lets you consciously delay urination even after the internal sphincter has relaxed, until you choose to release it.

NEET trap

Don’t swap the two sphincters: the internal urethral sphincter is smooth muscle and involuntary; the external one is skeletal muscle and voluntary. Voluntary control over urination exists only because of the external sphincter.

Other organs also excrete

The kidney is the star, but not the only excretory organ. The lungs remove large quantities of CO2 (and some water vapour) with every exhalation. The skin, via sweat and sebaceous glands, excretes water, NaCl, and small amounts of urea and lactic acid — sweating is simultaneously an excretory and a thermoregulatory process. The liver excretes bile pigments (bilirubin and biliverdin, breakdown products of haemoglobin), cholesterol, and some drug metabolites into bile, which ultimately leave the body via faeces rather than urine.

When the filter fails

Uremia is the abnormal build-up of urea and other nitrogenous wastes in the blood — a direct sign that the kidneys are no longer clearing them adequately. Left unaddressed, this progresses to renal failure (acute, a sudden drop in kidney function, often reversible if treated promptly; or chronic, a slow, usually irreversible decline), in which the kidneys can no longer maintain fluid, electrolyte, and waste balance on their own. Haemodialysis substitutes for lost kidney function mechanically: the patient’s blood is passed through a machine past a selectively permeable membrane bathed in a dialysing fluid whose composition mimics normal plasma minus the nitrogenous wastes, so those wastes diffuse out of the blood down their concentration gradient (built on the same counter-current diffusion logic as the nephron itself) before the cleaned blood is returned to the body.

Renal calculi (kidney stones) are hard, crystalline masses — commonly of calcium oxalate — that form when normally soluble salts crystallize out inside the kidney or urinary tract, causing pain and obstructing urine flow. Glomerulonephritis is inflammation of the glomeruli themselves, impairing filtration at its very first step. A simple urine analysis is diagnostically powerful precisely because of the filtration-minus-reabsorption logic above: glucose in urine points toward diabetes mellitus (filtered load exceeding PCT reabsorption capacity), while protein in urine (proteinuria) points toward glomerular damage, since intact glomeruli shouldn’t let plasma proteins through the filtration slits at all.

Think first: a patient's urine test shows normal glucose levels but significant protein in the urine. Based on the filtration-reabsorption-secretion model of this chapter, which step of urine formation is most likely disrupted, and why does this rule out a PCT reabsorption problem as the cause?

The disruption points to glomerular filtration, not reabsorption. Under normal conditions the glomerular filtration membrane (capillary endothelium, basement membrane, and podocyte filtration slits) is size-selective enough to exclude plasma proteins almost entirely — so healthy filtrate essentially shouldn’t contain protein in the first place, and there is normally nothing for the PCT to need to reabsorb. Finding protein in the final urine therefore means the filtration barrier itself has become abnormally leaky (as in glomerulonephritis), letting protein into the filtrate that a downstream reabsorption failure could never explain, since reabsorption problems only affect substances that were correctly filtered through to begin with. The normal glucose reading confirms the PCT’s active transport machinery is working fine — if PCT function generally were failing, you’d expect glucose to leak through too, since glucose reabsorption is one of the PCT’s core jobs. The two findings together localize the fault upstream, at the glomerulus, not downstream at the tubule.

Where this leaves you

Blood is now circulating, gas-exchanging, and having its nitrogenous waste and fluid balance continuously corrected — the internal chemistry of the body is stable enough to support sustained activity. But none of that activity is possible without a way to actually generate physical movement and hold the body upright while doing it.
