Chapter 10 · Part III — Plant Structure & Function

Energy Capture and Release
Photosynthesis and Respiration

Chapter 3 gave you the mirror-image summary equations and the honest accounting: photosynthesis is endergonic, respiration is exergonic, and ATP is the currency that couples one to the other. What it didn’t open up was the machinery — and the machinery is where NEET actually lives, because “6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂” is one line, but the pathway that makes it true runs through two photosystems, a carbon-fixing cycle, three different leaf strategies, and an entire second organelle running the reaction in reverse. This chapter opens all of it — and underneath the apparent complexity, you’ll find the same one machine doing almost all of the actual energy conversion, mounted in two different membranes.

One machine, mounted twice: the thread through this whole chapter

Every energy-releasing step you’ve met so far — ATP hydrolysis driving a pump, an enzyme lowering an activation-energy barrier — has been about a single reaction. Photosynthesis and respiration are bigger than that: each is a chain of reactions whose entire purpose is to move electrons downhill (or, in photosynthesis, first shove them uphill with light) in small, controlled steps rather than one uncontrolled burn. Controlled release matters because a cell can only capture useful energy from a step-by-step fall, the same way a series of small waterwheels captures far more usable work from a waterfall than letting the water crash straight down once.

Both organelles in this chapter build that controlled fall the same way: a chain of membrane-bound electron carriers pumps H+ ions across a membrane, building up a concentration gradient (and an electrical gradient) — a proton-motive force. That stored gradient then drives a single rotary enzyme, ATP synthase, which lets protons flow back down their gradient through it and uses that flow to spin a shaft that stitches ADP and Pi together into ATP. This is chemiosmosis (Peter Mitchell, 1961), and it is the mechanism behind both photophosphorylation in the thylakoid membrane and oxidative phosphorylation in the inner mitochondrial membrane. Different electron sources, different membranes, opposite overall direction of energy flow — the same rotary machine, spun by the same kind of gradient, cashing electrons in for ATP either way.

Core concept

Don’t memorise photophosphorylation and oxidative phosphorylation as two unrelated processes. Both are chemiosmosis: an electron transport chain pumps H+ across a membrane, and ATP synthase lets that gradient collapse through itself to make ATP. Once you see this, half of this chapter’s “mechanism” questions become one idea applied to two locations instead of two ideas to memorise separately.

The light reactions: photons in, electrons up, ATP and NADPH out

Photosynthesis happens in the chloroplast, and the light reactions specifically happen across the thylakoid membrane. Pigments embedded there — chlorophyll a (the primary pigment that actually participates in the reaction centre), plus accessory pigments chlorophyll b and various carotenoids/xanthophylls that absorb wavelengths chlorophyll a misses and hand that energy on — are organised into two functional clusters called photosystems, each built around a reaction-centre chlorophyll: Photosystem II (P680) and Photosystem I (P700), named for the wavelength (in nm) at which each reaction centre absorbs best. Light striking either photosystem's antenna pigments funnels energy to the reaction-centre chlorophyll, which responds by ejecting a high-energy electron — the moment light energy becomes electron energy.

Non-cyclic (linear) electron flow runs through both photosystems in series, and its path is worth tracing exactly once, because nearly every question in this section is really just asking you to place one step on this chain correctly:

  1. PSII absorbs light and ejects an electron, leaving behind a chlorophyll that is now a very strong oxidant — strong enough to pull electrons off water itself.
  2. Photolysis of water at PSII replaces that lost electron: 2H2O → 4H+ + 4e− + O2. This is the entire biological source of the O2 you breathe — every oxygen atom photosynthesis releases comes from water, never from CO2.
  3. PSII's ejected electron passes down an electron transport chain (plastoquinone → the cytochrome b6f complex → plastocyanin), losing energy at each step; that energy pumps H+ into the thylakoid lumen, building the proton gradient chemiosmosis needs.
  4. PSI absorbs light independently and re-energises the electron arriving from the cytochrome complex, ejecting its own high-energy electron.
  5. That electron is finally handed to the enzyme NADP+ reductase, which uses it (plus a second electron and an H+) to reduce NADP+ to NADPH — the light reaction's second product, alongside the ATP chemiosmosis has been building the whole way.

Plotting each carrier's redox potential against its position in this sequence traces two rises (at PSII and PSI, where light re-energises the electron) separated by a gradual fall (the electron transport chain) — a zigzag shaped like the letter Z lying on its side, which is exactly why this whole pathway is called the Z-scheme.

Non-cyclic flow is the default and it produces ATP, NADPH, and O2 together, in a fixed ratio the Calvin cycle can't quite use on its own — the cycle needs slightly more ATP per NADPH than non-cyclic flow supplies. Cyclic electron flow fills that gap: PSI's ejected electron, instead of reaching NADP+ reductase, is routed back into the electron transport chain between PSII and PSI, pumping more H+ and generating extra ATP — with no PSII involvement, no water splitting, and no NADPH or O2 produced at all.

NEET trap

“Which photophosphorylation produces O2?” Only non-cyclic — because only non-cyclic flow involves PSII, and PSII is the only site where water is split. Cyclic photophosphorylation uses PSI only, produces ATP alone (no NADPH, no O2), and exists purely to top up the ATP:NADPH ratio the Calvin cycle needs. If a question mentions O2 evolution or NADPH production in the same breath as “cyclic,” that pairing is the trap — cyclic flow makes ATP and nothing else.

The Calvin cycle: where the light reaction’s ATP and NADPH get spent

The light reactions never touch carbon at all — their entire output is ATP, NADPH, and O2. Fixing CO2 into sugar happens separately, in the chloroplast stroma, in a cycle (not a one-way path) discovered by Melvin Calvin and co-workers: the Calvin cycle, also called the C3 cycle because its first stable product is a three-carbon compound. It runs in three phases, and NEET tends to ask about the boundary between each phase more than the phase itself:

  • Carboxylation — the enzyme RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase-oxygenase, the most abundant protein on Earth) fixes one CO2 onto the five-carbon acceptor RuBP, producing an unstable six-carbon intermediate that immediately splits into two molecules of 3-phosphoglyceric acid (PGA) — the first stable product, and the reason this is the “C3” pathway.
  • Reduction — each PGA is phosphorylated by ATP and then reduced by NADPH (both supplied by the light reactions) into glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), the actual sugar-level product of the cycle. This is the step that consumes almost all of the light reaction's ATP and NADPH output — the endergonic climb Chapter 3 promised, happening literally here.
  • Regeneration — most of the G3P produced is used, at the cost of still more ATP, to regenerate RuBP so the cycle can keep running; only a fraction is exported to build starch, sucrose, and everything downstream.

Run the bookkeeping across six turns of the cycle (fixing six CO2, the number needed for one net hexose sugar) and it costs 18 ATP and 12 NADPH to net two exportable G3P (which combine to one glucose-equivalent), with the rest of the twelve G3P produced spent regenerating six RuBP. You don't need to reproduce that arithmetic from scratch on the exam, but the ratio it implies — more ATP consumed than NADPH, roughly 3:2 — is exactly why cyclic photophosphorylation exists at all: non-cyclic flow alone doesn't quite supply ATP fast enough relative to NADPH to keep this cycle running at full tilt.

NEET trap

The first stable product of carbon fixation is PGA, a three-carbon acid — not glucose, and not RuBP. “Glucose is directly produced by the Calvin cycle” is a common wrong-option; glucose is assembled later, outside the cycle proper, from two G3P molecules. Confusing PGA (the C3 product) with OAA (the C4 pathway's first product, next section) is the single most common mix-up in this whole topic.

Same enzyme, same flaw: why C4 and CAM plants exist at all

RuBisCO has an inconvenient dual personality: alongside its carboxylase activity (fixing CO2 onto RuBP, the reaction the Calvin cycle depends on), it also has oxygenase activity — it can just as readily bind O2 instead of CO2. When it does, the product feeds into photorespiration: a pathway that consumes O2 and releases CO2 like respiration, runs only in light like photosynthesis, but produces no ATP and no NADPH — it is pure loss, undoing some of what the light reactions just paid for. On a hot, bright day with stomata partly closed to conserve water (Chapter 9), CO2 inside the leaf drops and O2 (a photosynthesis byproduct, remember) accumulates right where RuBisCO is working — exactly the conditions that favour the oxygenase side reaction.

C4 and CAM plants are two independent evolutionary fixes to the same problem: keep the CO2:O2 ratio around RuBisCO high enough that oxygenase activity stays negligible. Both fixes rely on the same trick — fix CO2 first with a different enzyme, PEP carboxylase (PEPcase), which binds only CO2 (via its hydrated form, HCO3−), has no oxygenase activity at all, and has a far higher affinity for CO2 than RuBisCO does — and then release that concentrated CO2 next to RuBisCO under conditions RuBisCO likes. Where the two strategies differ is entirely in how they separate that first fixation step from the Calvin cycle proper:

  • C4 (spatial separation) — two cell types, two locations, same time. In mesophyll cells, PEPcase fixes CO2 onto the three-carbon acceptor PEP to form oxaloacetate (OAA) — the first stable product, a four-carbon acid, which is why this is the “C4” pathway. OAA is converted to malate (or aspartate), which is shuttled into adjoining bundle sheath cells, decarboxylated there to release CO2 at high local concentration, and that CO2 feeds a completely conventional Calvin cycle running in the bundle sheath chloroplasts. Maize, sugarcane, and sorghum are the classic NEET examples.
  • CAM (temporal separation) — one cell type, two times of day. Stomata open only at night, when it's cool and humid enough to lose relatively little water; PEPcase fixes CO2 into malic acid, which is stored in the large central vacuole. By day, stomata close completely (minimising water loss during the hottest, driest hours) and the stored malic acid is decarboxylated to release CO2 internally, feeding a Calvin cycle running in the very same cell, now sealed off from the atmosphere. Pineapple, Opuntia, and other succulents are the classic NEET examples — CAM stands for Crassulacean Acid Metabolism, named for the family it was first described in.

NEET trap

CAM plants are not exempt from the Calvin cycle — they still run it, in the same cell, by day, exactly like a C3 plant would. The only thing CAM changes is when and how CO2 first gets fixed. Don't answer “does this plant use the Calvin cycle?” with “no” just because you've spotted CAM or C4 in the question — every land plant that fixes carbon into sugar eventually runs the Calvin cycle; C4 and CAM only change how CO2 is delivered to it.

Glycolysis: the one pathway every cell, aerobic or not, shares

Respiration's job is the mirror of everything above: take the chemical energy photosynthesis stored in glucose and release it, in controlled steps, as ATP. The first stage, glycolysis (the EMP pathway, after Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas), happens in the cytoplasm of every cell — plant, animal, aerobic or anaerobic — and needs no oxygen at all. It splits one six-carbon glucose into two three-carbon pyruvate molecules through a sequence of enzyme-catalysed steps, with a net yield, per glucose, of 2 ATP (made by direct substrate-level phosphorylation, not chemiosmosis) and 2 NADH.

What happens to those two pyruvate molecules next is a genuine fork in the road, and it's the single biggest structural decision in this chapter:

  • Oxygen available — pyruvate enters the mitochondrion and is fully oxidised through the link reaction, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain (next section), extracting far more ATP per glucose.
  • Oxygen absent — fermentation. Pyruvate is reduced directly, regenerating the NAD+ glycolysis needs to keep running, but capturing no further energy. Yeast (and some plant tissue under waterlogged, anaerobic conditions) run alcoholic fermentation: pyruvate → acetaldehyde + CO2, then acetaldehyde + NADH → ethanol + NAD+. Your own muscle cells under an oxygen debt run lactic acid fermentation: pyruvate + NADH → lactate + NAD+, no CO2 released.
NEET trap

Fermentation's entire net ATP yield is the 2 ATP glycolysis already made — nothing further is captured. Reducing pyruvate to ethanol or lactate isn't an energy-harvesting step at all; it exists purely to regenerate NAD+ so glycolysis doesn't stall for lack of an electron acceptor. A question that implies fermentation “produces” energy beyond glycolysis's own 2 ATP is testing exactly this misconception.

The link reaction, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain: cashing in the electrons

Inside the mitochondrion, each pyruvate first passes through the link reaction (oxidative decarboxylation, catalysed by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex): pyruvate + NAD+ + CoA → acetyl-CoA + NADH + CO2. Acetyl-CoA then enters the Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle / TCA cycle) in the mitochondrial matrix, where its two carbons are progressively oxidised all the way to CO2 across eight steps that return the cycle to its starting point, oxaloacetate, ready to accept the next acetyl-CoA. One turn of the Krebs cycle (one acetyl-CoA, i.e. half a glucose) yields 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 1 ATP (again by substrate-level phosphorylation, at the succinyl-CoA → succinate step) and 2 CO2. Since one glucose yields two pyruvate, the cycle turns twice per glucose.

Notice what the Krebs cycle itself does not do: it makes almost no ATP directly. Its real product is electrons, loaded onto NADH and FADH2, handed to the electron transport system (ETS) in the inner mitochondrial membrane — the respiratory equivalent of the Z-scheme's electron transport chain. Electrons flow through a sequence of protein complexes (NADH dehydrogenase → ubiquinone → cytochrome complexes → cytochrome oxidase), losing energy at each transfer; that energy pumps H+ across the inner membrane, and at the very end, O2 accepts the spent electrons (plus H+) to form H2O — the final electron acceptor, and the reason aerobic respiration needs oxygen at all. The resulting H+ gradient spins the same ATP synthase this chapter opened with, in oxidative phosphorylation — chemiosmosis again, now running to extract energy rather than to store it.

Add it up by NCERT's convention — 4 ATP by substrate-level phosphorylation plus 34 ATP via the ETS — and one glucose yields a gross 38 ATP. Subtract 2 ATP spent shuttling glycolysis's cytoplasmic NADH into the mitochondrion (it can't cross the inner membrane directly), and the accepted net gain is 36 ATP per glucose, fully oxidised.

NEET trap

NCERT's 36 ATP net figure rests on explicit textbook assumptions: strictly sequential glycolysis → Krebs → ETS, glucose as the sole substrate, and no intermediates diverted elsewhere — and it uses the older conversion factors of 3 ATP per NADH and 2 ATP per FADH2. Modern biochemistry, measuring the actual H+-to-ATP-synthase stoichiometry more precisely, puts the real yield closer to 30–32 ATP. For NEET, use 36 (net) / 38 (gross) — it's the number the exam is built around, even though it isn't the most current biochemical estimate.

Think first: a metabolic poison blocks the electron transport chain completely, but glycolysis is untouched. Does the cell still make any ATP at all — and if so, how much per glucose?

Yes — but only glycolysis's own 2 ATP net, made by substrate-level phosphorylation, which needs no functioning ETS at all. Without a working ETS, NADH can't be re-oxidised there, so NAD+ runs out and glycolysis would stall — unless the cell falls back on fermentation to regenerate NAD+, exactly as it would under low oxygen. Either way, the Krebs cycle and oxidative phosphorylation contribute nothing once the chain is blocked; this is precisely why ETS poisons (cyanide is the classic example, blocking cytochrome oxidase) are so quickly lethal — they don't just slow ATP production, they collapse it down to glycolysis's bare 2 ATP per glucose.

Respiratory quotient: reading the substrate from the gas exchange

Respiration doesn't always burn carbohydrate. Fats and proteins can be fed into the same pathway at different entry points (fats broken down to acetyl-CoA via β-oxidation; amino acids deaminated and their carbon skeletons diverted into glycolysis or the Krebs cycle at various points), and which substrate is actually being oxidised leaves a measurable fingerprint in the ratio of gases exchanged — the respiratory quotient (RQ):

RQ = volume of CO2 evolved ÷ volume of O2 consumed

Respiratory substrateRQWhy
Carbohydrate= 1Already fully oxygenated (C6H12O6) — CO2 produced exactly equals O2 consumed
Fat< 1 (≈0.7)Fats are far more hydrogen-rich, oxygen-poor — extra O2 is needed to oxidise all that hydrogen to water, so O2 consumption outpaces CO2 release
Protein≈0.9Intermediate composition between carbohydrate and fat
Organic acids (e.g. tartaric acid, as a respiratory substrate)> 1Already oxygen-rich relative to their carbon and hydrogen content — less additional O2 is needed per CO2 released

This substrate flexibility is exactly what makes respiration an amphibolic pathway rather than a purely catabolic one: glycolysis and Krebs-cycle intermediates aren't just waypoints toward CO2 — they're also drawn off to build fatty acids, amino acids, and other biosynthetic precursors. The same pathway runs downhill to release energy and simultaneously supplies the raw material for growth, which is the thread this chapter hands forward: all of that photosynthate and respiratory intermediate has to go somewhere once it's made, and Chapter 11 is where the plant actually spends it on growth and development.

Core concept

RQ isn't just a lab number — it's a direct readout of what a tissue is burning at that moment. A germinating oil-rich seed (castor, groundnut) shows RQ well below 1 early on because it's respiring stored fat, and that RQ climbs toward 1 later in germination as the seedling's metabolism shifts toward carbohydrate. Reading RQ “backwards,” from a measured value to the substrate in use, is a recurring NEET question pattern.

Where this leaves you

Strip away the enzyme names and this chapter is one mechanism (chemiosmosis: pump H+, spin ATP synthase) run in two directions across two organelles, bracketing one shared currency, ATP, that Chapter 3 already told you had to be coupled to something exergonic to be worth anything. Photosynthesis spends light energy to build that gradient and, downstream of it, fix carbon into sugar — with C4 and CAM as two independent fixes for RuBisCO's one structural flaw. Respiration spends that sugar back down through glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the same chemiosmotic logic in reverse, with RQ betraying exactly which fuel is being burned at any moment. What this chapter has left open is what a plant actually does with all that captured energy and fixed carbon once it has them — how growth itself gets switched on, coordinated, and pointed in a direction. That's Chapter 11.
