Chapter 09 · Part III — Plant Structure & Function

Water and Minerals
Transport in Plants

Chapter 8 left one thread deliberately dangling: how does water get pulled from a root twenty metres underground to the top of a tree, against gravity, with no pump anywhere in the plant — and why did an anatomical detail as small as a waxy strip on an endodermal cell wall matter enough to flag in advance? This chapter answers both, and it does it with a single idea doing almost all of the work: every drop of water and every mineral ion that moves through a plant is moving down a gradient, from a region of higher water potential to one of lower water potential, or is being actively pushed against one at the cost of ATP. Diffusion, osmosis, the rise of sap up a hundred-metre-tall sequoia, and a stoma opening at dawn are not four separate mechanisms to memorise — they are one gradient-following logic, applied at four different scales.

Class 11 — Transport in Plants (diffusion, osmosis, plasmolysis, water potential, long-distance transport of water, transpiration, uptake and transport of mineral nutrients), read alongside Mineral Nutrition for deficiency symptoms and nutrient mobility.
NEET angle

Water potential: the one gradient behind everything in this chapter

Water, like every other diffusible thing, moves from where it has more free energy to where it has less. For water specifically, chemists give that free energy a name: water potential (Ψw), measured in pressure units (megapascals, MPa). Pure water at atmospheric pressure is defined as the reference point, Ψw = 0 — the highest value water potential can take. Add anything to water that lowers its free energy to do work, and Ψw drops below zero; water potential in a biological system is therefore always zero or negative, never positive. Two things push it around inside a cell:

  • Solute potential (Ψs, sometimes called osmotic potential) — dissolving solutes in water ties some of the water molecules up and lowers the free energy available to do work. Ψs is always zero or negative, and gets more negative as solute concentration rises.
  • Pressure potential (Ψp) — physical pressure applied to the water. Inside a walled plant cell, water flowing in pushes the protoplast against the rigid wall, and the wall pushes back; that back-pressure (turgor pressure) is usually positive and raises Ψw. In a xylem vessel under transpirational pull, by contrast, Ψp can go negative — the water is under tension, not compression, and this exact fact is what makes the next section's theory work.

Put the two together and you have the working equation for a plant cell: Ψw = Ψs + Ψp. Water always moves from the higher (less negative) Ψw to the lower (more negative) Ψw, full stop — that single rule is what drives osmosis across a membrane, water climbing a xylem vessel, and water evaporating out of a leaf into dry air. Nothing in this chapter needs a different rule; only the numbers going into Ψs and Ψp change from one situation to the next.

NEET trap

The sign trips up more students than the concept does. “More negative” water potential is a lower value, not a bigger pull toward itself in the everyday sense — water moves into the more negative value, the same way heat moves toward a colder body. A cell with Ψw = −0.8 MPa placed in a solution of Ψw = −0.2 MPa will lose water (−0.8 is lower than −0.2), even though a student in a hurry might eyeball “0.8 > 0.2” and pick the opposite direction. Always compare the signed values, not their magnitudes.

Osmosis, turgor, and plasmolysis: water potential at the scale of one cell

Osmosis is simply diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane, down its own water-potential gradient — the plasma membrane and tonoplast qualify because they let water through far more readily than most solutes. Drop a plant cell into a solution more dilute than its own cell sap (a hypotonic solution, higher Ψw outside) and water moves in: the protoplast swells against the cell wall, turgor pressure (Ψp) rises, and Ψp keeps rising until it exactly cancels Ψs and Ψw reaches equilibrium at zero net flow — the cell is now turgid. This is not a minor detail: turgor pressure is what keeps a non-woody plant standing upright and what keeps a stoma's guard cells able to bend open, so “turgid” is the plant-cell equivalent of a properly inflated tyre holding the whole vehicle's shape.

Reverse the solution — a hypertonic solution, lower (more negative) Ψw outside than inside — and water leaves the cell. The protoplast shrinks, and because the wall is rigid while the membrane is not, the shrinking protoplast pulls away from the wall, leaving a visible gap: this is plasmolysis. A cell in a solution of exactly matching Ψw (isotonic) shows no net movement either way and stays flaccid — neither turgid nor plasmolysed.

NEET trap

Plasmolysis is reversible — up to a point. Move a plasmolysed cell back into a hypotonic (or pure water) medium and it reabsorbs water and returns to turgor: deplasmolysis. Push the plasmolysis too far or hold it too long, though, and the protoplast is irreversibly damaged and the cell dies. Questions describing plasmolysis as observed under a microscope are almost always testing whether you know it's a live, reversible osmotic response — not cell death by itself.

From root hair to xylem: three routes, one checkpoint

A root absorbs water mainly through its root hairs — the epiblema's huge surface-area trick from Chapter 8 — because soil water almost always has a higher (less negative) Ψw than the root hair cell's cytoplasm, so water moves in by osmosis without the plant spending any energy on it. From there, water crosses the cortex toward the stele by up to three routes running in parallel:

  • Apoplast pathway — through the continuous meshwork of cell walls and the intercellular spaces between them, never crossing a membrane at all. It's the fastest route, because it faces no membrane resistance.
  • Symplast pathway — through the living cytoplasm of one cell, out through plasmodesmata into the next, cell after cell, crossing a membrane only once, at the root hair itself.
  • Transmembrane pathway — cell to cell, but crossing two membranes (in) and (out) at every single cell, often detouring through the vacuole. Slowest of the three, and a comparatively minor contributor.

The apoplast route is fast precisely because it's unregulated — and that's exactly why the plant can't leave it unregulated all the way to the xylem. This is where Chapter 8's Casparian strip earns its keep: a band of suberin and lignin running around the radial and transverse walls of every endodermal cell, completely impermeable to water and solutes. Water and dissolved ions travelling apoplastically hit that strip and are physically stopped — the only way through is to cross into the endodermal cell's cytoplasm (a membrane) and out the other side (another membrane), rejoining the symplast for that one cell layer before continuing into the pericycle and the xylem. The Casparian strip converts an open, indiscriminate apoplastic shortcut into a single, cell-membrane checkpoint the plant can control, which is exactly what a plant needs if it wants to admit water freely while being selective about which mineral ions it lets through.

Core concept

The apoplast pathway is fast but unselective; the symplast pathway is slower but living, membrane-bound, and controllable. The Casparian strip doesn't block water — overall flow barely slows down, because it just forces a one-cell detour through the symplast — it blocks unregulated apoplastic solute flow, forcing every mineral ion through at least one living membrane where the plant's transport proteins can decide whether it's let in.

Ascent of sap: pulled up, not pushed — the cohesion-tension theory

Once water is in the xylem, it has to rise — sometimes over a hundred metres in the tallest trees — with no living pump anywhere along a dead, hollow vessel. The accepted explanation, the cohesion-tension theory (Dixon and Joly, 1894), locates the engine at the top of the plant, not the bottom: water evaporating from the moist mesophyll cell walls inside a leaf, ultimately lost as vapour through open stomata, lowers the water potential of those cells. They pull water osmotically from the leaf's finest xylem veins, which pulls on the vessel below it, and so on, all the way down to the root — a continuous pull transmitted through an unbroken column of water, exactly the way pulling one end of a taut, wet string pulls the whole string.

That transmission only works because of three physical properties of water acting together:

  • Cohesion — hydrogen bonds between water molecules hold the column together with enough tensile strength to resist being pulled apart under tension, much like a chain resists being pulled apart along its length.
  • Adhesion — water molecules also hydrogen-bond to the polar walls of the narrow xylem vessels, which helps keep the column from simply falling back down or pulling away from the vessel wall.
  • Transpirational pull — the negative pressure (tension) generated at the leaf end, which is the actual force doing the pulling; cohesion and adhesion are what let that pull be transmitted the entire length of the plant instead of just yanking loose the topmost few water molecules.

The result is a continuous water-potential gradient running from a relatively high (least negative) Ψw in the wet soil, through progressively more negative values in root and stem xylem, to the most negative Ψw of all in the air just outside an open stoma on a dry day — often far more negative than anything inside the plant. Water simply follows that gradient, exactly as Section 1's single rule says it must; the “pump” is nothing more exotic than evaporation happening at the top of an unbroken, cohesive column.

NEET trap

Root pressure is not what lifts water up a tall tree. Root pressure is a real, separate phenomenon — active pumping of ions into the stele raises solute concentration there, water follows osmotically, and the resulting positive pressure can push xylem sap upward on its own, typically only a metre or two, usually at night or early morning when transpiration is low. Its visible signature is guttation — drops of liquid water forced out through specialised pores called hydathodes at leaf tips/margins, seen on grass and many herbaceous plants at dawn. Don't confuse it with dew, which is atmospheric water vapour condensing on a cool leaf surface from the outside, with no plant physiology involved at all. Root pressure alone cannot explain water reaching the top of a sequoia; transpirational pull via cohesion-tension does the overwhelming majority of that work.

Transpiration: the engine, its throttle, and its cost

Transpiration — evaporative water loss from the plant's aerial parts — is overwhelmingly a leaf phenomenon, and overwhelmingly a stomatal one: the great majority of water lost by a typical plant leaves through the stomata, with a much smaller amount lost directly through the waxy cuticle, and a negligible amount through lenticels in bark. That lopsided split is not a design flaw; it's the direct cost of a leaf needing its stomata open at all for CO₂ to diffuse in for photosynthesis (Chapter 10's subject) — every stoma is simultaneously a CO₂ door and, unavoidably, a water-vapour door, and the plant cannot have one without the other.

Because the stomatal door is the dominant one, whatever opens or closes it controls transpiration almost directly. That control is the same K+ ion flux mechanism in every guard cell pair: a proton pump (H+-ATPase) in the guard cell membrane actively pumps H+ out, which drives K+ into the guard cell through inward-rectifying channels; the accumulating K+ (along with accompanying anions and sugars) lowers the guard cell's Ψs, water follows osmotically, and the guard cell becomes turgid. Because the guard cell's cellulose microfibrils are arranged radially rather than lengthwise, that turgor makes the guard cell bow outward at its thinner outer wall and pull its thicker inner wall into a crescent — opening the pore between the pair. Pump the K+ back out and the whole sequence reverses: the guard cells lose turgor, go flaccid, and the pore closes.

Core concept

Transpiration isn't a leak the plant merely tolerates — it's the pull that powers the ascent of sap, it evaporatively cools a leaf sitting in direct sun, and it keeps a steady upward mineral-laden water stream flowing into the xylem. But it is a genuine cost in water, which is exactly why the K+-driven stomatal throttle exists at all: the plant is continuously trading CO₂ intake, cooling, and mineral transport against water it usually cannot afford to lose without limit.

Mineral uptake and transport: water's free ride is not the mineral's free ride

Water crosses the root hair membrane passively, down its own water-potential gradient, at no direct energy cost. Mineral ions usually cannot rely on the same free ride, because soil solution is frequently more dilute in a given ion than the root cell's cytoplasm already is — moving that ion in means moving it against its own concentration gradient, which diffusion alone will not do. Roots handle this with a mix of two strategies:

  • Passive uptake — ions that happen to be more concentrated outside than in, or that are pulled in along the electrical component of the electrochemical gradient, move in through selective ion channels with no direct ATP cost.
  • Active uptake — the majority case: membrane carrier/transporter proteins, powered directly or indirectly by ATP, pump specific ions into the root hair cell against their concentration gradient. This is also what makes mineral uptake selective — the plant can accumulate an ion it needs even from soil where that ion is scarce, and can decline one it doesn't, which bulk water flow could never do on its own.

Because active uptake requires ATP, it requires root respiration — which is why anything that starves root cells of oxygen or blocks respiration (waterlogged soil, metabolic poisons) suppresses mineral uptake far more sharply than it suppresses water uptake. Once inside the symplast, ions cross the same Casparian-strip checkpoint water does, load into the xylem, and then travel passively with the transpiration stream — mineral long-distance transport essentially hitches a ride on the water pulled up by cohesion-tension, even though getting into that stream in the first place was an active, selective, energy-spending step.

Not every mineral that reaches a leaf stays put. Some elements can be withdrawn from mature tissue and exported via the phloem to actively growing regions when supply runs short — these are mobile elements. Others are locked into structures (cell walls, chlorophyll, certain enzyme complexes) once deposited and effectively cannot be retrieved — immobile elements. That single distinction predicts where on the plant a deficiency symptom will show up first, which is exactly the pattern NEET likes to test.

NEET trap

Don't memorise the symptom lists in isolation — derive the location from mobility first. If a question describes chlorosis or necrosis appearing specifically in young leaves / shoot tips, the element is almost certainly immobile (Ca, S, Fe, B are the classic NEET set); if it appears in old / lower leaves first, it's mobile (N, P, K, Mg). This one rule resolves most “which element is deficient” questions faster than recalling each symptom from scratch.

Think first: two adjacent xylem vessels are severed and a dye is applied to the cut root end. The dye is later found throughout the stem's xylem but never appears in the phloem. A mineral tracer applied the same way, however, is later found in both xylem and phloem. Why the difference?

The dye simply traces the bulk flow of the transpiration stream, which only exists inside the xylem — xylem and phloem are physically separate conducting systems, so a passively-carried dye has no route into the phloem. A mobile mineral ion, by contrast, is actively loaded out of the xylem and into living tissue wherever it's needed, and from there it can be actively loaded into the phloem's sieve tubes and retranslocated to younger growth — a genuinely different, energy-spending process layered on top of the passive xylem ride, not just more bulk flow.

Where this leaves you

One rule carried this entire chapter: water, and everything dissolved or entrained in it, moves toward lower water potential, whether that's a cell drawing water in by osmosis, a mineral ion being actively pumped against a concentration gradient, or an entire xylem column being pulled skyward by evaporation at a leaf's stomata. The Casparian strip from Chapter 8 turned out to be the plant's one controllable checkpoint on that whole system — the place where a fast, indiscriminate apoplastic shortcut is forced to become a slower, selective, living one. What this chapter has left open is where all that pulled-up water and all that actively-loaded CO₂ actually go once they're inside a leaf cell: how a plant turns light, water, and carbon dioxide into sugar in the first place, and how it gets the energy back out of that sugar afterward. That's Chapter 10.
