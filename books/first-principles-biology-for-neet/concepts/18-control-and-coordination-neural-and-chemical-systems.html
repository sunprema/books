Chapter 18 · Part IV — Human Structure & Function

Control and Coordination
Neural and Chemical Systems

A muscle only moves when a nerve impulse tells it to — but that single neuromuscular event, from Chapter 17, is the last, local step of a body-wide problem: how do trillions of cells, scattered across a body that can be over a metre long, act like one coordinated organism instead of trillions of independent ones? Multicellular life solves this with two control systems running in parallel. The nervous system sends fast, precise, short-lived electrical signals down dedicated wires — built for split-second, localized decisions. The endocrine system releases slower, longer-lasting chemical signals into the bloodstream — built for broad, sustained, body-wide adjustments. This chapter builds both from first principles: how one neuron generates and conducts an impulse, how it hands that impulse to the next cell, how the brain and sense organs put that machinery to use, and how hormones extend the same coordinating job onto a much slower clock.

Two systems, one job: keeping a body acting as one

Chapter 12 introduced homeostasis as the goal every body system ultimately serves. Neural and chemical coordination are how that goal gets enforced, moment to moment — they are the body's control systems, not just two more organs to memorize alongside them. Almost every fact in this chapter is an instance of one underlying design trade-off, so it's worth naming before the details arrive.

Core concept

Nervous control is fast, precise, and short-lived: an electrical impulse travels down a dedicated neuron in milliseconds and acts on one specific target, then is gone. Endocrine control is slow, diffuse, and long-lasting: a hormone is released into the blood, reaches every cell in the body, and only the cells carrying the matching receptor respond — over seconds to days, not milliseconds. Most of this chapter is really just this one trade-off — speed and precision vs. reach and duration — worked out in two different molecular vocabularies: electrical impulses for one system, hormone–receptor binding for the other.

The neuron: one cell built to carry a signal

A neuron receives, conducts, and passes on a signal, and its shape is built entirely around that one-way flow. Dendrites are short, branched processes that receive signals and conduct them toward the cell body. The cell body (cyton/soma) holds the nucleus and organelles, including granular patches of rough ER called Nissl's granules. The axon is a single, usually long process that conducts the impulse away from the cell body; it typically arises from a cone-shaped region called the axon hillock, which packs the highest density of voltage-gated Na+ channels in the whole neuron and is therefore where an action potential is actually triggered. Many axons are wrapped in an insulating myelin sheath, interrupted at regular intervals by gaps called nodes of Ranvier, and the axon ends in a swollen synaptic knob packed with neurotransmitter-filled vesicles.

NEET trap

Myelin has two different manufacturers depending on location: Schwann cells myelinate axons in the peripheral nervous system, one segment per Schwann cell, while oligodendrocytes myelinate axons in the central nervous system — and a single oligodendrocyte can wrap segments of several different axons at once. Don’t swap PNS and CNS here.

Resting and action potentials: the signal itself

At rest, a neuron's inside is negatively charged relative to the outside — the resting membrane potential (RMP), about −70 mV. The axoplasm holds high K+, low Na+, and large, membrane-impermeable negative proteins; the extracellular fluid holds high Na+, low K+. Because the resting membrane is more permeable to K+ than Na+, K+ leaks out faster than Na+ leaks in, leaving the inside net-negative. That gradient is actively maintained by the Na+–K+ ATPase, which pumps 3 Na+ out for every 2 K+ in against their gradients, spending ATP — and because it moves more positive charge out than in, the pump itself contributes directly to the negative resting potential.

A stimulus that depolarizes the membrane to a threshold of about −55 mV triggers an action potential: voltage-gated Na+ channels snap open, Na+ rushes in, and the potential shoots up to roughly +30 mV (depolarization). Na+ channels then inactivate while voltage-gated K+ channels open, K+ flows out, and the potential falls back down (repolarization) — usually overshooting slightly below −70 mV before the Na+–K+ pump restores RMP (a brief hyperpolarization).

Two more facts complete the picture. The all-or-none law: once threshold is reached, the action potential fires at full amplitude regardless of how much stronger the stimulus gets — there is no "partial" impulse; sub-threshold stimuli simply produce nothing. And in myelinated axons, ion channels are concentrated only at the nodes of Ranvier, since myelin insulates everywhere else, so the impulse appears to jump from node to node — saltatory conduction — which is far faster (up to roughly 150 m/s) than the continuous, node-by-node-free conduction of an unmyelinated fibre.

NEET trap

Keep the numbers and the ion straight: depolarization = Na+ influx; repolarization = K+ efflux — a frequently swapped pair. And the pump ratio is 3 Na+ out : 2 K+ in, not 1:1 or 2:3.

The synapse: handing the signal to the next cell

A neuron's axon doesn't fuse with the next cell — it hands the signal across a junction called a synapse, and human synapses come in two flavours that trade off speed for control.

FeatureElectrical synapseChemical synapse
MechanismDirect current flow through gap junctionsNeurotransmitter released across a gap
SpeedVirtually instantaneous~1 ms delay
DirectionOften bidirectionalUnidirectional
Occurrence in humansRarePredominant type

At a typical chemical synapse, the impulse reaching the presynaptic synaptic knob opens voltage-gated Ca2+ channels; the resulting Ca2+ influx triggers neurotransmitter-filled vesicles to fuse with the presynaptic membrane and release their contents into the synaptic cleft by exocytosis. The neurotransmitter diffuses across and binds receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, opening or closing ion channels there — producing either an excitatory potential (depolarizing) or an inhibitory one (hyperpolarizing), depending on the neurotransmitter and receptor. At the neuromuscular junction, that neurotransmitter is acetylcholine (ACh), which generates a graded end-plate potential and is cleared from the cleft by the enzyme acetylcholinesterase (AChE) — not by simple diffusion.

NEET trap

A chemical synapse only has vesicles on the presynaptic side and only has receptors on the postsynaptic side — which is exactly why transmission is unidirectional, unlike many electrical synapses. And don’t assume ACh is only excitatory: it excites at the neuromuscular junction, but can be inhibitory at some targets, such as the heart (where vagal ACh slows the rate).

The reflex arc: coordination without waiting for the brain

A reflex is a fast, involuntary, automatic response to a stimulus, and the pathway it travels is the reflex arc: stimulus → receptor → sensory (afferent) neuron → spinal cord (a relay, sometimes via an interneuron) → motor (efferent) neuron → effector → response.

TypeSynapsesExample
MonosynapticOne — sensory neuron synapses directly onto the motor neuron, no interneuronKnee-jerk (patellar) reflex
PolysynapticTwo or more, via at least one interneuronWithdrawal reflex (pulling a hand off something hot)
Core concept

A reflex is fast because it never routes through the brain in the first place — the spinal cord itself relays sensory input straight to a motor response, and the brain is informed of the stimulus only after the reflex has already fired. Skipping the round trip to the brain (and the conscious processing that would happen there) is the entire reason a reflex beats a voluntary response for speed.

The human brain: three regions, one integrating hub

The brain is organized into a forebrain, midbrain, and hindbrain, each handling a different scope of the coordination job.

Three protective membranes, the meninges, wrap the brain and spinal cord — the tough outer dura mater, the thin web-like middle arachnoid mater, and the delicate, closely-adherent inner pia mater. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) fills the space around and within the brain, cushioning it against shock and supplying nutrition.

NEET trap

The cerebellum coordinates and fine-tunes movement and balance — it does not initiate voluntary movement (that's the cerebral cortex). And "vital" functions — heartbeat, breathing, blood pressure — belong to the medulla oblongata, not the cerebellum or pons; damage there is life-threatening precisely because these functions aren't backed up elsewhere.

Eye and ear: two purpose-built receptor organs

Both organs do the same generic job as any receptor — convert a physical stimulus into a nerve impulse — just for light and for sound (plus, in the ear's case, for head position).

Eye

Light passes through the transparent cornea, the aperture set by the iris (the pupil), and the lens (shaped by the ciliary body's muscles), then crosses the aqueous and vitreous humours to reach the retina. The retina carries two photoreceptor types: rods, packed with the pigment rhodopsin (a vitamin A derivative), tuned for dim light and shape, not colour; and cones, tuned for bright light and colour vision. The fovea is the retina's sharpest-vision spot, packed with cones only; the blind spot (optic disc), where the optic nerve exits the eye, has no photoreceptors of either kind.

Ear

The outer ear (pinna, ear canal) funnels sound to the tympanic membrane. In the air-filled middle ear, three tiny ossicles — malleus, incus, stapes — amplify and relay those vibrations to the fluid-filled inner ear, where the coiled cochlea houses the actual hearing receptor, the organ of Corti. The inner ear also holds the body's balance sensors: the utricle and saccule (linear acceleration and head position) and the three semicircular canals (rotational movement) — hearing and balance share the same fluid-filled labyrinth but use separate receptor patches within it.

NEET trap

The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear to the throat and equalizes air pressure across the eardrum — it plays no direct role in the hearing mechanism itself, a distinction NEET likes to probe. And don’t mix up the eye's two "empty" spots: the fovea has cones only (no rods), while the blind spot has neither rods nor cones.

Chemical coordination: broadcasting instructions in the blood

A hormone is a chemical messenger secreted directly into the blood by an endocrine gland, acting on distant target cells that carry the matching receptor. Much of the endocrine system answers to one control hub: the hypothalamus, which secretes releasing and inhibiting hormones (TRH, GnRH, GHRH, CRH, somatostatin, and more) that switch the anterior pituitary's hormone output up or down. The posterior pituitary, by contrast, doesn't synthesize its own hormones at all — it simply stores and releases oxytocin and ADH, both of which are actually made in the hypothalamus.

Core concept

Hormone levels are kept in range by negative feedback: rising hormone output turns its own trigger back down. The thyroid axis is the classic example — low blood T3/T4 prompts the hypothalamus to release TRH, which prompts the pituitary to release TSH, which prompts the thyroid to release T3/T4; as T3/T4 rises, it suppresses further TRH and TSH release, closing the loop. The same three-tier logic (hypothalamus → pituitary → target gland, feeding back on itself) also runs the adrenal axis (CRH–ACTH–cortisol) and the gonadal axis (GnRH–FSH/LH–sex steroids).

NEET trap

Despite the shared name, diabetes insipidus (an ADH/water problem) and diabetes mellitus (an insulin/glucose problem) are unrelated hormonally — only DM raises blood glucose. And gigantism vs. acromegaly is entirely a question of timing: both are GH excess, but gigantism strikes before the epiphyseal growth plates close (so long bones keep lengthening) and acromegaly strikes after (so only soft tissue and small bones thicken).

Think first: when something startles you, your heart pounds almost instantly — but you can still feel shaky, with your heart still racing, two or three minutes after the danger has clearly passed. Using this chapter's speed-vs-duration framing, why are both true at once?

Both responses trace back to the same startle, but through the two different systems this chapter opened with. The instant jump in heart rate is the nervous half: a sympathetic nerve impulse reaches the heart directly within a beat or two. But that same sympathetic signal also reaches the adrenal medulla, triggering it to dump adrenaline and noradrenaline into the bloodstream — and once a hormone is circulating, it keeps binding heart-muscle receptors and sustaining that fast rate for as long as it remains in the blood, typically several minutes, since it's only removed once it's broken down or cleared, not the instant the triggering neuron falls silent. So the nervous system explains the "instantly," and the endocrine system — recruited by that same nervous signal, but running on its own slower clock — explains the "for minutes afterward."

Where this leaves you

Every mechanism in this chapter — neurons, synapses, hormones, feedback loops — exists to keep one body's trillions of cells acting as a single coordinated organism for as long as that body is alive. But no body lasts forever, and a species only continues if new bodies get built before old ones run out. Chapter 19 turns from how one body stays coordinated to how life perpetuates itself across generations at all — starting from the simplest possible strategy and building up to why sexual reproduction exists in the first place.
