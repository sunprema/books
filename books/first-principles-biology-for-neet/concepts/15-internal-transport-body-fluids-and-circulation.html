Chapter 15 · Part IV — Human Structure & Function

Internal Transport
Body Fluids and Circulation

Chapter 14 got O2 into blood and CO2 out of it — but a molecule sitting in a lung capillary is useless to a muscle cell three feet away unless something moves it there, on schedule, under pressure. This chapter is that something: a closed network of vessels, a fluid that carries far more than just gases, and a dual pump built to run two separate circuits at once without ever letting them mix. Every fact in this chapter answers one of three questions — what is being carried (blood, plasma, lymph), what does the carrying (the heart and vessels), and what keeps the carrying regular and adequate (the conducting system, the cardiac cycle, and its regulation).

NCERT mapping
Class 11 — Body Fluids and Circulation, in full: blood composition and groups, blood clotting, lymph, human circulatory system, cardiac cycle and ECG, double circulation, and disorders of the circulatory system.
NEET angle
ECG wave identification, cardiac cycle timing and heart sounds, ABO/Rh blood group logic, the exact route of double circulation, and disorder-feature matching (hypertension, coronary artery disease, angina, heart failure).

Two ideas that carry the whole chapter

The first idea is that this is a closed circulatory system — blood never leaves the vessels to bathe tissues directly (that’s an open system, like in most arthropods and molluscs). It always stays inside a continuous tube of vessels, which means the body can control exactly where it goes and at what pressure, at the cost of needing a proper pump to keep it moving. The second idea is double circulation: because the heart is really two pumps fused into one organ, blood makes two separate, non-mixing loops per full circuit — a short pulmonary loop to the lungs and back to reload on O2, and a long systemic loop out to the body and back after delivering it. Every chamber, valve, and vessel in this chapter exists to keep those two loops flowing in the right direction without letting oxygenated and deoxygenated blood cross-contaminate.

Core concept

If you can trace one drop of blood from “just returned from the body, low on O2” all the way back to “just returned from the body” again — naming every chamber, valve, and vessel in order — you have the structural skeleton of this entire chapter. Everything else (cardiac cycle, ECG, regulation, disorders) is detail layered onto that one loop.

Blood: a tissue that flows

Blood is a fluid connective tissue: a matrix (plasma, about 55% of blood volume) suspending living cells (the remaining ~45%, the formed elements). Plasma is roughly 90–92% water carrying plasma proteins (albumins for osmotic balance, globulins mostly as antibodies, fibrinogen for clotting), glucose, amino acids, lipids, hormones, and dissolved gases; plasma minus its clotting factors, once clotting has occurred, is called serum.

The formed elements are three cell types with three distinct jobs. Erythrocytes (RBCs) — about 5–5.5 million per mm3, biconcave and, in mammals, lacking a nucleus at maturity — are packed with haemoglobin and exist purely to carry O2 (and some CO2), as Chapter 14 already covered in detail. Leucocytes (WBCs) — far fewer, 6,000–8,000 per mm3, nucleated, colourless — are the defense force, and they come in five distinct types split into two structural groups: granulocytes (neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils — grainy cytoplasm, lobed nucleus) and agranulocytes (lymphocytes, monocytes — no visible granules). Platelets (thrombocytes) — 1.5–3.5 lakh per mm3, cell fragments pinched off from bone-marrow megakaryocytes, not true cells — exist to plug damaged vessels and trigger clotting.

Order neutrophils > lymphocytes > monocytes > eosinophils > basophils (mnemonic: Never Let Monkeys Eat Bananas) — the most commonly swapped pair in questions is neutrophils vs. lymphocytes as “most abundant WBC,” and it is always neutrophils.

Blood clotting: a cascade, not a switch

An injury doesn’t clot blood in one step — it triggers a cascade in which each activated clotting factor activates the next, amplifying a small initial signal into a stable plug fast enough to matter. In brief: injury exposes the wound and activates platelets, which release chemicals that (together with tissue-damage factors) convert inactive prothrombin into active thrombin (with Ca2+ and vitamin K required as cofactors at multiple steps). Thrombin then converts soluble plasma fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin threads, which mesh into a net that traps blood cells and forms the clot.

Core concept

Reduce the whole cascade to one line: injury → prothrombin → thrombin → fibrinogen → fibrin (the clot). Every named factor and cofactor in a fuller diagram is just detail feeding into these four steps.

Blood groups: antigens on the cell, antibodies in the plasma

The ABO system is defined by which antigen(s) sit on the RBC surface: group A carries antigen A (with anti-B antibody in plasma), group B carries antigen B (with anti-A antibody), group AB carries both antigens (and neither antibody), and group O carries neither antigen (and both antibodies). A transfusion is safe only when the donor’s antigens won’t be attacked by the recipient’s antibodies — which is why O is the universal donor (no antigens to react against) and AB is the universal recipient (no antibodies to do the attacking).

The Rh system adds a second, independent antigen (the Rh/D antigen): Rh+ individuals carry it, Rh− individuals don’t and, critically, don’t normally carry anti-Rh antibodies either — those only form after an Rh− person is first exposed to Rh+ blood. This matters most in pregnancy: if an Rh− mother carries an Rh+ foetus, a small leak of foetal blood (typically at delivery) can sensitize her, and in a subsequent Rh+ pregnancy her anti-Rh antibodies can cross the placenta and destroy foetal RBCs — a condition called erythroblastosis foetalis (haemolytic disease of the newborn). The first Rh+ pregnancy is normally unaffected, because sensitization hasn’t happened yet.

NEET trap

Erythroblastosis foetalis threatens the second and later Rh+ pregnancies of an Rh− mother, not the first — the first pregnancy is what sensitizes her. Also don’t confuse the logic: donor safety depends on the donor’s antigens matching what the recipient’s antibodies will tolerate, not the other way around.

Lymph: the fluid that leaks out and finds its way back

As blood passes through capillaries, plasma constantly leaks out into the surrounding tissue spaces under capillary pressure — this interstitial fluid bathes every cell directly (it, not blood, is what actually touches tissue cells). Most of it is reabsorbed back into the capillaries, but the remainder drains into a separate, blind-ended network of lymphatic vessels, where it’s called lymph: similar to plasma but with much less protein, and rich in lymphocytes. Lymph is carried through lymph nodes (which filter it and add lymphocytes/antibodies — a key immune checkpoint) and eventually rejoins the bloodstream near the base of the neck, at the junction of major veins. Functionally, lymph is a one-way drainage and return system: it returns leaked plasma and proteins to the blood, transports absorbed fats from the intestine (via lacteals, Chapter 13), and helps fight infection.

The heart: four chambers, one-way valves, one wall between the sides

The human heart sits in the thoracic cavity, enclosed in a double-walled pericardium with pericardial fluid between the layers to cushion friction, and is built almost entirely of a specialized, involuntary, striated muscle called cardiac muscle (myocardium). It has four chambers: two thin-walled atria on top that receive blood, and two thick-walled ventricles below that pump it out — the left ventricle’s wall is markedly thicker than the right’s, because it has to generate enough pressure to push blood through the entire systemic circuit, not just to the nearby lungs. A muscular interatrial septum and interventricular septum keep the right (deoxygenated) and left (oxygenated) sides permanently separate — this septal wall is the single structural feature that makes double circulation possible at all.

Blood flows one-way through four valves, each simply a mechanical check-valve that opens under forward pressure and slams shut against backflow: the tricuspid valve (right atrium → right ventricle), the bicuspid/mitral valve (left atrium → left ventricle), and two semilunar valves guarding the exits — the pulmonary semilunar valve (right ventricle → pulmonary artery) and the aortic semilunar valve (left ventricle → aorta).

The conducting system: the heart sets its own rhythm

Cardiac muscle is myogenic — it initiates its own rhythmic contraction without an external nerve signal to start each beat (nerves only speed it up or slow it down, see Regulation below). That rhythm originates in the sinoatrial (SA) node, a patch of specialized nodal tissue in the wall of the right atrium that generates action potentials faster than any other cardiac tissue, earning it the name pacemaker. The signal spreads across both atria, triggering atrial systole, and reaches the atrioventricular (AV) node at the base of the right atrium, which deliberately delays the impulse briefly before passing it on — that delay is what lets the atria finish emptying into the ventricles before the ventricles contract. From the AV node the signal races down the bundle of His through the interventricular septum and out through the Purkinje fibres, spreading rapidly through both ventricle walls so they contract together, from the bottom up, squeezing blood efficiently out through the semilunar valves.

Core concept

SA node → AV node (delay) → bundle of His → Purkinje fibres is the entire conducting pathway. The AV delay isn’t a flaw to explain away — it’s the mechanism that sequences atrial systole before ventricular systole.

The cardiac cycle: one heartbeat, ~0.8 seconds

A cardiac cycle is one complete sequence of contraction (systole) and relaxation (diastole) through all four chambers, and at a resting rate of about 72 beats/min it takes roughly 0.8 seconds in total. It breaks into three named phases: joint diastole (~0.4 s) — both atria and both ventricles are relaxed together, and blood flows passively from the veins straight through the open AV valves into the ventricles, filling them to about 70% of their final volume before any active contraction happens; atrial systole (~0.1 s) — the SA node fires, the atria contract and push in the remaining ~30%; and ventricular systole (~0.3 s) — the AV valves snap shut (preventing backflow into the atria), pressure builds inside the ventricles until it exceeds the pressure in the aorta/pulmonary artery, the semilunar valves are forced open, and blood is ejected out. Then the whole cycle relaxes back into joint diastole and repeats.

The familiar “lub-dub” is the sound of valves slamming shut, not opening. Lub (first heart sound) is the AV valves (tricuspid + bicuspid) closing at the start of ventricular systole; dub (second heart sound) is the semilunar valves closing at the start of the next joint diastole, once ventricular pressure has fallen below arterial pressure and blood momentarily tries to flow backward before the valve seals it off.

NEET trap

Ventricles are mostly filled passively during diastole, before atrial systole even happens (~70% passive, ~30% from atrial contraction) — “the atria pump most of the blood into the ventricles” is a common wrong statement. Also, the two heart sounds mark valve closure, never valve opening.

ECG: the heart’s electrical signature, read on paper

An electrocardiogram (ECG) records the electrical activity generated as the SA node’s impulse spreads through the heart, picked up by electrodes on the skin. A normal trace repeats a fixed pattern of waves, each corresponding to a specific electrical event you can map directly onto the conducting-system pathway above: the P wave is atrial depolarization (the electrical trigger just before atrial systole); the QRS complex is ventricular depolarization (the trigger just before ventricular systole — it’s large because the ventricles have far more muscle mass than the atria, and it also masks atrial repolarization, which happens at the same time but produces too small a signal to see separately); and the T wave is ventricular repolarization, as the ventricles electrically reset for the next cycle.

NEET trap

Match each wave to its event exactly: P = atrial depolarization, QRS = ventricular depolarization, T = ventricular repolarization. There is no separate visible wave for atrial repolarization — a question asking “which wave represents atrial repolarization” is testing whether you know it’s hidden inside QRS, not asking you to name a wave that doesn’t exist on the trace.

Cardiac output: the pump’s throughput

Cardiac output is the volume of blood pumped by one ventricle in one minute, and it reduces to a single multiplication: cardiac output = stroke volume × heart rate. Stroke volume — the volume ejected per beat — averages about 70 mL; heart rate at rest averages about 72 beats/min; so resting cardiac output is roughly 70 × 72 ≈ 5,000 mL (5 L) per minute — which, not coincidentally, is approximately the body’s entire blood volume, meaning essentially all of your blood is recirculated once every minute at rest. During exercise, both stroke volume and heart rate rise, and cardiac output can increase several-fold above this resting value.

Core concept

Cardiac output is just stroke volume × heart rate — the same “how much per stroke times how many strokes” logic as any pump. Anything that changes either factor (blood volume, venous return, sympathetic stimulation, fitness level) changes cardiac output through this one equation.

Double circulation: two loops, one pump, no mixing

Trace the full loop starting from deoxygenated blood returning from the body: it enters the right atrium via the superior and inferior venae cavae, passes through the tricuspid valve into the right ventricle, and is pumped out through the pulmonary semilunar valve into the pulmonary artery — the only artery in the body that carries deoxygenated blood, because “artery” is defined by direction (away from the heart), not oxygen content. This is the short pulmonary circuit: pulmonary artery → lung capillaries (gas exchange, as in Chapter 14) → pulmonary veins (the only veins carrying oxygenated blood, for the same directional reason) → left atrium.

From the left atrium, blood passes through the bicuspid valve into the left ventricle, and is pumped out through the aortic semilunar valve into the aorta — starting the long systemic circuit: aorta → progressively smaller arteries → arterioles → capillary beds throughout every tissue (delivering O2 and nutrients, collecting CO2 and waste) → venules → veins → venae cavae → back to the right atrium, closing the loop. Both circuits run continuously and simultaneously off the same two-sided pump, which is exactly why the septum matters: any hole in it (a common congenital defect) lets oxygenated and deoxygenated blood mix, reducing the efficiency of O2 delivery to the body.

NEET trap

“Arteries carry oxygenated blood, veins carry deoxygenated blood” is only true for the systemic circuit. The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood and the pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood — the artery/vein label always tracks direction relative to the heart (away/toward), never oxygen content.

Regulation of cardiac activity: myogenic, but adjustable

Because the heartbeat is myogenic, the nervous system doesn’t initiate each beat — it only tunes the SA node’s intrinsic rate. A cardiac centre in the medulla oblongata sends two opposing signals: the sympathetic nervous system releases noradrenaline, which speeds the SA node up and increases contraction force (useful in stress, exercise, or falling blood pressure); the parasympathetic supply, via the vagus nerve, releases acetylcholine, which slows the SA node down (useful when blood pressure is already high, or at rest). Hormonally, adrenaline from the adrenal medulla reinforces the sympathetic effect, increasing both rate and force.

NEET trap

“Myogenic” does not mean unregulated — it only means the heart initiates its own beat without needing a nerve signal to start each contraction. The autonomic nervous system still actively speeds it up (sympathetic) or slows it down (parasympathetic/vagal) on top of that intrinsic rhythm.

Blood pressure: the force behind the flow

Blood pressure is the force blood exerts against vessel walls, normally measured in the brachial artery with a sphygmomanometer and expressed as two numbers: systolic pressure (~120 mmHg, the peak pressure during ventricular systole/ejection) over diastolic pressure (~80 mmHg, the pressure that remains during diastole, sustained by the arterial walls’ elastic recoil). A resting reading of roughly 120/80 mmHg is taken as normal for a healthy adult.

When the system breaks

Hypertension is chronically elevated blood pressure, typically defined at or above 140/90 mmHg — a persistent, symptomless overload on vessel walls and the heart’s own workload that is a major risk factor for the disorders below. Coronary artery disease (atherosclerosis) is the narrowing of the coronary arteries (the vessels feeding the heart muscle itself) by deposits of fat, cholesterol, and fibrous tissue along the artery wall, restricting blood flow to the myocardium. When that restriction is enough to starve heart muscle of O2 under exertion, the resulting chest pain is angina; if a coronary vessel is blocked outright, the starved muscle tissue dies — a myocardial infarction (heart attack). Heart failure (congestive heart failure) is a distinct condition: the heart, weakened, can no longer pump with enough force to meet the body’s circulatory demand — note this is a chronic pumping insufficiency, not the sudden stoppage implied by the popular (and NEET-tested) misuse of “heart attack” as a synonym for it.

Think first: a patient’s ECG shows a normal P wave and a normal QRS complex, but the gap between the P wave and the QRS complex (the P–R interval) is much longer than usual. Which part of the conducting pathway does that extra delay point to, and why does a longer-than-normal delay there specifically produce this pattern rather than an abnormal wave shape?

It points to the AV node. The P wave marks atrial depolarization and the QRS complex marks ventricular depolarization, and the gap between them is exactly the time the AV node spends deliberately delaying the impulse before releasing it down the bundle of His. If that delay is abnormally prolonged — a condition generally called a conduction block at the AV node — both waves still look individually normal, because the SA node is still firing correctly and the ventricles still depolarize correctly once the signal arrives; only the timing between the two events is disturbed. That’s the general signature of an AV-node problem on an ECG: normal wave shapes, abnormal spacing — as opposed to a problem inside the ventricles themselves, which would distort the shape of the QRS complex directly.

Where this leaves you

Blood is now reliably reaching every tissue, carrying O2, nutrients, hormones, and defense cells, and reliably returning with CO2 and metabolic waste in tow — but that waste still has to leave the body altogether, and the composition of blood itself (its water, its ion balance, its volume) has to be kept within tight limits or none of this plumbing means anything. Chapter 16 opens the filter at the end of the line: the kidney, and the excretory system that removes nitrogenous waste while conserving exactly the water and ions the body has worked this hard to keep circulating.
