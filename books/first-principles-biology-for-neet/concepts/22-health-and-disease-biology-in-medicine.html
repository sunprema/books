Chapter 22 · Part VI — Applications: Health, Food & Biotechnology

Health and Disease
– Biology in Medicine

Chapter 21 closed by turning from "is this system working as designed" to a broader question: what happens when the body is invaded, or malfunctions on its own, and how does it respond? This chapter answers that in two halves. The first half is defense — the layered system of barriers, cells, and memory that keeps most pathogens from ever becoming disease, and the handful of ways that system itself can misfire (allergy, autoimmunity) or be dismantled from the inside (HIV/AIDS). The second half is malfunction from within — cancer, where a cell's own growth controls fail, and substance abuse, where a normal signalling system (the brain's reward pathway) is hijacked by an external chemical. Different causes, but the same underlying question runs through all of it: what is this system built to do when everything is working, and what specifically breaks when it doesn't?

Health is a dynamic equilibrium, not an absence of germs

The WHO definition NCERT opens this chapter with is worth taking literally: health is "a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being," not merely the absence of disease. That framing matters because it sets up the chapter's real organizing idea — a healthy body isn't a body that has never encountered a pathogen, it's a body whose defenses are actively, continuously winning a contest it never fully stops fighting. Disease is simply what it looks like when that contest is lost, whether the attacker is external (a pathogen) or the system malfunctions on its own (cancer, an autoimmune reaction, an addiction).

Core concept

Every disease in this chapter fits one of two categories, and the category tells you what kind of biology to expect. Infectious (communicable) diseases are caused by pathogens — bacteria, viruses, protozoa, helminths, fungi — that can spread person to person or via a vector; the biology is about the pathogen's own life cycle and how the immune system recognizes and clears it. Non-infectious (non-communicable) diseases — cancer, allergies, addiction — aren't caused by a transmissible agent at all; the biology is about a normal process (cell division, immune signalling, neurotransmission) failing internally. Keep asking "is something being transmitted here, or is something malfunctioning?" and the chapter's four major topics — pathogens, immunity, cancer, drug abuse — sort themselves cleanly.

Common diseases: five kinds of pathogen, one recurring pattern

NCERT's list of common infectious diseases spans five very different kinds of organism, but each disease is defined by the same three facts worth learning as a set: the causative agent, the route of transmission, and the site or mechanism of damage. Get those three straight for each disease and the symptom list becomes a logical consequence rather than a separate fact to memorize.

Keep the causative agent separate from the vector: Plasmodium causes malaria, the female Anopheles mosquito only transmits it; Wuchereria causes filariasis, the female Culex mosquito only transmits it. A mosquito is never the pathogen in these questions. Also, "amoebiasis" and "ascariasis" are commonly swapped in symptom questions — amoebiasis gives mucus-and-blood-streaked stools (protozoan, large intestine), while ascariasis gives anaemia and possible intestinal blockage from a heavy worm load (helminth).

Innate immunity: defenses you were born with, not built

Every one of those pathogens has to get past the body's defenses before it can cause disease, and the first layer, innate immunity, is present from birth and acts the same way against every invader — non-specific, immediate, with no memory of past encounters. NCERT organizes it into four barrier types that act in sequence, from outside in.

Innate immunity buys time and clears most everyday exposures on its own. But it treats every pathogen identically, remembers nothing, and can't sharpen its response on a second exposure — which is exactly the gap the second, slower layer of defense is built to close.

Acquired immunity: specific, and it remembers

Acquired (adaptive) immunity is pathogen-specific and improves with each exposure — the property that makes vaccination possible at all. It has two coordinated arms, both run by lymphocytes. Humoral immunity is mediated by B-lymphocytes, which differentiate into plasma cells that secrete antibodies into blood and lymph to neutralize pathogens circulating outside cells. Cell-mediated immunity is mediated by T-lymphocytes, which directly recognize and destroy the body's own cells once those cells are infected — a job antibodies can't do, since antibodies can't reach a pathogen already hiding inside a host cell.

Core concept

The first time a pathogen is encountered, acquired immunity is slow — it takes days to select and expand the right B- and T-cells (the primary response). But that encounter leaves behind long-lived memory cells, so a second exposure to the same pathogen triggers a much faster, larger secondary response that can clear the infection before it ever produces symptoms. Vaccination exploits exactly this asymmetry: it deliberately triggers a harmless primary response (using a weakened, killed, or partial pathogen) so that memory cells are already standing by the first time the real pathogen ever shows up.

Active vs. passive immunity, and where vaccines fit

The distinction NEET tests most on this topic is whether your own body makes the antibodies, or whether it receives someone else's.

NEET trap

A vaccine is always active immunization, even though the antigen it contains is external — the antibodies that actually fight the disease are made by the vaccinated person's own B-cells. Don't confuse "the antigen came from outside" with "the antibody came from outside"; only the second one makes something passive. Colostrum and anti-venom, by contrast, deliver finished antibodies directly, so they're passive and wear off.

Lymphoid organs: where lymphocytes are made, and where they get to work

Lymphocytes need to be produced, matured, and then stationed somewhere they'll actually meet an antigen — and different organs handle each job. Primary lymphoid organs — bone marrow and the thymus — are where lymphocytes are produced and differentiate into antigen-sensitive cells; B-cells mature in the bone marrow, T-cells mature in the thymus (a gland that is large in a newborn but progressively shrinks with age). Secondary lymphoid organs — the spleen, lymph nodes, tonsils, Peyer's patches of the small intestine, and the appendix — are where mature lymphocytes actually encounter antigen and mount a response; lymph nodes in particular filter antigens out of the lymph as it drains through them, which is why lymph nodes swell during an infection nearby.

When the defense system itself misfires

An allergy is an exaggerated immune response to substances that are ordinarily harmless — allergens like dust, pollen, mites, or animal dander. The body produces IgE antibodies against the allergen; these bind to mast cells, and re-exposure to the same allergen triggers the mast cells to release histamine and serotonin, the chemical mediators actually responsible for sneezing, watery eyes, a runny nose, and breathing difficulty. Anti-allergic drugs — antihistamines, adrenaline, steroids — work by blocking or counteracting this chemical release, not the underlying antibody reaction.

In autoimmunity, acquired immunity loses the ability to distinguish self from non-self and attacks the body's own tissue as if it were foreign (rheumatoid arthritis is a commonly cited example). It's the same recognition machinery that fights pathogens, misdirected against the person it's supposed to protect.

AIDS: a virus that disables the defenders themselves

AIDS (Acquired Immuno Deficiency Syndrome) is caused by HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus), a retrovirus — its genome is RNA, and it copies that RNA into DNA using its own enzyme, running the central dogma in reverse. It spreads through sexual contact with an infected person (the most common route), transfusion of contaminated blood or blood products, sharing needles/syringes (notably among intravenous drug users), and from an infected mother to her child via the placenta. It is not transmitted by casual contact, sharing food, or insect bites.

Once inside the body, HIV first infects macrophages, where reverse transcriptase copies its RNA genome into viral DNA; this viral DNA is incorporated into the host cell's own DNA and directs the infected cell to produce more virus particles — macrophages effectively become a factory continuously releasing HIV. The virus then goes on to infect helper T-lymphocytes (CD4+ cells) in the same way, replicating and steadily killing them off. Because helper T-cells coordinate both the humoral and cell-mediated arms of acquired immunity, their progressive loss causes the immune system to collapse from the inside — leaving the person unable to fight off infections a healthy immune system would clear easily (opportunistic infections) and vulnerable to otherwise rare cancers such as Kaposi's sarcoma. ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immuno-Sorbent Assay), which detects antibodies against HIV in the blood, is the standard diagnostic test.

NEET trap

A person can carry HIV and test positive for years before AIDS symptoms appear — AIDS is the late-stage collapse of immunity after helper T-cell numbers have fallen substantially, not the moment of infection itself. Also, HIV replicates in macrophages first, then helper T-cells — not the reverse — a sequencing detail that shows up directly in "order the events" style questions.

Cancer: when the brakes on cell division fail

A normal cell divides in a controlled way and stops when it contacts neighbouring cells — a property called contact inhibition. Cancer cells lose this restraint entirely, dividing continuously to form a mass of new, often abnormal, cells — a neoplasm (tumour). A benign tumour stays localized, made of well-differentiated cells, and does relatively little harm as long as it stays contained; a malignant tumour is made of rapidly, abnormally dividing cells that invade and damage surrounding normal tissue and can spread — via blood or lymph — to distant sites in the body, a process called metastasis. Metastasis, not the primary tumour by itself, is what makes malignant cancers so dangerous and hard to treat.

Core concept

Every normal cell already carries proto-oncogenes — ordinary genes involved in regulating cell growth and division. Cancer isn't caused by some foreign "cancer gene" arriving from outside; it happens when a proto-oncogene is activated (by a carcinogen, radiation, or certain oncogenic viruses) into a cancer-causing oncogene, or when a tumour-suppressor gene that would normally halt abnormal division is itself damaged. The cell's own growth-control machinery is what breaks — cancer is a malfunction of the cell's internal regulation, not an invasion.

Treatment is matched to the tumour's nature and stage, and often combines more than one approach: surgery to physically remove the tumour, radiotherapy (lethal irradiation of the tumour while sparing surrounding normal tissue as much as possible), chemotherapy (anti-tumour drugs, which come with side effects like hair loss and anaemia because they also affect other rapidly dividing normal cells), and immunotherapy, which uses biological response modifiers such as alpha-interferon to boost the immune system's own ability to destroy tumour cells.

NEET trap

"Benign vs. malignant" questions almost always hinge on one word: metastasis. A tumour that stays put, however large, is benign; the defining feature of malignant is that it spreads. Don't confuse chemotherapy's side effects (hair loss, anaemia) with a failure of the drug — they happen precisely because chemotherapy drugs can't distinguish a cancer cell's rapid division from a normal rapidly-dividing cell's (e.g. hair follicles, bone marrow).

Drug and alcohol abuse: a normal reward system, hijacked

Several commonly abused substances act on receptors the nervous system already has for its own signalling molecules — which is precisely why they're addictive. Opioids are obtained from the poppy plant, Papaver somniferum, and act on specific opioid receptors in the central nervous system and gut; morphine is used medically to control pain, while heroin ("smack"), a chemically modified form, is illegal, generally taken by sniffing, smoking, or injecting, and acts as a depressant, slowing down body functions. Cannabinoids act on cannabinoid receptors in the brain and affect the cardiovascular system; they're obtained from the inflorescence of Cannabis sativa — marijuana, hashish, charas, and ganja — and are typically inhaled or ingested orally. Cocaine ("coke," "crack") is an alkaloid from the coca plant, Erythroxylum coca; it interferes with the transport of the neurotransmitter dopamine, is usually snorted, and acts as a strong CNS stimulant, producing a temporary euphoria and burst of energy.

Alcohol and tobacco carry their own well-documented harms: chronic tobacco chewing is linked to cancers of the oral cavity, smoking to cancers of the lung, urinary bladder, and throat, along with emphysema, bronchitis, and coronary heart disease. Alcohol abuse damages the liver and is linked to gastric ulcers among other effects.

NCERT frames why young people fall into substance abuse in largely social and psychological terms, not purely pharmacological ones: curiosity and a need for adventure or experimentation; a perceived need to boost academic or sports performance; an attempt to escape unpleasant, stressful, or depressive states; peer pressure; and an unstable or dysfunctional family environment. Warning signs worth recognizing include a sudden drop in academic performance, unexplained financial demands, withdrawal from family and usual friend groups, changes in sleeping and eating habits, and a decline in personal appearance and hygiene. The recommended response is consistently framed around support rather than punishment — open conversation with parents and peers, avoiding undue peer pressure, and seeking help from qualified counsellors or psychiatrists when warning signs appear.

NEET trap

Source-plant questions are a reliable NEET pattern: Papaver somniferum → opioids (morphine/heroin), Cannabis sativa → cannabinoids, Erythroxylum coca → cocaine. Also note that opioids and cannabinoids act on receptors already present in the human CNS for the body's own signalling molecules — these drugs aren't introducing an entirely foreign mechanism, they're overstimulating one that already exists, which is part of why tolerance and dependence develop.

Think first: innate immunity is fast but non-specific and has no memory; acquired immunity is specific and has memory, but takes days to mount on first exposure. Vaccination clearly exploits acquired immunity's memory. So why does a body still need innate immunity at all, once it has a working acquired immune system?

Because acquired immunity's biggest advantage — a fast, powerful secondary response — is only available after the first exposure has already happened, and that first exposure is exactly when a new or unfamiliar pathogen is most dangerous: there are no memory cells yet, and building a primary response from scratch takes days. In that gap, something has to slow the pathogen down or clear it outright, and that's innate immunity's entire job — physical barriers, physiological barriers, phagocytic cells, and interferons acting within minutes to hours, buying the days that acquired immunity needs to select the right B- and T-cells and expand them into an effective response. It's the same "fast-but-crude, slow-but-precise" division of labour that shows up elsewhere in the body — a reflex arc's fast, local response buying time for a slower, deliberate cortical one (Chapter 18), or a corpus luteum's stopgap progesterone buying time for the placenta to mature (Chapter 21). Innate immunity isn't a weaker version of acquired immunity; it's the layer that has to hold the line while acquired immunity is still being built, every single time a genuinely new pathogen shows up.

Where this leaves you

The pattern across this whole chapter is a system built in layers, each one covering for the last one's blind spot: innate immunity is fast but blind to specifics, acquired immunity is specific but slow to start, and memory cells make the second encounter fast and specific — which is the exact gap vaccination is engineered to exploit. HIV is dangerous precisely because it doesn't just evade this system, it disables the helper T-cells that coordinate it, and cancer is dangerous because it isn't an outside attacker at all, just a cell's own growth controls failing from within. Drug abuse closes the chapter on a different note — not a defense system breaking, but a normal signalling system being deliberately overridden. Chapter 23 turns the lens around: instead of pathogenic microbes to defend against, it looks at the far larger world of microbes humans have learned to put to work — in food, in industry, in the environment — the same organisms this chapter treated mostly as threats, now treated as tools.
