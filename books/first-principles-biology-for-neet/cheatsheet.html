The Quick Reference

Everything in this book was built so the reasoning would stick without memorizing — this page is the one place that breaks that rule on purpose. Four dense tables and a curated trap list, for the final week before the exam when you need recall, not re-derivation.

1 · Hormones & Glands

Plant hormones act without dedicated glands — they're made wherever they're needed and act locally or travel through the vascular system. Human hormones are gland-specific. Same underlying logic (a signal → a receptor → a response), two very different delivery systems.

4 · Common NEET Traps

The twenty traps below are the ones most worth having cold — distilled from the NEET-angle callouts across all 28 chapters, not an exhaustive dump. Each still assumes you understand the mechanism behind it; that's what the rest of this book is for.

Highest-value exam traps
  1. Ch.1 — Mitochondria & chloroplasts carry their own 70S ribosomes and circular DNA, unlike the 80S ribosomes elsewhere in the eukaryotic cell — the core evidence for the endosymbiotic theory.
  2. Ch.2 — “Which of these is not a protein” bait: chlorophyll (pigment), thyroxine (amino-acid derivative), steroid hormones (lipids), starch/cellulose (carbohydrates) — none are proteins despite sounding biological.
  3. Ch.4 — RNA polymerase assignment is a favourite swap: Pol I→rRNA, Pol II→mRNA, Pol III→tRNA.
  4. Ch.5 — “Dominant” does not mean common in the population — achondroplasia and Huntington's disease are dominant yet rare.
  5. Ch.6 — Sickle-cell anaemia is a qualitative Hb defect (abnormal structure); thalassemia is a quantitative defect (reduced/absent synthesis). Don't blur the two.
  6. Ch.7 — Hardy–Weinberg: take √ only from the homozygous-recessive frequency (q2), then compute p = 1 − q before plugging into 2pq.
  7. Ch.9 — Water moves toward the more negative water potential, not the larger magnitude — the sign, not the size, decides direction.
  8. Ch.10 — Cyclic photophosphorylation makes ATP only — no O2, no NADPH, because it never involves PSII or water-splitting.
  9. Ch.11 — A “short-day plant” is really a long-night plant — night-break experiments prove uninterrupted darkness, not day length, is what's actually measured.
  10. Ch.13 — Bile contains zero digestive enzymes — it only emulsifies fat; pancreatic/intestinal lipase does the actual hydrolysis.
  11. Ch.14 — Breathing rate is driven primarily by rising CO2/H+, not by falling O2 — the intuitive “low oxygen” answer is the trap.
  12. Ch.15 — “Arteries carry oxygenated blood” is true only for the systemic circuit — the pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood.
  13. Ch.16 — ADH controls water permeability directly; aldosterone controls Na+ reabsorption, with water following only as a secondary consequence. Don't swap them.
  14. Ch.17 — Rigor mortis is muscle stiffening from an ATP shortage that leaves myosin heads unable to detach — not a sign of relaxation.
  15. Ch.18 — Diabetes insipidus (an ADH/water problem) and diabetes mellitus (an insulin/glucose problem) are hormonally unrelated — only DM raises blood glucose.
  16. Ch.20 — Double fertilization uses one sperm cell per fusion — triple fusion is the second sperm plus both polar nuclei, three haploid nuclei in one step (2n embryo, 3n endosperm).
  17. Ch.22 — A vaccine is always active immunization — the antigen came from outside, but the antibodies are made by the vaccinated person's own B-cells.
  18. Ch.23 — Low BOD = clean water. A high BOD number means more unconsumed organic matter, i.e. more pollution — students commonly flip this.
  19. Ch.26 — For Plasmodium, the mosquito is the definitive (primary) host — sexual reproduction happens there; the human is only the intermediate host.
  20. Ch.27 — Only the pyramid of energy is guaranteed upright in every ecosystem — pyramids of numbers and biomass can each invert.

5 · 30-Second Summary, by Part

Part I — Foundations (Ch. 1–3)

Life is self-sustained metabolism housed inside a cell, prokaryotic or eukaryotic. Biomolecules are functional polymers; enzymes lower activation energy without being consumed by the reaction. Energy only flows one way (2nd law) while matter cycles, and ATP is the coupling currency between exergonic and endergonic reactions.

Part II — Information Flow & Genetics (Ch. 4–7)

DNA→RNA→Protein is the central dogma, copied semi-conservatively and read through a universal, degenerate codon table. Mendel's laws explain inheritance probabilistically; linkage and the chromosomal theory refine it. Genes aren't always “on” — operons and differential expression shape phenotype. Evolution is a population's allele frequencies changing under variation, selection, and time, testable via Hardy–Weinberg.

Part III — Plant Structure & Function (Ch. 8–11)

Meristematic and permanent tissues build root/stem/leaf anatomy optimized for light capture, gas exchange, and transport. Water moves down a water-potential gradient (osmosis, cohesion-tension, transpiration pull). Photosynthesis (light reaction + Calvin cycle, with C3/C4/CAM variants) and respiration (glycolysis→Krebs→ETS) are chemiosmotic mirror images. Five core hormones plus photoperiod/vernalization coordinate growth.

Part IV — Human Structure & Function (Ch. 12–18)

Cells→tissues→organs→systems, held in balance mostly by negative feedback. Digestion hydrolyzes food via a site-specific enzyme cascade; breathing and circulation move O2/CO2 and nutrients along partial-pressure and blood-pressure gradients; the kidney filters, reabsorbs, and secretes to balance water, ions, and waste. Muscles contract via sliding filaments; neurons (fast) and endocrine glands (slower) integrate all of it.

Part V — Reproduction & Continuity (Ch. 19–21)

Asexual reproduction clones a genome fast; sexual reproduction trades speed for variation via meiosis and fertilization. Flowering plants use double fertilization — one sperm to the egg (2n embryo), one plus two polar nuclei in triple fusion (3n endosperm). Human reproduction runs on cyclical hormonal control, and reproductive health covers contraception, STIs, and assisted reproductive technologies.

Part VI — Health, Food & Biotechnology (Ch. 22–25)

Disease is either an outside pathogen (matched to a specific agent, vector, and symptom) or an internal malfunction (cancer, allergy, addiction). Layered innate barriers sit under adaptive B-/T-cell memory, which vaccines exploit. Microbes are put to work as fermenters, drug factories, and decomposers. Selective breeding and rDNA technology (restriction enzymes, vectors, PCR) engineer better yield, nutrition, and disease resistance.

Part VII — Ecology (Ch. 26–28)

Organisms adapt to their habitat and niche; population growth is checked by resources (the logistic model) and shaped by interactions like competition, predation, and symbiosis. Ecosystems cycle matter but lose energy at every trophic transfer — only the energy pyramid is always upright. Biodiversity underwrites ecosystem stability, and habitat loss/fragmentation is its single biggest threat.
