Chapter 04 · Interconnects

Interconnects Are the New Bus

Old-school computer architecture drew "the bus" as one wire connecting the CPU to memory. That picture is hopelessly out of date. Today there isn't one bus — there's a whole nested set of them, one per distance you have to travel, and each one is a different, fiercely-competitive engineering problem with its own standards body. Distance is destiny in this chapter: the further your data has to go, the more it costs you, in ways that aren't just about raw throughput.

I used to think a "fast connection" was just a bigger bandwidth number. Turns out, once you're moving data between chips 30 times a second, the question that matters more is: how long does the round trip take? That's a different problem than bandwidth, and it's the one that decides whether your GPU cluster feels snappy or sluggish.

PCIe keeps doubling, and it's still not enough

PCIe is still the industry's general-purpose expansion bus, and it keeps doubling its raw signaling rate every generation — Gen6 reaches 64 GT/s (adding low-latency forward error correction to keep the link reliable at that speed), and Gen7 pushes to roughly 128 GT/s, with both generations landing in datacenters around 2025–2026. That sounds like progress, and it is — but remember Chapter 3's math: even a doubled PCIe link is nowhere near HBM's on-package bandwidth, or the purpose-built GPU-to-GPU links described next. PCIe remains the road everyone has to use for general expansion, which is exactly why it keeps showing up as the bottleneck in KV-cache offload and multi-GPU scaling.

NVLink: bandwidth built specifically for GPU-to-GPU

NVIDIA's answer, for the links that matter most in an AI cluster, is to not use PCIe at all where it can avoid it. The sixth-generation NVLink, built for the Rubin platform, delivers 3.6 TB/s of bandwidth per GPU — over 14× a PCIe Gen6 link — and NVLink Switch fabrics let that bandwidth scale out to dozens of GPUs behaving, for many purposes, like one enormous accelerator. The same philosophy shows up one level down: the NVLink-C2C link fusing a Grace CPU to a Hopper or Blackwell GPU runs at 900 GB/s — the very link that makes CPU-side KV-cache offload (Chapter 3) tolerable instead of ruinous.

UCIe: doing for chiplets what NVLink does for GPUs

One layer further in — connecting the chiplets inside a single package from Chapter 1 — there's a newer standard called UCIe (Universal Chiplet Interconnect Express), backed by Intel, AMD, Arm, Qualcomm, TSMC, and most of the rest of the industry. UCIe is built for the shortest possible hop: it targets bandwidth density above 10 Tbps per millimeter of die edge at roughly 0.3 picojoules per bit — astonishingly efficient, because the wires are only millimeters long instead of centimeters. Its real significance is economic as much as technical: because it's an open standard, a chiplet from one vendor can talk to a chiplet from a different vendor inside the same package — exactly the "mix and match" flexibility that makes the heterogeneous CPUs of Chapter 1 possible at all. The roadmap already points toward 1.6 Tbps-class I/O chiplets built specifically to this standard.

Why latency beats raw bandwidth for distributed inference

Here's the subtlety that catches people who only look at the big TB/s numbers: decode (Chapter 3) doesn't move one giant file once — it does thousands of small synchronizations, one per generated token. Splitting a big model across several GPUs (tensor parallelism) means those GPUs must exchange partial results and agree on an answer every single token; serving with separate prefill and decode pools means one machine has to fetch another machine's KV cache on every request. In that world, the number that actually determines how snappy the system feels isn't peak bandwidth — it's round-trip latency, because you're paying that latency tax over and over, thousands of times per response. That's why the industry keeps shortening the physical and logical distance data has to travel: UCIe inside the package, NVLink inside the node, and increasingly exotic scale-out fabrics between nodes — each one exists to buy back a few more microseconds per hop.

Rule of thumb On-die > on-package (UCIe) > on-node (NVLink) > on-rack (PCIe/network) — every step outward costs you roughly an order of magnitude of bandwidth and adds real, cumulative latency. Keep frequently-synchronized work as close to the center of that diagram as your budget allows.

Why this matters

The wire is no longer a boring commodity part. UCIe, NVLink, and PCIe are now first-class architecture decisions, argued over in standards bodies with the same intensity as instruction sets used to get. The next chapter is about the four big structural bets the industry is placing to push past all of this.
