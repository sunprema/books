Chapter 01 · Silicon

The Modern CPU Isn't What You Think

Pop open a 2026 server and pull out what the spec sheet still calls "the CPU." It is not one chip. It's a small city: several separate pieces of silicon, built on different manufacturing processes, wired together on a shared substrate, doing their best impression of a single processor. That's not a compromise — it's the plan. Let me show you why this actually matters.

I still remember when "the chip" meant one continuous piece of silicon you could photograph and point at. These days when I ask an engineer to show me "the CPU," they open the lid of a package and show me a grid of chips. That single fact explains half of what's in this book.

One die became many

Building one giant chip has a brutal problem: yield. The bigger the die, the more likely a random speck of dust ruins it, and the cost of a defect scales with the area around it. So the industry did something simple and clever — it broke the die into pieces, called chiplets, and reassembled them inside one package using dense on-substrate wiring. AMD's EPYC line has done this for years: a handful of identical compute chiplets (CCDs), each with its own cores and cache, surrounding a central I/O die (IOD) that handles memory channels and interconnects. Intel's Xeon 6 goes further — it lets you mix compute tiles built on its leading-edge Intel 3 process with I/O tiles left on the older, cheaper Intel 7 process, because I/O circuitry doesn't benefit much from the newest node anyway. Why pay leading-edge prices for a part of the chip that isn't leading-edge work?

P-cores and E-cores share a socket

The second surprise: not every core on the chip is the same kind of core. Intel's Xeon 6 platform ships both "Performance" tiles (Granite Rapids, big out-of-order cores tuned for single-thread speed) and "Efficiency" tiles (Sierra Forest, and its 2026 successor Clearwater Forest, small dense cores tuned for throughput per watt per dollar). The flagship Clearwater Forest part packs 288 Darkmont E-cores and 576 MB of L3 cache on a single socket — and a two-socket box takes that to 576 cores. AMD's answer for 2026, EPYC "Venice," goes a different route: up to 256 uniform Zen 6 cores on TSMC's 2 nm process, with a headline number that matters more than the core count — 1.6 TB/s of per-socket memory bandwidth, more than double the 614 GB/s of the previous EPYC "Turin" generation. Notice what both companies are actually competing on now: it isn't clock speed. It's how many useful operations you can retire per watt, per dollar, per byte you can actually feed the cores.

Stacking up, not just out

The newest trick doesn't spread chiplets sideways — it stacks them vertically. AMD's 3D V-Cache technology bonds an extra slab of cache die directly on top of a compute chiplet, connected by thousands of microscopic through-silicon vias (TSVs) drilled straight down through the silicon. The payoff has been dramatic: the Genoa-X generation shipped up to 1,152 MB of L3 cache per socket this way — cache capacity that would have needed far more die area (and cost) if laid out flat. Stacking silicon like pancakes, rather than spreading it like a subdivision, is quickly becoming the default move for anyone trying to keep more data close to the cores — and as you'll see in Chapter 5, the same trick is now being tried on memory dies themselves, with logic stacked underneath.

Key idea A "CPU" in 2026 is a packaging decision as much as a design decision. Chiplets let architects mix process nodes for cost, mix core types for the workload, and stack dies vertically for capacity — three knobs a single monolithic die never had.

Why this matters

Every trick in this chapter — disaggregation, heterogeneous cores, vertical stacking — exists to solve one problem: getting more useful bytes close to a core without paying full leading-edge-silicon prices for every square millimeter. That's the same problem the rest of this book is about. The CPU didn't get complicated for its own sake; it got complicated because compute got cheap and data movement got expensive, and packaging is where architects fight back.
