Chapter 03 · Inference

LLM Inference Exposes the Bottleneck

If chapters 1 and 2 sounded abstract, here's the workload that made everyone care: talking to a chatbot. Every time it prints the next word of its answer, it's doing a tiny amount of arithmetic and a huge amount of memory traffic. LLM inference didn't invent the memory wall — it just walked straight into it, in front of a billion daily users, which is why every hardware roadmap in this book is currently obsessed with it.

Here's the thing that surprised me most researching this: the "thinking" part of a chatbot — reading your prompt — is actually the easy part for the hardware. It's the typing-the-answer part that's brutal. Let me show you why.

Two very different phases: prefill and decode

Serving one request to a large language model happens in two phases with opposite personalities:

  • Prefill — the model reads your whole prompt at once. That's a big matrix-times-matrix computation, which keeps the GPU's math units genuinely busy. This phase is compute-bound: more FLOPs helps.
  • Decode — the model then generates your answer one token at a time, and to produce each new token it has to re-read the entire accumulated context via a matrix-times-vector operation. That's an enormous memory fetch (megabytes to gigabytes of weights and cached state) attached to a tiny amount of arithmetic.

The measure architects use for this is arithmetic intensity — FLOPs of math performed per byte of data moved. Decode runs at roughly 1 FLOP per byte, which is about two orders of magnitude below the "ridge point" where a modern accelerator's compute and memory capabilities are balanced. In plain terms: during decode, the GPU's math units spend most of their time waiting for the next byte to arrive, not running out of arithmetic horsepower. That's the whole chapter in one sentence — decode is memory-bound, not compute-bound, and no amount of extra FLOPs fixes that on its own.

The KV cache has to live somewhere

What decode is actually re-reading each step is the KV cache — the saved "keys" and "values" from every previous token's attention calculation, kept around so the model doesn't have to recompute the whole conversation from scratch on every new word. That cache grows with context length and with how many requests you're serving at once, and it can get big — tens of gigabytes for a long conversation. When it doesn't fit in the GPU's own HBM, the obvious move is to spill it out to ordinary CPU DRAM, which is far cheaper and more plentiful. The catch is the road between them: PCIe 5.0 delivers roughly 64 GB/s — about 2% of HBM's bandwidth. Concretely: moving a 50 GB KV cache takes about 15 ms from GPU HBM, but roughly 800 ms from CPU DRAM over PCIe — more than fifty times slower, for the same data, just because of which bus it rode in on.

MoE and long context make it worse, not better

You'd think Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models would ease this, since they only activate 2–4 "expert" sub-networks per token instead of the whole model. They do shrink the compute and the weight-loading bill — but the KV cache doesn't care which experts fired. Every token still has to store its full attention state regardless of routing, so a modest 7B-parameter MoE model with a 128K-token context and 16 experts can burn through 24 GB of KV cache alone. Long-context features (the whole industry trend toward million-token windows) multiply the same problem. The current mitigations are all variations on "store less, more cleverly": aggressive KV-cache quantization down to a few bits per value, expert-aware cache sharding, and GPU–CPU cooperative schemes that compute attention on the CPU side instead of shipping the whole cache back and forth. None of them make the wall disappear — they just help you live closer to it.

Key idea A chatbot feels slow to type not because your GPU can't do the math — it's because, every single token, it's waiting on a delivery truck. Speeding up the truck (Chapter 4) or shrinking the delivery (Chapter 5) are the only two real levers.

Why this matters

If you've ever wondered why NVIDIA ships GPUs and CPUs fused together on one coherent interconnect (Grace Hopper, Grace Blackwell), this chapter is the answer: at 900 GB/s, NVLink-C2C is about 7× a plain PCIe 5.0 link, which is precisely the gap this chapter just showed you. The next chapter is about that fix — and why the wire between chips has become as important as the chips themselves.
