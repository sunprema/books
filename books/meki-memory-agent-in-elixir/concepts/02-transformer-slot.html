The Transformer Slot

Before we can plug MeKi in, we need to be precise about where it plugs in. One decoder layer, one residual stream, one empty slot beside the FFN.

A modern decoder-only transformer is a stack of identical layers, each refining a running sum called the residual stream. Within one layer, two sub-blocks each read a normalized copy of the stream, compute something, and add their result back. The first is attention (tokens look at each other); the second is the feed-forward network, or FFN (each token is transformed on its own).

Writing A for the stream after attention has been added, a layer's FFN step is:

H = RMSNormpre-ffn(A)  ;  H′ = F(H) + A

Here F is the FFN (a SwiGLU MLP in Qwen-style models), H is the normalized hidden state, and the + A is the residual connection. That is the layer MeKi modifies — and it modifies it in the gentlest possible way.

🔌
The empty slot

MeKi adds a third parallel branch that reads the same normalized H as the FFN and adds its own contribution to the same residual. It does not sit in series — it never touches the FFN's input or output path.

3 ·

With MeKi present, the layer's update becomes a three-way sum:

H′ = F(H) + MeKi(H) + A
FFN and MeKi run side by side, both reading H and both adding into the residual.

Why parallel and not in series? The paper ablates four placements — parallel to the FFN, parallel to attention, after attention, after the FFN — and parallel to the FFN wins on both validation loss and downstream accuracy. Put MeKi after a block and its narrow internal dimension chokes the forward pass into an information bottleneck; put it beside the FFN and it behaves like a clean width expansion of the layer. We return to those numbers in Chapter 8.

🔑
Hold on to this

MeKi is an additive, parallel branch. Everything that follows — the experts, the gate, the re-parameterization — is just how that one extra term MeKi(H) is computed cheaply.
