Concept 04 · Fusion

Knowledge Injection

A looked-up vector knows about its token but nothing about its context. A small gate fixes that — then projects the result back into the residual stream.

The expert vector elt from the last chapter depends only on the token id. But the same word means different things in different sentences, and the hidden state hlt is precisely the model's contextual read on this token right now. MeKi uses it to adjust the expert before injecting it.

First, a low-rank gate. Project the (wide) hidden state down into the (narrow) memory dimension and squash it:

glt = σ( Wgate hlt ),   Wgate ∈ ℝdmem×dmodel

This is the only place context enters MeKi, and it is deliberately small: one thin matrix and a sigmoid. The gate is a channel-wise offset, added to the expert rather than multiplied:

vlt = elt + glt
➕
Additive, not multiplicative

The paper tried four fusions — additive vs. multiplicative, sigmoid vs. SiLU. Additive-Sigmoid (exactly the two equations above) gave the lowest training loss at both 0.6B and 1.7B. Additive gating optimizes more stably than multiplicative; a stray near-zero gate can't wipe out the expert.

The modulated vector vlt still lives in dmem. To rejoin the residual stream it must come back up to dmodel. One more thin matrix, with a normalize:

ylt = RMSNorm( Wout vlt ),   Wout ∈ ℝdmodel×dmem

and that ylt is the MeKi(H) term we promised in Chapter 2 — added straight onto the residual beside the FFN.

Gate in d_mem, add, project up to d_model, normalize, inject.

Count the cost. Both Wgate and Wout are dmem × dmodel — “low-rank” because dmem ≪ dmodel. Two skinny matmuls plus an add. That is the entire inference compute of MeKi. The expensive part — the SwiGLU Gl from Chapter 3 — is about to vanish.

🔑
Inference math, in full

g = σ(W_gate·h) → v = ẽ + g → y = RMSNorm(W_out·v). A lookup, two thin matmuls, two element-wise ops. Next chapter shows why ẽ needs no projection at all.
