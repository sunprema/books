Chapter 04 · The Honest Illusion

Idempotency & the Exactly-Once Illusion

You cannot deliver a message exactly once. You can, however, make it not matter.

Let me be blunt about something the brochures are coy about. Exactly-once delivery is not achievable over an unreliable network. It is not hard; it is impossible, and for the reason we met in Chapter 1: when you send a request and hear nothing back, you cannot tell whether it was lost on the way there or the reply was lost on the way back. Your only recovery is to send it again — which means the receiver must be prepared to see the same request twice. So the honest engineering does not chase exactly-once delivery. It builds exactly-once effect out of two humble parts.

The whole subject in one line

at‑least‑once delivery  +  idempotent processing  =  exactly‑once effect.

We give up on the network ever being tidy — we let it deliver a message one-or-more times, and we retry freely. Then we make the processing of that message idempotent, so that seeing it twice lands exactly the same as seeing it once. The duplicate still arrives; it simply does nothing the second time. That is the illusion, and it is a perfectly honest one.

What "idempotent" really asks of you

An operation is idempotent if applying it twice leaves the world in the same state as applying it once. Some operations are born that way. "Set the balance to 500" is idempotent — do it a hundred times, the balance is 500. "Cancel order 7" is idempotent — already cancelled, stays cancelled. The troublemakers are the accumulating ones: "add 100 to the balance," "charge the card," "send the email." Run those twice and the customer is out another hundred dollars and cross about it. Durable execution lives or dies on turning that second kind into the first.

The idempotency key: a receipt you check first

The standard device — Stripe's public design is the canonical write-up — is the idempotency key. The client generates a unique token (a random V4 UUID has plenty of entropy) and attaches it to the request. The server keeps a table keyed by that token. The first time it sees the key, it does the work and records, under that key, the outcome — the status and the response body — whether it succeeded or failed. Every later request bearing the same key skips the work entirely and replays the stored outcome. The operation takes effect at most once; combined with the client's willingness to retry until it gets an answer, that is exactly once, felt from the outside.

charge, made safe to repeatPOST /charges     Idempotency-Key: "5f2b9c…"

if key in receipts:                 # seen this exact request before
    return receipts[key]           # replay the stored (status, body) — do NOT charge
else:
    result = charge_card(...)       # do the real, dangerous work once
    receipts[key] = result             # record the outcome under the key…
    return result                     # …see the next callout for the crucial caveat
The subtlety that ruins naïve implementations

Look hard at those last two lines. If you charge the card and then, in a separate step, record the key, a crash in between is a disaster: the charge happened, the receipt didn't, and the next retry charges again. The key and the business effect must be committed in the same atomic transaction — one commit, both or neither. This is why idempotency and the transactional log of Chapter 2 are the same story: the receipt is only trustworthy if it becomes durable at the very instant the effect does. Get this ordering wrong and you have merely moved the double-charge window, not closed it.

Why durable engines cannot escape this

Recall from Chapter 3 that a workflow engine runs each activity at least once. Picture a worker that charges the card, and then dies in the millisecond before it could record "charge completed" into the history. The engine, seeing no result, does the only safe thing it can: it schedules the activity again. Your charge activity just ran twice. The engine did nothing wrong — it cannot possibly know the first attempt got through — and so the burden lands, unavoidably, on you: the activity, or the payment system it calls, must tolerate the repeat. The engine guarantees the work will happen; only your idempotency guarantees it happens once.

The cheapest idempotency is design, not machinery

Before you reach for a key table, ask whether the operation can simply be made naturally idempotent. "Set status = shipped" needs no receipt; "increment ships by one" does. Modelling effects as assignments to a known target rather than blind increments removes whole categories of double-apply bugs for free. When you can choose the shape of the operation, choose the idempotent one.

So we do not defeat the unreliable network — nobody does. We disarm it. We accept that our messages will sometimes arrive twice and simply make the second arrival a no-op. "Exactly once" was never a property of the wire; it is a property we construct, out of cheerful retries below and careful idempotence above. Name your operations honestly — which are naturally idempotent, which need a receipt — and the exactly-once illusion becomes something you can actually promise.
Back of the envelope

A receipt is small — a key, a status, a body, a timestamp. Keep them for as long as a client might retry: minutes to a day, rarely more. Too short and a late retry slips through and double-applies; keep them forever and the table grows without bound. The right expiry is just "longer than any sane retry, shorter than forever" — a knob, set deliberately, like every other durability knob in this book.

Think it through

  1. List five write operations in your system. Label each naturally idempotent or needs a key. The second list is your retry-safety backlog.
  2. For one "needs a key" operation, write the pseudo-transaction that commits the effect and the receipt together. Find the line where a crash used to double-apply, and confirm it can't anymore.
  3. Explain why an engine's "at-least-once activity" is not a weakness but an honest admission — and why the alternative ("exactly-once, promise!") would be a lie.
