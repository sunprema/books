Chapter 05 · Time, Made Durable

Durable Timers & the Passage of Time

A workflow that says "wait 30 days" must survive 30 days of crashes, deploys, and reboots.

Real business does not happen in a millisecond. "Charge the card, then wait three days, then send a reminder if they haven't paid." "Start the trial; in fourteen days, downgrade." Ordinary programs express a wait with sleep() — a thread parked in memory. But memory is the first thing a crash destroys. A workflow that must wait a month cannot hold a thread for a month; the machine will be redeployed a dozen times before then. So we need time itself to be durable, and the way we get it is the same way we got everything else: we write it down.

A wait is a fact on the log, not a parked thread

A durable timer is recorded in the log as an event: "a timer for workflow X should fire at 14:00 on the 30th." Having written that, the worker is free — it holds no thread, consumes no memory, and can crash, be replaced, or be redeployed a hundred times. The wait does not live in a process; it lives in the store. When the appointed moment arrives, the engine appends a "timer fired" event and wakes the workflow to continue. The passage of a month costs you exactly one row and one wake-up, not a month of held resources.

e12ActivityCompleted  charge = ok
e13TimerStarted  fire_at = +30d
⋯worker may crash / redeploy freely here
e14TimerFired  → wake & continue

Time is the sneakiest non-determinism of all

Chapter 3 warned you never to read the wall clock inside workflow code, and the durable timer is why the engine can afford that rule. Think about replay. If your code said if now() > deadline, then the first run and a replay six days later would read two different clocks and could take two different branches — the reconstruction breaks. So the engine forbids the raw clock and offers durable timers instead: you say "sleep until the deadline," the engine records the firing as an event, and on replay your code sees that same recorded firing, not a fresh reading. Time stops being a live, ever-changing input and becomes just another fact on the log — which is exactly what makes it safe to replay.

The reframing

In a durable workflow, the current time is not something you read — it is something the engine records for you. "Has enough time passed?" is answered by a timer event in the history, not by a clock in the CPU. Once you see time as logged fact rather than live input, the whole determinism rule of Chapter 3 stops feeling like a restriction and starts feeling like the only sane way to build something that can be reborn from its record.

Telling a slow worker from a dead one

Timers point the other way too. Suppose an activity is genuinely long — transcoding a video, crunching a report — and takes twenty minutes. How does the engine tell "still working, be patient" from "the worker died ten minutes ago"? It cannot, unless the worker speaks up. So long activities send periodic heartbeats: little "still alive" signals. The engine sets a heartbeat timeout; as long as beats keep arriving inside it, the activity is presumed healthy. If the beats stop, the engine declares the attempt failed and — being an at-least-once system — reschedules it. (Which is precisely why Chapter 4 insisted that activity be idempotent: a heartbeat timeout is one of the ordinary ways an activity runs twice.)

A timeout is a bet, not a fact

Every timeout is a wager: "if I haven't heard back in this long, I'll assume the worst and retry." Set it too tight and you abandon healthy-but-slow work — and double it up. Set it too loose and a truly dead worker stalls the workflow for ages before anyone notices. There is no universally right value; there is only a value chosen deliberately for this task's honest distribution of durations. Guessing is how you get mysterious duplicate work at the tail.

The queue-shaped version of the same idea

Not every durable timer lives inside a replay engine. In a job queue like Oban, the very same idea appears as a scheduled job: a row in the database with a scheduled_at timestamp, invisible to workers until its time comes, at which point the queue makes it available to run. "Remind them in three days" is a job scheduled for three days hence; "reconcile accounts nightly" is a cron entry the queue enqueues on the hour. The mechanism is humbler — a timestamped row, not an event in a replayed history — but the principle is identical: the wait is durable because it is written down in a store that survives the crash, not held in a thread that doesn't.

a durable wait as a scheduled row (Oban, Elixir)# "remind in 3 days" — the wait lives in Postgres, not in a process
%{user_id: id}
|> ReminderWorker.new(schedule_in: 3 * 24 * 3600)
|> Oban.insert()

# The app can restart a hundred times before it fires; the row waits patiently.
Notice the pattern holding across every layer. Whenever durable execution needs to remember something across a crash — a result, a decision, a debt, and now the passage of time — its answer is the same four words: write it down first. A wait is not an absence of work the process must sit through; it is a small durable fact the process is free to forget, trusting the log to remind it.
Back of the envelope

A held thread costs perhaps a megabyte of stack plus a scheduler slot, for the entire duration of the wait. A durable timer costs one row — call it a hundred bytes — and zero running threads. For a one-second wait the difference is academic; for a thirty-day wait across a million workflows it is the difference between a rack of idle machines and a single quiet table. Durability and efficiency, for once, pull the same direction.

Think it through

  1. Find a sleep or a scheduled thread in your system that waits more than a few seconds. Ask what happens to that wait if the process is redeployed. If the answer is "it's lost," it wanted to be a durable timer.
  2. Pick a long-running job you own and decide its honest heartbeat timeout from its real duration distribution — not a round number. Then say what happens on a timeout, and confirm the job is idempotent enough to survive it.
  3. Re-read Chapter 3's determinism rule with time in mind. Explain, in one sentence, why a durable timer is what permits that rule rather than what fights it.
