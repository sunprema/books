Chapter 02 · The Substrate

The Log Never Lies

One idea underneath every durable system: write down what you're about to do, before you do it.

If you take only one thing from this book, take this. The durable systems I have helped build all rest on the same small, stubborn trick: keep an append-only log — an ordered list of records, each describing one change — and force each record to stable storage before you touch the real data it describes. That ordering is called write-ahead logging, and it is the closest thing our field has to a law of physics. Get it right and recovery becomes possible; get it backwards and no amount of cleverness above will save you.

Why write the log first? Because of Chapter 1's crash-in-the- middle. Suppose you update a bank balance in place and the machine dies before you record what you did. On restart you find a number, and you have no idea whether it is old, new, or half-written. But if the intention — "debit A by 100" — is already safely in the log, then whatever you find in the data pages, you can make it right: you know what should have happened, and you can finish it or undo it. The log is the promise; the data is just the current best attempt to keep it.

LSN 41BEGIN   txn 7
LSN 42UPDATE  txn 7  acct A  −100
LSN 43UPDATE  txn 7  acct B  +100
LSN 44COMMIT  txn 7  ← durable here

The transfer is not "done" when the balances change in memory. It is done the instant LSN 44, the COMMIT record, is safely on stable storage. Before that line lands, we are allowed to forget the whole thing ever started.

Every record gets a number

Give each log record a unique, ever-increasing Log Sequence Number — an LSN. This little integer does more work than it looks. Because the log is ordered by LSN, it is a precise, total history of what happened and in what order. And here is the elegant part, from the ARIES recovery method: stamp every data page with the LSN of the last change applied to it. Now, on recovery, you can compare a page's stamp to the log and know exactly which changes it already reflects and which it is missing. No guessing. The number tells you.

Why the LSN makes recovery safe to retry

Recovery replays log records against pages. What if you crash during recovery and have to start over? You must not apply a change twice. The page LSN is the guard: before redoing record N, check the page — if its stamp is already ≥ N, that change is present, skip it. This makes redo idempotent, and an idempotent recovery is one you can run as many times as the universe demands. Remember this word idempotent; it is the hero of Chapter 04.

Repeat history, then undo — the ARIES dance

When Mohan and his colleagues at IBM wrote down ARIES in 1992, they gave recovery a shape so clean it still runs, in spirit, inside the database you used this morning. On restart it makes three passes over the log:

  1. Analysis. Scan forward to work out the state of the world at the moment of the crash: which transactions were in flight, which pages were dirty.
  2. Redo. Scan forward again and repeat history — reapply every logged change, even those of transactions that never committed, until the pages are exactly as they were the instant before the lights went out.
  3. Undo. Scan backward and roll back the transactions that were still in flight at the crash, leaving only the committed work behind.

That middle step surprises people. Why redo the work of transactions you are about to undo? Because it makes the whole procedure restartable. After Redo, the system is in one known, well-defined state — "exactly as it was at the crash" — from which Undo is a clean, ordinary operation. If you crash again mid-recovery, you simply repeat the same idempotent passes and arrive at the same place. Recovery that is safe to interrupt is recovery you can trust, and "repeat history to a known point, then reconcile" is a pattern you will meet again, dressed in new clothes, when we replay workflows in Chapter 3.

The log is the truth; the state is a cache

Here is the mental flip that unlocks everything ahead. We are used to thinking the current state is real and the log is a dreary audit trail we keep for the accountants. Invert it. The log is the ground truth — durable, ordered, append- only, the one thing we refuse to lose — and the materialised state (the balances, the row, the object in memory) is merely a cache: a convenient, rebuildable summary of the log so far. Lose the cache and you shrug and rebuild it by replaying. Lose the log and you have lost the world.

A double-entry echo

Renaissance merchants worked this out five centuries before we did. Double-entry bookkeeping is an append-only log: you never erase an entry, you post a new one that corrects it. The current balance is derived, not stored as gospel. When we get to event sourcing next chapter, notice you already know it — it is a ledger, and the balance is just a running total you can always recompute from the entries.

So the humble log is not plumbing beneath the interesting parts — it is the interesting part. Everything else in durable execution is a variation on one theme: write the intention down, in order, before you act; treat that record as the truth; and rebuild whatever you need from it. Master the log and the rest of this book is commentary.
Back of the envelope

Appending to a log is nearly the cheapest durable write there is: it is sequential, so even a spinning disk streams it without seeking. That is why systems that would choke updating data in a thousand random places happily write a single append-only log at full disk bandwidth. The log is not only safer than in-place updates — it is usually faster.

Think it through

  1. Open your database's docs and find its write-ahead log (Postgres calls it the WAL; others say redo log). Note the setting that controls when it is flushed. That knob is the durability dial from Chapter 1, made real.
  2. Sketch a two-account transfer as four log records like the ledger above. Mark the exact line after which a crash leaves the transfer done rather than forgotten.
  3. Convince yourself that redo guarded by a page LSN is safe to run twice. That argument is the one we will reuse, almost word for word, to justify replaying a workflow.
