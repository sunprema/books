Chapter 07 · Ideas, Made Real

The Engines: Temporal, Oban & Ash Reactor

The same handful of ideas, assembled three different ways, at three different altitudes.

We have built a workshop of ideas — the log, replay, idempotency, durable timers, sagas. Now let us watch three real systems put them together. I want you to notice something as we go: they are not really competitors. They sit at different altitudes. One persists an entire program's control flow; one persists individual units of work; one structures the steps of a single transaction and borrows durability from a neighbour. Knowing which altitude you need is most of knowing which tool to reach for.

Temporal — persist the whole program

Temporal is the purest expression of durable execution in this book. You write a workflow as ordinary code; Temporal event-sources it. Every step it takes lands in a server-held event history; the code is replayed against that history to reconstruct state after any crash (Chapter 3); side-effecting activities run at-least-once, so you supply idempotency (Chapter 4); waits are durable timers recorded as events (Chapter 5). The price is the determinism discipline — no clocks, no dice, no direct I/O inside workflow code. In return you get something remarkable: a long-running function that survives its own death and simply continues.

Oban — persist the units of work

Oban comes at it from the database up. It is a job queue for Elixir backed by Postgres (and now SQLite and MySQL): a job is a row. Because jobs are rows in your transactional database, enqueuing a job can share a transaction with your business write — the transactional-outbox idea, and a very clean way to get Chapter 2's atomicity for free. Execution is at-least-once; completed jobs are retained for inspection, so work isn't lost across crashes. Oban offers uniqueness to suppress duplicate jobs, though with a caveat worth knowing: the open-source uniqueness check runs at insert time and is not backed by a database unique constraint, so it can race under concurrency; Oban Pro's Smart Engine uses real constraints for a stronger guarantee. And Oban Pro adds Workflows — jobs linked into a DAG, where a job waits for all its upstream dependencies before it runs, giving you fan-out and fan-in that survive retries.

Reactor — structure the steps and their undo

Reactor, from the Ash project, is the saga orchestrator of Chapter 6. It resolves dependencies between named steps, runs independent ones concurrently, and — its reason for being — gives each step compensate/4 and undo/4 callbacks so a failure late in the graph can walk back the successful steps in reverse. On its own it orchestrates within one process; it does not, by itself, survive a node crash mid-run. For durability you pair it with Oban, and AshOban adds the periodic pieces — "triggers" that run over Ash resources on a schedule, and cron-style "scheduled actions." Reactor supplies the shape of a transaction; Oban supplies the crash-proof floor beneath it.

Reading the table

Look down the "unit of durability" row — it is the whole story. Temporal makes an entire program durable; Oban makes each unit of work durable; Reactor makes a transaction's structure explicit and leans on Oban to make it durable. Reach for Temporal when you have a long, branching, stateful process to keep alive across weeks. Reach for Oban when you have discrete jobs to run reliably against a database you already trust. Reach for Reactor when one operation has several steps that must be undone together on failure.

Same bones underneath

However different they look, x-ray all three and you find the identical skeleton from this book: a durable record that survives the crash (an event history; a job row; an Oban row behind a step), at-least-once execution off that record, and idempotency supplied by you to turn the repeats into an exactly-once effect. They differ in what they write down and how much of your program they wrap — not in the physics.

Do not let the branding fool you into thinking these are three rival philosophies. They are three altitudes over the same landscape. When someone asks "should we use Temporal or Oban?", the useful reply is a question back: what is the unit you need to survive a crash — a whole process, a job, or the undo of a transaction? Answer that honestly and the tool chooses itself.
Back of the envelope

A durable-execution engine is only as durable as the store beneath it. Temporal is as durable as its persistence layer; Oban is as durable as your Postgres, with its replication and fsync settings; a Reactor saga is as durable as the Oban jobs backing it. There is no durability that isn't, ultimately, someone's disk being honest about a flush. When you adopt an engine, you are really adopting the guarantees of the database under it — so go read those.

Think it through

  1. For a workflow you actually run, name its "unit of durability": is the thing that must survive a crash a whole process, a job, or the undo of a transaction? Let that pick your altitude.
  2. If you use Oban, check whether your "unique" jobs rely on the OSS insert-time check or on a real database constraint. Decide whether that difference matters for the duplicate you're guarding against.
  3. Take a Reactor (or any in-memory) saga and ask the blunt question: if this node dies at step 3 of 5, what happens? If the answer is "the run is lost," it needs a durable backing before it goes near production.
