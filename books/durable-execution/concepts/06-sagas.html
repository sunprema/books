Chapter 06 · When You Can't Just Retry

Sagas & Compensation

Some failures can't be replayed away. They have to be unwound.

So far our tools have all been about pressing forward through failure: log it, replay it, retry it until it sticks. But some work cannot simply be retried to completion. You booked the flight and the hotel, and then the car rental failed and will keep failing — the lot is sold out. You cannot go forward. And you cannot leave the customer with a flight and a hotel and no car and call it done. You must go backward: cancel the hotel, cancel the flight, and return the world to something sensible. That backward journey is a saga, and it is the oldest idea in this book that isn't about logging.

Why not just one big transaction?

A database person's first instinct is: wrap it all in one ACID transaction, and if the car fails, roll the whole thing back. Lovely — and impossible here. These steps run against different systems, over minutes or hours, each an external service with its own database. You cannot hold a lock across an airline, a hotel chain, and a rental company for an afternoon; you would freeze half the travel industry. Garcia-Molina and Salem saw this clearly back in 1987 and named the problem the long-lived transaction: a unit of work too long, and too spread out, to hold under one lock. Their answer was the saga.

A saga, defined

A saga is a long-lived transaction written as a sequence of smaller transactions T₁, T₂, …, Tₙ, each with a matching compensating transaction C₁, C₂, …, Cₙ. The system guarantees one of two honest outcomes: either every Tₖ completes, or a prefix runs and then its compensations undo it — Cₖ, Cₖ₋₁, … C₁, in reverse — leaving the world in an acceptable state. Not "all or nothing" in the tidy ACID sense, but "all, or all-undone."

Compensation is not rollback

Here is the subtlety that separates a saga from a database transaction, and it is worth dwelling on. A database rollback restores the exact prior bytes — as if the transaction never touched a thing. A compensation does no such thing. It semantically undoes the step. A refund offsets a charge, but the ledger still shows a charge and then a refund — the money moved twice, and the customer's statement remembers both. Garcia-Molina and Salem were careful about this: a compensation restores the world to an acceptable approximation of the state before the step, not a bit-for-bit copy. The flight is cancelled, but the fact that it was once booked is part of history now.

This matters because some effects cannot be perfectly undone at all. You can refund a charge; you cannot un-send an email, un-fire a missile, un-ship a package already on a truck. When you design a saga, you are really designing its compensations, and the honest question for each step is: what is the best acceptable approximation of undoing this? Sometimes it is a clean reversal; sometimes it is a follow-up email saying "please disregard." The saga does not pretend the step never happened — it makes a considered, visible amends.

Compensations crash too

Everything from Chapters 1 through 4 still applies to the compensations themselves. The machine can die halfway through cancelling the hotel; the cancel request can be lost; the engine will retry it. So a compensation must be idempotent and retryable, exactly like any other step — cancelling an already-cancelled hotel must be a harmless no-op. A saga whose compensations aren't safe to repeat gets stuck half-undone, which is the worst place of all: neither forward nor back. The unwinding needs the same durability as the winding.

What this looks like in code

In the Elixir world, Reactor (from the Ash project) is a saga orchestrator built around exactly this shape. Each step is a named unit of work that may define two recovery callbacks. compensate/4 is called if that step itself fails — its chance to retry or clean up after its own attempt. undo/4 is called on a step that already succeeded when some later step fails unrecoverably — its chance to reverse a committed effect. When a step gives up, Reactor walks back through the previously successful steps and runs their undo/4 callbacks in reverse. It is the 1987 paper made executable.

a saga step with its compensation (Reactor, Elixir — shape, not full API)step :book_hotel do
  run fn args, _ -> Hotels.book(args.trip) end

  # later step failed → reverse this committed booking
  undo fn booking, _args, _ctx ->
    Hotels.cancel(booking.id)   # must be idempotent: cancel-of-cancelled = :ok
  end
end
In-memory saga, durable saga

Reactor by itself orchestrates a saga within one running process — wonderful for a request that completes in seconds, but if the node dies mid-saga, the in-flight run is gone. To make a saga survive a crash, you back it with a durable store: in the Ash world, that means pairing Reactor with Oban (via AshOban) so each step is a durable job. This is the whole book in one sentence: a saga gives you the backward logic, and durable execution gives that logic somewhere crash-proof to stand.

The saga is the humility mechanism. It admits that across many independent systems you cannot have neat, invisible, all-or-nothing atomicity — so it offers the honest next-best thing: all, or a careful, visible undoing of what got done. You pay for it in lost isolation — intermediate states are briefly real, a flight genuinely booked before it is cancelled — but you gain the ability to coordinate a dozen services that will never share a transaction. Design the compensations first; they are the part that earns their keep.

Think it through

  1. Take a multi-step operation you own that spans two or more services. Write the compensation for each step. Find the one step whose effect can't be cleanly undone — that is where your saga needs a human, a notification, or a rethink.
  2. For one compensation, prove it is idempotent: what does it do when the thing is already undone? If the answer isn't "nothing, harmlessly," it isn't safe to retry yet.
  3. Argue when a saga is the wrong tool — when giving up isolation and exposing intermediate state would be unacceptable. (Hint: think about money others can see and act on between your steps.)
