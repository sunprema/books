Chapter 08 · The Honest Ledger

What Is Actually Promised

A guarantee you can't state precisely is a guarantee you don't have.

We end where a transaction person always ends: with the fine print. The most dangerous thing about durable execution is not that it fails — it rarely does — but that it is easy to believe it promises more than it does, and to build on the imagined promise. So let me set out, plainly, what these systems actually guarantee and what they quietly leave to you. I have found that a team which can recite this ledger correctly writes very different code from one that cannot.

Exactly-once effect, not exactly-once delivery

Read the marketing word "exactly-once" as a claim about effect, never about delivery. Delivery cannot be exactly-once over an unreliable network — Chapter 4, and it is a theorem, not a limitation of the current release. What the engine gives you is at-least-once execution. The exactly-once effect is manufactured, downstream, by your idempotency. If you skip that work, the engine will cheerfully run your activity twice and your customer will be charged twice, and the system did exactly what it promised. The exactly-once-ness was always your half of the bargain.

Draw the line clearly

The engine promises: your progress will not be lost, and your workflow will resume and eventually complete despite crashes. The engine does not promise: that any individual side effect runs exactly once. That second property is yours to build with idempotency. Keep these two columns separate in your head and you will never be surprised by a duplicate.

Determinism is a precondition you must uphold

Replay reconstructs your state correctly only if your workflow is deterministic. That is not a gift the engine grants; it is a contract you sign. Reach for the wall clock, roll a die, read a database directly from inside workflow code, or change the code's shape under a live history, and the reconstruction quietly goes wrong — or the engine halts with a non-determinism error. The durability is real, but it rests on your keeping this bargain. A guarantee with a precondition is only as strong as your discipline about the precondition.

Sagas promise all-or-compensated, and cost you isolation

A saga guarantees that a long-lived transaction either completes or is compensated back to an acceptable state — eventually. What it explicitly does not give you is isolation. Intermediate states are real and visible: the flight is genuinely booked for the minutes before the failure cancels it, and anyone looking can see it. That is a deliberate trade, not an oversight — you gave up isolation to escape holding a lock across a dozen services. Use a saga only where those visible intermediate states, and the semantic (not perfect) nature of compensation, are acceptable. Where they aren't, a saga is the wrong tool, however fashionable.

Your durability is your store's durability

And the foundation under all of it: a durable-execution engine is exactly as durable as the store its log lives in — no more. If that database loses a committed write on a power failure because someone relaxed its flush settings for speed, then your "durable" workflow loses it too. Durability is not conjured by the engine; it is inherited from the disk, the replication, the fsync honesty of the layer beneath. When you audit a durable system, always follow the log all the way down to the platter. The chain of trust ends at something physical.

The full ledger, on one line each

Progress: survives crashes — guaranteed by the engine.
Exactly-once effect: your job, via idempotency.
Correct replay: your job, via determinism.
All-or-compensated: guaranteed by the saga — minus isolation.
Never losing a committed write: only as strong as your store's durability.

The one idea, one last time

Look back over the whole book and you will find we said one thing, in eight ways. Write down the truth before you act on it; treat that written record as reality; rebuild everything else from it; and make every action safe to repeat. The log defeats the crash. Replay rebuilds the program. Idempotency disarms the duplicate. Durable timers make even the passage of time a written fact. Sagas write down how to walk backward. And the honest engineer, always, keeps a clear ledger of what is promised and what is merely hoped. That is durable execution: not the absence of failure, but bookkeeping so faithful that failure becomes a pause rather than a catastrophe.

A colleague once asked me for the shortest true definition of this whole field. Here it is: a durable system is one that can lose everything except its log, and rebuild the rest. Everything we have built — every mechanism, every engine, every careful compensation — is in service of making that one sentence come true, and of being honest about exactly how far it reaches. Keep the log, keep the ledger of promises, and your workflows really will survive anything on the list we made in Chapter 1.
Back of the envelope, one last time

The cost of all this durability is a handful of forced writes on the critical path — a few milliseconds each, paid only at the moments that matter. The cost of skipping it is a corrupt ledger, a double charge, and a very long night. In thirty years I have never once regretted paying the milliseconds. That is the whole trade, and it is not a close call.

Carry it forward

  1. Write your system's promise ledger — the five lines from the callout above, filled in with what your stack actually guarantees. Circle every line whose truth depends on your own code.
  2. Find one place where your team has been reading "exactly-once" as a delivery promise. Fix the belief before it becomes a bug.
  3. Trace one "durable" write in your system all the way down to a disk flush. If you can't find the fsync, you haven't found your durability yet.
