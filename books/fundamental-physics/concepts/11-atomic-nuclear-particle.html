Chapter 11
Inside the atom

Atomic, Nuclear & Particle Physics

Everything is made of atoms. That is the most important single statement in all of science. Now let us look inside the atom and see what we find. What we find is a story of Russian dolls: open one layer and there is another inside, each with its own rules, its own surprises, its own beautiful mathematics. In this chapter, we crack open the atom, peer into the nucleus, and catalogue the truly fundamental pieces of matter that Nature has chosen to build the universe from.

"If, in some cataclysm, all of scientific knowledge were to be destroyed, and only one sentence passed on to the next generation of creatures, what statement would contain the most information in the fewest words? I believe it is the atomic hypothesis — that all things are made of atoms." Let us honour that hypothesis by going deeper than the atom itself.

11.1 · The Evolving Picture of the Atom

The story of atomic models is a story of how we learn. Each model was not wrong — it was a stepping stone. Each one explained more facts than the one before. That is how physics works: you never get the final answer, but each answer gets closer.

Thomson's Plum-Pudding Model (1897)

J.J. Thomson discovered the electron — a tiny, negatively charged particle — by deflecting cathode rays in electric and magnetic fields. He measured the charge-to-mass ratio e/m and found it was the same regardless of the cathode material. These were universal constituents of matter.

Thomson imagined the atom as a sphere of uniformly distributed positive charge, with electrons embedded inside it like plums in a pudding. The model explained electrical neutrality: positive and negative charges balanced. It even predicted (roughly) the frequencies at which atoms might vibrate and emit light. But it had no nucleus, no concentrated mass. It would take a bombardment experiment to reveal that.

Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911)

Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden, working under Rutherford at Manchester, fired alpha particles (helium nuclei) at a thin gold foil. Most alphas sailed straight through. But a few — about 1 in 8,000 — bounced back at large angles. Rutherford said it was as if you fired a cannon shell at tissue paper and it came back and hit you.

Key Result The large-angle scattering proved that the positive charge and nearly all the mass of the atom are concentrated in a tiny core — the nucleus — roughly 10−15 m across, some 100,000 times smaller than the atom itself (about 10−10 m).

Rutherford's model placed electrons orbiting a central nucleus, like planets around the Sun. Beautiful, but it had a fatal flaw: accelerating charges radiate electromagnetic energy. An orbiting electron should spiral inward, radiating a continuous spectrum, and crash into the nucleus in about 10−11 s. Atoms would not be stable. Classical physics had hit a wall.

Bohr's Model (1913)

Niels Bohr saved the atom by postulating what he could not yet prove. He declared:

  1. Electrons orbit the nucleus in certain stationary orbits without radiating.
  2. Light is emitted or absorbed only when an electron jumps between orbits, and the photon energy equals the difference in orbital energies: hν = Ei − Ef.
  3. Angular momentum is quantized: L = nℏ, where n = 1, 2, 3, …

From these postulates, Bohr derived the energy levels of hydrogen:

En = − 13.6 eVn2

This gave the Balmer series, the Lyman series, and every other known spectral line of hydrogen with stunning accuracy. For the first time, the colours of light from an element could be calculated, not merely catalogued.

Note for BSc exams The Bohr radius a0 = 0.529 Å = 5.29 × 10−11 m is the radius of the n=1 orbit in hydrogen. Know this number. It sets the scale of all atoms.

But the Bohr model could not handle helium, could not explain fine structure, and treated the electron as a particle on a rail. Something deeper was needed.

The Quantum-Mechanical Atom

The full quantum treatment (Schrödinger, Heisenberg, Dirac, 1925–28) replaces orbits with orbitals — probability clouds described by wave functions ψ(r, θ, φ). The electron does not travel a definite path; rather, |ψ|2 gives the probability density of finding it at each point in space.

Each orbital is labelled by four quantum numbers:

The Pauli exclusion principle says no two electrons in the same atom can share all four quantum numbers. This single rule builds the periodic table: it determines how many electrons fit in each shell, how elements are ordered, and why chemistry works the way it does.

"The same equations that make a hydrogen atom also make chemistry, and biology, and cooking, and love — but we cannot solve them exactly for anything bigger than hydrogen! The art of physics is knowing which approximations to make."

11.2 · Inside the Nucleus

The nucleus is a fantastically dense, tightly-bound cluster of nucleons: protons (charge +e, mass 938.3 MeV/c2) and neutrons (neutral, mass 939.6 MeV/c2). A nucleus with Z protons and N neutrons has mass number A = Z + N. We write it as AZX.

Nuclear Size and Density

Experiments (electron scattering, muonic atoms) show the nuclear radius scales as:

R = R0 A1/3,    R0 ≈ 1.2 fm

This A1/3 law means nuclear volume is proportional to A, so all nuclei have roughly the same density: about 2.3 × 1017 kg/m3. A teaspoon of nuclear matter would weigh hundreds of millions of tonnes.

The Nuclear Force

What holds protons together despite their electrostatic repulsion? The strong nuclear force — a short-range (∼1–2 fm), extremely powerful, charge-independent attractive force between nucleons. It is the residual effect of the colour force between quarks (more on this in Section 11.5). Key features:

  • Short-range: essentially zero beyond about 3 fm.
  • Charge-independent: p-p, n-n, and p-n strong interactions are nearly equal.
  • Saturating: each nucleon interacts mainly with its nearest neighbours, not with all others.
  • Repulsive core: at very short distances (< 0.5 fm), the force becomes repulsive, preventing nucleons from collapsing.

Binding Energy

The mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of its parts. That "missing mass" (the mass defect Δm) appears as binding energy via Einstein's E = mc2:

B = [Zmp + Nmn − mnucleus] c2

The quantity that matters for nuclear stability is the binding energy per nucleon, B/A. Plot it against A and you get one of the most important curves in all of physics.

Iron-56 (and its neighbour Ni-62) sits at the very peak — about 8.79 MeV per nucleon. This is the most tightly bound nucleus. Everything in nuclear energy comes down to this curve:

  • Fusion of light nuclei (left side of peak): climbing the curve releases energy. This is what powers the Sun.
  • Fission of heavy nuclei (right side of peak): sliding down the curve also releases energy. This is what powers nuclear reactors.

11.3 · Radioactive Decay

Unstable nuclei transform spontaneously, emitting radiation. This is radioactivity, discovered by Becquerel in 1896 and investigated brilliantly by Marie and Pierre Curie. There are three classic types:

Alpha Decay (α)

A heavy nucleus ejects a 42He nucleus (two protons + two neutrons). The parent loses 4 in mass number and 2 in atomic number:

AZX → A−4Z−2Y + 42He

Alpha particles are heavy, doubly charged, and strongly ionizing. They are stopped by a sheet of paper. The phenomenon is explained by quantum tunnelling: the alpha particle has insufficient energy to classically surmount the nuclear Coulomb barrier, but it tunnels through with a probability that depends exponentially on the barrier height and width. This is why alpha emitters with higher decay energies have shorter half-lives (the Geiger–Nuttall law).

Beta Decay (β)

β− decay: A neutron converts into a proton, emitting an electron and an antineutrino:

n → p + e− + νe

β+ decay: A proton converts into a neutron, emitting a positron and a neutrino. Electron capture: an inner-shell electron is absorbed by a proton. In all cases, the mass number A stays the same; only Z changes by ±1.

Historical note The continuous energy spectrum of beta electrons was a great puzzle. Bohr even considered abandoning energy conservation! Pauli saved the day by postulating the neutrino (1930) — a neutral, nearly massless particle carrying away the "missing" energy. It was not detected until 1956 (Reines and Cowan).

Gamma Decay (γ)

A nucleus in an excited state drops to a lower energy state by emitting a high-energy photon. No change in Z or A. Gamma rays are the most penetrating of the three: they require thick lead or concrete to be attenuated.

The Law of Radioactive Decay

Radioactive decay is a purely statistical process. Each nucleus has a constant probability λ of decaying per unit time. For a large sample of N identical nuclei:

N(t) = N0 e−λt

The half-life t½ is the time for half the nuclei to decay:

t½ = ln 2λ ≈ 0.693λ

"Here is something wonderful: every single nucleus is completely unaware of how long it has been sitting there. It has no memory, no aging. The probability of decay in the next second is exactly the same whether it was created a microsecond ago or a billion years ago. Nature plays dice — truly random dice — at the nuclear level."

11.4 · Nuclear Fission and Fusion

Fission

Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus into two (or more) lighter nuclei, typically releasing 2–3 neutrons and about 200 MeV of energy per event. The classic reaction:

23592U + n → 14156Ba + 9236Kr + 3n + 200 MeV

The released neutrons can trigger further fissions, creating a chain reaction. If on average exactly one neutron from each fission causes another fission, the reaction is critical (steady power). If more than one does, it is supercritical (exponential growth — a bomb or a meltdown). The minimum amount of fissile material needed is called the critical mass.

Indian context India's nuclear programme uses a three-stage strategy: (1) natural uranium in pressurised heavy water reactors (PHWRs), (2) plutonium-239 in fast breeder reactors, and (3) thorium-232/uranium-233 cycle, exploiting India's vast thorium reserves. The Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor (PFBR) at Kalpakkam represents stage 2.

Fusion

Nuclear fusion is the merging of light nuclei into a heavier one. The Sun's core fuses hydrogen into helium through the proton–proton chain:

4 11H → 42He + 2e+ + 2νe + 26.7 MeV

The difficulty of fusion on Earth is the Coulomb barrier: you must bring two positive nuclei close enough (~1 fm) for the strong force to take over, which requires temperatures of ~108 K. Approaches include magnetic confinement (tokamaks like ITER) and inertial confinement (laser-driven implosion).

"The Sun is a gigantic, self-regulating fusion reactor. It has been burning for 4.6 billion years and has enough hydrogen left for another 5 billion. That is engineering on a cosmic scale — and we are trying to build a small copy of it in a laboratory. It is, to put it mildly, not easy."

Key Comparison Fission: heavy → medium nuclei, ~200 MeV per event, radioactive waste, chain reaction. Fusion: light → medium nuclei, ~26.7 MeV per 4H, minimal waste, requires extreme conditions. Per kilogram of fuel, fusion releases roughly 4 times more energy than fission and about 10 million times more than chemical combustion.

11.5 · The Standard Model of Particle Physics

Now we reach the deepest layer we know. The Standard Model is the quantum field theory that describes all known fundamental particles and three of the four fundamental forces (electromagnetic, weak nuclear, strong nuclear — but not gravity). It is arguably the most successful scientific theory ever constructed, tested to 12 decimal places in some predictions.

"The Standard Model is not the final answer. It is too complicated, with too many arbitrary numbers — 19 free parameters that we just have to measure. A truly fundamental theory should explain those numbers. But let me tell you: it works. Boy, does it work. It has predicted every particle experiment for fifty years, and every time we hoped it would fail (so we could find something deeper), it stubbornly refused."

The Particle Zoo, Organised

All matter is built from two families of spin-½ particles (fermions):

  • Quarks (6 flavours, in 3 generations): experience all four forces. They carry fractional electric charge (+2/3 or −1/3) and colour charge (red, green, blue). Quarks are never found alone — they are confined inside hadrons (protons, neutrons, pions, etc.).
  • Leptons (6 flavours, in 3 generations): do not feel the strong force. The electron, muon, and tau each come with a corresponding neutrino.

Forces are mediated by spin-1 particles (gauge bosons):

  • Photon (γ) — electromagnetic force, massless.
  • W± and Z0 — weak force, massive (~80–91 GeV).
  • Gluons (8) — strong force, massless, carry colour charge themselves.

And finally, the spin-0 Higgs boson (discovered at CERN in 2012), the quantum of the Higgs field that gives mass to the W, Z, and fermions through the Higgs mechanism.

Quarks and Hadrons

Quarks combine according to the rules of quantum chromodynamics (QCD):

  • Baryons = three quarks (qqq). Example: proton = uud, neutron = udd.
  • Mesons = quark + antiquark (qq). Example: pion π+ = ud.

The "colour" charge comes in three varieties (red, green, blue), and all observed particles must be "colour-neutral" — either rgb (baryons) or colour-anticolour (mesons). This is confinement: you can never isolate a single quark. Pull two quarks apart and the energy in the colour field creates new quark-antiquark pairs before they separate — you just get more hadrons.

Common exam mistake Do not confuse "colour charge" with actual colour. It is a quantum number with three values that happen to be labelled red, green, blue by analogy. It has nothing to do with visible light.

The Electroweak Unification

Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam showed (1960s–70s) that the electromagnetic and weak forces are different manifestations of a single electroweak force. At low energies, the W and Z bosons are massive (acquired through the Higgs mechanism) and the weak force is short-ranged. At very high energies (> ~100 GeV), the symmetry is restored and both forces behave alike.

To study the smallest things, we need the highest energies (remember de Broglie: smaller wavelength = higher momentum = finer resolution). Particle accelerators use electric fields to push charged particles to enormous energies and magnetic fields to bend them in circles or focus them.

"You accelerate protons to nearly the speed of light, smash them together, and look at what comes out. It is the most expensive way to do archaeology ever invented — but we are digging into the structure of spacetime itself."

11.7 · Conservation Laws in Particle Physics

Symmetries of Nature dictate what reactions can occur. Every conservation law corresponds (via Noether's theorem) to a symmetry. In particle physics, the key conserved quantities are:

QuantitySymbolConserved in
Energy-momentumE, pAll interactions
Electric chargeQAll interactions
Baryon numberBAll interactions (so far observed)
Lepton numberLe, Lμ, LτApproximately; neutrino oscillations violate individual L but total L is conserved
Colour charger, g, bStrong interaction (all final states are colour-neutral)
IsospinI, I3Strong interaction only
StrangenessSStrong and EM; violated in weak decays (ΔS = ±1)
Parity (P)—Strong and EM; violated in weak interactions (Wu experiment, 1957)
CP—Nearly conserved; tiny violations observed in kaon and B-meson systems
Key Principle Any reaction not forbidden by a conservation law will occur. This is the guiding maxim of particle physics. If you can write a reaction that conserves all relevant quantum numbers, Nature will find a way to make it happen (given sufficient energy). The rate may be tiny, but it will not be zero.

Feynman Diagrams

Feynman diagrams are pictorial representations of particle interactions. They are not just cartoons — each diagram corresponds to a precise mathematical expression (a term in a perturbative expansion). Straight lines represent fermions, wavy lines represent photons, curly lines represent gluons, and dashed lines represent the Higgs. At each vertex, charge, flavour, and other quantum numbers are conserved.

"I invented these diagrams because I am too lazy to write down all the integrals. But they have a beauty of their own. You can see the physics in the picture: an electron comes in, emits a photon, and another electron absorbs it. That is electromagnetism, drawn with a few lines."

11.8 · Beyond the Standard Model

The Standard Model, for all its success, leaves deep questions unanswered:

  • Gravity is not included. There is no quantum theory of gravity yet. General relativity and quantum mechanics are the two pillars of physics, and they refuse to merge.
  • Dark matter (~27% of the universe's energy) does not correspond to any Standard Model particle.
  • Dark energy (~68%) drives the accelerating expansion of the universe. Its nature is unknown.
  • Neutrino masses: The original Standard Model assumed massless neutrinos, but neutrino oscillation experiments (Super-Kamiokande 1998, SNO 2001) proved they have tiny masses. The mechanism is unknown.
  • Matter-antimatter asymmetry: Why is there more matter than antimatter in the universe? CP violation in the Standard Model is too small to explain this.
  • The hierarchy problem: Why is the Higgs mass (~125 GeV) so much lighter than the Planck mass (~1019 GeV)? Quantum corrections should drive it up enormously.

These open questions drive current research in particle physics, cosmology, and quantum gravity. Proposed solutions include supersymmetry (SUSY), extra dimensions, string theory, loop quantum gravity, and grand unified theories (GUTs) — none yet confirmed by experiment.

"We are at the frontier. Behind us lies a century of stunning discoveries: the atom, the nucleus, quarks, the Higgs. Before us lies the unknown. The greatest adventure in physics is still ahead. And if you are a young student reading this, the job of finding the next layer is yours."

Investigation

Carbon-14 Dating

Living organisms maintain a constant ratio of 14C to 12C by exchanging carbon with the atmosphere. When an organism dies, the 14C decays with t½ = 5730 years and is not replenished.

Problem: A wooden artefact from an archaeological site in Harappa shows 14C activity that is 58.2% of the activity in a living tree. Estimate the age of the artefact.

Solution: From N = N0 e−λt, we have N/N0 = 0.582. Taking the natural logarithm:

t = − ln(0.582)λ = − ln(0.582) × t½ln 2 = − (−0.541)(5730)0.693 ≈ 4470 years

This is consistent with the mature Harappan civilisation period (~2600–1900 BCE).

Chapter Summary Atomic models evolved from Thomson's plum pudding through Rutherford's nucleus and Bohr's quantized orbits to the full quantum-mechanical description with orbitals and four quantum numbers. Nuclear physics revealed that nucleons are bound by the strong force, with binding energy per nucleon peaking at iron-56. Radioactive decay follows exponential statistics with a characteristic half-life. Fission splits heavy nuclei; fusion joins light ones; both release energy by moving toward the iron peak. The Standard Model classifies all known particles into quarks, leptons, gauge bosons, and the Higgs boson, describing three of the four fundamental forces. Conservation laws (charge, baryon number, lepton number, strangeness, etc.) govern which reactions are allowed. Despite its extraordinary success, the Standard Model does not include gravity, dark matter, dark energy, or neutrino masses — the frontier remains wide open.
