Chapter 08
The nature of light

Optics

Light does something nobody expects: it bends when it changes medium, and it splits into colors. Why? Let me show you. Stand outside after a rainstorm. The sun is low, behind you, and you look up and there it is — a rainbow. You have seen it a hundred times and yet each time it takes your breath away. But what is actually happening? Sunlight enters a raindrop, slows down, bends, bounces off the back of the drop, bends again on the way out, and — because every wavelength of light slows down by a slightly different amount — the white light fans out into the whole visible spectrum. Red on the outside, violet on the inside. That is optics in a single raindrop. Everything else in this chapter is just finding the rules that govern that bending, that splitting, that bouncing, and then pushing those rules until we discover interference, diffraction, and polarization — phenomena so subtle they forced us to admit that light is a wave.

"Nature uses only the longest threads to weave her patterns, so that each small piece of her fabric reveals the organisation of the entire tapestry." That applies beautifully to light. The same principles that explain a rainbow explain fibre optics, camera lenses, laser interferometers, and the iridescence of a soap bubble.

8.1   Reflection

Let us start with the simplest thing light can do: bounce. When a light ray hits a smooth surface — a polished mirror, a calm lake — it reflects. The rule is almost embarrassingly simple:

Law of Reflection The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, and the incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the surface all lie in the same plane.
θi = θr

Both angles are measured from the normal — the line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence. Not from the surface itself. Students trip on this constantly; do not be one of them.

Why does the angle equal the angle? If you really want to get to the bottom of it, there is a beautiful argument due to Fermat: light takes the path of least time. You can show, using a little calculus, that the only path from point A to the mirror to point B that minimises total travel time is the one where the two angles are equal. Nature is lazy — or efficient, depending on your temperament.

Specular vs. diffuse reflection

A mirror gives you a clear image because the surface is smooth on the scale of a light wavelength (~500 nm). A sheet of paper is white because it reflects almost all wavelengths, but in random directions — diffuse reflection. The law of reflection holds at each tiny facet; it is the random orientations of those facets that scatter the light.

8.2   Refraction & Snell’s Law

Now for the bending. When light crosses from one transparent medium to another — air to glass, water to air — it changes speed. In vacuum, light travels at c = 3 × 108 m/s. In glass, it is about 2 × 108 m/s. We define the refractive index of a medium:

n = cv

For vacuum, n = 1. For air, n ≈ 1.0003 (so we often call it 1). For water, n ≈ 1.33. For crown glass, n ≈ 1.52. For diamond, n ≈ 2.42.

When a ray crosses the boundary at an angle, it bends. The precise relationship was discovered experimentally by Willebrord Snellius in 1621:

Snell’s Law
n1 sin θ1 = n2 sin θ2
where θ1 is the angle of incidence and θ2 is the angle of refraction, both measured from the normal.

When light enters a denser medium (n2 > n1), it bends toward the normal. When it enters a less dense medium, it bends away from the normal. Simple enough, but the consequences are staggering.

You can derive Snell's law from Fermat's principle too. Light takes the fastest path between two points, and when one medium is slower, the optimal strategy is to spend less distance in the slow medium — exactly like a lifeguard running along the beach and then swimming to save a drowning swimmer. The lifeguard instinctively bends their path at the water's edge. Light does the same thing, and the math gives you exactly n1 sin θ1 = n2 sin θ2.

8.3   Total Internal Reflection

Here is where the bending story gets dramatic. When light travels from a denser medium to a rarer one (n1 > n2), the refracted ray bends away from the normal. As you increase the angle of incidence, the refracted angle grows faster. At some critical angle, the refracted ray would have to bend 90 degrees — it would skim along the surface. Beyond that angle, no refraction occurs at all: the light bounces back entirely. This is total internal reflection (TIR).

Critical Angle Set θ2 = 90° in Snell's law:
sin θc = n2n1
Valid only when n1 > n2. For glass-to-air (n = 1.52): θc ≈ 41.1°.

Total internal reflection is not some exotic lab curiosity. Every optical fibre in every internet cable on the planet works by TIR. Light enters one end of a thin glass fibre, hits the walls at angles greater than the critical angle, bounces and bounces and bounces, and shoots out the other end — carrying your video call across an ocean. The entire modern internet is, at its core, a triumph of Snell's law.

Investigation

Measuring the critical angle

Take a semicircular glass block and a ray box. Shine a ray from the curved side toward the flat face (so it enters perpendicular and does not refract on entry). Rotate the ray box slowly, increasing the angle at the flat face. Note the angle at which the refracted ray vanishes and only the reflected ray remains. This is θc. Use it to compute nglass = 1 / sin θc.

8.4   Mirrors — Concave & Convex

Flat mirrors are boring — they just flip left and right (technically, front and back). Curved mirrors are where the magic happens. A concave mirror (or converging mirror) curves inward like the inside of a spoon. A convex mirror curves outward like the back of a spoon.

Key definitions

  • Centre of curvature (C): The centre of the sphere the mirror is a slice of.
  • Radius of curvature (R): The radius of that sphere.
  • Pole (P): The centre of the mirror surface.
  • Principal axis: The line through C and P.
  • Focal point (F): Midway between C and P for a spherical mirror. f = R/2.

The Mirror Equation

Mirror / Thin Lens Equation
1v + 1u = 1f
Magnification:   m = − vu

Sign convention (New Cartesian): Distances are measured from the pole. Distances in the direction of incident light are positive. For a concave mirror, f is negative (focal point is in front); for convex, f is positive. Object distance u is always negative (object is in front). A negative v means a real image (in front of mirror); positive v means virtual (behind).

I know, I know — sign conventions are the bane of every optics student. Here is my advice: pick one convention, stick with it, and always draw the ray diagram first. The diagram never lies. If your equation gives you one sign and the diagram says the image is on the other side, you made an algebra mistake. Trust the diagram.

Ray diagram rules (concave mirror)

  1. A ray parallel to the principal axis reflects through F.
  2. A ray through F reflects parallel to the principal axis.
  3. A ray through C reflects back on itself.

For a convex mirror, the same rules apply, but the focal point and centre of curvature are behind the mirror. All reflected rays diverge — so the image is always virtual, upright, and diminished. That is why convex mirrors are used in vehicle side mirrors: they give a wide field of view (but "objects in mirror are closer than they appear").

8.5   Thin Lenses

A lens does with refraction what a mirror does with reflection: it redirects light rays to form images. A converging lens (convex, thicker in the middle) brings parallel rays to a focal point. A diverging lens (concave, thinner in the middle) spreads parallel rays so they appear to come from a focal point on the same side as the incoming light.

The Thin Lens Equation

The equation is identical in form to the mirror equation:

1v − 1u = 1f

(Note: with the sign convention used here, object distance u is negative when the object is on the same side as the incoming light.) The power of a lens is P = 1/f, measured in dioptres (D) when f is in metres.

Note — Lensmaker’s Equation For a lens of refractive index n with radii of curvature R1 and R2:
1f = (n − 1) ( 1R1 − 1R2 )

Ray diagram rules (converging lens)

  1. A ray parallel to the principal axis refracts through F on the other side.
  2. A ray through the optical centre passes straight through undeviated.
  3. A ray through F on the object side refracts parallel to the axis.

8.6   Wave Optics — When Rays Are Not Enough

Everything we have done so far — reflection, refraction, mirrors, lenses — can be explained by treating light as rays. This is geometric optics, and it works beautifully when the objects light interacts with are much larger than its wavelength. But when things get small — narrow slits, thin films, fine gratings — light starts doing things that rays cannot explain. It bends around corners. It creates bright and dark bands where you would expect uniform illumination. It proves, beyond any doubt, that light is a wave.

Newton believed light was a stream of particles — "corpuscles," he called them. Huygens said no, it is a wave. They argued. For over a century, Newton's prestige carried the day. Then Thomas Young did his famous experiment, and the wave theory won. (Later, quantum mechanics would say they were both right, in a way that neither could have imagined. But that is Chapter 10.)

Coherence

For interference to be observable, you need coherent sources — sources that maintain a constant phase relationship. Two light bulbs are not coherent; their atoms emit randomly. A single source split into two (as in Young's experiment) is coherent. Lasers are naturally coherent. Coherence length tells you how far along the beam the phase relationship persists.

8.7   Interference — Young’s Double Slit

In 1801, Thomas Young performed one of the most beautiful experiments in physics. He let sunlight fall on a screen with two narrow, closely-spaced slits. On a distant screen, instead of two bright patches, he saw a pattern of alternating bright and dark bands — an interference pattern. This was the smoking gun for the wave nature of light.

Here is how it works. Each slit acts as a source of a new wavefront (by Huygens' principle). The two wavefronts spread out and overlap. At some points, crest meets crest — constructive interference — and the light is bright. At other points, crest meets trough — destructive interference — and the light cancels to darkness.

Young’s Double Slit — Fringe Positions

For two slits separated by distance d, with a screen at distance D:

Bright fringes (constructive):

d sin θ = mλ   (m = 0, ±1, ±2, ...)

Dark fringes (destructive):

d sin θ = (m + ½)λ

Fringe width (for small angles):

β = λ Dd

Notice something remarkable: the fringe width β is inversely proportional to the slit separation d. Bring the slits closer together and the fringes spread farther apart. Make the slits farther apart and the fringes bunch up. This is the signature of wave behaviour.

Worked Example

Double-slit fringe spacing

A pair of slits separated by d = 0.5 mm is illuminated with sodium light (λ = 589 nm). The screen is at D = 1.2 m. Find the fringe width.

β = λ Dd = 589 × 10−9 × 1.20.5 × 10−3 = 1.41 × 10−3 m = 1.41 mm

What I love about Young's experiment is how little you need. Two slits, a light source, and a wall. That is it. And yet it reveals one of the deepest truths about nature: light is a wave. Two centuries later, the same experiment done with single electrons, one at a time, would reveal something even deeper — quantum mechanics. But we are getting ahead of ourselves.

8.8   Diffraction

Interference is what happens when waves from two discrete sources overlap. Diffraction is what happens when a wave encounters an obstacle or a slit comparable in size to its wavelength — every point on the wavefront acts as a source (Huygens' principle), and all those sources interfere with each other.

Single-slit diffraction

When monochromatic light passes through a single narrow slit of width a, the intensity pattern on a distant screen shows a broad central maximum flanked by much weaker secondary maxima, separated by dark minima.

Single-Slit Diffraction Minima
a sin θ = mλ   (m = ±1, ±2, ±3, ...   minima only)
The central maximum has angular width 2λ/a. It is twice as wide as the secondary maxima.

The central maximum contains about 84% of the total light energy. As the slit narrows (smaller a), the pattern spreads out. As the slit widens, the pattern shrinks. In the limit of a very wide slit, you get geometric optics back — just a bright band with sharp edges.

Diffraction gratings

A diffraction grating is a plate with thousands of parallel slits (or grooves) per centimetre. Each slit acts as a source, and the interference of light from all those sources produces extremely sharp, bright lines at specific angles.

Grating Equation
d sin θ = mλ   (m = 0, ±1, ±2, ...)
where d is the grating spacing (distance between adjacent slits). With N slits, the principal maxima have intensity proportional to N2 and angular width proportional to 1/N.

Gratings are used in spectrometers to split light into its component wavelengths with far better resolving power than a prism. The resolving power of a grating is R = mN, where m is the order and N is the total number of slits illuminated. A grating with 5000 lines illuminated in second order has R = 10,000 — it can distinguish wavelengths differing by 0.05 nm in the visible range.

This is why astronomers love diffraction gratings. Point a telescope at a star, pass the light through a grating, and you get a spectrum — sharp emission and absorption lines that tell you what elements the star is made of, how fast it is moving (Doppler shift), and even its temperature. All from a few grooves on a piece of glass. That is the power of wave optics.

8.9   Polarization

Here is something that interference and diffraction alone could not tell you: light is a transverse wave. The electric field oscillates perpendicular to the direction of travel. Polarization is the phenomenon that proves this and exploits it.

In unpolarised light (from the sun, a bulb), the electric field vector vibrates in all directions perpendicular to the propagation. A polariser (like a Polaroid filter) transmits only the component along one direction — the transmission axis. The emerging light is linearly polarised.

Malus’s Law When linearly polarised light of intensity I0 passes through a second polariser (analyser) at angle θ to the first:
I = I0 cos2 θ
At θ = 0°: full transmission. At θ = 90°: zero transmission (crossed polarisers).

Polarisation by reflection — Brewster’s angle

When light reflects off a transparent surface at a particular angle, the reflected beam is completely polarised. This happens when the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular:

tan θB = n2n1

For air-glass (n = 1.5): θB ≈ 56.3°. This is why polarised sunglasses reduce glare — light reflected from roads, water, and glass at near-Brewster angles is partially polarised horizontally, and the sunglasses block horizontal polarisation.

Other types of polarisation

  • Circular polarisation: The E-field vector rotates as the wave travels, tracing a circle. Produced by passing linearly polarised light through a quarter-wave plate.
  • Elliptical polarisation: The general case. The E-field traces an ellipse.
  • Polarisation by scattering: Sunlight scattered by atmospheric molecules is partially polarised. Maximum polarisation at 90° to the sun. This is why a polarising filter darkens a blue sky most when pointed 90° from the sun.
  • Double refraction (birefringence): Crystals like calcite split an unpolarised beam into two polarised beams with different refractive indices (ordinary and extraordinary rays). Place a calcite crystal on printed text and you see double.

Here is a party trick. Take two polarising filters. Look through both — you can see through clearly when they are aligned. Rotate one to 90° — everything goes dark (crossed polarisers, Malus's law gives zero). Now here is the weird part: insert a third polariser at 45° between the crossed pair, and light comes through again! How? The first filter polarises at 0°. The middle one at 45° lets through cos2(45°) = 50%. That 50% is now polarised at 45°, so the last filter (at 90°) lets through cos2(45°) = 50% of that. You end up with 25% of the original intensity coming through three filters, where two filters alone gave zero. Beautiful and counterintuitive.

8.10   Optical Instruments — A Quick Tour

With mirrors and lenses understood, we can build instruments. Here is the essential physics.

The human eye

The cornea and eye lens form a converging system that focuses light on the retina. The least distance of distinct vision (near point) is about 25 cm for a normal eye. The eye accommodates by changing the curvature of the lens (not by moving it, as in a camera). Defects:

  • Myopia (short-sightedness): image forms before retina. Corrected with a diverging lens.
  • Hypermetropia (long-sightedness): image forms behind retina. Corrected with a converging lens.
  • Astigmatism: cornea is not spherically symmetric. Corrected with cylindrical lenses.

Simple magnifier

A converging lens used so the object is within the focal length. The virtual, magnified, upright image forms at or beyond the near point. Magnifying power:

M = 1 + Df   (image at near point)   or   M = Df   (image at infinity)

where D = 25 cm (near point).

Compound microscope

Two converging lenses: the objective (short f) and the eyepiece (longer f). The objective forms a real, magnified, inverted image inside the focal length of the eyepiece. The eyepiece then acts as a magnifier.

M = Lfo × Dfe

where L is the tube length (distance between the focal points of the two lenses).

Astronomical telescope

Two converging lenses: objective (long f) and eyepiece (short f). In normal adjustment (final image at infinity), the separation equals fo + fe.

M = fofe
Resolving Power The minimum angular separation that a telescope (or any circular aperture) can resolve is given by the Rayleigh criterion:
θmin = 1.22 λD
where D is the aperture diameter. Bigger telescopes resolve finer detail — that is the primary reason we build large telescopes, not just to collect more light (though that matters too).

8.11   Dispersion — Back to the Rainbow

We started with a rainbow, so let us close the loop. Dispersion is the dependence of refractive index on wavelength. In most transparent materials, violet light (shorter wavelength) has a higher refractive index than red light (longer wavelength). When white light enters a prism, each colour refracts by a different amount, and the beam fans out into a spectrum.

Cauchy’s Equation (approximate)
n(λ) = A + Bλ2 + Cλ4 + ···
Constants A, B, C are material-specific. Because shorter wavelengths yield larger n, violet bends more than red.

The dispersive power of a prism material is defined as:

ω = nV − nRnY − 1

where nV, nR, nY are the refractive indices for violet, red, and yellow (mean) wavelengths.

A rainbow is a natural spectrometer. Each raindrop acts like a tiny prism. Sunlight enters, refracts (disperses), reflects off the back of the drop, refracts again on exit. The primary rainbow at about 42° from the antisolar point has red on the outside, violet inside. The secondary rainbow (about 51°) has the colours reversed because the light reflects twice inside the drop. Between the two bows is Alexander's dark band — a region where no light from the raindrops reaches your eye. Next time you see a double rainbow, look for the dark band between them. You will never unsee it.

We have come a long way — from a raindrop to a diffraction grating, from Snell to Huygens to Young. The story of optics is the story of learning to take light seriously as a wave, and then learning that even that is not the whole story. In Chapter 10 we will see that light also comes in little packets called photons, and the full truth about light is stranger and more wonderful than either Newton or Huygens could have guessed. But for now, go outside, find a rainbow, and appreciate the beautiful physics hiding in plain sight.
