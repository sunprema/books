The edge of knowledge

Frontiers & Unsolved Questions

I would rather have questions that can’t be answered than answers that can’t be questioned. Here’s what keeps physicists up at night. After thirteen chapters of things we do know — Newton’s laws, Maxwell’s equations, quantum mechanics, general relativity, the Standard Model — you might imagine the story is nearly finished. It isn’t. Not by a long shot. The most exciting part of physics is what we don’t understand, and I want to be honest with you about that. The purpose of this final chapter is not to hand you neat answers, but to show you where the frontier is — because that is where you come in.

“People say to me, ‘Are you looking for the ultimate laws of physics?’ No, I’m not. I’m just looking to find out more about the world, and if it turns out there is a simple ultimate law which explains everything, so be it — that would be very nice to discover.”

14.1   The Great Divorce: Quantum Gravity

Here is the central embarrassment of modern physics. We have two spectacularly successful theories: general relativity (GR), which describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime, and quantum mechanics (QM), which describes everything else at tiny scales. Both are tested to extraordinary precision. And they are fundamentally incompatible.

GR says spacetime is a smooth, continuous fabric that bends and stretches. QM says everything — energy, angular momentum, charge — comes in discrete lumps and fluctuates wildly at very short distances. When you try to apply quantum rules to gravity itself, you get infinities that cannot be tamed by the usual tricks (renormalisation). The mathematics breaks down at a scale called the Planck length:

lP = √(ℏG)c3/2 ≈ 1.6 × 10−35 m

That is about 1020 times smaller than a proton — hopelessly beyond any experiment we can do today. Yet this is precisely where a theory of quantum gravity is needed: inside black holes, at the Big Bang, whenever you squeeze too much energy into too small a space.

Key Idea General relativity and quantum mechanics are individually verified to extraordinary precision, but they give contradictory answers in extreme conditions. A unified theory of quantum gravity is the great unsolved problem.

The situation reminds me of a fellow trying to fit a cat into a box that’s slightly too small — whichever end you push in, the other end pops out. We haven’t found the right box yet.

14.2   String Theory

One candidate box is string theory. The basic idea is radical: instead of treating elementary particles as zero-dimensional points, imagine them as tiny one-dimensional vibrating strings. Different vibration modes of the same fundamental string produce different particles — just as different vibration modes of a sitar string produce different musical notes.

The beautiful thing is that one particular vibration mode automatically gives you a massless spin-2 particle — which is exactly what a graviton (the quantum of gravity) must be. So string theory naturally includes gravity, which is why theorists got so excited about it.

Note String theory requires extra dimensions of space — six or seven beyond the three we see — curled up so tightly we cannot detect them with current experiments. The total number of spacetime dimensions is 10 (or 11 in M-theory).

The difficulty is that string theory has so far produced no testable prediction that distinguishes it from other approaches. The “landscape” of possible solutions is estimated at 10500 — an absurdly large number of possible universes, each with different physical constants. Critics call it unfalsifiable. Supporters say it is the only consistent framework we have for quantum gravity. The jury remains out.

14.3   Loop Quantum Gravity

A rival approach is loop quantum gravity (LQG). Instead of adding new ingredients (strings, extra dimensions), LQG takes general relativity seriously and directly quantises spacetime itself. The result is a granular spacetime — space is not infinitely divisible but comes in discrete chunks, like atoms of geometry. The smallest possible volume is roughly the Planck volume, about (10−35 m)3.

In LQG, these chunks of space are described by mathematical objects called spin networks — graphs whose nodes carry quantum numbers representing volume and whose edges carry quantum numbers representing area. The geometry of space emerges from these networks the way the smooth surface of a fabric emerges from a weave of threads.

String Theory vs. LQG String theory modifies the matter side — it replaces point particles with strings and demands extra dimensions. LQG modifies the geometry side — it quantises spacetime directly, with no extra dimensions required. Both are incomplete, and neither has experimental confirmation yet.

14.4   The Dark Sector

Dark Matter

One of the most astonishing discoveries of modern astrophysics is that about 85% of all matter in the universe is dark matter — stuff that exerts gravitational pull but does not emit, absorb, or scatter light. We know it is there because galaxies rotate too fast, galaxy clusters are too heavy, and the cosmic microwave background has a particular pattern of fluctuations that requires it. But we do not know what it is.

Dark Energy

If dark matter is mysterious, dark energy is downright baffling. In 1998, two teams (the Supernova Cosmology Project and the High-z Supernova Search Team) discovered that the expansion of the universe is accelerating. Something is pushing the universe apart, acting like a repulsive gravity. This “something” constitutes about 68% of the total energy content of the universe.

The simplest explanation is Einstein’s cosmological constant Λ — a constant energy density filling all of space. But when you try to calculate Λ from quantum field theory (summing zero-point energies of all fields), you get a number that is roughly 10120 times too large. This is arguably the worst prediction in the history of physics, and it goes by the name of the cosmological constant problem.

ρobserved ≈ 10−120 × ρQFT prediction
The 120-Order-of-Magnitude Problem The mismatch between the observed dark energy density and the naive quantum field theory prediction is the largest discrepancy between theory and experiment in all of science. Nobody knows how to resolve it.

We used to wonder what the universe is made of. Now we know: 5% ordinary matter, 27% dark matter, 68% dark energy. In other words, 95% of the universe is stuff we do not understand. That’s not a failure — it’s an invitation.

14.6   The Measurement Problem

You learned in Chapter 10 that quantum mechanics gives you probabilities of outcomes, not certainties. The wave function ψ evolves smoothly and deterministically via the Schrödinger equation — until you make a measurement, at which point it seems to “collapse” to a definite value. But what counts as a measurement? Where does “quantum” end and “classical” begin?

This is the measurement problem, and after nearly a century, there is no consensus. The main interpretations are:

InterpretationWhat it saysImplications
CopenhagenWave function collapse is a real process triggered by observationDraws an arbitrary line between quantum and classical realms
Many-WorldsNo collapse; the universe splits into branches for each outcomeAll possible outcomes happen in parallel realities
DecoherenceInteraction with the environment selects a preferred basis; collapse is apparent, not fundamentalExplains why we don’t see superpositions of cats but doesn’t pick a single outcome
Pilot-wave (Bohmian)Particles have definite positions guided by a “pilot wave”Deterministic but nonlocal; reproduces all quantum predictions

I think I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics. The question is not “Do you understand it?” but “Which way of not understanding it do you prefer?”

14.7   Quantum Computing and Qubits

One frontier where quantum weirdness becomes a practical resource is quantum computing. A classical bit is either 0 or 1. A qubit can be in a superposition of both:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩,   |α|2 + |β|2 = 1

The amplitudes α and β are complex numbers. Any single-qubit pure state can be parameterised by two angles θ and φ on the Bloch sphere:

|ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + eiφ sin(θ/2)|1⟩

The top of the sphere is |0⟩, the bottom is |1⟩, the equator holds equal-superposition states, and every other point is some intermediate superposition. Try the interactive below:

Key Idea The power of quantum computing comes from superposition (a qubit can be in many states simultaneously), entanglement (qubits can be correlated in ways impossible classically), and interference (amplitudes can cancel or reinforce). For certain problems — factoring large numbers, simulating quantum systems, optimising — quantum algorithms can be exponentially faster than classical ones.

India has launched the National Quantum Mission (2023), with a budget of Rs 6,003 crore, aiming to build 50–1000 qubit quantum computers by 2031 and develop quantum communication networks, quantum sensors, and a domestic supply chain for quantum hardware.

14.8   Neutrino Oscillations and Mass

The Standard Model originally assumed neutrinos were massless. Then came a stunning discovery: neutrinos change flavour as they travel. An electron neutrino (νe) produced in the Sun can arrive at Earth as a muon neutrino (νμ) or tau neutrino (ντ). This phenomenon, called neutrino oscillation, is only possible if neutrinos have mass — contradicting the original Standard Model.

The oscillation probability depends on the difference in squared masses Δm2 and a set of mixing angles. For two flavours, the survival probability is:

P(να → να) = 1 − sin2(2θ) sin2&bigl(Δm2L / 4E&bigr)

Here L is the distance travelled and E is the neutrino energy. The India-based Neutrino Observatory (INO), proposed for a cavern in the Bodi West Hills of Tamil Nadu, was designed to study atmospheric neutrinos and measure the neutrino mass hierarchy — whether ν3 is the heaviest or lightest mass eigenstate. Though INO has faced delays, understanding neutrino mass remains a top priority in Indian physics.

Open Questions on Neutrinos Are neutrinos their own antiparticles (Majorana particles)? What is the absolute mass scale? Why are their masses so tiny compared to other fermions? Is there a fourth “sterile” neutrino? Each question connects to deeper puzzles about the Standard Model.

14.9   Matter–Antimatter Asymmetry (Baryogenesis)

The Big Bang should have produced equal amounts of matter and antimatter. When matter meets antimatter, they annihilate into pure energy. Yet here you are, made entirely of matter, reading this on a planet made of matter, in a galaxy made of matter. Where did all the antimatter go?

This is the problem of baryogenesis. Sakharov (1967) showed that three conditions must be met for a matter-antimatter asymmetry to develop:

  1. Baryon number violation — processes must exist that create more baryons than antibaryons.
  2. C and CP violation — the laws of physics must not be perfectly symmetric between matter and antimatter.
  3. Departure from thermal equilibrium — these processes must occur out of equilibrium.

The Standard Model satisfies all three in principle, but the amount of CP violation it contains is far too small to explain the observed asymmetry. Something beyond the Standard Model is needed. This is one of the deepest open questions in physics and cosmology.

14.10   The Hierarchy Problem

Why is gravity so absurdly weak compared to the other forces? You can hold up a paperclip with a small magnet, easily defeating the gravitational pull of the entire Earth. In numbers, gravity is about 1038 times weaker than electromagnetism.

In the Standard Model, this shows up as the hierarchy problem: why is the Higgs boson mass (~125 GeV) so much lighter than the Planck mass (~1019 GeV)? Quantum corrections should push the Higgs mass up to the Planck scale unless there is an extraordinarily precise cancellation — a “fine-tuning” to about one part in 1034. Physicists find such fine-tuning suspicious.

Proposed solutions include supersymmetry (every particle has a heavier partner whose quantum corrections cancel the dangerous ones), extra dimensions (gravity is diluted because it spreads into extra spatial dimensions), and compositeness (the Higgs is not elementary but made of deeper constituents). The LHC has so far found none of these, deepening the puzzle.

14.11   The Multiverse Hypothesis

If string theory allows 10500 possible vacuum states, and if eternal inflation can realise all of them in different regions of space, then our observable universe may be just one “bubble” in an infinite multiverse. In other bubbles, the laws of physics — the particle masses, the strengths of forces, the number of dimensions — could be completely different.

This idea is both thrilling and troubling. It could explain why our universe’s physical constants appear fine-tuned for the existence of structure (stars, planets, life) — we simply live in a bubble where the constants happen to permit observers. This is the anthropic principle.

Warning The multiverse hypothesis is currently untestable by direct observation. Many physicists regard it as philosophy rather than science. Others argue it is a legitimate consequence of our best theories and that indirect tests (statistical predictions for cosmological parameters) may eventually be possible. The debate is lively and unresolved.

It doesn’t matter how beautiful your theory is, it doesn’t matter how smart you are — if it doesn’t agree with experiment, it’s wrong. The multiverse makes that test very difficult to apply. That worries me.

14.13   India at the Frontier

Indian physics has a distinguished history — from Raman’s discovery of inelastic light scattering to Bose’s work on quantum statistics to Chandrasekhar’s limit for white dwarfs. Today, Indian research is actively contributing to the global frontier:

ProjectWhat it doesWhy it matters
LIGO-India A gravitational-wave observatory in Aundha Nagnath, Maharashtra, part of the global LIGO network. Planned for first observations around 2030. A third detector in India dramatically improves sky localisation of gravitational-wave sources — from hundreds of square degrees to tens. This enables rapid electromagnetic follow-up, letting astronomers see the light from merging neutron stars.
INO India-based Neutrino Observatory. A 50,000-tonne magnetised iron calorimeter detector, proposed for the Bodi West Hills, Tamil Nadu. Aims to determine the neutrino mass hierarchy by studying atmospheric neutrinos. Would make India a key player in neutrino physics.
AstroSat India’s first multi-wavelength space telescope (launched 2015), observing in UV, optical, and X-ray bands simultaneously. Studies neutron stars, black holes, active galaxies, and star formation — providing data relevant to extreme-gravity physics and high-energy astrophysics.
Aditya-L1 ISRO’s solar observatory at the Sun-Earth Lagrange point L1 (launched 2023), studying the solar corona, solar wind, and magnetic fields. Solar physics connects to fundamental plasma physics, magnetohydrodynamics, and particle acceleration — processes relevant to astrophysical phenomena across the universe.
Chandrayaan & Gaganyaan Lunar exploration missions and India’s human spaceflight programme. Beyond engineering, these missions carry scientific payloads studying lunar geology, the solar wind, and testing technologies for future deep-space physics experiments.
National Quantum Mission Rs 6,003 crore initiative (2023–2031) to develop quantum computing, communication, sensing, and materials in India. Aims to position India among the leading nations in quantum technology, with applications in cryptography, drug discovery, and materials science.
For the Indian Student You are entering physics at an extraordinary time. Major new facilities are being built in India, international collaborations welcome Indian researchers, and the questions that remain are among the most profound ever asked. The tools you have learned in this book — classical mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum mechanics, relativity — are exactly the foundation you need to contribute.

14.14   The Joy of Not Knowing

“I can live with doubt and uncertainty and not knowing. I think it’s much more interesting to live not knowing than to have answers which might be wrong.”

We have come to the end of this book, but certainly not to the end of physics. Let me be honest: we do not know what dark matter is. We do not know why the cosmological constant is what it is. We do not know how to quantise gravity. We do not know what happens to information inside a black hole. We do not know why there are three generations of fermions. We do not know whether the universe is unique or one of infinitely many.

And that is wonderful.

The history of physics teaches us that the deepest puzzles are not dead ends — they are doors. Newton’s inability to explain the perihelion of Mercury was not a failure; it was a signpost pointing toward general relativity. The ultraviolet catastrophe was not a defeat; it was the birth announcement of quantum mechanics. Every crisis in understanding has, so far, led to a deeper and more beautiful picture of nature.

You, the BSc student reading this in India in the 2020s, have a unique opportunity. The tools exist — particle accelerators, gravitational-wave detectors, space telescopes, quantum computers — and the questions are clear and well-defined. What is missing is the idea. The flash of insight that no amount of computing power can replace. That has to come from a human mind. Perhaps yours.

I want to end with something personal. The reason I fell in love with physics was not that it had all the answers. It was that it had the best questions. Every equation we write, every experiment we design, every late night spent staring at a blackboard — it is all in service of a single impulse: the desire to understand. Not to be certain. Not to be right. Just to understand a little more today than we did yesterday.

Nature uses only the longest threads to weave her patterns, so each small piece of her fabric reveals the organisation of the entire tapestry. You have, in the thirteen chapters before this one, learned to read some of those threads. Now go pull on the ones that are still loose.

The universe is not obligated to make sense to you. But it does, piece by piece, if you are patient and honest and willing to be wrong. That is the joy of science — the joy of finding things out.

The imagination of nature is far, far greater than the imagination of man.
