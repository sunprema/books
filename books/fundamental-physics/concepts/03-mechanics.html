Chapter 03
Forces and motion

Mechanics

Hold a ball at arm's length and let go. It falls. Every human being who ever lived has seen this happen. But Newton had the courage to say something extraordinary: "I don't know why it falls — but I can tell you exactly how." That single shift in attitude — from asking "why does nature do this?" to asking "what is the precise rule nature follows?" — is the birth of mechanics, and really, the birth of all modern physics.

In this chapter we are going to build, piece by piece, the machinery that lets you predict the motion of anything — a cricket ball bowled at 140 km/h, a satellite orbiting the Earth, a gyroscope that refuses to topple. We start with the simplest possible question: if I know where something is right now and how fast it's moving, where will it be one second later?

The trick in physics is always the same: find the right abstraction. A "particle" is not a lie — it is the truth about any object whose size doesn't matter for the question you're asking. A planet is a particle when you're computing its orbit. You are a particle when the question is "how long before you hit the ground?"

1 · Kinematics in One Dimension

Kinematics is the description of motion without worrying about what causes it. We ask: given certain initial conditions, what path does the object follow? The "why" comes later, with Newton's laws.

Position, displacement, velocity, acceleration

Imagine a car on a straight highway. Its position x is a number on a number line (we choose the origin and the positive direction). Displacement is the change in position: Δx = x2 − x1. Unlike distance, displacement has a sign — it tells you the direction.

Average velocity is displacement divided by elapsed time:

vavg = ΔxΔt

and instantaneous velocity is what you get as Δt shrinks to zero — the derivative:

v = dxdt

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity:

a = dvdt = d²xdt²
Notice that acceleration is not "how fast you're going." It is how fast your velocity is changing. You can be moving very fast with zero acceleration (cruise on the highway) or be momentarily at rest with enormous acceleration (a ball at the top of its arc — it's still accelerating downward at g).

The constant-acceleration equations

When acceleration a is constant (for example, free fall near Earth's surface where a = g ≈ 9.8 m/s² downward), the calculus integrates neatly into three workhorse equations:

Key — The Kinematic Equations (constant a)
v = v0 + at
x = x0 + v0t + 12at²
v² = v0² + 2a(x − x0)

These three equations are not three independent facts. They all come from integrating a = constant. The third equation is just the first two with time eliminated — useful when nobody told you when something happened, only where.

Worked Example

Free fall from a temple tower

A stone is dropped (not thrown) from the top of the Brihadeeswarar Temple in Thanjavur, 66 m high. Ignoring air resistance, how long does it take to hit the ground, and how fast is it going when it does?

Take downward as positive: a = 9.8 m/s², v0 = 0, x0 = 0, x = 66 m.

x = 12at²  ⇒  t = √(2 × 66 / 9.8) ≈ 3.67 s
v = at = 9.8 × 3.67 ≈ 36.0 m/s  (≈ 130 km/h)

That is roughly the speed of a fast bowler's delivery — and the stone started from rest!

2 · Projectile Motion

Now here is the beautiful insight: a ball thrown at an angle follows a parabola because its horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent. Horizontally, nothing pushes it (ignore air), so it moves at constant speed. Vertically, gravity pulls it down with constant acceleration g. The two motions combine to give a parabolic arc.

If you launch a projectile at angle θ with speed v0 from ground level:

Key — Projectile Motion Equations
v0x = v0 cos θ     v0y = v0 sin θ
x(t) = v0 cos θ · t
y(t) = v0 sin θ · t − 12gt²
Range = v0² sin 2θg     Max height = v0² sin²θ2g
Here is a lovely fact: the range is maximised at θ = 45°. And for any other angle θ, the same range is achieved at (90° − θ). Try it in the simulation below — launch at 30° and then at 60° and watch them land at the same spot.

In artillery with varying elevation, the optimal angle changes further. Physics gives you the ideal case first; then you add the complications.

3 · Newton's Three Laws of Motion

Kinematics tells you what happens. Dynamics tells you why. Newton compressed the entire "why" of motion into three statements that fit on a postcard. Let us examine them one at a time.

The First Law (Law of Inertia)

Newton's First Law An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in uniform motion stays in uniform motion, unless acted upon by a net external force.

This sounds obvious, but it was revolutionary. Aristotle thought the natural state of an object was rest — that you need a force to keep something moving. Newton said the opposite: the natural state is whatever it was already doing. Force doesn't cause motion; force causes change in motion.

Think of it this way: you are sitting on a bus. The bus brakes suddenly. You lurch forward. But nothing pushed you forward — the bus decelerated, and you kept doing what you were already doing. Inertia is not a force. It is the stubborn tendency of matter to resist changes in velocity.

The First Law also defines what we mean by an inertial reference frame: a frame in which a free particle (no forces) moves in a straight line at constant speed. The surface of the Earth is approximately inertial for most experiments (the corrections due to its rotation are small).

The Second Law

Newton's Second Law
Fnet = ma

The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration. This is the central equation of classical mechanics.

Mass m is the measure of inertia: how hard it is to accelerate an object. A cricket ball (m ≈ 0.16 kg) and a truck (m ≈ 10,000 kg) feel the same gravitational pull per kilogram, but the truck is enormously harder to get moving.

The Second Law is a vector equation — it holds independently in each direction:

Fx = max,    Fy = may,    Fz = maz
I want you to feel the power of this equation. You draw a free-body diagram, add up all the forces, set the sum equal to ma, and out pops the acceleration. From the acceleration you integrate to get velocity, and integrate again to get position. That is the entire programme of classical mechanics.

The Third Law

Newton's Third Law For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. If body A exerts force F on body B, then B exerts force −F on A.

The key subtlety: the action and reaction forces act on different bodies. When you stand on the floor, you push the floor down (your weight), and the floor pushes you up (the normal force). These are not "equal and opposite forces that cancel" — they act on different objects. On your free-body diagram, only the forces on you appear.

Worked Example

Atwood's machine

Two masses m1 = 5 kg and m2 = 3 kg are connected by a light string over a frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration and the tension.

For m1 (heavier, moves down): m1g − T = m1a

For m2 (lighter, moves up): T − m2g = m2a

Adding the two equations eliminates T:

a = (m1 − m2)(m1 + m2)g = 28 × 9.8 = 2.45 m/s²
T = 2m1m2m1 + m2g = 2 × 5 × 38 × 9.8 = 36.75 N

Notice the tension is not equal to either weight — it is between the two. The string accelerates, so the tension must be intermediate.

4 · Work, Energy, and the Work-Energy Theorem

Newton's laws are beautiful, but sometimes they are the hard way to solve a problem. If you want to know how fast a ball is going at the bottom of a ramp, you could integrate the forces along the entire path — or you could use energy, and get the answer in one line.

Energy methods are like having a shortcut through a forest. The Second Law is the main road — it always works, but it can be long and winding. The energy approach cuts straight through, as long as you know the starting and ending points.

Work

The work done by a constant force F on an object that undergoes displacement d is:

W = F · d = Fd cos θ

where θ is the angle between the force and the displacement. If the force is perpendicular to the motion (like the normal force on a flat surface, or gravity on a satellite in circular orbit), it does zero work.

Kinetic energy and the work-energy theorem

Key — Work-Energy Theorem
Wnet = ΔKE = 12mv2 − 12mv02

The net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

This is not a new law — it is a direct consequence of F = ma integrated over displacement. But it repackages the information in a scalar form (no vectors needed!) that is often much easier to work with.

Potential energy

For conservative forces (gravity, springs — forces where the work done depends only on start and end points, not the path), we define a potential energy U such that the work done by the force equals the decrease in potential energy:

Wconservative = −ΔU
ForcePotential EnergyReference
Gravity near EarthU = mghAny convenient height = 0
Spring (Hooke)U = 12kx²Natural length x = 0
Gravity (general)U = −GMmrU → 0 as r → ∞

Conservation of mechanical energy

Key — Conservation of Energy
KE1 + U1 = KE2 + U2

When only conservative forces do work, the total mechanical energy E = KE + U is constant.

This is one of the deepest ideas in all of physics. Energy is not created or destroyed — it just changes form. The ball at the top of a hill has potential energy. As it rolls down, that potential energy converts into kinetic energy. At the bottom, all the potential energy has become motion. The total never changes. This is so important that when physicists found cases where energy seemed to disappear, they invented new forms of energy (heat, chemical, nuclear) until the books balanced again. And the books always balanced.

5 · Momentum and Collisions

Newton originally wrote his second law not as F = ma but as: force is the rate of change of momentum.

F = dpdt    where    p = mv

For constant mass this reduces to F = ma. But the momentum form is more fundamental — it also works when mass changes (a rocket burning fuel) and it carries over into relativity and quantum mechanics.

Conservation of momentum

Key — Conservation of Momentum

When no external force acts on a system, the total momentum is constant:

ptotal = ∑ mivi = constant

This is a direct consequence of Newton's third law: the internal forces between objects in a system cancel in pairs, so only external forces can change the total momentum.

Types of collisions

TypeMomentum conserved?KE conserved?Example
Perfectly elasticYesYesBilliard balls (approximately)
InelasticYesNo — some KE lostCar crash (deformation absorbs energy)
Perfectly inelasticYesMaximum KE lostTwo lumps of clay sticking together
Here is a delightful fact. In a one-dimensional elastic collision between two equal masses, the first stops dead and the second moves off with the first's velocity. You can see this in Newton's cradle: one ball swings in, one ball swings out. Two balls in, two balls out. Why? Because both momentum and kinetic energy must be conserved, and those two constraints together force this particular solution.
Worked Example

The ballistic pendulum

A bullet of mass m = 10 g is fired into a wooden block of mass M = 2 kg hanging from a string. The block (with bullet embedded) swings up to a height h = 5 cm. Find the bullet's initial speed.

Step 1 — Collision (momentum conservation):

mv = (m + M)V

Step 2 — Swing (energy conservation):

12(m + M)V² = (m + M)gh  ⇒  V = √(2gh) = √(2 × 9.8 × 0.05) = 0.99 m/s

From Step 1: v = (m + M)V / m = (2.01)(0.99) / 0.01 = 199 m/s.

This example is beautiful because it uses both conservation laws in sequence — momentum during the fast collision (when external forces don't have time to act), then energy during the slow swing (when no non-conservative forces act).

Impulse

The impulse delivered by a force is the integral of force over time, and it equals the change in momentum:

J = ∫ F dt = Δp

This is why a cricket fielder "gives" with the ball while catching: by extending the time of deceleration, they reduce the peak force, even though the total impulse (change in momentum) is exactly the same.

6 · Rotational Motion

Everything we have done so far treats objects as particles — points with mass. But real objects can also rotate. A spinning top, a rolling wheel, a pirouetting dancer — these all involve rotation about an axis, and we need new variables to describe it.

Here is the good news: rotational mechanics is a perfect mirror of translational mechanics. Every translational quantity has a rotational analogue. Once you see the pattern, you already know the whole subject.

Moment of inertia

The moment of inertia I is the rotational analogue of mass. It depends not just on how much mass there is, but on how far that mass is from the axis of rotation. A hollow cylinder has a larger I than a solid cylinder of the same mass and radius, because its mass is farther from the centre.

Common Moments of Inertia

Solid sphere about diameter: I = (2/5)MR²

Solid cylinder about axis: I = (1/2)MR²

Thin rod about centre: I = (1/12)ML²

Thin hoop about axis: I = MR²

Angular momentum conservation

Key — Conservation of Angular Momentum

When no external torque acts on a system, the total angular momentum is constant:

L = Iω = constant

This is why an ice skater spins faster when she pulls her arms in. Her I decreases (mass moves closer to the axis), so ω must increase to keep L constant. The same principle governs the spin of neutron stars: a collapsing star's I shrinks enormously, and it ends up spinning hundreds of times per second.

7 · Gravitation and Kepler's Laws

We come now to the crown jewel of Newtonian mechanics. Newton's great triumph was showing that the same force that makes an apple fall also holds the Moon in its orbit. The law of universal gravitation:

Key — Newton's Law of Gravitation
F = GMmr²

Every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. G = 6.674 × 10−11 N m² kg−2.

Think about what Newton achieved. He looked at a falling apple and the orbiting Moon and said: same force, different geometry. The apple falls straight because it starts from rest. The Moon "falls" too — but it also has sideways velocity, so by the time it has fallen a certain distance, the ground (which is curved) has receded by the same amount. The Moon is perpetually falling around the Earth. That is what an orbit is.

Gravitational field and acceleration

Near the surface of a planet of mass M and radius R, the gravitational acceleration is:

g = GMR²

For Earth: g ≈ 9.8 m/s². This is why all objects (in vacuum) fall with the same acceleration, regardless of mass — the mass cancels in F = ma.

Kepler's three laws

Before Newton, Johannes Kepler distilled decades of astronomical observations by Tycho Brahe into three empirical laws. Newton later showed that all three follow from his law of gravitation plus his second law.

Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

First Law (Law of Ellipses): Every planet moves in an ellipse with the Sun at one focus.

Second Law (Law of Areas): A line from the Sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times. (Planets move faster when closer to the Sun.)

Third Law (Harmonic Law):

T² = 4π²GM a³

where T is the orbital period and a is the semi-major axis. For a given central body, T² ∝ a³.

Kepler's second law is really a statement about angular momentum conservation in disguise. Gravity is a central force — it always points toward the Sun — so it exerts no torque about the Sun, and the planet's angular momentum is constant. That is why the planet speeds up at perihelion (close to the Sun) and slows down at aphelion (far from the Sun): L = mvr = constant, so when r is small, v must be large.

Orbital velocity and escape velocity

For a circular orbit of radius r around a mass M, the orbital speed is found by setting gravitational force equal to centripetal force:

vorbit = √(GMr)

The escape velocity — the minimum speed needed to leave a gravitational field permanently — is found from energy conservation (set total energy to zero):

vescape = √(2GMr) = √2 · vorbit
Note — ISRO's orbital mechanics When ISRO launched Chandrayaan-3 to the Moon, the spacecraft did not fly in a straight line. It was placed in progressively wider elliptical orbits around Earth (using Kepler's laws!), each burn at perigee adding energy. Once the orbit was elongated enough to reach the Moon, a precisely timed burn transferred it into a lunar orbit. Every stage is a direct application of the equations on this page.

Gravitational potential energy

For two masses separated by distance r:

U = −GMmr

The negative sign means the system is bound. You must add energy (do positive work) to separate the masses. At infinite separation, U = 0 (the reference point).

The total energy of an orbiting body is:

E = KE + U = −GMm2a

where a is the semi-major axis. For a bound orbit, E < 0. For escape, E = 0. For a hyperbolic trajectory (fly-by), E > 0.

Worked Example

Geostationary orbit altitude

A geostationary satellite orbits with T = 24 hours = 86400 s. Find its altitude above Earth.

From Kepler's third law:

a = (GMT²4π²)1/3

With GMEarth = 3.986 × 1014 m³s−2:

a = (3.986 × 1014 × 86400² / 4π²)1/3 ≈ 42,164 km

Altitude = a − REarth = 42,164 − 6,371 ≈ 35,793 km.

That is where India's INSAT communication satellites orbit — always hovering above the same point on the equator.

8 · The Big Picture

Let us step back and see what we have built. Starting from Newton's three laws and the law of gravitation — a total of four statements that fit on a napkin — we derived:

  • The motion of any object under any combination of forces (the Second Law + kinematics)
  • The conservation of energy (from integrating force over displacement)
  • The conservation of momentum (from the Third Law)
  • The conservation of angular momentum (from the absence of torque)
  • Kepler's three laws of planetary motion (from gravitation + the Second Law)
  • Orbital mechanics — how to send spacecraft to the Moon and Mars
This is the power of physics. A handful of principles, relentlessly applied, explain an enormous range of phenomena. We have not invoked any special laws for planets, or for springs, or for collisions. It is all the same machinery — F = ma — pointed at different situations. That is what makes physics beautiful: the extraordinary compression of nature into a few deep ideas.
Caution — The limits of Newtonian mechanics Newton's mechanics works spectacularly well for objects that are (1) much larger than atoms (otherwise use quantum mechanics), (2) moving much slower than light (otherwise use special relativity), and (3) in gravitational fields that are not too strong (otherwise use general relativity). Within those bounds — which cover everything from wristwatch gears to satellite orbits — it is essentially exact. But nature has more to say, and we will hear it in later chapters.

Problem-solving strategy

Strategy — The Mechanics Toolkit
  1. Draw a diagram. Identify the system. Draw all forces (free-body diagram).
  2. Choose your approach:
    • Force approach (F = ma): When you need acceleration, or when forces are constant and time matters.
    • Energy approach: When you know start and end states and want speeds or heights, especially when the path is complicated.
    • Momentum approach: When you have collisions, explosions, or no external forces.
  3. Set up coordinates. Choose axes that simplify the math (e.g., along and perpendicular to an inclined plane).
  4. Write equations. Apply the relevant law. Check units at every step.
  5. Solve and sanity-check. Does the answer have the right units? Is the magnitude reasonable? What happens in extreme cases (mass → 0, angle → 0)?
We have covered a lot of ground in this chapter. The remarkable thing is that none of it is arbitrary — every result follows logically from Newton's laws. In the next chapter, we will see what happens when forces push things back and forth in a repeating pattern. That is the world of waves and oscillations — and it will connect mechanics to sound, light, and eventually quantum mechanics. The adventure continues.
