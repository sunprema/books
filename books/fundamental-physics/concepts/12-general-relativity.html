Curved spacetime

General Relativity

Gravity is not a force. It’s the shape of spacetime itself. That sounds crazy, but let me show you what Einstein meant. You’ve been told since school that the Earth pulls the apple down. Newton said there’s a force, an invisible rope tugging masses together. And it works — beautifully, for three hundred years. But Einstein looked at Newton’s gravity and saw something deeply unsatisfying: how does the Earth “know” the apple is there? How does the force travel across empty space, instantaneously? Newton himself called it “so great an absurdity.” Einstein’s answer was to throw out the force altogether, and replace it with geometry.

The idea is this: mass and energy warp the fabric of spacetime. Objects don’t get “pulled” — they follow the straightest possible paths through curved spacetime. A planet orbiting a star isn’t being yanked around by an invisible tether. It’s rolling along the most natural path in a geometry that the star has bent. That is general relativity, and it is one of the most beautiful ideas a human being has ever had.

12.1   The Equivalence Principle

Einstein called it “the happiest thought of my life.” He was sitting in the patent office in Bern, in 1907, and he imagined a man falling off a roof. During the fall, the man would feel weightless. He wouldn’t feel gravity at all. And then it hit Einstein: there is no experiment you can perform inside a sealed box that distinguishes between being in free fall in a gravitational field and floating in empty space with no gravity at all.

Turn it around: there is no experiment inside a sealed box that distinguishes between standing on the surface of the Earth and being in a rocket accelerating at g = 9.8 m/s² through empty space. Gravity and acceleration are locally indistinguishable.

Key Principle The Equivalence Principle: In a small enough region of spacetime, the effects of gravity are indistinguishable from the effects of acceleration. A freely falling reference frame is locally equivalent to an inertial frame in the absence of gravity.

This is not a vague analogy — it is exact, and it has enormous consequences. If acceleration and gravity are the same thing, then gravity isn’t a force acting on objects — it’s a property of the spacetime those objects move through. Let me make this concrete.

Suppose you are inside a rocket accelerating upward. You shine a laser beam horizontally across the cabin. Since the rocket is accelerating upward, the floor rises to meet the beam while the light crosses the cabin, so the light hits the far wall slightly below where it was aimed. The light path curves downward (relative to the rocket). Now, by the equivalence principle, the same thing must happen in a gravitational field. Gravity must bend light. Einstein predicted this in 1911, four years before he had the full theory.

“Think about what that means. The equivalence principle isn’t some minor technical detail — it’s the seed from which all of general relativity grew. One single insight: falling is the same as floating. Everything else follows.”

Weak vs. Strong Equivalence

The weak equivalence principle (WEP) states that the trajectory of a freely falling test body depends only on its initial position and velocity, not on its composition. This is equivalent to saying that inertial mass equals gravitational mass: mi = mg. This has been tested to extraordinary precision — the Eötvös experiment and its modern descendants (like the MICROSCOPE satellite) confirm it to about one part in 1015.

The Einstein equivalence principle (EEP) goes further: it includes WEP, plus the requirement that in any freely falling reference frame, the laws of non-gravitational physics are the same as in special relativity. This is the version Einstein used to build general relativity.

12.2   Curved Spacetime and Geodesics

In special relativity we learned about spacetime — a four-dimensional arena with three spatial dimensions and one time dimension, stitched together into a single continuum. In the absence of gravity, spacetime is flat: it obeys the Minkowski metric, and straight lines in spacetime represent the paths of free particles.

Now, Einstein’s great leap: in the presence of mass and energy, spacetime is curved. Free particles still follow the “straightest possible paths” — but in curved spacetime, the straightest possible path is itself curved. These straightest-possible paths are called geodesics.

Analogy Think of the surface of the Earth. On a flat table, the shortest path between two points is a straight line. On the surface of a sphere, the shortest path is a great circle — a curve. An airplane flying from Delhi to San Francisco doesn’t fly “straight” on a flat map; it follows a great circle, which is the geodesic on a sphere. Similarly, a planet orbiting the Sun is following a geodesic in the curved spacetime created by the Sun’s mass.

The Metric Tensor

In flat spacetime, the spacetime interval between two nearby events is given by the Minkowski metric:

ds² = −c² dt² + dx² + dy² + dz²

In curved spacetime, we generalise this to:

ds² = gμν dxμ dxν

where gμν is the metric tensor — a 4×4 symmetric matrix that varies from point to point in spacetime. It encodes all the information about the curvature of spacetime. In flat spacetime, gμν reduces to the Minkowski metric ημν = diag(−1, +1, +1, +1). Near a massive body, the components of gμν deviate from these flat-space values, and that deviation is gravity.

“The metric tensor is the star of the show. Everything about the geometry — distances, angles, curvature, the paths of light and matter — is all packed into those ten independent components of gμν. If you know the metric, you know the gravitational field completely.”

The Geodesic Equation

A free particle follows a geodesic — a path that extremises the spacetime interval. The geodesic equation is:

d²xμdτ² + Γμαβ dxαdτ dxβdτ = 0

Here Γμαβ are the Christoffel symbols (connection coefficients), computed from the metric and its first derivatives. They represent the “forces” that arise from the curvature of spacetime — what Newton would have called gravity. Notice: there is no force in this equation. The particle is simply following the natural geometry of spacetime.

12.3   Einstein Field Equations

Now we arrive at the heart of the theory. We need an equation that connects the curvature of spacetime (the geometry) to the distribution of mass and energy (the matter). Einstein worked on this from 1907 to 1915 — eight years of the most intense intellectual effort in the history of physics. The result is the Einstein field equations:

Gμν + Λ gμν = 8πGc4 Tμν

Let me unpack every symbol for you:

  • Gμν — the Einstein tensor. This encodes the curvature of spacetime. It is built from the Ricci tensor Rμν and the Ricci scalar R:   Gμν = Rμν − ½ R gμν.
  • Λ gμν — the cosmological constant term. Einstein originally introduced Λ to allow a static universe; today we know it drives the accelerating expansion of the universe (dark energy). If Λ = 0 we recover the original 1915 equation.
  • G — Newton’s gravitational constant, 6.674 × 10−11 N m² kg−2.
  • c — the speed of light.
  • Tμν — the stress-energy tensor. This describes the distribution of energy, momentum, pressure, and stress in spacetime. For a perfect fluid, Tμν = (ρ + p/c²) uμ uν + p gμν.
The Punchline “Matter tells spacetime how to curve; spacetime tells matter how to move.” — John Archibald Wheeler. The left side of the equation (Gμν) is geometry. The right side (Tμν) is matter. The equals sign is the deepest statement in all of gravity.

“The Einstein field equations look simple, but they’re actually ten coupled, nonlinear, second-order partial differential equations. They’re monstrously difficult to solve in general. In the century since Einstein wrote them down, exact solutions have been found for only a few special cases. But those few cases have told us about black holes, gravitational waves, and the expansion of the entire universe.”

Newtonian Limit

A good theory must reproduce the old one in the appropriate limit. In the weak-field, slow-motion limit (where gravitational fields are weak and velocities are much less than c), the Einstein field equations reduce to Newton’s law of gravitation:

∇² Φ = 4πGρ

where Φ is the Newtonian gravitational potential and ρ is the mass density. The geodesic equation reduces to Newton’s second law with the gravitational force F = −m∇Φ. So Newtonian gravity is a special case of general relativity, just as Galilean relativity is a special case of special relativity. The old theory lives inside the new one.

12.4   The Schwarzschild Solution and Black Holes

Just weeks after Einstein published his field equations in November 1915, the German astronomer Karl Schwarzschild — while serving on the Russian front in World War I — found the first exact solution. He solved the equations for the simplest possible case: a spherically symmetric, non-rotating, uncharged mass in empty space.

The Schwarzschild metric is:

ds² = −(1 − 2GMc²r ) c² dt² + (1 − 2GMc²r )−1 dr² + r² dΩ²

where dΩ² = dθ² + sin²θ dφ² is the angular part. Look at what happens when r = 2GM/c². The coefficient of dt² goes to zero and the coefficient of dr² blows up. This special radius is called the Schwarzschild radius:

rs = 2GMc²

For the Sun, rs ≈ 3 km. For the Earth, it’s about 9 mm. For ordinary stars and planets, the Schwarzschild radius lies deep inside the body where the vacuum Schwarzschild solution doesn’t apply. But if you could compress a mass into a region smaller than its Schwarzschild radius, something extraordinary happens.

Black Holes

A black hole forms when mass is compressed within its Schwarzschild radius. The surface at r = rs is the event horizon — a point of no return. Once anything crosses this boundary, including light, it can never escape. The event horizon is not a physical surface; it is a boundary in spacetime geometry.

Common Misconception A black hole is not a cosmic vacuum cleaner. At large distances, its gravity is the same as that of any other object with the same mass. If the Sun were magically replaced by a 1 M☉ black hole, the Earth’s orbit would not change at all. The difference is only in the strong-field region near the event horizon.

At the very centre of a Schwarzschild black hole, r = 0, the curvature becomes infinite. This is the singularity — a point where general relativity itself breaks down and we expect quantum gravity effects to become important. The singularity is a sign that our theory is incomplete, not a physical prediction we should take literally.

“Nature is telling us something when the equations blow up. She’s saying: ‘You haven’t got the full story yet.’ The singularity is where general relativity and quantum mechanics must shake hands — and we don’t yet know what that handshake looks like.”

12.5   Gravitational Time Dilation

One of the most startling predictions of general relativity: time runs slower in stronger gravitational fields. This is not a metaphor — clocks literally tick at different rates depending on their position in a gravitational field.

From the Schwarzschild metric, the rate of proper time τ relative to coordinate time t (time measured by a distant observer) is:

dτ = dt √(1 − 2GMc²r )

Closer to the mass (smaller r), the factor under the square root is smaller, so dτ < dt: time passes more slowly closer to a gravitating body. At the event horizon (r = rs), this factor goes to zero — a distant observer would see a clock at the horizon frozen in time forever.

Worked Example

Gravitational time dilation on Earth

How much slower does a clock on the Earth’s surface tick compared to a clock far from Earth?

For Earth: GM/c² = 4.43 × 10−3 m, and r = RE = 6.371 × 106 m.

2GMc² RE = 2 × 4.43 × 10−36.371 × 106 ≈ 1.39 × 10−9

So a clock on Earth’s surface runs slower by about 0.7 parts per billion compared to a clock at infinity. Tiny — but measurable. This is exactly what atomic clocks confirm.

GPS and General Relativity

Here is a real-world application that every Indian student with a smartphone uses daily. The Global Positioning System (GPS) relies on signals from satellites orbiting at about 20,200 km altitude. There are two relativistic effects at play:

  1. Special relativistic time dilation: The satellites move at about 3.87 km/s relative to the ground. Special relativity says their clocks run slower by about 7 μs/day.
  2. Gravitational time dilation: The satellites are higher up in the gravitational field (weaker gravity), so their clocks run faster by about 45 μs/day.

The net effect: satellite clocks run faster by about 38 μs/day compared to ground clocks. If this were not corrected, GPS position errors would accumulate at about 10 km per day. The corrections are built into the GPS software — general relativity is engineered into your phone.

Key Result GPS correction: Net relativistic clock drift = +45 μs/day (GR) − 7 μs/day (SR) = +38 μs/day. Without correction, position error ≈ 10 km/day. General relativity is not abstract philosophy — it is precision engineering.

12.6   Gravitational Lensing

Since spacetime is curved by mass, and light follows geodesics through spacetime, massive objects bend the paths of light passing near them. This effect is called gravitational lensing.

The deflection angle for a light ray passing a mass M at closest approach distance b (the impact parameter) is:

Δφ = 4GMc² b

For light grazing the Sun, this gives Δφ ≈ 1.75 arcseconds — tiny, but measurable.

When the lensing mass lies almost exactly between us and a distant source, the source’s light is bent symmetrically around the mass, forming a luminous ring called an Einstein ring. If the alignment is imperfect, we see arcs or multiple images of the source.

Gravitational lensing has become one of the most powerful tools in observational astrophysics. It allows us to “weigh” galaxy clusters, detect dark matter (which lenses light even though it doesn’t emit any), and even discover exoplanets.

12.7   Gravitational Waves

Einstein predicted in 1916 that accelerating masses should produce ripples in spacetime itself — gravitational waves. Think of them as the gravitational analogue of electromagnetic waves. A shaking charge produces electromagnetic waves; a shaking mass produces gravitational waves.

But there’s a crucial difference. Electromagnetic waves are dipole radiation (the lowest multipole), while gravitational waves are quadrupole radiation. This means you need a time-varying mass quadrupole moment to generate them. A spherically symmetric pulsation won’t do it; you need asymmetric motion, like two masses orbiting each other.

In the weak-field limit, the Einstein equations can be linearised, and the perturbation hμν of the metric satisfies a wave equation:

□ hμν = − 16πGc4 Tμν

where □ is the d’Alembertian (wave operator). The solutions are transverse waves propagating at the speed of light. A gravitational wave passing through a ring of test particles stretches the ring in one direction and squeezes it in the perpendicular direction, alternating at the wave frequency. The two polarisation modes are called “plus” (+) and “cross” (×).

LIGO and the First Detection

On 14 September 2015 — exactly one hundred years after Einstein completed general relativity — the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) made the first direct detection of gravitational waves. The event, designated GW150914, was produced by the merger of two black holes, each about 30 solar masses, roughly 1.3 billion light-years away.

The signal matched the theoretical “chirp” waveform with extraordinary precision: the frequency swept from about 35 Hz to 250 Hz in about 0.2 seconds as the black holes spiralled together and merged. The peak strain (fractional change in length) measured by LIGO was about h ≈ 10−21 — meaning the 4 km arms of the detector changed length by about one-thousandth the diameter of a proton.

Indian Connection India is building LIGO-India (in Aundha Nagnath, Maharashtra), the third LIGO detector. Adding a third detector to the network dramatically improves the ability to locate gravitational wave sources on the sky. LIGO-India is expected to begin operations in the late 2020s and will be a world-class facility. Several key contributions to LIGO data analysis have come from Indian physicists and institutions, including the Inter-University Centre for Astronomy and Astrophysics (IUCAA, Pune).

Since 2015, LIGO and its European partner Virgo have detected dozens of gravitational wave events — binary black hole mergers, binary neutron star mergers (GW170817, which was also seen in electromagnetic radiation, inaugurating “multi-messenger astronomy”), and black hole–neutron star mergers. The 2017 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Rainer Weiss, Barry Barish, and Kip Thorne for the detection.

12.8   Tests of General Relativity

A theory is only as good as its experimental confirmation. General relativity has been tested with extraordinary precision over more than a century. Let me walk you through the classic tests and some modern ones.

1. Precession of Mercury’s Perihelion

Mercury’s orbit is an ellipse that slowly rotates — its perihelion (closest approach to the Sun) advances with each orbit. Newtonian gravity, accounting for perturbations from other planets, predicts a precession rate that is 43 arcseconds per century too small compared to the observed value of 574″/century. This tiny discrepancy had puzzled astronomers since Le Verrier noted it in 1859.

General relativity predicts an additional precession of:

Δφ = 6πGMc² a(1 − e²)   per orbit

For Mercury, this gives exactly 43″/century. When Einstein first calculated this in November 1915, he reportedly had heart palpitations. It was the first triumph of general relativity.

2. Deflection of Light by the Sun

During the total solar eclipse of 29 May 1919, Arthur Eddington led expeditions to Sobral (Brazil) and Príncipe (off West Africa) to photograph stars near the Sun’s limb. The measured deflection was 1.61″ ± 0.30″ (Príncipe) and 1.98″ ± 0.12″ (Sobral), consistent with Einstein’s prediction of 1.75″ and twice the Newtonian value. This made Einstein a global celebrity overnight.

Modern radio observations of quasars passing behind the Sun have confirmed the light-bending prediction to better than 0.01% accuracy.

3. Shapiro Time Delay

In 1964, Irwin Shapiro predicted that radar signals bouncing off planets should be slightly delayed when their path passes near the Sun, because the signal traverses curved spacetime. This Shapiro delay (sometimes called the “fourth test” of GR) was confirmed using radar signals bounced off Venus and Mercury, and later with much higher precision using the Cassini spacecraft. The Cassini measurement (2003) confirmed the predicted delay to within 0.002%.

Δt = 4GMc3 ln( 4 r1 r2b² )

4. Gravitational Redshift

Photons climbing out of a gravitational well lose energy and become redshifted. The fractional change in frequency is:

Δνν ≈ GMc² r

This was confirmed by the Pound–Rebka experiment (1959) at Harvard, which measured the redshift of gamma rays falling 22.5 m in the Earth’s gravitational field. The result agreed with the prediction to within 1%. Modern hydrogen maser clocks on rockets (Gravity Probe A, 1976) confirmed it to 0.007%.

12.9   Conceptual Summary

Let me pull it all together. General relativity rests on a few ideas, each following logically from the one before:

  1. The equivalence principle: Gravity and acceleration are locally indistinguishable. Free fall is the natural, force-free state.
  2. Spacetime is a dynamic entity: It is not a fixed stage on which physics plays out. It bends, stretches, and ripples in response to matter and energy.
  3. Geometry replaces force: The “force” of gravity is replaced by the curvature of spacetime. Free particles follow geodesics.
  4. Einstein’s equation links geometry to matter: Gμν = (8πG/c4) Tμν.
  5. Predictions: Time dilation, light bending, gravitational waves, black holes, expanding universe — all confirmed by experiment.
Exam Essentials For your BSc exams, make sure you can: (a) state the equivalence principle and explain its consequences; (b) write the Schwarzschild metric and identify the event horizon; (c) derive the gravitational time dilation formula from the Schwarzschild metric; (d) compute the light deflection angle; (e) explain the GPS relativistic correction quantitatively; (f) describe LIGO and explain what gravitational waves are.

“General relativity is not just an improvement on Newton. It is a completely different way of thinking about gravity — about space and time themselves. Newton gave us a force; Einstein gave us geometry. And the geometry is so beautiful, so elegant, so right, that once you understand it, you can never see the universe the same way again. That’s the joy of physics — it changes the way you look at everything.”
