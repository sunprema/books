Chapter 02
The language of nature

Math Tools for Physics

Nature speaks mathematics. Not because she chose it at some committee meeting, but because when you strip away the fog of words and feelings and get down to how things actually behave, what’s left is structure — patterns, proportions, rates of change — and mathematics is simply the language we invented to describe structure precisely. If you want to understand physics, you don’t need to become a mathematician. You need to learn enough of the language to read what Nature is telling you. That’s what this chapter is for.

“To those who do not know mathematics it is difficult to get across a real feeling as to the beauty, the deepest beauty, of nature.” — I wrote that once, and I meant every word. But here’s the thing: the math we need right now isn’t abstract or scary. It’s geometry with arrows, slopes of hills, and areas under curves. That’s it. Let’s go.

1 · Vectors: Arrows That Carry Physics

Suppose I tell you a car is moving at 60 km/h. That’s useful, sure — but if the car is heading straight towards you, you’d very much like to know the direction. Speed alone won’t save your life. This is why physics invented the vector — a quantity that carries both magnitude (how much) and direction (which way).

Key Idea A scalar is a single number (temperature, mass, speed). A vector is a number plus a direction (velocity, force, electric field). We write vectors in bold (A) or with an arrow on top: A⃗.

1.1 · Representing Vectors

In two dimensions, a vector A can be broken into components along the x and y axes:

A = Ax &hat;i + Ay &hat;j

where &hat;i and &hat;j are unit vectors — arrows of length 1 pointing along the x and y axes. Think of them as building blocks: any vector in the plane can be built by scaling and combining these two fundamental directions.

The magnitude (length) of A is simply Pythagoras at work:

|A| = √(Ax2 + Ay2)

And the direction angle θ from the x-axis:

θ = tan−1 AyAx

You already know all this — you’ve been resolving triangles since Class 10. The only new thing is that now the triangle is a physical arrow — a force, a velocity, a field — and the components tell you how much of that arrow points in each direction.

1.2 · Vector Addition: The Parallelogram Law

When two forces act on a body simultaneously, how do they combine? Not by simply adding their magnitudes — direction matters. You place the vectors tip-to-tail (or equivalently, form a parallelogram) and the resultant is the diagonal.

R = A + B = (Ax + Bx)&hat;i + (Ay + By)&hat;j

Watch the animation below. Two vectors (cyan and amber) combine: place them tail-to-tail, complete the parallelogram, and the diagonal (green) is their sum.

Notice something beautiful: it doesn’t matter which order you add vectors. A + B = B + A. The parallelogram is the same shape either way. Mathematicians call this commutativity; I call it “Nature doesn’t care who arrived first.”

1.3 · The Dot Product (Scalar Product)

Sometimes you don’t want to add two vectors — you want to ask: how much do they agree? When you pull a suitcase along the floor with a rope at an angle, only the horizontal component of your force actually moves the suitcase. The dot product captures exactly this idea.

A · B = |A| |B| cos θ

Or, equivalently, in components:

A · B = AxBx + AyBy + AzBz
Note The dot product gives a scalar, not a vector. It tells you the “projection” of one vector onto another, scaled by the other’s length. If the vectors are perpendicular, cos 90° = 0, so the dot product vanishes. If they are parallel, cos 0° = 1, and you get the full product of their magnitudes.
Angle θcos θA · BMeaning
0°1|A||B|Parallel — maximum agreement
90°00Perpendicular — no overlap

Physics use: Work done by a force is W = F · d — only the component of force along the displacement does work.

1.4 · The Cross Product (Vector Product)

The cross product answers a different question: how much do two vectors disagree in direction? The result is a new vector that is perpendicular to both inputs — it points “out of the table” when the two original vectors lie on the table.

A × B = |A| |B| sin θ  &hat;n

where &hat;n is perpendicular to both A and B, following the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of your right hand from A towards B, and your thumb points along A × B.

Watch Out The cross product is not commutative! A × B = −(B × A). Swap the order and the result flips direction. This is anti-commutativity, and it matters when you compute torques and magnetic forces.

In components (using the determinant formula):

A × B = (AyBz − AzBy)&hat;i − (AxBz − AzBx)&hat;j + (AxBy − AyBx)&hat;k

Physics uses: Torque (τ = r × F), magnetic force (F = qv × B), angular momentum (L = r × p).

Investigation

Verify the Cross Product Formula

Take A = 3&hat;i + 1&hat;j + 0&hat;k and B = 1&hat;i + 2&hat;j + 0&hat;k. Compute A × B using the component formula. Verify that the result is perpendicular to both A and B by checking that its dot product with each is zero. Then compute |A||B|sinθ and confirm it matches the magnitude of your cross product.

2 · Coordinate Systems: Choosing Your Perspective

Here’s a question that confused me as a young student: does it matter where I put my coordinate axes? The answer is beautifully simple: physics doesn’t change, but the mathematics can get much easier or much harder depending on your choice. A wise choice of coordinates can turn a page of algebra into a single line.

Think of it this way: whether you describe the Taj Mahal in Hindi, English, or Tamil, the building doesn’t change. But some languages may describe certain features more gracefully. Coordinates are the same — different languages for the same geometry.

2.1 · Cartesian Coordinates (x, y, z)

The most familiar system. Three mutually perpendicular axes form a right-handed frame. Every point in space is described by three numbers: how far along each axis. This is excellent for problems with flat boundaries — blocks on ramps, circuits on flat boards, crystals with rectangular unit cells.

r = x &hat;i + y &hat;j + z &hat;k

Distance between two points:

d = √(Δx2 + Δy2 + Δz2)

2.2 · Polar Coordinates (r, θ)

Now suppose you’re studying a stone tied to a string, whirling in a circle. In Cartesian coordinates, both x and y are constantly changing — you’d need two equations to describe the motion. But in polar coordinates, the stone sits at constant r (the string length) and only θ changes. One variable instead of two. Beautiful.

x = r cos θ    y = r sin θ
r = √(x2 + y2)    θ = tan−1(y/x)
Key Idea Use polar coordinates when the problem has circular symmetry. Planetary orbits, circular motion, waves radiating from a point, current loops — all become simpler in polars. The coordinate system should match the symmetry of the problem.

2.3 · Spherical Coordinates (r, θ, φ)

In three dimensions, if the problem has spherical symmetry (gravity around a planet, electric field around a charge, hydrogen atom wavefunctions), you use spherical coordinates:

  • r — distance from origin (always ≥ 0)
  • θ — polar angle from the z-axis (0 to π)
  • φ — azimuthal angle in the x-y plane (0 to 2π)
x = r sinθ cosφ   y = r sinθ sinφ   z = r cosθ
Coordinate SystemVariablesBest For
Cartesianx, y, zFlat geometry, boxes, straight-line motion
Polar (2D)r, θCircular motion, radial symmetry in a plane
Cylindricalr, θ, zSolenoids, wires, pipes, disc problems
Sphericalr, θ, φPoint charges, gravity, atoms, stars

I cannot overstate this: choosing the right coordinate system is half the battle. I’ve seen students spend two pages of algebra solving a problem in Cartesian coordinates that takes two lines in polars. Don’t fight the symmetry — embrace it.

3 · Calculus: The Mathematics of Change

Newton invented calculus because he needed it to describe motion. That’s not a historical footnote — it’s the entire point. Calculus is the mathematics of how things change, and physics is the science of why things change. They were born together, and they need each other.

Here’s my philosophy: don’t memorize calculus rules. Understand what a derivative is — it’s the slope of a curve, the rate at which something changes — and the rules will feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

3.1 · The Derivative: Instantaneous Rate of Change

Imagine you’re driving from Delhi to Agra. Your average speed is total distance divided by total time. But at any single instant, your speedometer shows something more specific — the instantaneous speed. This is the derivative.

Formally, the derivative of a function f(x) is:

dfdx = limΔx→0 f(x + Δx) − f(x)Δx

In plain language: zoom in on a tiny interval, measure the slope, and let the interval shrink to zero. What survives is the exact slope at that point.

Key Idea The derivative of position with respect to time is velocity. The derivative of velocity is acceleration. Every time you see “rate of change” in physics, you’re looking at a derivative.
v = dxdt    a = dvdt = d2xdt2

Do you see what’s happening? When the curve goes uphill, the slope is positive. When it’s flat, the slope is zero. When it goes downhill, negative. The derivative is just the steepness of the hill at each point. That’s all there is to it.

Chain Rule, Product Rule — The Essentials

Nature rarely gives you a simple function. You get functions of functions (a ball swinging on a pendulum attached to a train), and products of functions (a vibrating string whose amplitude grows). Two rules handle these:

Chain Rule If y = f(g(x)), then:
dydx = dfdg · dgdx

Example: y = sin(3t). Let g = 3t, so dy/dt = cos(3t) · 3 = 3 cos(3t).

Product Rule If y = u(x) · v(x), then:
dydx = u dvdx + v dudx

Example: y = t2 sin t. Then dy/dt = t2 cos t + 2t sin t.

3.2 · The Integral: Undoing the Derivative

If the derivative is “taking the slope,” the integral is “adding up the area.” They are inverse operations — the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says so, and it’s one of the most beautiful results in all of mathematics.

Imagine you know the velocity v(t) of a car at every instant. How far did it travel between time a and time b? You chop the time interval into tiny pieces, multiply each velocity by the tiny time interval Δt, and add up all the little displacements. In the limit of infinitely many, infinitely thin slices:

x(b) − x(a) = ∫ab v(t)  dt
Key Idea The integral is the area under the curve. If velocity is plotted against time, the area under the curve equals the total displacement. If force is plotted against distance, the area equals the total work done.

Some essential integrals you’ll use constantly:

FunctionIntegralPhysics Use
xnxn+1n+1 + CKinematic equations from constant acceleration
sin x−cos x + CEnergy in oscillating systems
cos xsin x + CFourier analysis, wave superposition

1/xln|x| + CElectric potential of line charge

The constant C — the “constant of integration” — is just saying: “I know the shape of the curve, but I don’t know where it started.” You need an initial condition (a boundary condition) to pin it down. In physics, Nature always gives you one. She tells you where the ball was at t = 0, or what the potential is at infinity.

Investigation

From Acceleration to Position

A ball is thrown upward with initial velocity v0 = 20 m/s from height y0 = 0. Acceleration is a = −g = −9.8 m/s2 (constant). Integrate once to find v(t), then again to find y(t). At what time does the ball reach maximum height? What is that height? (Hint: at maximum height, v = 0.)

3.3 · Partial Derivatives: When Things Depend on Many Variables

Temperature in a room depends on position (x, y, z) and time (t). If you want to know how temperature changes as you walk in the x-direction at a fixed time and fixed y, z, you take the partial derivative:

∂T∂x

The curly ∂ symbol (as opposed to the straight d) is a reminder: “I’m only varying one variable and holding everything else fixed.” This becomes essential in thermodynamics, electromagnetism (Maxwell’s equations), and quantum mechanics (the Schrödinger equation).

Note Partial derivatives follow the same rules as ordinary derivatives — you just treat all other variables as constants. If f = x2y + 3xy3, then ∂f/∂x = 2xy + 3y3 (treat y as a constant), and ∂f/∂y = x2 + 9xy2 (treat x as a constant).

4 · Differential Equations: Nature’s Recipes

Here’s the grand punchline of this chapter: almost every law of physics is a differential equation. Newton’s second law, Maxwell’s equations, Schrödinger’s equation, Einstein’s field equations — they all say the same kind of thing: “the rate of change of this equals that.”

A differential equation (DE) is an equation that relates a function to its own derivatives. Solving it means finding the function.

Think of a differential equation as a recipe, not an answer. Newton’s law F = ma doesn’t tell you where the ball is. It tells you how the ball’s acceleration relates to the forces on it. You have to solve the equation to find the trajectory. The recipe says “stir at this rate”; the solution is the finished dish.

4.1 · First-Order ODEs

The simplest type: the equation involves only the first derivative.

Example 1 — Radioactive Decay: The number of undecayed atoms N(t) decreases at a rate proportional to how many are left:

dNdt = −λN

This says: the more atoms you have, the faster they decay. To solve it, we separate variables:

dNN = −λ dt

Integrate both sides:

ln N = −λt + C

Exponentiate and apply the initial condition N(0) = N0:

N(t) = N0 e−λt
Key Idea The exponential decay solution appears everywhere: radioactive nuclei, discharging capacitors, cooling objects (Newton’s law of cooling), damped oscillations. If the rate of change is proportional to the current value, the solution is always exponential.

Example 2 — Newton’s Law of Cooling:

dTdt = −k(T − Tenv)

The temperature difference decays exponentially: T(t) = Tenv + (T0 − Tenv)e−kt. A cup of chai left on your desk in a Kolkata summer follows this law precisely.

4.2 · Second-Order ODEs: The Harmonic Oscillator

The most important differential equation in all of physics — and I do not exaggerate — is the simple harmonic oscillator:

d2xdt2 = −ω2x

In words: the acceleration is proportional to the displacement, but opposite in sign. Push a mass on a spring away from equilibrium, and the spring pulls it back. Overshoot, and it pushes the other way. The result is oscillation.

The solution is:

x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)

where A is the amplitude, ω is the angular frequency (related to the spring constant and mass by ω = √(k/m)), and φ is the phase.

Why is this equation so important? Because almost any system near equilibrium behaves like a harmonic oscillator. Pendulums, atoms in a crystal, electromagnetic fields in a cavity, even the spacetime around a black hole in certain perturbation regimes. Learn this equation, and you’ve learned the skeleton key to half of physics.

Verification Let’s verify: if x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ), then:
dxdt = −Aω sin(ωt + φ)
d2xdt2 = −Aω2 cos(ωt + φ) = −ω2x   ✓

4.3 · What About PDEs?

When a function depends on multiple variables (e.g., a vibrating string that depends on both position x and time t), you get partial differential equations (PDEs). Three of the most famous in physics:

EquationNameDescribes
∂2y/∂t2 = v2 ∂2y/∂x2 Wave equation Sound, light, strings, water waves
∂T/∂t = α ∇2T Heat equation Temperature diffusion, Brownian motion
∇2V = −ρ/ε0 Poisson’s equation Electrostatic potential, gravitational potential

You won’t solve these yet — but you should recognize them. They will appear again and again throughout this book, and each time, we’ll attack them with the tools we’ve built here: derivatives, integrals, separation of variables, and boundary conditions.

5 · A Glimpse Ahead: Gradient, Divergence, Curl

Before we close, let me plant three seeds that will bloom later. When you study electromagnetism and fluid mechanics, you’ll meet three operations that combine partial derivatives with vectors:

  • The gradient ∇f — points in the direction of steepest ascent of a scalar field. If temperature varies across a room, ∇T tells you which way to walk to warm up fastest.
  • The divergence ∇ · F — measures how much a vector field “spreads out” from a point. Positive divergence = source (like electric charge radiating field lines).
  • The curl ∇ × F — measures how much a vector field “swirls” around a point. A whirlpool has curl; a uniform flow does not.
∇f = ∂f∂x&hat;i + ∂f∂y&hat;j + ∂f∂z&hat;k

Maxwell wrote the laws of electromagnetism in terms of gradient, divergence, and curl. When you see those four equations for the first time in Chapter 7, you’ll be glad you met the del operator ∇ here first. It’s just a vector of partial derivatives — nothing to be frightened of.

Summary: Your Mathematical Toolkit

Let’s take stock of what we’ve built. You now have:

  1. Vectors — arrows with magnitude and direction. You can add them (tip-to-tail), project them (dot product), and find perpendicular outcomes (cross product).
  2. Coordinate systems — Cartesian for flat problems, polar for circular ones, spherical for atoms and planets. Match the coordinate system to the symmetry.
  3. Derivatives — the slope at a point, the rate of change. Velocity is the derivative of position; acceleration is the derivative of velocity.
  4. Integrals — the area under a curve, the accumulation. Displacement is the integral of velocity; work is the integral of force over distance.
  5. Differential equations — Nature’s recipes. The laws of physics tell you how things change; solving the DE tells you what actually happens.
Key Idea These are not separate topics — they are one connected language. Vectors tell you about directions in space. Calculus tells you about change in time. Differential equations connect the two: forces (vectors) determine how motion (calculus) evolves. This language is the backbone of everything that follows.

I’ve given you the vocabulary. Starting from the next chapter — Mechanics — we’ll use it to write poetry. Newton’s laws, conservation of energy, orbital dynamics — they’re all sentences in this mathematical language. Now you can read them.
