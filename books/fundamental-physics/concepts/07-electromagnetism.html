Chapter 07
The electromagnetic force

Electromagnetism

The force between two charged particles is the same force that makes lightning, runs your phone, and holds atoms together. It’s all one thing. That single idea — that static cling and starlight are siblings — took humanity two thousand years to figure out, and when we finally did, we got Maxwell’s equations: four compact statements that wrap up all of electricity, all of magnetism, and all of light in one tight package. This chapter is the story of how we get there, one experiment at a time.

“From a long view of the history of mankind, seen from, say, ten thousand years from now, there can be little doubt that the most significant event of the nineteenth century will be judged as Maxwell’s discovery of the laws of electrodynamics.”

1 · Electric Charge & Coulomb’s Law

Everything begins with electric charge. Rub a glass rod with silk and the rod picks up bits of paper. The ancient Greeks noticed this with amber (elektron in Greek — that’s where we get the word). But what is going on?

Matter is made of atoms, and atoms are made of protons (positive charge), neutrons (no charge), and electrons (negative charge). When you rub glass with silk, electrons hop from glass to silk. The glass now has a deficit of electrons and becomes positively charged; the silk has an excess and becomes negatively charged. Charge is never created or destroyed — it is conserved.

Key principle — Conservation of charge The total electric charge in an isolated system never changes. You can move charge around, but you cannot create it from nothing or destroy it. The elementary unit of charge is e = 1.6 × 10−19 C. All observed charges are integer multiples of e.

Two charges sitting in space — how hard do they push or pull on each other? Charles-Augustin de Coulomb measured this in 1785 with a torsion balance, and the answer is beautifully simple:

F = 14πε0 q1 q2r2

Here q1 and q2 are the charges (in coulombs), r is the distance between them, and ε0 = 8.854 × 10−12 C2/(N·m2) is the permittivity of free space. The constant 1/(4πε0) ≈ 9 × 109 N·m2/C2.

Notice the family resemblance with Newton’s law of gravitation: both go as 1/r2. Nature has a fondness for inverse-square laws — and that’s no coincidence; it’s geometry. The “influence” spreads over the surface of a sphere, and a sphere’s area grows as r2.

Sign convention Like charges (both positive or both negative) repel: F > 0. Unlike charges attract: F < 0. The force acts along the line joining the two charges — it is a central force.

Superposition

If you have more than two charges, the total force on any one charge is simply the vector sum of the forces from all the others. This is the principle of superposition — each pair interacts independently, and you just add up the vectors. It sounds obvious, but it is a deep experimental fact. Nature could have been nonlinear, but for classical electrostatics, she isn’t.

Worked example

Force between a proton and an electron in hydrogen

The Bohr radius is a0 = 0.529 × 10−10 m. Each carries charge ±e.

F = (9 × 109)(1.6 × 10−19)2 / (0.529 × 10−10)2 ≈ 8.2 × 10−8 N

That looks tiny, but the electron’s mass is only 9.1 × 10−31 kg, so the acceleration is about 1022 m/s2. The electric force is absolutely enormous on the atomic scale — roughly 1039 times stronger than gravity between the same two particles.

2 · Electric Fields & Field Lines

Coulomb’s law tells you the force between two charges. But there is a more powerful way to think about it. Forget the second charge for a moment. Ask: what has the first charge done to the space around it? It has created an electric field.

E = Fq0 = 14πε0 Qr2 &hat;r

The field E is a vector at every point in space. Put a tiny test charge q0 there and it feels force F = q0E. The field exists whether or not you put the test charge there — the source charge creates it, and any other charge that wanders in will feel it.

Field lines

Michael Faraday — a self-taught bookbinder’s apprentice who became one of the greatest experimenters in history — invented a beautiful way to visualise fields. Draw curves such that:

  • The tangent at any point gives the direction of E.
  • The density of lines (how tightly they’re packed) is proportional to |E|.
  • Lines start on positive charges, end on negative charges (or go off to infinity).
  • Field lines never cross (the field has a unique direction at each point).

Faraday couldn’t do the mathematics, but his physical intuition through field lines was so precise that Maxwell later said all he did was translate Faraday’s pictures into equations. The pictures came first!

Dipoles and why they matter

A dipole is a pair of equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance d. The dipole moment is p = qd, pointing from − to +. Dipoles are everywhere in nature: the water molecule is a permanent dipole, and that’s why water is such a fantastic solvent. In a uniform external field, a dipole feels a torque τ = p × E that tries to align it with the field.

3 · Electric Potential

Force and field are vectors — they have direction. Sometimes it’s easier to work with a single number at each point: the electric potential V.

V(r) = 14πε0 Qr

The potential is related to the field by E = −∇V (the field points in the direction of steepest decrease of potential). The potential difference between two points equals the work done per unit charge in moving a test charge between them:

VA − VB = − ∫AB E · dl

This is what a voltmeter measures. When you say “a 1.5 V battery,” you mean there is a 1.5 volt potential difference between its terminals. The unit is the volt: 1 V = 1 J/C.

Equipotential surfaces An equipotential surface is a surface on which V is constant. The electric field is always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential — that’s why conductors in electrostatic equilibrium are equipotentials. Toggle “Show equipotential lines” in the interactive above to see this.

Potential is like altitude on a topographic map. The field is like the slope of the terrain. Charges “roll downhill” in potential — positive charges from high V to low V, negative charges the other way.

4 · Gauss’s Law

Here is one of the most elegant results in all of physics. Take any closed surface — any shape you like, a sphere, a cube, a potato — and measure the total electric flux (the net “number of field lines”) passing outward through it:

∮ E · dA = Qencε0

The total flux depends only on the charge enclosed inside the surface — not on the shape of the surface, not on where the charges sit inside, not on any charges outside. This is Gauss’s law, and it is the first of Maxwell’s four equations.

Why Gauss’s law is powerful When the charge distribution has symmetry — spherical, cylindrical, or planar — Gauss’s law lets you find E with almost no computation. Choose a Gaussian surface that shares the symmetry, and the integral becomes trivial.

5 · Capacitors

Take two conducting plates, separate them by a small gap, and put charge +Q on one and −Q on the other. You’ve stored energy in the electric field between the plates. This device is a capacitor.

C = QV = ε0 Ad  (parallel plate)

The capacitance C (in farads, F) tells you how much charge the capacitor holds per volt of potential difference. Bigger plates (more area A) and smaller gaps (less d) mean more capacitance. Inserting a dielectric material between the plates increases C by a factor κ (the dielectric constant).

Energy stored in a capacitor

U = 12 CV2 = Q22C

This energy lives in the electric field itself. The energy density (energy per unit volume) in any electric field is u = ½ ε0 E2. The field is not just a mathematical convenience — it carries real energy.

Capacitors in circuits In parallel: Ctotal = C1 + C2 + … (they add). In series: 1/Ctotal = 1/C1 + 1/C2 + … (reciprocals add). Note this is the opposite of resistors!

6 · Current, Resistance & Ohm’s Law

So far we’ve dealt with charges at rest — electrostatics. Now let them move. A steady flow of charge is electric current:

I = dQdt

Current is measured in amperes (A): 1 A = 1 C/s. By convention, current flows in the direction positive charges would move (from high V to low V), even though in metals it is actually electrons moving the other way. This historical accident is annoying but harmless.

Ohm’s law

For many materials (metals, at constant temperature), the current through a conductor is proportional to the voltage across it:

V = IR

The proportionality constant R is the resistance (in ohms, Ω). Ohm’s law is not a fundamental law of nature — it’s an empirical observation that works well for ordinary conductors. Semiconductors, superconductors, and gas discharges don’t obey it.

Common BSc exam trap Ohm’s law is not V = IR in its most general form. It says the ratio V/I is constant for a given conductor at a given temperature. A diode has a definite V-I curve, but the ratio is not constant — it does not obey Ohm’s law.

Resistivity and conductivity

The resistance of a wire depends on its material, length, and cross-section:

R = ρ LA

where ρ is the resistivity of the material (in Ω·m). Copper has ρ ≈ 1.7 × 10−8 Ω·m; glass is around 1012 Ω·m. That’s a factor of 1020 — one of the largest ranges of any physical property.

Power dissipation

P = IV = I2R = V2R

Current flowing through a resistance converts electrical energy into heat. This is Joule heating, and it’s why your phone gets warm when charging, why power lines are run at high voltage (to reduce I and hence I2R losses), and why fuses work.

7 · Magnetic Fields

Now something marvellous happens. A moving charge — a current — creates a completely new kind of field: a magnetic field B. And a magnetic field exerts a force on other moving charges. This is the other half of electromagnetism.

F = q(v × B)

The magnetic force is perpendicular to both the velocity and the field. It never does work on the charge (because force ⊥ velocity), so it can change the direction of motion but not the speed. This is why charged particles spiral in magnetic fields — they undergo uniform circular motion.

Put your right hand so the fingers point along v, then curl them toward B. Your thumb gives the direction of F for a positive charge. For negative charges, flip the direction. This is the right-hand rule, and you will use it in every exam.

Biot–Savart law

A small current element I dl produces a magnetic field at a point r away:

dB = μ04π I dl × &hat;rr2

where μ0 = 4π × 10−7 T·m/A is the permeability of free space. This is to magnetism what Coulomb’s law is to electricity.

Classic result

Magnetic field of a long straight wire

Integrating the Biot–Savart law for an infinite straight wire carrying current I:

B = μ0 I2π r

The field wraps around the wire in circles (use the right-hand rule: thumb along current, fingers curl in the direction of B). It falls off as 1/r — not 1/r2. That’s because the source is a line, not a point.

Ampère’s law

Just as Gauss’s law gives a shortcut for calculating E with symmetry, Ampère’s law does the same for B:

∮ B · dl = μ0 Ienc

The line integral of B around any closed loop equals μ0 times the current threading through the loop. This is Maxwell’s second equation (in its original form — Maxwell will add a crucial correction later).

Solenoid A solenoid is a coil of wire, n turns per unit length, carrying current I. Ampère’s law gives a beautifully uniform field inside: B = μ0nI. Outside, B ≈ 0. This is how we make controllable, uniform magnetic fields in the laboratory.

8 · Electromagnetic Induction

Here is where the story gets thrilling. In 1831, Michael Faraday discovered that a changing magnetic field produces an electric field — and therefore can drive a current. This is electromagnetic induction, and it is the principle behind every electric generator, transformer, and induction cooktop on Earth.

Faraday’s law

ε = − dΦBdt

The induced EMF in a loop equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux ΦB = ∫B · dA through the loop. The minus sign is Lenz’s law: the induced current flows in whatever direction opposes the change that caused it. Nature resists change — she pushes back.

Lenz’s law — nature’s conservatism If you push a magnet toward a coil, the induced current creates its own magnetic field that repels the magnet. If you pull the magnet away, the induced field attracts it. The coil always fights you. This is a consequence of energy conservation: if the induced current helped the magnet, you’d get energy for free. That doesn’t happen.

Faraday’s law is Maxwell’s third equation. It’s the bridge between electricity and magnetism — a changing B creates an E. And as Maxwell will show, the reverse is also true: a changing E creates a B. This reciprocity is the heartbeat of electromagnetic waves.

Applications of Faraday’s law

  • Electric generator: Rotate a coil in a magnetic field. The flux changes sinusoidally, producing AC voltage.
  • Transformer: Two coils wound on the same iron core. A changing current in the primary induces an EMF in the secondary. The voltage ratio equals the turns ratio: Vs/Vp = Ns/Np.
  • Eddy currents: A changing B induces currents in bulk conductors. Used in braking systems, induction cooktops, and metal detectors.

9 · Inductance

A coil carrying current produces a magnetic field, and that field creates flux through the coil itself. If the current changes, the flux changes, and by Faraday’s law, an EMF is induced that opposes the change. The coil resists changes in its own current. This property is self-inductance (or simply inductance), denoted L.

ε = −L dIdt

The unit of inductance is the henry (H). For a solenoid of N turns, length l, and cross-sectional area A:

L = μ0 N2l A

Energy stored in an inductor: U = ½ LI2. This energy lives in the magnetic field, just as a capacitor’s energy lives in the electric field. The energy density in a magnetic field is u = B2 / (2μ0).

Mutual inductance When two coils are near each other, a changing current in one induces an EMF in the other. This is mutual inductance M, and it is the principle behind transformers. ε2 = −M dI1/dt.

LC oscillations

Connect an inductor and a capacitor. Energy sloshes back and forth between the electric field of the capacitor and the magnetic field of the inductor, just like a mass on a spring. The natural frequency is:

f = 12π√(LC)

This is how radio tuning works: you adjust C (or L) until the LC circuit resonates at the frequency of the station you want to receive.

10 · Maxwell’s Equations

“Maxwell’s equations are, in a way, the most dramatic result of physics. They unify electricity, magnetism, and optics into one subject. They predict electromagnetic waves that travel at the speed of light — indeed, they tell us that light is an electromagnetic wave.”

James Clerk Maxwell took the four experimental laws we’ve discussed, noticed an inconsistency in Ampère’s law, fixed it by adding the displacement current, and produced the four equations that govern all classical electromagnetic phenomena:

#NameIntegral formWhat it says
I Gauss (E) ∮ E·dA = Qenc/ε0 Electric charges produce electric fields. Field lines begin and end on charges.
II Gauss (B) ∮ B·dA = 0 There are no magnetic monopoles. Magnetic field lines always form closed loops.
III Faraday ∮ E·dl = −dΦB/dt A changing magnetic field creates an electric field (electromagnetic induction).
IV Ampère–Maxwell ∮ B·dl = μ0(I + ε0 dΦE/dt) Currents and changing electric fields produce magnetic fields.
Maxwell’s great addition — the displacement current Ampère’s original law had a problem: it gave contradictory answers for a charging capacitor. Maxwell realized that a changing electric field (dΦE/dt) acts like a current — he called it the displacement current ε0 dΦE/dt. With this addition, the equations became self-consistent, and they predicted something nobody expected: electromagnetic waves.

The symmetry is stunning. Equation III says: a changing B creates an E. Equation IV says: a changing E creates a B. So a disturbance in the electromagnetic field can sustain itself — the changing electric field creates magnetic field, which changes and creates electric field, which changes and creates magnetic field — leapfrogging through space forever. That self-sustaining disturbance is an electromagnetic wave.

Maxwell computed the speed of this wave from the constants ε0 and μ0:

c = 1√(μ0 ε0) = 3 × 108 m/s

This matched the known speed of light. Maxwell wrote, in one of the most triumphant sentences in the history of science: “We can scarcely avoid the inference that light consists of the transverse undulations of the same medium which is the cause of electric and magnetic phenomena.”

Think about what happened here. Two constants, ε0 and μ0, were measured in tabletop experiments with static charges and current-carrying wires. Nobody was thinking about light. And yet when you combine them, out pops the speed of light. Electricity, magnetism, and optics — three subjects that seemed completely unrelated — turned out to be one thing. That is the power of physics.

11 · Electromagnetic Waves

An electromagnetic wave is a self-propagating oscillation of electric and magnetic fields. The key properties:

  • E and B oscillate perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation (transverse wave).
  • They are in phase: both reach their maximum at the same point in space and time.
  • The ratio of their amplitudes is |E| / |B| = c.
  • They travel at speed c = 3 × 108 m/s in vacuum.
  • They carry energy: the Poynting vector S = (1/μ0) E × B gives the energy flux (W/m2).
  • They carry momentum: radiation pressure = S/c for absorption, 2S/c for reflection.

The electromagnetic spectrum

All electromagnetic waves obey c = fλ. They differ only in frequency (and therefore wavelength). From low frequency to high:

Energy and intensity

The average intensity (power per unit area) of an EM wave is:

I = 12 ε0 c E02 = E022μ0c

where E0 is the amplitude of the electric field. For sunlight at Earth’s surface, I ≈ 1400 W/m2, giving E0 ≈ 1000 V/m. The magnetic field amplitude is B0 = E0/c ≈ 3.3 μT.

12 · Bringing It All Together

Let us stand back and admire what we have built. We started with rubbing amber and ended with electromagnetic waves — with light itself. The journey from Coulomb to Maxwell took about a century of careful experiment and bold theorizing. Here is the logical chain:

  1. Charges exist and exert forces on each other (Coulomb).
  2. Charges create electric fields in the space around them (Faraday’s insight).
  3. Moving charges (currents) create magnetic fields (Oersted, Biot, Savart, Ampère).
  4. Changing magnetic fields create electric fields (Faraday’s law of induction).
  5. Changing electric fields create magnetic fields (Maxwell’s displacement current).
  6. Steps 4 and 5 together mean electromagnetic disturbances can propagate through space as waves, at speed c = 1/√(μ0ε0).
  7. That speed equals the speed of light — so light is an electromagnetic wave.

“The whole of electricity and magnetism, and the whole of optics, are just the consequences of four equations. And the beauty of it is that those four equations contain in them all the phenomena you can think of. You don’t need anything else.”

The Lorentz force — the complete electromagnetic force law If you know E and B everywhere, the force on a charge q moving with velocity v is:
F = q(E + v × B)
Maxwell’s equations plus the Lorentz force law: that is the complete classical theory of electromagnetism. Everything else — circuits, motors, generators, antennas, radio, television, the Internet — is engineering detail.

Problems to try

  1. Two charges of +3 μC and −5 μC are 0.2 m apart. Find the force between them and the electric field at the midpoint.
  2. Use Gauss’s law to find the electric field inside and outside a uniformly charged sphere of total charge Q and radius R.
  3. A parallel-plate capacitor has plates of area 0.01 m2 separated by 1 mm. Find its capacitance, and the energy stored when charged to 100 V.
  4. A solenoid has 500 turns, length 0.3 m, and carries 2 A. Find B inside and the inductance if the cross-sectional area is 5 cm2.
  5. A circular loop of radius 0.1 m sits in a magnetic field that increases uniformly from 0 to 0.5 T in 0.02 s. Find the induced EMF.
  6. Show that the units of 1/√(μ0ε0) reduce to m/s.
