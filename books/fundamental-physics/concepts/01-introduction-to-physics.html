The beginning

Introduction to Physics

What is the world made of? That is the oldest question. Before there were universities or laboratories or even written language, someone sat under the night sky and wondered: why do things fall? Why does fire burn? Why does the moon come and go? Physics begins with that wondering. It is not a collection of formulas in a dusty textbook. It is the art of asking "why?" and then refusing to stop until you get an answer that actually works.

Now here's the thing most people get wrong about physics. They think it is about memorising equations. They think if you know F = ma and can plug in the numbers, you are doing physics. That is like saying you can play cricket because you own a bat. The equations are tools. The physics is in the thinking — in the struggle to understand what the equations mean.

"The trick is to ask the right question. Nature is not going to volunteer the answer. You have to corner her, politely but firmly, and say: 'Look, I know something is going on here. Tell me what it is.' That is the spirit of physics."

You are beginning a BSc in Physics, which means you have signed up for one of the greatest intellectual adventures human beings have devised. Over the next three years you will learn to describe how planets orbit, why metals conduct electricity, what holds an atomic nucleus together, and why the universe is expanding. But before we dive into any of that, we need to understand what physics is, how physicists think, and what tools they use.

1.1 — What Is Physics?

The word physics comes from the Greek physis, meaning "nature." Physics is the study of the fundamental rules that govern everything in the natural world — from the smallest quarks inside a proton to the largest galaxy clusters stretching across billions of light-years.

But that definition, while correct, misses the flavour of the thing. Physics is really about patterns. You drop a stone and it falls. You drop a heavier stone and it also falls — at the same rate, as Galileo showed us. That sameness, that pattern, is what a physicist lives for. Once you spot the pattern, you write it down as a law. And then you test the hell out of it.

Key Idea Physics is not about memorising facts. It is about finding the simplest, deepest rules that explain the widest range of phenomena. One good law — like Newton's law of gravitation — can explain falling apples, orbiting moons, and the tides of the ocean, all at once.

Think about this for a moment. The same force that makes a mango fall from a tree in Ratnagiri is the force that keeps the Earth going around the Sun. That is an extraordinary claim, and Newton had the audacity to make it — and the genius to prove it.

"The universe is not required to be in perfect harmony with human ambition. But it happens to be, at least partly, understandable. And that is the miracle."

Physics vs. the Other Sciences

Physics is sometimes called the "fundamental" science. Chemistry is built on top of physics — chemical bonds are electromagnetic interactions between atoms. Biology is built on top of chemistry. Geology relies on physics for understanding plate tectonics (convection, radioactive decay), and astronomy is essentially physics applied to the sky. This does not mean physics is "better" than these other sciences. It means physics provides the foundation.

A biologist studying how a frog's muscles work will, at the bottom of the chain of explanations, find actin and myosin proteins, which are held together by electrostatic forces, which are described by Coulomb's law, which emerges from quantum electrodynamics. The chain always ends in physics.

1.2 — The Scientific Method

Physics does not run on opinion or authority. It runs on evidence. The process by which physicists (and all scientists) build reliable knowledge is called the scientific method. It is not a rigid recipe — real science is messier than any flowchart — but the core loop looks like this:

  1. Observation: You notice something. The sky is blue. A compass needle points north. Hot water cools faster when it starts hotter (the Mpemba effect).
  2. Question: Why does it do that?
  3. Hypothesis: You propose a tentative explanation. "Maybe the sky is blue because air molecules scatter short wavelengths of light more than long ones."
  4. Prediction: If the hypothesis is right, what else should follow? "Then the sky should be redder at sunset, when light passes through more atmosphere."
  5. Experiment: You test the prediction. You measure the spectrum of skylight at noon and at sunset.
  6. Analysis: Does the data match the prediction? If yes, the hypothesis gains support. If no, you revise or discard it.
  7. Theory: After extensive testing, a well-supported hypothesis gets promoted to a theory — a framework that explains a broad range of phenomena and has survived every attempt to disprove it.
Common Misconception In everyday language, "theory" means a guess. In science, a theory is the highest rank an explanation can achieve. General relativity is a theory. Evolution is a theory. They are not guesses — they are the most rigorously tested explanations we have.

"It does not matter how beautiful your guess is. It does not matter how smart you are, who made the guess, or what his name is. If it disagrees with experiment, it is wrong. That is all there is to it."

This loop — observe, hypothesise, predict, test, revise — is the engine of physics. Every equation in your textbook started as someone's wild guess that survived experiment after experiment until nobody could knock it down.

Falsifiability

A genuine scientific hypothesis must be falsifiable. That means there must be some conceivable experiment that could prove it wrong. If your "theory" cannot be tested — if no observation could ever contradict it — then it is not science. It might be philosophy, or poetry, or religion, but it is not physics.

Karl Popper made this point clear in the 20th century, but physicists had been living by it for centuries. When Einstein proposed general relativity in 1915, he predicted that starlight passing near the Sun would bend by a specific angle (1.75 arcseconds). Arthur Eddington tested this during the 1919 solar eclipse. If the light had not bent, or bent by a different amount, Einstein's theory would have been in serious trouble. It bent by the right amount.

1.3 — Measurement, Units, and the SI System

Physics is a quantitative science. Saying "the ball is going fast" is not physics. Saying "the ball is travelling at 42.3 metres per second in the positive x-direction" — that is physics. Measurement converts vague impressions into precise numbers, and numbers are the language of nature.

The SI System

To avoid chaos (imagine if every state in India used a different unit for length!), scientists worldwide have agreed on the Système International d'Unités (SI), which defines seven base quantities and their units:

This is a triumph of physics: we anchor our measurement system to the universe itself.

CGS and Other Systems

Older textbooks (and many theoretical physics papers) use the CGS system: centimetre, gram, second. In CGS, force is measured in dynes (1 dyne = 10−5 N) and energy in ergs (1 erg = 10−7 J). You will encounter CGS in electromagnetism especially, where the Gaussian CGS system uses statcoulombs instead of coulombs. It is a nuisance, but a manageable one.

Conversion Quick-Reference 1 m = 100 cm  |  1 kg = 1000 g  |  1 N = 105 dyn  |  1 J = 107 erg

Derived Units

All other physical quantities are derived from the seven base quantities. Velocity is length/time (m/s). Force is mass × acceleration (kg·m/s2 = newton). Energy is force × distance (kg·m2/s2 = joule). Pressure is force/area (kg/(m·s2) = pascal).

1.4 — Dimensional Analysis

Now here's the thing that will save your life in exams more than any formula sheet: dimensional analysis. Every physical equation must be dimensionally consistent. You cannot add metres to seconds, any more than you can add apples to oranges.

Every physical quantity has dimensions expressed in terms of the base quantities: mass [M], length [L], time [T], electric current [I], temperature [Θ], amount [N], and luminous intensity [J]. For most of mechanics, you only need [M], [L], and [T].

The Golden Rule In any valid physical equation, the dimensions on the left side must equal the dimensions on the right side. If they do not match, the equation is guaranteed wrong. If they do match, the equation might be right (but you still need to check the numerical factors).

Example: Checking an Equation

Suppose someone claims the period of a simple pendulum is:

T = 2π l g

Let us check the dimensions. The left side is time: [T]. The right side: l has dimensions [L], and g has dimensions [L T−2]. So [L] / [L T−2] = [T2]. That gives dimensions of [T2], not [T]. Something is wrong! The correct formula must involve a square root:

T = 2π √ l g

Now the dimensions are √[T2] = [T]. It checks out. See? Dimensional analysis caught the error before we ever had to set up a pendulum.

Example: Deriving a Formula

How does the speed of waves on a stretched string depend on the string's properties? Let us guess that the wave speed v depends on the tension F (dimensions [M L T−2]) and the linear mass density μ (dimensions [M L−1]).

We write v = C · Fa · μb, where C is a dimensionless constant. Matching dimensions:

[L T−1] = [M L T−2]a · [M L−1]b = [Ma+b La−b T−2a]

Comparing exponents: for M: a + b = 0, for T: −2a = −1 so a = 1/2, and then b = −1/2. For L: a − b = 1, which gives 1/2 − (−1/2) = 1. It checks. So:

v = C √ F μ

The exact solution (which you will derive in the waves chapter) gives C = 1. Dimensional analysis got us the entire functional form — for free!

"Dimensional analysis is like a sanity check. It will not tell you the answer, but it will tell you when you are insane. And in physics, that is already half the battle."

1.5 — Orders of Magnitude

Physics covers an absurd range of scales. The observable universe is about 1026 metres across. A proton is about 10−15 metres across. That is 41 orders of magnitude — a 1 followed by 41 zeros. No other science operates across such a staggering range.

An order of magnitude is a factor of ten. When we say something is "three orders of magnitude larger," we mean it is roughly 1000 times bigger. Physicists use order-of-magnitude estimates all the time — quick, rough calculations that tell you whether an answer is in the right ballpark.

Fermi Estimation

The great physicist Enrico Fermi was famous for making order-of-magnitude estimates with minimal data. The classic example: "How many piano tuners are there in Chicago?" You do not need to know the exact answer. You estimate the population (about 3 million), guess what fraction own pianos (maybe 1 in 20, so 150,000 pianos), estimate how often a piano needs tuning (once a year), how many pianos a tuner can service per day (4), and how many days a tuner works per year (250). So each tuner handles about 1,000 pianos per year, giving 150,000/1,000 = 150 piano tuners. The actual number? Roughly 100–200. Close enough!

Fermi Estimation Challenge

  2. If you stacked all the people in India on top of each other, how tall would the stack be? Would it reach the Moon?

  4. How long would it take to count to one billion if you said one number every second?

1.6 — Significant Figures and Precision

When you measure something, your answer is only as good as your instrument. If your ruler has millimetre markings, you cannot claim to measure a length to the nearest micrometre. The number of significant figures in a measurement tells you how precise it is.

Rules for counting significant figures:

  • All non-zero digits are significant: 2.35 has 3 significant figures.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant: 1007 has 4 significant figures.
  • Leading zeros are not significant: 0.0042 has 2 significant figures.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant: 2.500 has 4 significant figures.
  • Trailing zeros in a whole number are ambiguous: 1500 could be 2, 3, or 4 significant figures. Use scientific notation (1.50 × 103) to be clear.
Warning When multiplying or dividing, the result should have no more significant figures than the least precise input. When adding or subtracting, round to the least number of decimal places. Getting this wrong will cost you marks, and more importantly, it can cost lives in engineering applications.

1.7 — The Four Fundamental Forces

Everything that happens in the universe — every push, pull, collision, explosion, chemical reaction, nuclear decay, the beating of your heart, the light from a star — is caused by just four fundamental forces. Four! That is all nature needs to build everything you see.

"You might think the world is complicated, with its billions of phenomena, but underneath it all, there are only four forces pulling the strings. That is an outrageous simplification, and the best part is: it is true."

1. Gravitational Force

Gravity is the most familiar force. It pulls you toward the Earth, keeps the Moon in orbit, and holds galaxies together. Every object with mass attracts every other object with mass. The force is described by Newton's law of universal gravitation:

F = G m1 m2 r2

Gravity is the weakest of the four forces — fantastically weak, in fact. The gravitational attraction between two protons is about 1036 times weaker than their electromagnetic repulsion. Yet gravity dominates at large scales because it is always attractive (no "negative mass" to cancel it out) and has infinite range.

2. Electromagnetic Force

The electromagnetic force acts between electrically charged particles. It is responsible for light, radio waves, chemical bonds, the rigidity of solids, friction, the nerve impulses in your brain — basically everything you experience in daily life that is not gravity. It is described by Maxwell's equations and, at the quantum level, by quantum electrodynamics (QED).

The electromagnetic force can be attractive (opposite charges) or repulsive (like charges). It has infinite range, like gravity, but because charges come in positive and negative varieties, large-scale electromagnetic effects tend to cancel out.

3. Strong Nuclear Force

The strong force is what holds the atomic nucleus together. Protons are positively charged and should repel each other violently at the tiny distances inside a nucleus. The strong force overpowers that electromagnetic repulsion — but only at very short range (about 10−15 m, roughly the size of a nucleus). Beyond that range, it essentially vanishes.

At a deeper level, the strong force binds quarks together to form protons and neutrons. This is described by quantum chromodynamics (QCD), where the "charge" is called colour charge (nothing to do with actual colours — it is just a name).

4. Weak Nuclear Force

The weak force is responsible for certain types of radioactive decay, such as beta decay, where a neutron transforms into a proton by emitting an electron and an antineutrino. It has an even shorter range than the strong force (about 10−18 m) and is much weaker. But without it, the Sun would not shine — the fusion process that powers stars relies on the weak force to convert protons into neutrons.

Unification In the 1960s and 1970s, Sheldon Glashow, Abdus Salam, and Steven Weinberg showed that the electromagnetic force and the weak force are actually two aspects of a single electroweak force. At high enough energies (above about 100 GeV), they become indistinguishable. Physicists dream of unifying all four forces into a single "Theory of Everything" — but we are not there yet.

Notice the incredible disparity. If the strong force has a strength of 1, then gravity is 10−39. That means gravity is a million trillion trillion trillion times weaker than the strong force. And yet gravity shapes the large-scale structure of the entire universe! The trick is that gravity is always attractive and never cancels out.

1.8 — The Branches of Physics

Physics is a vast subject, and over the centuries it has branched into many specialisations. But here is the beautiful thing: they are all connected. Thermodynamics is statistical mechanics applied to large numbers of particles. Optics is a special case of electromagnetism. Nuclear physics is an application of quantum mechanics. The branches are not separate trees — they are limbs of the same tree.

Classical Mechanics

The study of motion and forces, founded by Newton and refined by Lagrange, Hamilton, and others. Covers everything from falling apples to rocket trajectories. Valid when objects are large (much bigger than atoms) and slow (much slower than light).

Electromagnetism

The unified theory of electricity and magnetism, perfected by Maxwell in the 1860s. Explains light, radio waves, circuits, magnets, and how your phone works. At the quantum level, it becomes quantum electrodynamics (QED) — the most precisely tested theory in all of science.

Thermodynamics and Statistical Mechanics

Thermodynamics deals with heat, work, and energy transformations using macroscopic quantities like temperature and entropy. Statistical mechanics explains these same phenomena by tracking the behaviour of vast numbers of individual particles. Together, they explain why your chai gets cold, why engines cannot be 100% efficient, and why time has a direction.

Optics

The study of light — reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference, polarisation. Technically a branch of electromagnetism, but so rich and practically important that it stands on its own.

Quantum Mechanics

The physics of the very small. At atomic and subatomic scales, particles behave in ways that defy everyday intuition — they can be in two places at once, tunnel through barriers, and are described by probability waves rather than definite trajectories. Quantum mechanics is the foundation of modern chemistry, electronics, and materials science.

Special and General Relativity

Einstein's theories. Special relativity (1905) says the speed of light is the same for all observers and that space and time are intertwined into spacetime. General relativity (1915) says gravity is the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy. Essential for GPS satellites, black holes, and cosmology.

Nuclear and Particle Physics

Nuclear physics studies the structure of atomic nuclei and processes like fission and fusion. Particle physics goes deeper, studying the fundamental constituents of matter (quarks, leptons, bosons) and their interactions. The Standard Model of particle physics is our best theory of the subatomic world.

Condensed Matter Physics

The physics of solids and liquids — how atoms arrange themselves into crystals, how electrons flow through conductors and semiconductors, why some materials are magnetic, and what makes a superconductor super. This branch has given us transistors, LEDs, and the entire semiconductor industry.

Astrophysics and Cosmology

Astrophysics applies physics to understand stars, galaxies, and the interstellar medium. Cosmology asks the biggest questions of all: How did the universe begin? How will it end? What is dark matter? What is dark energy?

Connections Your BSc programme will typically start with classical mechanics and electromagnetism, then move to quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and statistical mechanics. Think of it as building a house: you need the foundation (mechanics, EM) before the walls (quantum, thermo) before the roof (condensed matter, nuclear, particle). This textbook follows that natural progression.

1.9 — Why Physics Matters

Let me be direct with you. Some of your friends may have chosen engineering or medicine because "it leads to a job." They are not wrong about the job part. But here is what physics gives you that no other subject can: a way of thinking.

A physicist who has never seen a particular problem before can often solve it, because physics teaches you to reason from first principles. You do not memorise recipes; you learn to cook. And that skill — reasoning from first principles — is valuable in every field, from data science to finance to public policy.

Technology

Virtually every technology you use daily exists because of physics:

  • Smartphones: Semiconductors (quantum mechanics + condensed matter), touchscreens (electrostatics), GPS (general relativity + atomic clocks), cameras (optics), wireless communication (electromagnetism).
  • Medical imaging: X-rays (atomic physics), MRI (nuclear magnetic resonance), ultrasound (acoustics), PET scans (nuclear physics + antimatter).
  • Power generation: Turbines (thermodynamics), solar cells (quantum mechanics), nuclear reactors (nuclear physics).
  • The internet: Optical fibres (optics), lasers (quantum mechanics), transistors (condensed matter).

"Physics is not an ivory tower. Every time you flick a switch, make a phone call, or take a photograph, you are using physics. The question is not whether physics matters. The question is whether anything happens without it."

Understanding Nature

But physics is not just about gadgets. It is about understanding. Why is the sky blue? Why do stars shine? What is time? These are not abstract questions for philosophers — they are questions that physics can answer with precision and beauty. And once you understand why the sky is blue (Rayleigh scattering of short-wavelength photons by nitrogen and oxygen molecules), you see the sunset differently. You see it with comprehension. And comprehension, in my experience, makes beauty more beautiful, not less.

India's Physics Legacy

India has a magnificent tradition in physics. Satyendra Nath Bose's work on quantum statistics gave us Bose-Einstein condensation and the word "boson." C.V. Raman discovered the Raman effect — the inelastic scattering of light — and won the Nobel Prize. Meghnad Saha's ionisation equation revolutionised astrophysics. Homi Bhabha laid the foundation for India's nuclear programme. S. Chandrasekhar's work on stellar structure (the Chandrasekhar limit) told us when a star must collapse into a white dwarf or explode. You are inheriting a rich tradition. Take it seriously.

1.10 — How to Study Physics

Since you are beginning your BSc, let me give you some practical advice that I wish someone had given me.

  1. Do problems. Reading physics is not learning physics. You learn physics by doing problems. Lots of them. Work through examples, struggle with exercises, and only look at the solution after you have genuinely tried. The struggle is where the learning happens.
  2. Build intuition first, formalism second. Before you memorise a formula, make sure you understand what it means physically. What does each term represent? What happens if you double this variable? What limit does the equation approach?
  3. Draw pictures. Physicists think in pictures. Draw free-body diagrams, ray diagrams, circuit diagrams, potential energy curves. A good diagram is worth a hundred equations.
  4. Check dimensions. Always. If the dimensions do not match, you have made an error. This catches mistakes faster than anything else.
  5. Check limits. Does your answer make sense in extreme cases? If you set the mass to zero, does the equation give a sensible result? If you let time go to infinity? Checking limits is how physicists debug their work.
  6. Discuss with friends. Physics is not a solitary activity. Explain concepts to your classmates. If you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it well enough.

"The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool. So always check. Check the dimensions. Check the limits. Check against experiment. And check again."

Chapter Summary
  • Physics is the study of nature's fundamental rules, from quarks to galaxies.
  • The scientific method — observe, hypothesise, predict, test, revise — is the engine of physics.
  • The SI system provides seven base units; all other units are derived from these.
  • Dimensional analysis lets you check equations and even derive unknown relationships.
  • Physics spans 41 orders of magnitude in length alone.
  • Only four fundamental forces — gravity, electromagnetism, strong, weak — drive all phenomena.
  • Physics branches (mechanics, EM, quantum, thermo, etc.) are all interconnected.
  • Physics matters because it underlies all technology and deepens our understanding of nature.
