Chapter 04
Rhythm of nature

Waves & Oscillations

Why does pushing a child on a swing at the right moment make them go higher? That is resonance, and it is one of the most powerful ideas in all of physics. In this chapter we will discover how nature loves to repeat herself — swinging pendulums, vibrating strings, sound echoing through a temple corridor, light shimmering across a soap bubble. Every one of these phenomena is a story of something going back and forth, and the mathematics that describes all of them is astonishingly the same.

The universe is a great symphony, and oscillations are its notes. Learn the notes and you can read any score nature writes. — R. Feynman

4.1   Simple Harmonic Motion

Imagine you attach a ball to a spring on a frictionless table and pull the ball to one side. When you let go, the ball rushes back, overshoots the middle, compresses the spring on the other side, and comes back again. This back-and-forth is what we call oscillation. The simplest flavour — where the restoring force is exactly proportional to the displacement — is called simple harmonic motion (SHM).

The restoring force and the equation of motion

For a mass m attached to a spring of stiffness k, Hooke’s law gives the restoring force:

F = −kx

Apply Newton’s second law, F = ma, and remember that acceleration is the second derivative of position:

m d2xdt2 = −kx

Dividing both sides by m and defining ω2 = km , we get the canonical SHM equation:

d2xdt2 + ω2x = 0
Key Result The general solution is x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) , where A is the amplitude, ω is the angular frequency, and φ is the phase constant set by initial conditions.

The period and frequency follow immediately:

T = 2πω = 2π √ mk    f = 1T = ω2π

Energy in SHM

The total mechanical energy shuttles between kinetic and potential forms without any loss (in the ideal case). At any instant:

E = 12kx2 + 12mv2 = 12kA2

At the extreme positions (x = ±A), all energy is potential; at the equilibrium point (x = 0), all energy is kinetic. The crossover happens smoothly, like money moving between two bank accounts that always total the same amount.

Nature never loses her bookkeeping. Energy is always conserved — it just changes costume.

The simple pendulum

A pendulum is just gravity’s version of a spring. The restoring “force” here is the component of gravity tangent to the arc: Ftangent = −mg sin θ. For small angles (say θ < 15°), sin θ ≈ θ in radians, and we recover SHM:

d2θdt2 + gLθ = 0  ⇒  T = 2π √ Lg
Note The pendulum period depends only on length L and gravitational acceleration g, not on the mass or the amplitude (for small swings). This independence from mass was one of Galileo’s great discoveries, reportedly made by watching a chandelier swing in the cathedral at Pisa.
Investigation

Measure g with a pendulum

Take a string of known length L (about 1 m) and a heavy bob. Time 20 full oscillations, divide by 20 to get T. Then compute g = 4π2L / T2 . Repeat for five different lengths and plot T2 vs L. The slope gives you 4π2/g. How close do you get to 9.81 m/s2?

Phase space: a deeper look

If you plot velocity v on the vertical axis and displacement x on the horizontal axis, the state of the oscillator traces out an ellipse. This is called a phase portrait. At the rightmost point of the ellipse the mass is at +A with zero velocity; at the top of the ellipse it passes through the centre at maximum speed. The ellipse never closes in on itself (no energy loss) — it just goes round and round forever. This is the geometric fingerprint of perpetual, undamped oscillation.

4.2   Damped & Forced Oscillations

The real world has friction. A swing in the park eventually stops if nobody pushes it. To model this, we add a damping force proportional to velocity:

m d2xdt2 + b dxdt + kx = 0

Here b is the damping coefficient. Define the damping ratio γ = b2m . Three regimes emerge:

RegimeConditionBehaviour
Underdamped γ < ω0 Oscillates with exponentially decaying amplitude. Most musical instruments live here.
Critically damped γ = ω0 Returns to equilibrium as fast as possible without overshooting. Car shock absorbers aim for this.
Overdamped γ > ω0 Sluggish return without oscillation. A door closer on a heavy fire door.

The underdamped solution is the most interesting:

x(t) = Ae−γt cos(ωdt + φ)   where   ωd = √ ω02 − γ2

The envelope Ae−γt is nature’s gentle hand, slowly hushing the oscillation to silence. It is the exponential decay you see everywhere — in vibrating tuning forks, in ringing bells, in the aftertaste of a plucked sitar string.

Forced oscillations and resonance

Now suppose you push the system periodically with an external force F0 cos(ωdt). The equation of motion becomes:

m d2xdt2 + b dxdt + kx = F0 cos(Ωt)

After the transients die out, the system settles into a steady-state oscillation at the driving frequency Ω. The amplitude of this steady state depends on how close Ω is to the natural frequency ω0:

A(Ω) = F0 / m √ (ω02 − Ω2)2 + 4γ2Ω2
Key Result — Resonance When Ω ≈ ω0, the amplitude peaks dramatically. This is resonance. The less the damping, the sharper and taller the peak. In the extreme case of zero damping, the amplitude would grow without bound — which is why soldiers break step when crossing a bridge, and why opera singers can (in principle) shatter a wine glass.
Warning The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed in 1940 partly because wind vortices drove the bridge at a frequency close to its natural frequency. Resonance is beautiful in music but catastrophic in engineering if not accounted for. Always design with damping in mind!

Resonance is Nature’s amplifier. Push at the right rhythm and tiny forces build up to huge effects. This is the child on the swing — each push is small, but timed just right, the swing soars.

4.3   Waves: The Travelling Oscillation

An oscillation confined to one spot is interesting, but when it travels, it becomes a wave. Imagine a long row of coupled pendulums: when you push the first one, it nudges the second, which nudges the third, and so on. The disturbance propagates down the line while each individual pendulum merely oscillates about its own equilibrium. That is the essential magic of a wave: energy travels, but matter does not.

Transverse vs longitudinal waves

In a transverse wave, the oscillation is perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Shake a rope up and down and the wave moves horizontally. Light, radio waves, and waves on a string are all transverse.

In a longitudinal wave, the oscillation is parallel to the direction of propagation. Push and pull one end of a Slinky along its length and you create compressions and rarefactions that travel down the coil. Sound waves in air are longitudinal.

The wave equation

A sinusoidal wave travelling in the +x direction can be written:

y(x, t) = A sin(kx − ωt + φ)

where k = 2π/λ is the wave number and λ is the wavelength. The wave speed is:

v = ωk = λf

This relation — speed = wavelength × frequency — is universal. It holds for water waves, sound waves, light waves, every kind of wave. Memorise it. Tattoo it on your brain.

The wave function satisfies the classical wave equation:

∂2y∂x2 = 1v2 ∂2y∂t2
Note for BSc students This is a second-order partial differential equation. In your mathematics course you will learn to solve it by separation of variables. For now, verify by direct substitution that y = A sin(kx − ωt) is indeed a solution when v = ω/k.

Wave speed on a string

For a string under tension T with linear mass density μ (mass per unit length), Newton’s laws applied to a tiny segment give:

v = √ Tμ

This is why a sitar’s bass strings are thick (high μ, low v, low pitch) and the treble strings are thin (low μ, high v, high pitch). Tightening a string increases T and raises the pitch. Every musician who tunes an instrument is doing physics.

4.4   Superposition, Interference & Beats

The principle of superposition says that when two waves overlap, the net displacement at any point is simply the algebraic sum of the individual displacements. This principle holds for all linear waves, and it leads to two beautiful phenomena: interference and beats.

Constructive and destructive interference

When two waves of the same frequency arrive in phase (crest meets crest), they add up to a wave with double the amplitude — constructive interference. When they arrive exactly out of phase (crest meets trough), they cancel — destructive interference. Noise-cancelling headphones exploit this: they generate an anti-phase copy of the ambient noise, and the two signals annihilate each other in your ear.

Beats

When two waves of slightly different frequencies superpose, the result is a wave whose amplitude waxes and wanes periodically. This pulsation is called beats. Add two cosines:

cos(ω1t) + cos(ω2t) = 2 cos ( ω1 − ω22 t ) cos ( ω1 + ω22 t )

The first cosine is a slowly varying envelope; the second is a rapidly oscillating carrier. The beat frequency you hear is:

fbeat = |f1 − f2|

Musicians use beats to tune instruments: when two strings are nearly in unison, you hear a slow wobble. As you tighten one string, the wobble speeds up or slows down. When it vanishes completely, the strings are in perfect tune.

4.5   Standing Waves

When two identical waves travel in opposite directions on the same string, their superposition produces a pattern that does not move — a standing wave. Mathematically:

y = A sin(kx − ωt) + A sin(kx + ωt) = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt)

Notice that the spatial part sin(kx) and the temporal part cos(ωt) are now separated. The wave oscillates in time, but the shape does not travel.

Nodes and antinodes

Points where sin(kx) = 0 never move at all — they are called nodes. Points where sin(kx) = ±1 oscillate with the maximum amplitude — they are antinodes. On a string fixed at both ends (length L), the boundary conditions force nodes at each end, which restricts the allowed wavelengths:

λn = 2Ln    fn = n v2L    n = 1, 2, 3, …

The n = 1 mode is the fundamental (or first harmonic). The n = 2 mode is the second harmonic (first overtone), and so on. A sitar string vibrates as a superposition of all these harmonics simultaneously — the relative amplitudes determine the timbre (tone colour) of the sound.

Key Idea Boundary conditions quantise the allowed modes. This is a classical preview of quantum mechanics, where the same mathematics — standing waves in a potential well — gives rise to discrete energy levels of electrons in atoms.

Standing waves in pipes

Standing waves also form inside air columns. An organ pipe open at both ends has pressure nodes (displacement antinodes) at each end, giving harmonics just like a string: fn = nv/2L. A pipe closed at one end has a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end, so only odd harmonics are present: fn = nv/4L for n = 1, 3, 5, … This is why a clarinet (effectively a closed pipe) sounds different from a flute (open pipe) even when playing the same note — the missing even harmonics change the timbre.

4.6   The Doppler Effect

You have heard it a hundred times: an ambulance siren sounds higher in pitch as it approaches and lower as it recedes. This is the Doppler effect, discovered by Christian Doppler in 1842. The key idea is that motion compresses or stretches the wavefronts.

The formula

Let v be the speed of sound, vs the speed of the source, and vo the speed of the observer. The observed frequency is:

f′ = f ( v ± vo v ∓ vs )
Sign Convention Use the upper signs when source and observer move towards each other (frequency increases), and the lower signs when they move apart (frequency decreases). A handy mnemonic: “toward = top sign.”

Special cases

SituationFormula
Source approaching, observer at rest f′ = f · v / (v − vs)
Source receding, observer at rest f′ = f · v / (v + vs)
Observer approaching, source at rest f′ = f · (v + vo) / v
Observer receding, source at rest f′ = f · (v − vo) / v
Investigation

Doppler with a buzzer

Attach a small piezo buzzer to a string and whirl it in a circle. Stand a few metres away and listen: you will hear the pitch rise and fall as the buzzer swings toward and away from you. If you know the buzzer frequency and can estimate the rotation speed, calculate the expected frequency shift and compare with what you hear.

The Doppler effect is not just about ambulances. Astronomers use the redshift of starlight (the optical Doppler effect) to measure how fast galaxies are moving away from us. That is how Hubble discovered the expansion of the universe. Radar guns use the Doppler shift of microwaves to clock your car’s speed. Same physics, wildly different applications.

Shock waves and the sonic boom

What happens when the source moves faster than sound? The wavefronts pile up into a cone-shaped shock wave. The half-angle θ of this Mach cone satisfies:

sin θ = vvs = 1Ma

where Ma = vs/v is the Mach number. When a fighter jet crosses Mach 1, the conical shock front sweeps over you as a thunderous sonic boom. The same physics explains the sharp crack of a bullwhip — the tip exceeds the speed of sound.

4.7   Energy Transport & Intensity

Waves carry energy without transporting matter. The intensity I of a wave is the power per unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation:

I = PA   [W/m2]

For a sinusoidal wave on a string, the average power transmitted is:

Pavg = 12 μω2A2v

Two crucial dependences: power goes as the square of the amplitude and the square of the frequency. Double the amplitude and you transmit four times the energy. This quadratic scaling is why high-frequency ultrasound can deliver enough energy to shatter kidney stones.

Inverse-square law A point source radiating equally in all directions spreads its power over a sphere of area 4πr2. So the intensity falls as 1/r2: I = P4πr2 . This is why a distant thunder is a rumble while close thunder is a crack — same energy, spread over a much bigger sphere.

4.8   Summary & Connections

Let us step back and see the big picture. We started with a mass on a spring — the simplest oscillator — and extracted the mathematics of SHM: sinusoidal motion governed by a second-order differential equation with the restoring force proportional to displacement. We added friction (damping) and external driving (forced oscillation), discovering the magnificent phenomenon of resonance. Then we set oscillations free to travel, becoming waves, and found:

  • The superposition principle lets waves combine constructively, destructively, or in beats.
  • Standing waves arise from counter-propagating waves and explain musical instruments.
  • The Doppler effect connects wave frequency to relative motion of source and observer.
  • Wave energy goes as the square of amplitude and obeys the inverse-square law for spreading.

The same differential equation, the same sinusoidal solutions, the same superposition principle — they appear again and again. In electricity (LC circuits oscillate). In optics (light is an electromagnetic wave). In quantum mechanics (the Schrodinger equation is a wave equation). Master this chapter, and you have the skeleton key to half of physics.

Selected exercises

  1. A 0.5 kg mass attached to a spring of constant k = 200 N/m is displaced 4 cm and released. Find the period, maximum velocity, and total energy.
  2. Two tuning forks of frequencies 256 Hz and 260 Hz are sounded together. What is the beat frequency? How many beats do you hear per second?
  3. A guitar string of length 65 cm vibrates in its third harmonic. If the wave speed on the string is 300 m/s, find the frequency and wavelength of this mode.
  4. An ambulance siren emits at 800 Hz. If the ambulance approaches you at 30 m/s and the speed of sound is 343 m/s, what frequency do you hear?
  5. A pendulum clock keeps perfect time at sea level where g = 9.81 m/s2. If you take it to a mountain where g = 9.78 m/s2, does it run fast or slow, and by how many seconds per day?
