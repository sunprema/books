Chapter 13
The whole universe

Cosmology

The universe is a big place, and it's getting bigger. Here's how we know — and what we still don't. If you have ever looked up at the night sky and felt that little chill run down your spine — that sense of something unimaginably vast staring back at you — then you already know the first thing about cosmology. It is the study of everything: the origin, structure, evolution, and ultimate fate of the entire cosmos. And I must tell you, the more we learn, the more astonishing it gets.

"The universe is not only queerer than we suppose, but queerer than we can suppose." — J. B. S. Haldane. I have always loved that line, because in cosmology you keep bumping into things that make your intuition throw up its hands.

13.1   Olbers' Paradox: Why Is the Night Sky Dark?

Here is a question so simple it is embarrassing: why is the sky dark at night? Think about it. If the universe is infinite, eternal, and filled uniformly with stars, then every line of sight should eventually hit the surface of a star. The entire sky should be as bright as the surface of an average star — blazing white in every direction, day and night.

This puzzle is called Olbers' paradox, after the German astronomer Heinrich Olbers who stated it clearly in 1823 (though others noticed it before him). The fact that the sky is dark tells us something profound: at least one of our assumptions must be wrong.

Key Idea The darkness of the night sky is evidence that the universe has a finite age. Light from very distant stars has not had time to reach us. The universe is roughly 13.8 billion years old, so we can only see light from a finite sphere around us — the observable universe.

There is a second factor too: the expansion of the universe stretches light from distant sources to longer, redder wavelengths, diluting the energy of that light. Between finite age and cosmic expansion, the paradox dissolves — but in dissolving, it opens the door to a much bigger story.

13.2   Hubble's Law and the Expanding Universe

In 1929, Edwin Hubble made one of the most important observations in the history of science. He measured the distances to nearby galaxies (using Cepheid variable stars as "standard candles") and the speeds at which they were moving away from us (using the Doppler redshift of their spectral lines). He found a stunning relationship:

v = H0 ⋅ d

where v is the recession velocity of a galaxy, d is its distance from us, and H0 is a constant now called the Hubble constant. The modern best-fit value is approximately H0 ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc (kilometres per second per megaparsec).

Note A megaparsec (Mpc) is about 3.26 million light-years. So a galaxy 100 Mpc away is receding at roughly 7,000 km/s. That is about 2.3% of the speed of light — just from the expansion of space itself!

Now here is the crucial point, and it is one that trips people up endlessly: the galaxies are not flying apart through space. Space itself is expanding. Every point in the universe sees every other point receding, and the farther away something is, the faster it recedes. There is no centre to the expansion — it looks the same from everywhere.

Imagine raisins in a loaf of bread that is rising in the oven. Every raisin sees every other raisin moving away, and the more distant raisins move faster. No raisin is "the centre." The expansion is a property of the dough — of space itself.

The Cosmological Principle

Hubble's observation is consistent with the cosmological principle: on the largest scales, the universe is homogeneous (the same everywhere) and isotropic (the same in every direction). This does not mean it looks the same on small scales — obviously there are stars, planets, voids, and clusters — but averaged over hundreds of megaparsecs, the universe is remarkably uniform.

13.3   The Big Bang Theory

If the universe is expanding now, then it was smaller in the past. Run the movie backwards and you reach an inescapable conclusion: at some finite time in the past, the universe was in an extraordinarily hot, dense state. We call this the Big Bang.

Common Misconception The Big Bang was not an explosion in space. It was an expansion of space itself. There was no "centre" where the bang happened. Every point in the universe was the centre — because the entire universe was compressed into that initial state.

The Big Bang model rests on three great pillars of evidence:

  1. The expansion of the universe (Hubble's law)
  2. The cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB)
  3. The abundances of light elements (Big Bang nucleosynthesis)

Each of these was a prediction that was later confirmed. That is the gold standard of science. Let me walk you through each.

13.4   The Cosmic Microwave Background

In 1948, George Gamow and his students Ralph Alpher and Robert Herman made a remarkable prediction. If the early universe was incredibly hot, then as it expanded and cooled, there should have come a moment when it cooled enough for atoms to form. Before that moment, the universe was an opaque fog of plasma — photons could not travel freely because they kept scattering off free electrons. But once atoms formed, the photons were set free.

Those freed photons should still be filling the universe today, stretched by the expansion into microwave wavelengths. Gamow's team predicted a cosmic microwave background (CMB) with a temperature of a few kelvins.

In 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, two radio astronomers at Bell Labs in New Jersey, accidentally discovered this radiation. They were getting a persistent, uniform "hiss" in their antenna that they could not eliminate — they even cleaned out the pigeon droppings, thinking that was the cause! But the signal was real: a nearly perfect blackbody spectrum at a temperature of 2.725 K, coming from every direction in the sky.

Key Result The CMB is the oldest light in the universe, released approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang, at a redshift of z ≈ 1100. It tells us that the universe was once a hot, dense plasma. The COBE, WMAP, and Planck satellites have mapped its tiny temperature fluctuations (ΔT/T ∼ 10−5), which are the seeds of all the structure we see today.

Those tiny fluctuations — one part in a hundred thousand — are perhaps the most important map ever made. They show us the quantum seeds that gravity amplified over billions of years into galaxies, clusters, and the vast cosmic web. Every galaxy, every star, every atom in your body traces back to a faint ripple in the primordial plasma.

13.5   Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

During the first few minutes after the Big Bang, the universe was hot enough for nuclear fusion. Protons and neutrons combined to form the lightest nuclei: deuterium (2H), helium-3 (3He), helium-4 (4He), and trace amounts of lithium-7 (7Li). This process is called Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN).

The theory makes precise predictions about the abundances of these elements:

Element Predicted abundance (by mass) Observed
4He ~24–25% ~24%
Deuterium (2H) ~2.5 × 10−5 ~2.5 × 10−5
3He ~1 × 10−5 ~1 × 10−5
7Li ~5 × 10−10 ~1.6 × 10−10 *

* The lithium problem: observed lithium-7 is about a factor of three lower than predicted. This is an active area of research — one of those loose threads that reminds us the story is not finished.

Note for BSc students BBN is sensitive to the baryon-to-photon ratio η = nb/nγ ≈ 6 × 10−10. This single parameter, determined independently from the CMB, gives concordant element abundances — a powerful consistency check of the Big Bang model.

Heavier elements — carbon, oxygen, iron, gold — were not made in the Big Bang. They were forged later, inside stars and in supernova explosions. We covered that story in Chapter 12. But the lightest elements? Those are a gift from the first three minutes of creation.

13.6   Dark Matter

Now we come to one of the great mysteries. When we look at the universe, we see stars, gas, dust, planets — baryonic matter, made of the familiar protons, neutrons, and electrons. But all of that accounts for only about 5% of the total energy content of the universe. About 27% is something we call dark matter — matter that does not emit, absorb, or reflect light, but whose gravitational effects we can clearly see.

Evidence 1: Galaxy Rotation Curves

In the 1970s, Vera Rubin and Kent Ford measured the rotation speeds of stars in spiral galaxies as a function of distance from the galactic centre. From Newton's law of gravity (and Kepler's third law), we expect that stars far from the centre, where most of the visible mass is concentrated, should orbit more slowly — just as the outer planets in our solar system orbit more slowly than the inner ones.

The expected rotation velocity should fall off as:

v(r) = √(G M(r) / r)

where M(r) is the mass enclosed within radius r. Beyond the visible disc, M(r) should be roughly constant, so v should decline as r−1/2.

But Rubin found that the rotation curves stay flat — stars far from the centre orbit just as fast as those near it. This implies that M(r) keeps increasing with r, even where there is no visible matter. Something unseen is contributing mass — a vast dark matter halo surrounding each galaxy.

Evidence 2: Gravitational Lensing

Einstein's general relativity tells us that mass bends spacetime, and therefore bends the path of light. When we look at distant galaxies through a massive foreground cluster, we see the background images distorted, stretched into arcs, or even duplicated. By measuring the distortion, we can map the total mass of the cluster — and it is far more than the visible matter alone. The extra mass is dark matter.

One of the most spectacular examples is the Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-558), where two galaxy clusters have collided. The visible hot gas (seen in X-rays) slowed down due to the collision and lies in the centre. But gravitational lensing reveals that most of the mass — the dark matter — has passed right through, unimpeded. This is strong evidence that dark matter interacts only gravitationally, not electromagnetically.

Evidence 3: The CMB Power Spectrum

The pattern of tiny temperature fluctuations in the CMB has a characteristic structure — a series of peaks in the angular power spectrum. The heights, positions, and spacings of these peaks are exquisitely sensitive to the composition of the universe. The data from the Planck satellite yields:

Component Fraction of total energy
Ordinary (baryonic) matter~5%
Dark matter~27%
Dark energy~68%

Think about that for a moment. Everything you have ever seen, touched, or measured — every atom in every star, planet, and person — is only 5% of what the universe is made of. The rest is dark. We are not just in the dark about the universe; the universe is mostly dark.

13.7   Dark Energy and Accelerating Expansion

In 1998, two independent teams — the Supernova Cosmology Project and the High-z Supernova Search Team — made a discovery that shocked the physics world. They were using Type Ia supernovae as "standard candles" to measure the expansion history of the universe, expecting to find that gravity was slowing the expansion down. Instead, they found that the expansion is speeding up.

Something is pushing the universe apart, working against gravity. We call this mysterious force dark energy. Its simplest description is Einstein's cosmological constant Λ, which represents a constant energy density pervading all of space.

H2 = 8πG3 ρ − kc2a2 + Λ3

This is the Friedmann equation, the master equation of cosmology. Here H is the Hubble parameter, ρ is the energy density of matter and radiation, k is the spatial curvature, a is the scale factor, and Λ is the cosmological constant. The Λ term drives accelerating expansion.

Key Result The equation of state parameter for dark energy is w = P / (ρc2) ≈ −1. This means dark energy has negative pressure — it acts as a repulsive gravitational effect. In general relativity, pressure contributes to gravity, and negative pressure drives expansion.

I confess, dark energy is the thing that keeps me awake at night. We can measure what it does — but we have essentially no idea what it is. Is it truly a cosmological constant? Is it a dynamical field (quintessence)? Is it a sign that general relativity breaks down on cosmic scales? Nobody knows. And that, my friends, is what makes physics so exciting.

13.8   The Cosmic Timeline

Let us now put the whole story together. The history of the universe, from the Big Bang to the present moment, unfolds across 13.8 billion years. Here is the grand narrative, epoch by epoch:

The Epochs in Brief

Planck era (t < 10−43 s): All four forces are thought to be unified. We have no tested theory for this epoch — it requires quantum gravity.

Inflation (t ∼ 10−36 to 10−32 s): A brief period of exponential expansion, driven by a hypothetical scalar field called the inflaton. Inflation solves the horizon problem (why the CMB is so uniform), the flatness problem (why space is so nearly flat), and the monopole problem (why we do not see magnetic monopoles). It also generates the primordial density fluctuations that seed cosmic structure.

Quark epoch (t ∼ 10−12 to 10−6 s): The universe is a quark-gluon plasma. Too hot for protons and neutrons to exist as bound states.

Nucleosynthesis (t ∼ 3 minutes): Protons and neutrons fuse into light nuclei. After about 20 minutes, the universe is too cool and dilute for further fusion.

Recombination (t ∼ 380,000 years): Electrons combine with nuclei to form neutral atoms. The universe becomes transparent. The CMB is released.

Dark Ages (t ∼ 380,000 yr to ∼200 Myr): No stars yet. The universe is filled with neutral hydrogen, slowly clumping under gravity.

First Stars and Reionisation (t ∼ 200–500 Myr): The first massive stars (Population III) form and their ultraviolet light reionises the surrounding gas.

Galaxy Formation (t ∼ 1–6 Gyr): Galaxies assemble through mergers and accretion. The cosmic star formation rate peaks around z ≈ 2 (about 3 Gyr after the Big Bang).

Solar System (t ∼ 9.2 Gyr): Our Sun and planets form from a collapsing molecular cloud enriched with heavy elements from earlier generations of stars.

Now (t = 13.8 Gyr): You are reading this textbook. The universe continues to expand, and that expansion is accelerating.

Investigation

Estimating the Age of the Universe

If the universe has been expanding at a roughly constant rate, then the age is approximately the inverse of the Hubble constant:

t0 ≈ 1H0 = 170 km/s/Mpc ≈ 14.0 Gyr

Convert: 1 Mpc = 3.086 × 1022 m. So H0 = 70 / (3.086 × 1022) s−1 = 2.27 × 10−18 s−1. Then 1/H0 = 4.41 × 1017 s ≈ 14.0 × 109 years. The more precise value (accounting for the varying expansion rate) is 13.8 Gyr.

Try it: What age would you get for H0 = 50 km/s/Mpc? For 100 km/s/Mpc?

13.9   Inflation: The Very Early Universe

The standard Big Bang model has three nagging problems that inflation resolves beautifully:

The Horizon Problem. The CMB is uniform to one part in 105 across the entire sky. But regions on opposite sides of the sky are so far apart that light could never have travelled between them in the age of the universe. How did they "know" to be at the same temperature? Inflation solves this: before inflation, these regions were close enough to thermalise, then inflation stretched them apart.

The Flatness Problem. The geometry of the universe is very nearly flat (Euclidean). But the Friedmann equation shows that any tiny deviation from flatness grows over time. For the universe to be this flat today, it must have been flat to one part in 1060 at the Planck time. That is an absurd fine-tuning — unless inflation drove the curvature to essentially zero, like inflating a balloon until its surface looks flat.

The Monopole Problem. Grand unified theories predict that the early universe should have produced copious magnetic monopoles. We see none. Inflation dilutes them to undetectable levels.

Key Equation During inflation, the scale factor grows exponentially:
a(t) ∝ eHinft

where Hinf is the (nearly constant) Hubble parameter during inflation. In about 10−32 seconds, the universe expanded by a factor of at least e60 ≈ 1026.

13.10   The Fate of the Universe

What happens in the end? The answer depends on the interplay between the density of matter (which decelerates expansion through gravity) and dark energy (which accelerates it). There are three classic scenarios:

The Big Freeze (most likely)

If dark energy is a cosmological constant (and current data strongly supports this), the expansion will continue to accelerate forever. Galaxies outside our local group will recede beyond our cosmic horizon — we will never see them again. Stars will exhaust their fuel. White dwarfs will cool to black dwarfs. Black holes will evaporate via Hawking radiation over unimaginable timescales (~1067 years for stellar black holes, ~10100 years for supermassive ones). The universe will become cold, dark, and empty — an eternal, dilute void. This is also called the Heat Death.

The Big Crunch

If the matter density were high enough and dark energy weak enough, gravity could eventually halt and reverse the expansion. The universe would collapse back on itself, ending in a singularity — the reverse of the Big Bang. Current observations rule this out for our universe, but it remains a theoretical possibility.

The Big Rip

If dark energy is not constant but instead grows stronger over time (a "phantom" energy with w < −1), the expansion rate would increase without bound. Eventually, the expansion would tear apart galaxy clusters, then galaxies, then solar systems, then planets, then atoms themselves. This scenario is called the Big Rip. Current data does not favour it, but neither does it conclusively rule it out.

Scenario Condition Outcome Status
Big Freeze / Heat Death w = −1 (Λ = const) Eternal cold expansion Most likely
Big Crunch Ωm > 1, no dark energy Collapse to singularity Ruled out
Big Rip w < −1 (phantom energy) All structures torn apart Not favoured
Density Parameters Cosmologists express densities as fractions of the critical density: Ω = ρ/ρcrit, where ρcrit = 3H2/(8πG) ≈ 9.5 × 10−27 kg/m3. If Ωtotal = 1, the universe is flat. Current measurements give Ωtotal = 1.000 ± 0.002 — the universe is flat to extraordinary precision.

The Big Freeze is, I think, the most poignant fate. Not with a bang, but a whimper. The last stars will die, the last black holes will evaporate, and the universe will go on expanding forever into cold, empty darkness. But here is the thing — knowing this does not diminish the wonder. It enhances it. We are here, right now, in the brief cosmic window when stars shine, planets form, and beings made of stardust can look up and ask: how did all this happen?

13.11   The Cosmological Distance Ladder

How do we actually measure distances in cosmology? No single method works at all scales. Instead, we use a cosmic distance ladder, where each rung calibrates the next:

  1. Parallax (< 1 kpc): The Earth's orbital motion shifts the apparent position of nearby stars.
  2. Cepheid variables (up to ~30 Mpc): Their pulsation period is proportional to their luminosity (the Leavitt law). Measure the period, deduce the luminosity, compare with observed brightness to get distance.
  3. Type Ia supernovae (up to ~1000 Mpc): These thermonuclear explosions have a standardisable peak brightness. The 1998 discovery of accelerating expansion used this rung.
  4. Redshift (the entire observable universe): Once Hubble's law is calibrated, the redshift z of a galaxy gives its distance.

13.12   The Hubble Tension

One of the most exciting open problems in cosmology today is the Hubble tension. The value of H0 measured from the nearby universe (using Cepheids and supernovae) gives about 73 km/s/Mpc. But the value inferred from the early universe (from the CMB, via the Planck satellite) gives about 67.4 km/s/Mpc. The discrepancy is statistically significant (~5σ) and has not been resolved.

Either there is a subtle systematic error in one or both measurements, or there is new physics beyond the standard cosmological model. This is an active frontier of research.

13.13   Summary

Chapter Summary
  1. The darkness of the night sky (Olbers' paradox) tells us the universe has a finite age.
  2. Hubble's law (v = H0d) reveals that the universe is expanding.
  3. The Big Bang theory is supported by three pillars: expansion, the CMB, and light element abundances.
  4. The CMB is relic radiation from 380,000 years after the Big Bang, at T = 2.725 K.
  5. Dark matter (~27% of the universe) is detected via rotation curves, gravitational lensing, and the CMB.
  6. Dark energy (~68%) drives the accelerating expansion of the universe.
  7. The Friedmann equation governs cosmic evolution; inflation solves the horizon, flatness, and monopole problems.
  8. The most likely fate is the Big Freeze — eternal expansion and heat death.
Practice Problems

Exercises for Chapter 13

  1. A galaxy has a measured redshift of z = 0.05. Using Hubble's law with H0 = 70 km/s/Mpc, estimate its distance in Mpc and its recession velocity in km/s.
  2. The CMB has a temperature of 2.725 K today. At redshift z = 1100 (the epoch of recombination), the temperature was T = T0(1 + z). Calculate the temperature at recombination. Is this consistent with the ionisation temperature of hydrogen (~3000 K)?
  3. Using the Friedmann equation and the critical density formula, compute ρcrit for H0 = 70 km/s/Mpc. Express your answer in kg/m3 and in units of hydrogen atoms per cubic metre.
  4. The Milky Way's rotation curve is flat at about 220 km/s beyond a radius of 8 kpc. If the visible mass within 8 kpc is 6 × 1010 solar masses, estimate the total mass within 50 kpc. What fraction must be dark matter?
  5. If the equation of state parameter for dark energy were w = −1.5 (phantom energy), describe qualitatively how the future of the universe would differ from the w = −1 case. Would the Big Rip occur sooner or later for more negative w?

Cosmology is the grandest detective story there is. The clues are photons that have been travelling for billions of years, faint ripples in a microwave bath, and the curious motions of distant galaxies. We have learned an astonishing amount — but the deepest questions remain open. What is dark matter? What is dark energy? What happened before inflation? What is the ultimate fate of it all? These are not just questions for physicists. They are questions for anyone who has ever looked up at the sky and wondered.
