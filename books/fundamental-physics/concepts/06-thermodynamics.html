Chapter 06
Heat, work, and entropy

Thermodynamics

If I had to reduce all of physics to one sentence, it would be: hot things cool down. Everything else follows from that. Think about it. A cup of chai left on your desk in July — it doesn’t stay hot. The heat leaks out, the tea reaches room temperature, and the universe has become just a tiny bit more disordered. That simple, everyday observation contains the seeds of all of thermodynamics: energy flows, temperature equalizes, and disorder always wins in the end. In this chapter, we’re going to chase that idea all the way from your tea cup to the stars.

“Thermodynamics is the only physical theory of universal content which I am convinced will never be overthrown.” — Albert Einstein

6.1   Temperature and the Zeroth Law

Before we can talk about heat or energy or entropy, we need to get one thing absolutely straight: what is temperature? You might say, “Oh, it’s what the thermometer reads.” But that’s circular — it tells us how we measure it, not what it is.

Here’s the real story. Take three objects: A, B, and C. If A is in thermal equilibrium with C (meaning no heat flows between them when they touch), and B is also in thermal equilibrium with C, then — and this is the punchline — A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. No heat will flow between A and B either.

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This is what makes the concept of temperature meaningful — it is the property that is the same for all bodies in mutual thermal equilibrium.

Why “zeroth”? Because the first and second laws were already named when physicists realized this one was even more fundamental. It had to go before everything else, so they called it the zeroth law. A bit embarrassing, but that’s how science works sometimes.

Temperature scales follow from this. The Kelvin scale sets its zero at absolute zero — the point where molecular motion reaches its quantum-mechanical minimum. We have:

T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15

Now, when I say “temperature measures the average kinetic energy of molecules,” I’m being slightly sloppy. For an ideal gas it’s precise: &frac32; kBT per molecule. For solids and complex systems, it’s more subtle. But the intuition is golden — hotter means faster jiggling.

6.2   Heat and Internal Energy

Let’s get our vocabulary clean. There are two ideas that students constantly confuse, and you must not:

Internal energy (U) is the total energy stored inside a system — the kinetic energy of all those jiggling atoms plus the potential energy from their mutual interactions. It’s a state function: it depends only on the current state of the system (temperature, pressure, volume), not on how it got there.

Heat (Q) is energy in transit due to a temperature difference. It’s not something a body “has” — it’s something that flows. You never say “the body contains heat” — you say “heat was transferred to the body.”

Common Mistake Heat is not a property of a system. A hot object has high internal energy and high temperature, but it does not “contain heat.” Heat is defined only when energy crosses a boundary due to a temperature difference. Think of it like “work” — a lifted weight doesn’t “contain work.”

Work (W) is the other way energy crosses a boundary — through macroscopic, organized force acting over distance. A piston pushing against a gas does work on the gas. The gas pushing the piston outward does work on the surroundings.

For a gas expanding against a piston:

W = ∫ P   dV

This integral is the area under the curve on a P–V diagram — and that’s why PV diagrams are so powerful. We’ll exploit this shortly.

6.3   The First Law of Thermodynamics

Here is arguably the most important equation in all of thermal physics:

ΔU = Q − W
First Law The change in internal energy of a system equals the heat added to it minus the work done by it. This is conservation of energy applied to thermal systems. Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred or converted.

Let’s parse the signs carefully. We use the convention (common in physics):

  • Q > 0 means heat flows into the system
  • W > 0 means work is done by the system (expansion)

Some chemistry and engineering textbooks use the opposite sign convention for work: ΔU = Q + W, where W is work done on the system. Both are correct — just be consistent. Always check which convention your exam uses!

This is really just the conservation of energy wearing a thermal hat. If you add 500 J of heat to a gas and it does 200 J of work pushing a piston, the internal energy goes up by 300 J. No energy is lost; no energy is created. It is a perfect accounting system.

Specific Heat Capacity

When heat flows into a substance and no work is done, all the heat goes into raising the internal energy and hence the temperature:

Q = m c   ΔT

where c is the specific heat capacity — how much energy per kilogram is needed to raise the temperature by one kelvin. Water has a famously high value: c = 4186 J/(kg·K). That’s why it takes forever for your chai to boil and forever for the ocean to warm up.

6.4   The Ideal Gas Law

Now here’s a beautiful thing. Take a gas — any gas, as long as it’s not too dense and not too cold — and you find that pressure, volume, and temperature are linked by one elegant equation:

PV = nRT
Variables P = pressure (Pa), V = volume (m³), n = number of moles, R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) (universal gas constant), T = absolute temperature (K). Equivalently, PV = NkBT where N is the number of molecules and kB = 1.381 × 10−23 J/K is Boltzmann’s constant.

This law unifies three older laws you might have learned separately:

  • Boyle’s Law (constant T): PV = const
  • Charles’s Law (constant P): V/T = const
  • Gay-Lussac’s Law (constant V): P/T = const

For an ideal gas, the internal energy depends only on temperature:

U = f2 nRT

where f is the number of degrees of freedom (3 for monatomic, 5 for diatomic at moderate temperatures). This is the equipartition theorem — each degree of freedom gets ½kBT of energy on average.

6.5   Thermodynamic Processes

When a gas changes state, the path it takes through the space of pressure, volume, and temperature matters enormously. There are four canonical processes, and you need to know each one cold (or hot, as the case may be):

Process What’s constant Work done by gas ΔU Q
Isothermal Temperature T nRT ln(V2/V1) 0 = W
Adiabatic No heat transfer (Q=0) −ΔU nCvΔT 0
Isobaric Pressure P PΔV nCvΔT nCpΔT
Isochoric Volume V 0 nCvΔT = ΔU

Notice the adiabatic process: no heat in, no heat out, yet the temperature changes! The gas does work at the expense of its own internal energy (or vice versa). An adiabat is steeper than an isotherm on a PV diagram because both pressure and temperature change simultaneously.

Key relations for an adiabatic process

PVγ = const
TVγ−1 = const

where γ = Cp/Cv is the heat capacity ratio (5/3 for monatomic, 7/5 for diatomic gases).

6.7   The Second Law of Thermodynamics

The first law tells you energy is conserved. Great. But it doesn’t tell you which way things go. If I showed you a video of a hot cup of chai cooling down, you’d say “normal.” If I showed you the same video reversed — a lukewarm cup spontaneously getting hotter while the surroundings cool — you’d say “impossible!” Yet both scenarios conserve energy perfectly. So what’s the extra rule that nature follows?

That’s the second law. It comes in two classic formulations:

Clausius Statement Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body without external work being done.
Kelvin–Planck Statement No cyclic process can convert heat entirely into work with no other effect. In other words, a perfect heat engine — one that wastes nothing — is impossible.

These two statements look different, but they are logically equivalent. If you could violate one, you could build a machine that violates the other. The proofs are beautiful exercises in logic — try them!

Now, you might ask: “Why? Why can’t heat flow from cold to hot?” And the deepest answer is statistical. There are overwhelmingly more microscopic arrangements (microstates) consistent with heat flowing from hot to cold than the reverse. It’s not that the reverse is impossible in principle — it’s just so fantastically improbable that you’d have to wait longer than the age of the universe to see it happen. The second law is a statement about probability, elevated to the status of certainty by sheer numbers.

6.8   The Carnot Cycle

Sadi Carnot, in 1824, asked a magnificent question: what is the best a heat engine can possibly do? He imagined a perfect, reversible engine — no friction, no wasted heat, every process executed infinitely slowly so the system is always in equilibrium. The result is the Carnot cycle.

The cycle has four steps:

  1. Isothermal expansion at temperature TH: The gas absorbs heat QH from the hot reservoir and expands, doing work.
  2. Adiabatic expansion: The gas continues expanding with no heat exchange. Its temperature drops from TH to TC.
  3. Isothermal compression at temperature TC: The gas is compressed, releasing heat QC to the cold reservoir.
  4. Adiabatic compression: The gas is compressed further with no heat exchange. Temperature rises from TC back to TH.

The efficiency of a Carnot engine is:

ηCarnot = 1 − TCTH
Carnot’s Theorem No heat engine operating between two given temperatures can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between those same temperatures. This sets an absolute upper limit on the efficiency of any heat engine.

If your hot reservoir is at 600 K and your cold reservoir (the atmosphere) is at 300 K, the best you can ever do is 50% efficiency. A real engine will always do worse, because real processes have friction, turbulence, and heat leaks. This is why car engines convert only about 25–30% of fuel energy into motion, and why power plants obsess over raising TH.

6.9   Entropy

We’ve been circling around this idea, and now it’s time to face it directly. Entropy is the quantity that makes the second law precise. Clausius defined it as:

dS = δQrevT

For a reversible process, the change in entropy is the heat transferred divided by the temperature at which it’s transferred. For an irreversible process in an isolated system:

ΔS ≥ 0

Entropy never decreases in an isolated system. This is the second law restated in mathematical form.

The Statistical View: Boltzmann’s Insight

But what is entropy, really? Boltzmann gave us the deepest answer. Consider a macroscopic state of a system — say, “gas evenly fills the box at temperature T.” How many microscopic arrangements of individual molecules are consistent with that macrostate? Call that number Ω — the number of microstates. Then:

S = kB ln Ω
Boltzmann’s Entropy Formula Entropy is proportional to the logarithm of the number of microstates. More microstates means higher entropy. This equation is so important it is engraved on Boltzmann’s tombstone in Vienna.

Here is the reason hot things cool down. When a hot object touches a cold one, the combined system has access to enormously more microstates in the state where energy is shared than in the original state where one is hot and one is cold. The system doesn’t “want” to reach equilibrium — it’s simply overwhelmingly more likely to end up there. Entropy increase is the universe rolling downhill on a probability landscape.

Let’s make this visual. Imagine particles initially confined to one half of a box. When the partition is removed, they spread out. The number of microstates increases explosively.

The beauty of entropy is that it turns the vague notion of “disorder” into a precise, calculable quantity. When you calculate the entropy change of the universe (system + surroundings) and find it’s positive, you know the process can happen spontaneously. If it’s zero, the process is reversible — a perfect idealization. If it’s negative, you’ve made an error, or you’ve forgotten to include the surroundings.

6.10   The Third Law of Thermodynamics

We close with the third law, which speaks about the unattainable frontier:

Third Law (Nernst’s Theorem) As the temperature of a system approaches absolute zero, the entropy approaches a minimum value (zero for a perfect crystal). It is impossible to reach absolute zero in a finite number of steps.

At absolute zero, a perfect crystal has exactly one microstate — every atom in its unique position, no disorder at all. So S = kB ln(1) = 0. This gives us a natural zero point for entropy, unlike energy, which can only be defined up to an arbitrary constant.

Why can’t you reach absolute zero? Each successive cooling step gives diminishing returns. You can get asymptotically close — laboratories have reached nanokelvin temperatures — but never quite zero. It’s like trying to reach the speed of light: the closer you get, the harder each increment becomes.

Investigation

The Arrow of Time

Here is a deep puzzle for you. Newton’s laws are time-reversible — if you reverse every particle’s velocity, the system retraces its path perfectly. Yet we never see broken eggs reassemble or cold coffee reheat. The second law gives us an arrow of time: entropy increases toward the future. But where does this asymmetry come from, if the underlying laws are symmetric?

The answer lies in initial conditions. The universe began in an extraordinarily low-entropy state (the Big Bang). Given that starting point, the overwhelming probability is for entropy to increase going forward. The arrow of time is not in the laws — it’s in the boundary conditions. Think about that the next time your chai cools down.

For Your Exams

The BSc exam loves numerical problems on Carnot efficiency, entropy change calculations, and first-law bookkeeping. Remember:

  • Always convert to Kelvin for Carnot efficiency.
  • For adiabatic processes, use PVγ = const and TVγ−1 = const.
  • Entropy is a state function: ΔS depends only on initial and final states, not on the path (even though you might calculate it along a reversible path).
  • Free expansion does no work (W=0) and has no heat transfer (Q=0), but ΔS > 0 because it’s irreversible. Calculate ΔS using a reversible path between the same states.

We began with a cup of cooling chai, and we’ve ended up with the arrow of time and the fate of the universe. That’s thermodynamics for you. It starts with the obvious and arrives at the profound.
