Chapter 09
When time bends

Special Relativity

Imagine you are riding on a beam of light. What do you see? Einstein asked this at age 16, and the answer broke physics. If you are moving at the speed of light alongside an electromagnetic wave, that wave should appear frozen — a static ripple in the fields of electricity and magnetism, hanging motionless in space. But Maxwell’s equations say no such thing can exist. There is no solution to Maxwell’s equations that gives you a stationary electromagnetic wave. Something had to give, and what gave was our most basic intuition about space and time.

For ten years that thought experiment gnawed at Einstein, and in 1905 — his annus mirabilis — he published a paper that threw out two centuries of Newtonian assumptions about absolute time and absolute space. The paper was titled “On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies,” and it didn’t require any new mathematics beyond algebra. What it required was courage: the courage to take the laws of physics at face value, follow them to their logical conclusions, and accept what they told you — even if it meant that time itself runs differently for different observers.

Here is the thing about special relativity that people get wrong: they think it is some exotic, impractical theory. But every GPS satellite orbiting above your head right now has to correct for relativistic time dilation. Without those corrections, your phone’s location would drift by about 10 km per day. Relativity is not abstract — it is engineering.

9.1   Einstein’s Two Postulates

The entire theory of special relativity rests on exactly two postulates. That is the beauty of it — from just two simple statements, an entire revolution follows.

Postulate 1 — The Principle of Relativity The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames. No experiment performed inside a sealed laboratory moving at constant velocity can tell you whether you are “really” moving or at rest.
Postulate 2 — The Constancy of the Speed of Light The speed of light in vacuum, c = 3 × 108 m/s, is the same for all inertial observers, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer.

Now, Postulate 1 is not new — Galileo and Newton would agree with it wholeheartedly. If you are in a train moving at constant speed with the curtains drawn, you cannot tell from the physics inside the train whether you are moving or standing still. That is old news. The revolution is Postulate 2.

Think about how strange this is. If I am standing on a railway platform and I throw a ball at 20 m/s, and you are in a train going 30 m/s in the same direction, you see the ball moving at only 20 − 30 = −10 m/s, i.e., 10 m/s backward. Velocities add and subtract. But light does not play this game. If I shine a torch, I measure the light going at c. You, in your speeding train, also measure it going at c — not c − 30 m/s. The speed of light is stubbornly, unshakeably constant.

The Michelson–Morley experiment of 1887 tried to detect Earth’s motion through the supposed “luminiferous aether” — the medium everyone assumed light must travel through. They found nothing. Zero. The speed of light was the same in every direction, regardless of Earth’s motion. It was one of the most famous “failed” experiments in history, and one of the most important.

If the speed of light is the same for everyone, then something else must change to make the arithmetic work out. What changes is space and time themselves. And that is the story of this chapter.

9.2   Galilean vs. Lorentz Transformations

The Galilean Way (Newton’s World)

In Newtonian mechanics, if frame S′ moves at velocity v along the x-axis relative to frame S, the coordinates transform as:

x′ = x − vt    y′ = y    z′ = z    t′ = t

Notice that last equation: t′ = t. Time is universal, absolute, ticking at the same rate for everyone. Newton took this as self-evident. It is wrong.

The Lorentz Way (Einstein’s World)

When we demand that the speed of light be invariant, the transformation equations become:

x′ = γ(x − vt)    t′ = γ (t − vxc2)

where the Lorentz factor γ is:

γ = 1√(1 − v2/c2)

At low speeds (v ≪ c), γ ≈ 1, and the Lorentz transformations reduce to the Galilean ones. That is essential — Newtonian physics works perfectly well for cricket balls and ISRO rockets. It is only when v becomes a significant fraction of c that the strange effects become noticeable.

Why “Lorentz”? Hendrik Lorentz and George FitzGerald actually discovered these transformation equations before Einstein, but they thought the transformations described a physical contraction caused by motion through the aether. Einstein’s genius was to show that the transformations are not about a mechanical effect — they reveal the geometry of spacetime itself.

9.3   Time Dilation

Here is where things get truly strange. Imagine you have a clock — a very simple clock. It consists of two mirrors, one above the other, with a pulse of light bouncing between them. Every time the pulse hits a mirror, the clock “ticks.” This is called a light clock, and it is the most honest clock you can build, because its ticking rate depends only on the speed of light and the distance between the mirrors.

Now, suppose this light clock is at rest in your frame. The light bounces straight up and down, and the time for one tick is:

Δt0 = 2Dc

where D is the distance between the mirrors. Simple enough.

But now watch that same clock from a frame where it is moving sideways at velocity v. The light still has to go from one mirror to the other, but now the mirrors have moved sideways while the light is in transit. The light traces a diagonal path — a longer path. And since the speed of light is c in this frame too (Postulate 2!), the light takes longer to make the trip. The clock ticks slower.

A bit of Pythagoras gives us the exact result:

Δt = γ Δt0 = Δt0√(1 − v2/c2)
Time Dilation A moving clock runs slow. The time interval Δt measured by an observer watching a moving clock is always longer than the proper time Δt0 measured in the clock’s rest frame. The factor is γ ≥ 1.

This is not an illusion. It is not that the clock “appears” to run slow due to signal delay or some optical trick. The clock genuinely ticks fewer times. Time itself passes differently in different frames.

Watch the tick counters. After a while, the rest clock will have accumulated noticeably more ticks. This is not a trick of the animation — this is what actually happens. Muons produced by cosmic rays in the upper atmosphere survive long enough to reach the ground precisely because their internal “clocks” (their decay timers) run slow from our perspective. Without time dilation, they would decay long before reaching sea level.

9.4   Length Contraction

If time dilates, then lengths must contract — the two effects are two sides of the same coin. Consider measuring the length of a moving train. You need to note where the front and back of the train are at the same instant. But “the same instant” depends on your reference frame (as we’ll see in the next section). The upshot is:

L = L0γ = L0 √(1 − v2/c2)
Length Contraction (Lorentz–FitzGerald Contraction) The length of an object measured by an observer relative to whom the object is moving is shorter than the proper length L0 measured in the object’s rest frame. The contraction occurs only along the direction of motion.

A few critical points:

  • The contraction is real, not an optical illusion. But it is frame-dependent — each observer measures the other’s ruler as shorter.
  • Contraction is only along the direction of motion. A spaceship moving at 0.9c looks pancaked along its direction of travel, but its height and width are unchanged.
  • At v = 0.866c, γ = 2, so the length is halved. At v = 0.99c, γ ≈ 7.09, so a 100 m train appears only about 14.1 m long.

9.5   Relativity of Simultaneity

This is, I think, the most mind-bending consequence of all — and the one most students struggle with until it clicks.

Imagine a train car with a light source in the exact centre. The light fires a flash, and the light travels outward in both directions at speed c. For a passenger on the train, the light reaches the front wall and the back wall at the same time — the events are simultaneous.

But an observer on the platform sees the train moving forward. The back wall is moving toward the light flash, while the front wall is moving away from it. Since both pulses travel at c (Postulate 2!), the pulse hits the back wall first. The two events that were simultaneous in the train frame are not simultaneous in the platform frame.

No Universal “Now” Events that are simultaneous in one inertial frame are in general not simultaneous in another. There is no absolute, frame-independent meaning of “at the same time” for events separated in space. This is not a matter of signal delay — it is fundamental.

Mathematically, the Lorentz transformation for time makes this explicit. If two events occur at the same time (t1 = t2) but at different positions (x1 ≠ x2) in frame S, then in frame S′:

Δt′ = γ v Δxc2 ≠ 0

The time difference depends on the spatial separation Δx and the relative velocity v. Simultaneity is relative.

9.6   Spacetime Diagrams (Minkowski Diagrams)

In 1908, Hermann Minkowski — who had been one of Einstein’s mathematics professors in Zurich — gave a famous lecture. He declared: “Henceforth, space by itself, and time by itself, are doomed to fade away into mere shadows, and only a kind of union of the two will preserve an independent reality.” He was talking about spacetime.

A Minkowski diagram (or spacetime diagram) is the essential visual tool for thinking about relativity. Here is how it works:

  • The horizontal axis represents space (usually one spatial dimension, x).
  • The vertical axis represents time (usually ct, so both axes have units of length).
  • A point in the diagram represents an event — something that happens at a specific place and time.
  • The trajectory of a particle through spacetime is called its worldline.
  • Light rays travel at 45° (since x = ct). The set of all light paths from an event forms the light cone.

The extraordinary thing about a Minkowski diagram is how a Lorentz boost looks: the x′ and ct′ axes tilt toward the light cone. As v → c, both axes approach 45°. Try it yourself:

A and B now have different ct′ values. Simultaneity is frame-dependent. The light cone at 45° never changes — all observers agree on it.

9.7   The Spacetime Interval

In Euclidean geometry, the distance between two points is invariant under rotations: Δs2 = Δx2 + Δy2. Different observers who use rotated coordinate systems will disagree on Δx and Δy individually, but they all agree on Δs2.

In Minkowski spacetime, there is an analogous invariant — the spacetime interval:

Δs2 = (cΔt)2 − Δx2 − Δy2 − Δz2

This quantity is the same in all inertial frames. It is the fundamental invariant of special relativity, playing the role that distance plays in Euclidean geometry. Notice the crucial minus sign — time and space enter with opposite signs. This is what makes spacetime geometry hyperbolic rather than Euclidean.

Δs2TypeMeaning
> 0TimelikeEvents can be connected by a particle traveling slower than light. A frame exists where both events occur at the same place.
= 0Lightlike (null)Events connected by a light signal. On the light cone.
< 0SpacelikeEvents too far apart to be connected by any signal. A frame exists where both events occur at the same time.

The spacetime interval is to special relativity what the Pythagorean theorem is to Euclidean geometry. If you understand the interval, you understand the geometry. And the geometry is the physics.

9.8   Relativistic Momentum & Energy

Newton defined momentum as p = mv. This works beautifully at low speeds. But if you try to use it at relativistic speeds, momentum is no longer conserved in collisions. Something needs to be fixed, and what needs fixing is the definition of momentum:

p = γmv = mv√(1 − v2/c2)

where m is the rest mass (invariant mass) of the particle. As v → c, γ → ∞, and the momentum diverges. You would need infinite momentum — and therefore infinite energy — to accelerate a massive particle to the speed of light. This is why nothing with mass can reach c.

Relativistic Energy

The total energy of a particle in special relativity is:

E = γmc2

This includes both the kinetic energy and the rest energy. When the particle is at rest (v = 0, γ = 1), the total energy is:

E0 = mc2

This is it. The most famous equation in all of physics. Mass is a form of energy. A kilogram of matter, if fully converted to energy, would release 9 × 1016 joules — roughly the energy of a 21-megaton nuclear weapon. The Sun converts about 4 million tonnes of mass into energy every second through nuclear fusion, and it has been doing so for 4.6 billion years.

The Energy–Momentum Relation The total energy, momentum, and rest mass are related by the invariant:
E2 = (pc)2 + (mc2)2
This is the “full” version of E = mc2. For a photon (m = 0): E = pc. For a particle at rest (p = 0): E = mc2.

The relativistic kinetic energy is the total energy minus the rest energy:

K = (γ − 1)mc2

At low speeds, using the binomial approximation γ ≈ 1 + v2/(2c2), this reduces to the familiar ½mv2. As it must.

Worked Example

Proton at the LHC

At CERN’s Large Hadron Collider, protons are accelerated to v = 0.999999991c. The rest mass of a proton is mp = 938.3 MeV/c2.

γ = 1/√(1 − 0.9999999912) ≈ 7461

Total energy: E = γmpc2 = 7461 × 938.3 MeV ≈ 7000 GeV = 7 TeV

The proton’s kinetic energy is about 7460 times its rest energy. In the lab frame, the proton’s “effective mass” is 7461 times larger than its rest mass. This is why the LHC needs superconducting magnets producing 8.3 T fields just to bend these protons in a circle.

9.9   The Twin Paradox

Now here is a puzzle that has confused people for over a century — and I want to resolve it clearly, because the resolution is beautiful.

Suppose there are twins, Asha and Bharat. Asha stays on Earth. Bharat boards a spaceship that travels to a star 10 light-years away at v = 0.9c, then turns around and comes back at the same speed.

From Asha’s perspective: the round trip takes 10/0.9 × 2 ≈ 22.2 years. But Bharat’s clock runs slow by a factor of γ = 1/√(1−0.81) = 1/√0.19 ≈ 2.29. So Bharat ages only about 22.2/2.29 ≈ 9.7 years.

When they reunite, Asha is 22.2 years older, but Bharat is only 9.7 years older. Bharat is genuinely younger. This is not a paradox of perception — it is a real, measurable age difference.

But Wait — Where Is the Paradox? Here is the supposed paradox: from Bharat’s point of view, it is Asha who is moving (away and then back). So shouldn’t Bharat see Asha’s clock running slow? Shouldn’t each twin think the other is younger? The situation seems symmetric.

The resolution: the situation is not symmetric. Bharat accelerates — he has to turn around. During that turnaround, he is in a non-inertial frame. Asha stays in a single inertial frame the entire time. The asymmetry breaks the apparent paradox.

On the spacetime diagram, Asha’s worldline is a straight vertical line. Bharat’s worldline is a bent line — two straight segments with a kink at the turnaround. The proper time along a straight worldline through spacetime is always longer than the proper time along a bent worldline between the same two events. (This is the spacetime analogue of “a straight line is the shortest distance between two points” — but with the minus sign in the metric, the straight worldline gives the longest proper time.)

This is not just theory. In 1971, Hafele and Keating flew atomic clocks around the world on commercial airliners and compared them with clocks that stayed on the ground. The flying clocks lost time, exactly as relativity predicted — about 59 nanoseconds. The universe plays no favourites: time dilation is real, and it doesn’t care whether you believe in it.

9.10   The Relativistic Doppler Effect

You know the classical Doppler effect from Chapter 4: a siren sounds higher-pitched as the ambulance approaches and lower-pitched as it recedes. Light does the same thing, but with a relativistic twist.

For a source moving directly toward or away from the observer, the relativistic Doppler formula is:

fobs = fsrc √ 1 + β1 − β   (approaching)
fobs = fsrc √ 1 − β1 + β   (receding)

where β = v/c. For an approaching source, the frequency is blueshifted (increased). For a receding source, it is redshifted (decreased).

Transverse Doppler Effect Unlike the classical Doppler effect, the relativistic version predicts a frequency shift even when the source moves perpendicular to the line of sight:
fobs = fsrcγ
This is a purely relativistic effect — a consequence of time dilation alone. It has no classical analogue and was confirmed experimentally by Ives and Stilwell in 1938.

The cosmological redshift of distant galaxies — Hubble’s great discovery — is intimately related to this effect (though the full story involves general relativity and the expansion of space). When astronomers say a galaxy has redshift z = 2, they mean the light was stretched by a factor of (1 + z) = 3 during its journey to us.

9.11   Four-Vectors and Relativistic Invariance

The language of special relativity is the language of four-vectors. Just as ordinary vectors have three spatial components, four-vectors have four components — one for time and three for space. The beauty is that the “length” of a four-vector (computed using the Minkowski metric) is the same in all frames.

The four-position:

xμ = (ct, x, y, z)

The four-momentum:

pμ = (E/c, px, py, pz)

The invariant “length” of the four-momentum gives us the energy–momentum relation we already saw:

pμpμ = E2c2 − p2 = m2c2

Four-vectors are not just mathematical elegance — they are a computational powerhouse. In particle physics at institutions like TIFR and the IITs, almost every collision calculation is done using four-momentum conservation. It is faster, cleaner, and less error-prone than working with three-vectors and energies separately.

9.12   Bringing It All Together

Let us step back and see the whole picture. Special relativity follows from two postulates — the invariance of the laws of physics and the constancy of the speed of light. From these, we derive:

The Deep Lesson Special relativity is not about things being “relative” in some vague philosophical sense. It is about identifying what is invariant — the speed of light, the spacetime interval, the rest mass — and recognizing that quantities like length, time, and simultaneity, which we once thought were absolute, are actually frame-dependent projections of the deeper invariant reality of spacetime.

You know, people say Einstein was a genius, and of course he was. But what made him special was not some superhuman mathematical ability — Lorentz and Poincaré had the mathematics before him. What made Einstein special was his willingness to take the physics seriously. He said: if the speed of light is really the same for everyone, then let us follow that to its conclusion, no matter how strange. And the conclusion was that space and time are not what we thought they were. That takes a different kind of genius — the courage to trust the equations over your intuition.
