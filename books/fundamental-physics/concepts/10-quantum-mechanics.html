Chapter 10
The quantum world

Quantum Mechanics

I think I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics. But let me show you what it does — and what it does is stranger than anything you’ve ever imagined. In this chapter we leave behind the comfortable certainties of Newton and Maxwell, and step into a world where particles behave like waves, where you can never know everything about a system at once, and where the very act of looking at something changes what you see. This is not philosophy — this is physics, backed by the most precisely verified predictions in all of science. Nature is not classical, dammit, and if you want to understand her you’ve got to think quantum-mechanically.

10.1   The Crisis of Classical Physics

By the close of the nineteenth century, physics appeared nearly complete. Newton’s mechanics predicted planetary orbits. Maxwell’s equations unified electricity, magnetism, and light. Thermodynamics governed engines and entropy. Many physicists believed only a few “minor clouds” remained on the horizon. Those clouds turned out to be thunderstorms.

Blackbody Radiation and the Ultraviolet Catastrophe

Heat any object — a piece of iron, the filament of a bulb, the Sun itself — and it glows. A blackbody is an idealised object that absorbs all radiation falling on it and re-emits energy in a characteristic spectrum that depends only on its temperature, not on what it is made of. Classical physics, via the Rayleigh–Jeans law, predicted that the energy radiated should increase without bound as wavelength decreases — the so-called ultraviolet catastrophe. The formula predicted infinite total energy at short wavelengths, which is absurd: a warm oven would kill you with gamma rays.

The Ultraviolet Catastrophe The Rayleigh–Jeans law gives the spectral energy density as u(λ) = 8πkBT / λ4 This diverges as λ → 0, predicting infinite energy at short wavelengths. Experiment shows the spectrum peaks at a finite wavelength and drops to zero beyond. Classical physics had no way to explain this.

In 1900, Max Planck found a formula that fit the experimental blackbody curve perfectly — but only by introducing a radical assumption: energy is not emitted continuously, but in discrete packets he called quanta. Each quantum of radiation at frequency f carries energy:

E = h f

where h = 6.626 × 10−34 J·s is Planck’s constant. Planck himself called this “an act of desperation” — he did not fully believe that energy was truly quantised. But the genie was out of the bottle.

Planck’s Radiation Law The spectral energy density of a blackbody at temperature T: u(ν, T)  =  8πhν3c3  ·  1ehν/kBT − 1 This agrees with experiment at all wavelengths and reduces to the Rayleigh–Jeans law when hν ≪ kBT.

You see, Nature refused to be divided smoothly. She insisted on coming in lumps. And once you accept that — that the universe is fundamentally lumpy — everything else follows.

10.2   The Photoelectric Effect

In 1887, Heinrich Hertz noticed that ultraviolet light falling on a metal surface ejected electrons. Classical wave theory predicted that brighter light should eject faster electrons, and that any frequency should work if you wait long enough. Both predictions were wrong.

The experimental facts were stubborn:

  • Electrons are ejected only if the light frequency exceeds a threshold f0, regardless of intensity.
  • Above the threshold, the kinetic energy of ejected electrons depends on frequency, not intensity.
  • Brighter light ejects more electrons per second, but each has the same maximum kinetic energy.
  • Emission is essentially instantaneous — no time delay as classical theory would require for weak light to “build up” enough energy.

In 1905, Albert Einstein explained everything with a single bold hypothesis: light itself comes in quanta — particles we now call photons. Each photon carries energy E = hf. When a photon strikes a metal surface, it transfers all its energy to one electron. If this energy exceeds the minimum binding energy φ (the work function of the metal), the electron is liberated with kinetic energy:

Kmax = h f − φ
Einstein’s Photoelectric Equation Kmax = hf − φ
  • Kmax — maximum kinetic energy of ejected electron
  • h = 6.626 × 10−34 J·s — Planck’s constant
  • f — frequency of incident light
  • φ = hf0 — work function (minimum energy to free an electron)

If f < f0, no electrons are ejected, no matter how bright the light. This was deeply counter-intuitive: light, which Maxwell had shown to be a wave, was also behaving as a stream of particles. Einstein received the 1921 Nobel Prize for this work — not for relativity, but for the photoelectric effect.

Numerical Example

Photoelectric Effect Calculation

UV light of wavelength λ = 200 nm strikes a sodium surface with work function φ = 2.28 eV. Find the maximum kinetic energy of the ejected electrons.

Solution: First, find the photon energy: E = hf = hcλ = (6.626 × 10−34)(3 × 108)200 × 10−9 = 9.94 × 10−19 J = 6.21 eV Then: Kmax = 6.21 − 2.28 = 3.93 eV

The electron is ejected with considerable kinetic energy because the photon energy is well above the threshold.

Einstein’s explanation was beautiful in its simplicity. Light is made of little bullets of energy. Each bullet either has enough energy to knock an electron out, or it doesn’t. No amount of throwing pebbles will accomplish what a single well-aimed rock can do.

10.3   Wave–Particle Duality & de Broglie Waves

By the early 1920s, physics was in a curious state. Light — which had been decisively shown to be a wave through diffraction and interference — was now also shown to behave as a particle (photon). In 1924, a young French prince turned physicist, Louis de Broglie, asked a revolutionary question in his doctoral thesis: if waves can behave as particles, can particles behave as waves?

De Broglie proposed that every particle with momentum p has an associated wavelength:

λ = hp = hmv

This is the de Broglie wavelength. For a macroscopic object like a cricket ball (mass ~160 g at 40 m/s), λ ≈ 10−34 m — far too small to observe. But for an electron (mass 9.1 × 10−31 kg) accelerated through 100 V, the de Broglie wavelength is about 0.12 nm — comparable to the spacing between atoms in a crystal.

Experimental Confirmation In 1927, Clinton Davisson and Lester Germer at Bell Labs scattered electrons off a nickel crystal and observed a diffraction pattern — exactly as one would expect for waves with the de Broglie wavelength. This was direct proof that electrons have wave-like properties. G.P. Thomson independently confirmed this using thin metal foils.
Numerical Example

de Broglie Wavelength of an Electron

An electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of 150 V. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

Solution: The kinetic energy gained equals the work done by the electric field:
K = eV = (1.6 × 10−19)(150) = 2.4 × 10−17 J

The momentum: p = √(2mK) = √(2 × 9.1 × 10−31 × 2.4 × 10−17) = 6.61 × 10−24 kg·m/s

Therefore: λ = h/p = 6.626 × 10−34 / 6.61 × 10−24 = 1.0 × 10−10 m = 0.10 nm

This is comparable to X-ray wavelengths, which is why electron diffraction is used to probe crystal structures.

Every bullet is also a wave, and every wave is also a bullet. Nature doesn’t force you to choose — she uses both descriptions, depending on what question you ask. The electron is not “really” a particle, and it is not “really” a wave. It is something for which there is no word in any human language. We call it a quantum object.

10.4   The Double-Slit Experiment

If you want to see quantum mechanics at its most mysterious, there is no better place to look than the double-slit experiment. Feynman called it “a phenomenon which is impossible, absolutely impossible, to explain in any classical way, and which has in it the heart of quantum mechanics.”

Here is the setup: a source fires particles — photons, electrons, even whole molecules — one at a time towards a barrier with two narrow slits. Behind the barrier is a detector screen.

What classical physics predicts: If particles are little bullets, each one goes through one slit or the other. You should see two bright bands behind the slits, just like spraying paint through two holes in a stencil.

What actually happens: When you let thousands of particles accumulate on the screen, they form an interference pattern — alternating bright and dark bands. This is the hallmark of wave behaviour, the same pattern you see with water waves or light waves passing through two openings.

The mystery deepens: even when particles arrive one at a time, so there is never more than one particle in the apparatus, the interference pattern still builds up. Each individual particle seems to “know about” both slits. Each particle interferes with itself.

Now comes the truly shocking part: if you place a detector at the slits to determine which slit each particle went through — the interference pattern vanishes. You get the two-band classical pattern. The act of observation changes the outcome. This is not a failure of the equipment; it is a fundamental feature of nature.

Key Insight Complementarity: A quantum object exhibits wave behaviour (interference) or particle behaviour (which-path information), but never both simultaneously. The type of experiment you perform determines which aspect you see. This is Niels Bohr’s principle of complementarity.

The intensity pattern on the screen for a double slit is given by:

I(θ) = I0 cos2(π d sinθλ)

where d is the slit separation, λ the de Broglie wavelength, and θ the angle from centre. Bright fringes occur when d sinθ = nλ (constructive interference).

The double-slit experiment contains the only mystery of quantum mechanics. If you can come to terms with it, you can come to terms with anything. And the thing you must come to terms with is this: the electron goes through both slits. Not “we don’t know which slit” — it genuinely goes through both. Until you look.

10.5   The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

In classical physics, you can in principle measure the position and momentum of a particle to any desired precision simultaneously. Quantum mechanics says: no, you cannot. There is a fundamental limit, discovered by Werner Heisenberg in 1927.

Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ2

where ℏ = h/(2π) = 1.055 × 10−34 J·s is the reduced Planck constant, Δx is the uncertainty in position, and Δp is the uncertainty in momentum.

This is not a statement about the clumsiness of our instruments. It is a statement about nature. The universe does not possess simultaneously precise values of position and momentum for a quantum particle. The more precisely you pin down where something is, the less you can know about how fast it is going, and vice versa.

Energy-Time Uncertainty There is a parallel relation for energy and time: ΔE · Δt ≥ ℏ/2 A state that exists for a very short time Δt must have an energy uncertainty ΔE at least ℏ/(2Δt). This allows “virtual” particles to borrow energy from nothing — provided they pay it back quickly enough.
Numerical Example

Uncertainty in Electron Position

An electron’s speed is measured to be 5.0 × 106 m/s with an uncertainty of 1%. What is the minimum uncertainty in its position?

Solution:

Δp = m · Δv = (9.1 × 10−31)(5.0 × 104) = 4.55 × 10−26 kg·m/s

Δx ≥ ℏ/(2Δp) = (1.055 × 10−34)/(2 × 4.55 × 10−26) = 1.16 × 10−9 m ≈ 1.2 nm

This is about 10 atomic diameters — the electron cannot be localised more precisely than this when its speed is known to 1%.

The uncertainty principle is not about what you can know. It is about what exists. Before you measure an electron’s position, it does not have a position. It has a cloud of possibilities, and measurement forces one of them to become real. This is not a metaphor — this is the mathematics.

10.6   The Schrödinger Equation

If particles behave as waves, they must be described by a wave equation. In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger wrote down the equation that governs the behaviour of quantum systems — the most important equation in quantum mechanics. For a particle in a potential V(x), the time-dependent Schrödinger equation reads:

iℏ ∂Ψ∂t = −ℏ22m ∂2Ψ∂x2 + V(x)Ψ

Here Ψ(x,t) is the wavefunction — a complex-valued function whose squared magnitude gives the probability of finding the particle.

Wavefunctions and Probability Density

The wavefunction Ψ itself is not directly observable. What is observable is the probability density:

P(x) = |Ψ(x, t)|2

This tells you the probability per unit length of finding the particle at position x. The total probability of finding the particle somewhere must be 1:

∫−∞∞ |Ψ(x)|2 dx = 1

This is the normalisation condition. Only wavefunctions that satisfy this condition are physically meaningful.

Born’s Rule The wavefunction encodes all the information about a quantum system. Max Born’s interpretation: |Ψ|2 dx is the probability of finding the particle in the interval [x, x + dx]. The particle does not have a definite position until measured — the wavefunction gives only probabilities.

The Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

For stationary states (states with definite energy), we can write Ψ(x,t) = ψ(x)e−iEt/ℏ, and the Schrödinger equation reduces to:

−ℏ22m d2ψdx2 + V(x)ψ = Eψ

This is an eigenvalue equation: the allowed energies E are the eigenvalues, and the corresponding ψ(x) are the eigenfunctions. The act of solving this equation for a given potential is the central task of quantum mechanics.

The Particle in a Box

The simplest application: a particle confined to a one-dimensional box of length L with infinitely high walls (V = 0 inside, V = ∞ outside). The boundary conditions demand ψ(0) = ψ(L) = 0, leading to:

ψn(x) = √(2/L) · sin(nπx / L),   n = 1, 2, 3, …
En = n2 π2 ℏ22mL2 = n2 h28mL2
Quantisation Emerges Naturally Notice that n can only take positive integer values. The energy is quantised — not because we forced it to be, but because the boundary conditions demand it. The particle cannot have zero energy either: E1 > 0. This zero-point energy is a direct consequence of the uncertainty principle — confining a particle to a box means Δx ≤ L, so Δp ≠ 0, so the particle must be moving.

Schrödinger’s equation is to quantum mechanics what F = ma is to Newtonian mechanics: the law of motion. You give it the forces (the potential), and it gives you the future (the wavefunction). The difference is that it gives you the future only as a set of probabilities — and that is the deepest, most unsettling thing about quantum physics.

10.7   Quantum Tunnelling

Here is something classical physics says is absolutely impossible: a ball rolling towards a hill does not have enough energy to get over the top, so it rolls back. Always. End of story. But in quantum mechanics, the ball — or rather, the particle — has a non-zero probability of appearing on the other side of the hill. This is quantum tunnelling.

Consider a particle of energy E approaching a potential barrier of height V0 > E and width a. Classically, the particle is reflected. Quantum-mechanically, the wavefunction does not abruptly go to zero inside the barrier — instead, it decays exponentially:

ψ(x) ∼ e−κx,   where   κ = √(2m(V0 − E))ℏ

If the barrier is thin enough, the wavefunction is still non-zero on the far side, meaning there is a finite probability for the particle to tunnel through.

Tunnelling Transmission Coefficient For a rectangular barrier of height V0 and width a, the approximate transmission probability is: T ≈ e−2κa = exp(−2aℏ√(2m(V0 − E))) The transmission drops exponentially with barrier width and height.

Tunnelling is not a theoretical curiosity — it underpins real technology and natural phenomena:

  • Alpha decay: An alpha particle inside a nucleus is trapped by the nuclear potential. It tunnels through the Coulomb barrier to escape. Gamow’s theory (1928) successfully explains the enormous range of alpha-decay half-lives.
  • Tunnel diodes: Semiconductor devices that exploit tunnelling for ultra-fast switching.
  • Scanning Tunnelling Microscope (STM): A sharp tip is brought within ~1 nm of a surface. Electrons tunnel across the gap, and the tunnelling current is exquisitely sensitive to distance — allowing images of individual atoms.
  • Nuclear fusion in the Sun: Protons in the solar core do not have enough thermal energy to overcome their mutual Coulomb repulsion. Tunnelling is what makes the Sun shine.

Numerical Example

Tunnelling Through a Barrier

An electron with energy E = 5.0 eV encounters a rectangular barrier of height V0 = 10.0 eV and width a = 0.50 nm. Estimate the tunnelling probability.

Solution:

κ = √(2m(V0 − E))ℏ = √(2 × 9.1×10−31 × 5.0 × 1.6×10−19)1.055 × 10−34 = 1.145 × 1010 m−1

T ≈ e−2κa = e−2 × 1.145×1010 × 5×10−10 = e−11.45 ≈ 1.1 × 10−5

About 1 in 100,000 electrons tunnels through. While small, this is not zero — and with 1020 electrons per second striking the barrier in a typical device, the tunnelling current is very measurable.

Tunnelling is one of those things where quantum mechanics thumbs its nose at common sense. A particle has no business being on the other side of a wall it doesn’t have the energy to climb. But the wavefunction doesn’t care about your classical intuition — it leaks through. And the Sun, every star in the sky, owes its light to this impossibility.

10.8   The Hydrogen Atom

The hydrogen atom — one proton, one electron — is the simplest atom in nature and the testing ground for quantum mechanics. Solving the Schrödinger equation for an electron in the Coulomb potential of a proton, V(r) = −e2/(4πε0r), yields the allowed energy levels:

En = −13.6 eVn2,   n = 1, 2, 3, …

This is the Bohr formula — but now derived rigorously from the Schrödinger equation, not from ad hoc assumptions. The negative sign means the electron is bound; n = 1 is the ground state with E1 = −13.6 eV. As n → ∞, En → 0 and the electron is free (ionisation).

Spectral Series

When an electron transitions from level ni to level nf, a photon is emitted (or absorbed) with energy:

1λ = R∞ (1nf2 − 1ni2)

where R∞ = 1.097 × 107 m−1 is the Rydberg constant. The famous spectral series of hydrogen:

Numerical Example

Wavelength of the H-alpha Line

Calculate the wavelength of the first Balmer line (Hα), where an electron drops from n = 3 to n = 2.

Solution:

1λ = R∞ (14 − 19) = 1.097 × 107 × 536 = 1.524 × 106 m−1

λ = 1 / (1.524 × 106) = 656.3 nm  (red light)

This is the famous red line visible in hydrogen discharge tubes and in the spectra of nebulae across the universe.

The orbitals of hydrogen are not orbits. The electron is not circling the nucleus like a planet around the Sun. It is spread out in a cloud of probability, and the shape of that cloud is determined by the quantum numbers. These clouds are the foundations upon which all of chemistry is built.

10.10   Spin

In 1922, Stern and Gerlach sent a beam of silver atoms through a non-uniform magnetic field. Classical physics predicted a continuous spread of deflections. Instead, the beam split into exactly two discrete spots. This revealed a fourth quantum number that the Schrödinger equation, in its original form, had missed.

In 1925, Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit proposed that the electron possesses an intrinsic angular momentum called spin, with quantum number s = 1/2. This is not the electron physically spinning on an axis — the electron is a point particle and has no spatial extent to spin. Spin is a purely quantum-mechanical property with no classical analogue.

QuantitySymbolValues for Electron

With spin, the complete specification of an electron’s state in hydrogen requires four quantum numbers: (n, l, ml, ms). The maximum number of electrons in a shell with principal quantum number n is 2n2.

Pauli Exclusion Principle No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers (n, l, ml, ms). This is Wolfgang Pauli’s exclusion principle (1925). It explains the structure of the periodic table, the rigidity of matter, and why atoms have the sizes they do. Without it, all electrons would collapse into the 1s orbital and chemistry would not exist.
Spin is Ubiquitous All fundamental particles have spin. Particles with half-integer spin (1/2, 3/2, …) are called fermions (electrons, protons, neutrons, quarks). They obey the Pauli exclusion principle. Particles with integer spin (0, 1, 2, …) are called bosons (photons, gluons, the Higgs). They do not obey the exclusion principle — in fact, they prefer to be in the same state. This distinction underlies the difference between matter and force.

Spin is, I think, one of the most beautiful things in physics. It tells you that the universe has a kind of internal geometry that goes deeper than space and time. When Pauli was asked “What does spin mean?”, he answered: “It is a classically non-describable two-valuedness.” Which is Pauli’s way of saying: there is no picture. Just the mathematics. And the mathematics works.

We have peeled back just one layer of the quantum onion. In the next chapter, we will use these ideas to understand atoms, nuclei, and the zoo of subatomic particles. But never forget: quantum mechanics is not a theory about small things. It is the theory of everything. The classical world you see around you is the special case — the shadow on the wall of Plato’s cave. The quantum world is the fire.
