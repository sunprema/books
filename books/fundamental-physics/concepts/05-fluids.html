Chapter 05
The physics of flow

Fluids

Why does an airplane fly? Most people get the answer wrong. Let me tell you what's really going on. They'll say something about the air going faster over the curved top of the wing and slower underneath, and then they'll wave their hands and invoke Bernoulli's principle, and that's supposed to be the end of it. But that explanation has a gaping hole — it never tells you why the air goes faster on top. The real story involves Newton's third law, the deflection of air downward, and yes, Bernoulli too, but in a way that's far more interesting than the textbook hand-wave. To understand it properly, we need to understand fluids — what they are, how they push, how they flow. And that is what this chapter is about.

A fluid is anything that flows. That includes liquids and gases. Water in a river, air around a cricket ball, mercury in a barometer, the molten iron in Earth's core — all fluids. The distinction between solid and fluid isn't about hardness; it's about whether the material can resist a shear stress indefinitely. Solids can. Fluids cannot. Apply a sideways force to a fluid and it just keeps deforming, forever. That's the defining property.

1 · Pressure and Density

Let's start with two quantities that characterise a fluid at every point in space: density and pressure.

Density is mass per unit volume — how much stuff is packed into a given space:

ρ = mV

Water has a density of about 1000 kg/m³. Mercury is 13,600 kg/m³. Air at sea level is roughly 1.2 kg/m³. These numbers matter enormously — they determine how heavy things are, how much they resist being pushed, and whether objects float or sink.

Pressure is force per unit area, acting perpendicular to any surface within or bounding the fluid:

P = FA

The SI unit is the pascal (Pa) — one newton per square metre. Atmospheric pressure is about 1.013 × 105 Pa, which we also call 1 atm.

Here is something that confuses people: pressure in a fluid at rest acts equally in all directions at any given point. It doesn't have a preferred direction. If it did, the fluid would start moving. Since it's at rest, the pressure must be isotropic. That's not a law you impose — it's a consequence of equilibrium.

Pressure variation with depth

Consider a thin horizontal slab of fluid at depth h below the surface, with area A and thickness dh. The slab is in equilibrium: the upward pressure force on its bottom face must balance the downward pressure on its top face plus the weight of the slab. This gives us:

P = P0 + ρgh

where P0 is the pressure at the surface (usually atmospheric), ρ is the fluid density, g is gravitational acceleration, and h is depth. This is the hydrostatic pressure equation. It tells you that pressure increases linearly with depth for an incompressible fluid of uniform density.

Note At the bottom of a 10-metre-deep swimming pool, the pressure is about 2 atm — one atmosphere from the air above, and one more from the water column. Every 10 metres of water depth adds roughly 1 atm. This is why deep-sea creatures are built to withstand pressures of hundreds of atmospheres.

2 · Pascal's Law and Hydraulic Systems

In 1653, Blaise Pascal articulated a principle that is breathtaking in its simplicity:

Key Principle — Pascal's Law A change in pressure applied to an enclosed, incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container.

Think about what this means. If you push on a fluid in a sealed container, that extra pressure appears everywhere inside the fluid, instantly. The fluid doesn't absorb it, doesn't attenuate it — it passes it along perfectly. Why? Because the fluid is incompressible (or nearly so). If you squeeze one part, the whole thing must respond.

Pascal's law is the reason a hydraulic brake system works. When you press the brake pedal in your car, you push a piston into a small cylinder of brake fluid. That pressure increase is transmitted through the brake lines to pistons at all four wheels. The fluid carries your foot's intention to the brakes faithfully, without any gears or cables.

The hydraulic multiplier

Now here is the truly clever bit. Suppose we have two cylinders connected by a tube, filled with fluid. One cylinder has a small piston of area A1, and the other has a large piston of area A2. By Pascal's law, the pressure is the same everywhere:

F1A1 = F2A2   ⇒   F2 = F1 · A2A1

If the large piston has ten times the area of the small one, the output force is ten times the input force. You've built a force multiplier! But there's no free lunch — conservation of energy still holds. The small piston moves a large distance d1, while the big one moves a small distance d2, such that:

F1 d1 = F2 d2

The work in equals the work out. You trade distance for force, exactly like a lever. In fact, a hydraulic lift is a lever — a fluid lever.

But notice: if the small piston moves down by 10 cm, the large piston rises only 1 cm. Same work, different packaging.

3 · Archimedes' Principle — Buoyancy

Legend has it that Archimedes leapt from his bath shouting "Eureka!" when he discovered this principle. Whether or not the story is true, the physics is magnificent:

Key Principle — Archimedes' Principle Any object wholly or partially submerged in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces.
Fb = ρfluid · Vdisplaced · g

Let me tell you why this works, not just that it works. Before you put the object in the water, the water was sitting there quite happily. The chunk of water that used to be where the object now is — what was holding it up? The surrounding water, pushing on it from all sides! The net upward push from the surrounding water exactly equalled the weight of that chunk of water. Now you replace that chunk with something else — a rock, a ship, a rubber duck. The surrounding water doesn't know what's inside. It pushes exactly the same way. So the buoyant force equals the weight of the displaced water. It's not a new force — it's the old pressure distribution still doing its job.

Float or sink?

An object's fate depends on a simple comparison:

  • If ρobject < ρfluid → it floats (partially submerged)
  • If ρobject = ρfluid → it is neutrally buoyant
  • If ρobject > ρfluid → it sinks

When an object floats, the fraction submerged equals the ratio of densities:

VsubmergedVobject = ρobjectρfluid

Ice has a density of about 917 kg/m³ compared to water's 1000 kg/m³. So about 91.7% of an iceberg is underwater. That is where the saying "tip of the iceberg" comes from — you only see about 8% of the total volume.

4 · Fluid Dynamics: The Moving Fluid

So far we've talked about fluids at rest — hydrostatics. Now let's let the fluid move. This is where things get really beautiful.

We'll restrict ourselves to an idealisation called ideal fluid flow: incompressible, non-viscous, irrotational, and steady (meaning the flow pattern doesn't change with time). Real fluids are messier, but this idealisation captures the essential physics remarkably well.

The continuity equation

Imagine fluid flowing through a pipe that changes diameter. In any steady flow, the mass flowing in per second must equal the mass flowing out — fluid doesn't pile up or disappear. For an incompressible fluid:

A1v1 = A2v2

where A is the cross-sectional area and v is the fluid velocity. This is the equation of continuity. It says: when the pipe narrows, the fluid speeds up. When it widens, the fluid slows down.

You've seen this every day. Put your thumb over a garden hose and the water shoots out faster. The area decreases, so the velocity must increase to maintain the same volume flow rate. There's nothing mysterious about it — it's just conservation of mass.

Bernoulli's principle

Now here is Daniel Bernoulli's great insight, published in 1738. Apply conservation of energy to a flowing fluid (doing the work-energy theorem on a small element of fluid as it moves along a streamline), and you get:

P + 12ρv2 + ρgh = constant

This is Bernoulli's equation. Along any streamline in a steady, incompressible, non-viscous flow, the sum of pressure, kinetic energy per unit volume, and potential energy per unit volume is constant.

Key Insight Where the fluid moves faster, the pressure is lower. This is the central message of Bernoulli's principle. It sounds counterintuitive — shouldn't faster-moving fluid push harder? No! The pressure is not a measure of how hard the fluid hits you; it's a measure of the internal stress within the fluid. When fluid accelerates into a narrow region, it must have been pushed there by a pressure difference — high pressure behind, low pressure ahead. The low pressure in the fast region is what caused the fluid to speed up.

And this is the piece that's relevant to the airplane wing. The wing is shaped and angled so that air flows faster over the top than the bottom. Bernoulli tells us the pressure on top is therefore lower. The pressure difference between bottom (higher) and top (lower) creates a net upward force — lift. But remember: the deeper reason the air goes faster on top involves the downward deflection of the airstream. The full story uses Newton and Bernoulli together.

5 · Viscosity and Stokes' Law

Real fluids have viscosity — internal friction. When fluid layers slide past each other, viscosity resists the relative motion. Think of it as the "thickness" of the fluid: honey has high viscosity, water has low viscosity, air has even lower.

Newton modelled viscous friction between fluid layers with:

F = ηA dvdy

where η (eta) is the coefficient of viscosity (SI unit: Pa·s or "poiseuille"), A is the area of the layer, and dv/dy is the velocity gradient perpendicular to the flow. A fluid obeying this linear relation is called a Newtonian fluid.

Stokes' law

When a small sphere moves slowly through a viscous fluid, George Gabriel Stokes showed that the drag force is:

Fdrag = 6πηrv

where r is the sphere's radius, v its velocity, and η the fluid's viscosity. This is Stokes' law, valid for low Reynolds number flow (laminar, non-turbulent conditions).

Stokes' law is one of the most useful formulas in experimental physics. It gives you a way to measure viscosity: drop a steel ball through glycerine, time its fall, and work backwards to find η. It explains why raindrops fall at a terminal velocity instead of accelerating all the way to the ground. It tells you why fog droplets hang in the air while hailstones plummet. Size matters — the drag force is proportional to radius, but gravity is proportional to radius cubed. Small things fall slowly.

Terminal velocity

A sphere falling through a viscous fluid reaches terminal velocity when drag + buoyancy = weight:

6πηrvt + 43πr3ρfg = 43πr3ρsg

Solving for terminal velocity:

vt = 2r2(ρs − ρf)g9η

Notice the r2 dependence. Double the radius and the terminal velocity quadruples. This is why large raindrops fall much faster than drizzle.

Investigation

Measuring viscosity with Stokes' law

Drop a steel ball (density 7800 kg/m³, radius 2 mm) through a tall cylinder of glycerine (density 1260 kg/m³, viscosity 1.5 Pa·s). Predict the terminal velocity:

vt = 2 × (0.002)2 × (7800 − 1260) × 9.89 × 1.5 ≈ 0.0076 m/s ≈ 7.6 mm/s

That's less than 1 cm per second! Time the ball over a marked distance and compare. This is a standard BSc lab experiment.

Reynolds number

How do you know when flow is smooth (laminar) versus chaotic (turbulent)? The Reynolds number gives the answer:

Re = ρvLη

where L is a characteristic length (pipe diameter, sphere diameter, etc.). It is the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces. When Re is small (below about 2000 for pipe flow), viscosity dominates and flow is orderly — laminar. When Re is large (above about 4000), inertia dominates and flow becomes turbulent — chaotic, with eddies and mixing.

Turbulence is one of the great unsolved problems in classical physics. We can describe laminar flow with elegant equations, but turbulence? Nobody has a complete theory. As the joke goes, when I meet God, I'm going to ask two questions: Why relativity? And why turbulence? I really think God may have an answer to the first one.

6 · Surface Tension and Capillarity

Molecules in the bulk of a liquid are pulled equally in all directions by their neighbours. But molecules at the surface have neighbours only on one side — below and beside them, not above (where there's air). The result is a net inward pull that makes the surface behave like an elastic membrane under tension.

This is surface tension, denoted γ (or sometimes T or S). It is defined as force per unit length along the surface:

γ = FL

The SI unit is N/m. For water at 20°C, γ ≈ 0.073 N/m.

Surface tension is why small water drops are spherical — a sphere has the minimum surface area for a given volume, and the surface tension acts to minimise that area. It's why insects can walk on water. It's why you can float a needle on a water surface if you place it carefully. The surface film supports the needle's weight, not buoyancy — a steel needle is much denser than water.

Excess pressure in a drop

Surface tension squeezes a spherical drop, creating an excess pressure inside. For a drop (one surface):

ΔP = 2γR

For a soap bubble (two surfaces — inner and outer):

ΔP = 4γR

Smaller bubbles have higher internal pressure. This is why when you connect a small bubble to a large one, the small bubble shrinks and the large one grows — air flows from higher pressure to lower.

Capillarity

When a narrow tube is dipped into a liquid, the liquid may rise or fall inside the tube, depending on whether the liquid wets the tube wall or not.

  • Wetting (contact angle θ < 90°): adhesive forces between liquid and glass dominate. The meniscus curves upward. Water in a glass tube rises. This is capillary rise.
  • Non-wetting (contact angle θ > 90°): cohesive forces within the liquid dominate. The meniscus curves downward. Mercury in a glass tube is depressed.

The height of capillary rise is:

h = 2γ cos θρgr

where r is the tube radius, θ is the contact angle, ρ is the liquid density, and g is gravitational acceleration. Notice the inverse dependence on r — narrower tubes give higher rise. This is why water wicks up through paper towels, why sap rises in the fine vessels of plants, and why a dry sponge soaks up water.

Note — Capillarity in daily life The wick of an oil lamp draws oil upward by capillary action. The towel that dries you after a bath uses the same principle. The fine pores in soil draw groundwater upward to plant roots. Capillarity is everywhere, working quietly in tubes thinner than a hair.

Investigation

Capillary rise calculation

A glass capillary tube of inner radius 0.5 mm is dipped in water (γ = 0.073 N/m, θ ≈ 0, ρ = 1000 kg/m³). How high does the water rise?

h = 2 × 0.073 × cos 0°1000 × 9.8 × 0.0005 = 0.1464.9 ≈ 0.030 m = 3.0 cm

Now try it with a 0.1 mm tube: you'll get about 15 cm. And with a 0.01 mm tube (10 microns, similar to plant xylem vessels): 150 cm — 1.5 metres! This is part of how trees transport water from roots to leaves, though the full mechanism also involves transpiration and cohesion-tension.

7 · Putting It All Together

The beautiful thing about fluid mechanics is how few principles you need. Conservation of mass gives you the continuity equation. Conservation of energy gives you Bernoulli's equation. Newton's second law applied to a fluid element gives you the Navier-Stokes equations (which we haven't derived here, but which are the foundation of all fluid dynamics). Pascal's law, Archimedes' principle, Stokes' law — they all follow from these basic conservation laws applied to continuous media. That's the power of physics: a handful of principles, endlessly applied.

Chapter Summary
  • Pressure = F/A; increases with depth as P = P0 + ρgh
  • Pascal's law: pressure changes transmit undiminished through enclosed fluids
  • Archimedes: buoyant force = weight of displaced fluid
  • Continuity: A1v1 = A2v2 (mass conservation)
  • Bernoulli: P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant (energy conservation)
  • Stokes' law: Fdrag = 6πηrv for a sphere in viscous flow
  • Surface tension: γ = F/L; capillary rise h = 2γcosθ/(ρgr)
