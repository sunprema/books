Custom-Training: LoRA & QLoRA

"Custom-training" almost never means training from scratch. It means taking a finished model and nudging it toward your task — cheaply enough to do on one GPU. Here's the idea that makes that possible.

First, three words that get muddled. Pretraining is next-token prediction on trillions of tokens of raw text; it produces a base model and costs millions of dollars — you will never do it. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) continues training on (prompt, response) pairs so the model follows instructions in your style and domain; this is where "custom" lives. Alignment (RLHF, DPO) then tunes toward human preferences. For Acme, we want SFT: show the model thousands of Acme tickets and their ideal replies, and let it absorb the pattern.

🧭
Where you start

You almost always start from an already instruction-tuned checkpoint — Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct or Qwen3-8B — not a raw base model. It already speaks; you're teaching it an accent and a job.

Why not just fine-tune everything?

Full fine-tuning updates all the weights, and the memory to do that is brutal. With Adam in mixed precision you need roughly 16 bytes per parameter — weights, gradients, fp32 master copy, and two optimizer moments:

2 (weights) + 2 (grads) + 4 (master) + 4 (moment m) + 4 (moment v) = 16 B/param

For a "small" 7B model that's ~112 GB — before activations — already past a single 80 GB H100. Full FT means multi-GPU sharding and real money.

· 1

LoRA: train a tiny patch, freeze the rest

Here's the insight (Hu et al., 2021). You don't need to move every weight. Freeze the whole pretrained matrix W₀ and represent the change you want as a low-rank product of two skinny matrices:

h = W₀·x + ΔW·x,   where ΔW = B·A
A is r×k, B is d×r, with rank r ≪ d

Only A and B are trained; W₀ never moves. Since r is tiny (8, 16, 32…), the trainable parameters are typically under 1% of the model — and the optimizer state, the thing that blew up full FT, now scales only with that 1%. Init B to zero so training starts exactly at the base model and departs smoothly.

The update ΔW is forced to be low-rank — few numbers, big effect.

The LoRA knobs you'll set

  • r — rank8, 16, 32, 64
  • lora_alpha — scalingeffective = α / r
  • target_modulesq/k/v/o + MLP
  • lora_dropout~0.05

The adapter is applied to specific linear layers — the attention projections q_proj, k_proj, v_proj, o_proj and usually the MLP gate_proj, up_proj, down_proj. The scale α/r controls how loudly the adapter speaks; raise α to amplify it. Higher r = more capacity to learn, more memory.

🔑
The payoff, from the paper

On GPT-3 175B, LoRA cut trainable parameters ~10,000× and GPU memory ~3× versus full Adam fine-tuning — with quality on par or better. And because ΔW can be folded back into W₀ after training, LoRA adds zero inference latency.

· 3

QLoRA: LoRA on a 4-bit base

LoRA shrinks the optimizer; the frozen base weights still sit in memory in 16-bit. QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) shrinks those too: store the frozen base in 4-bit NF4, dequantize each block to bf16 just-in-time for the math, and train the bf16 LoRA adapters on top. Three ingredients:

  • NF4 — a 4-bit type optimal for normally-distributed weights.
  • Double quantization — quantize the quantization constants too, saving a bit more.
  • Paged optimizers — page optimizer state to CPU on memory spikes to dodge OOM.
🔑
The headline result

QLoRA fine-tunes a 65B model on a single 48 GB GPU while preserving full 16-bit fine-tuning quality. In practice a 7–8B fine-tunes comfortably on a 24 GB consumer card. This is the technique that put custom training in reach of individuals.
