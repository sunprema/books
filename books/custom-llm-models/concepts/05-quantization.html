Concept 05 · Memory

Precision & Quantization

A weight is just a number. How many bits you spend storing each one decides whether a model fits on your GPU — or doesn't. Quantization is the lever, and it's the difference between "needs an H100" and "runs on my laptop."

We ended the last chapter with a memory budget. Weights are the biggest line item, and their size is simply parameters × bytes-per-parameter. The parameter count is fixed by the model you chose. The bytes-per-parameter is yours to negotiate — that negotiation is called quantization.

The precision ladder

FormatBytes/paramNotes
FP324Full precision. Rarely needed for inference.
FP162Half precision. 5-bit exponent, 10-bit mantissa.
BF162Same range as FP32, less mantissa. The modern default.
FP81Two flavours: E4M3 (precision) and E5M2 (range).
INT818-bit integer. Usually near-lossless.
INT40.5~¼ of FP16. The workhorse of local inference.
🔑
Do the multiplication

A 7B model: ~14 GB in FP16, ~3.5 GB in INT4. Llama-3-8B: ~16 GB → ~4 GB. The same weights, one-quarter the memory. That's the whole reason quantization is everywhere.

FP16 vs BF16 — a five-second aside

Both are 16 bits, both are "half precision," but they split those bits differently. BF16 keeps FP32's full exponent (its range) and sacrifices mantissa (its precision); FP16 does the reverse. For deep learning, range matters more than fine precision, so BF16 rarely overflows and has become the default on modern GPUs. When you see bnb_4bit_compute_dtype=bfloat16 in Chapter 7, this is why.

Weight-only quantization: keep the smarts, shrink the storage

The trick that makes 4-bit work is weight-only quantization: store the weights in 4 bits, but dequantize them back to 16-bit on the fly for the actual math (activations stay in fp16/bf16). You get the memory win without wrecking accuracy — because the sensitive arithmetic still happens in higher precision.

📉
The quality curve

8-bit (INT8/FP8): usually near-lossless. 4-bit (the methods opposite): ~4× smaller with typically small quality loss. Below 4-bit (3-bit, 2-bit): degradation grows and gets model- and task-dependent. 4-bit is the sweet spot most people live at.

🧠
Not all weights are equal

The clever methods all exploit one fact: a small fraction of weights matter far more than the rest. Protect those, quantize the rest hard. Different methods just find the important ones differently — via the Hessian, via activations, via per-block bit budgets.

The four methods you'll actually meet

GPTQ

Post-training, layer-by-layer, uses approximate second-order (Hessian) info to minimize error. Typically 4-bit, group-wise. GPU-oriented, strong accuracy.

AWQ

Activation-aware. Finds the ~1% of salient weight channels via activation magnitude and protects them. INT4/INT3, hardware-friendly kernels; often matches or beats GPTQ. (MLSys 2024 best paper.)

GGUF (llama.cpp)

A file format with many quant levels (Q2_K…Q8_0). "k-quants" mix bit budgets per block; Q4_K_M is the common default. CPU + GPU-offload; the format Ollama uses.

NF4 (bitsandbytes)

4-bit "NormalFloat," optimal for normally-distributed weights, from the QLoRA paper. Plus double-quantization. Fine-tuning-first — this is what makes QLoRA fit (Ch. 6).

🧭
Which one, when

Serving on a GPU → AWQ or GPTQ (works with vLLM/TGI). Laptop / CPU / Apple Silicon → GGUF via Ollama or llama.cpp. Fine-tuning a big model on a small GPU → NF4 (QLoRA). They solve overlapping problems from different starting points.

The complete serving memory budget

Now you can assemble the whole picture. Total GPU memory to serve a model is:

GPU_mem ≈ weights (params × bytes)
    + KV cache (Ch. 4 formula, × batch × context)
    + overhead (activations, CUDA context, buffers)

Every dial you have moves one of those terms. Quantize the weights → first term shrinks, freeing room for the second → more concurrent users on the same card. This is the through-line from here to the end of the book.

🧪
Acme check-in

Acme's Qwen-8B at fp16 needs ~16 GB just for weights — nearly all of a 24 GB GPU, leaving little for KV cache. Quantize to 4-bit (AWQ) and weights drop to ~5 GB, leaving ~18 GB for cache — room for many more concurrent tickets, on the exact same hardware. Same model, quadruple the seats.

⚠️
Free lunch has a bill

Quantization is lossy. For a customer-facing draft, validate that the 4-bit model still writes acceptable replies before you ship it. Measure, don't assume — 4-bit is usually fine, not always.
