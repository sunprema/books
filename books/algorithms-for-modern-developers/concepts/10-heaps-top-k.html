10 Scale

Heaps & Top-K

A heap (or priority queue) answers one question superbly fast: "what's the smallest — or largest — thing I have right now?" It hands you the extreme in O(1) and re-balances after a push or pop in O(log n). That single trick powers schedulers, leaderboards, and streaming analytics.

Picture a tournament bracket as a tree where every parent beats its children. The overall winner sits at the root, instantly visible. Add a newcomer and it bubbles up only as far as it deserves; remove the champion and the tree heals in log n steps. Cleverly, this tree is stored in a plain array — node i's children live at 2i+1 and 2i+2 — so there are no pointers, just arithmetic.

A min-heap: every parent ≤ its children, so the minimum is always at index 0.

The flagship use: Top-K without sorting

You have a firehose of a billion events and want the 10 biggest. Sorting all of it is O(n log n) and needs it all in memory. A heap of size K does it in O(n log k) with only K items resident — perfect for streams that don't fit in RAM.

top-K from a stream with a size-K min-heapimport heapq

def top_k(stream, k):
    heap = []                      # min-heap of the best k so far
    for x in stream:
        if len(heap) < k:
            heapq.heappush(heap, x)
        elif x > heap[0]:           # bigger than our weakest?
            heapq.heapreplace(heap, x) # evict the min, add x
    return heap                     # the k largest, unsorted

# or just: heapq.nlargest(k, stream)
★
The counter-intuitive bit

To keep the largest K, you use a min-heap. Its root is the weakest of your current champions — so each new item only has to beat that one number to earn a spot, and the loser is evicted in O(log k). Tiny memory, one pass, no full sort.

The other big role is as a scheduler's to-do list: a priority queue that always runs the most urgent task next. Push tasks with priorities, pop the winner. This is exactly what made Dijkstra (chapter 7) efficient — "expand the nearest node" is a heap pop.

◎
Where you'll meet it

OS & runtimes: process/thread schedulers, timer queues, event loops. Leaderboards & trending: "top 100 players," "most-viewed this hour." Streaming & analytics: top-K heavy hitters, k-nearest-neighbors in ML/recommendations. Networking: QoS priority queues, packet scheduling. Algorithms: Dijkstra/A* frontiers, Huffman coding, merging K sorted files/log streams. Task systems: job queues with priorities (Celery, Sidekiq).

▣
In your standard library

heapq (Python), PriorityQueue (Java), container/heap (Go), BinaryHeap (Rust), std::priority_queue (C++). Almost nobody hand-rolls a heap — but knowing the O(n log k) trick tells you when to grab one instead of sorting.

Heaps gave us exact answers cheaply. But at truly massive scale — billions of items, kilobytes of RAM — even O(n) memory is too much. The final chapter trades a sliver of accuracy for staggering efficiency.
