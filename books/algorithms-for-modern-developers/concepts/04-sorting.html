04 Core

Sorting — What Your Stdlib Really Does

You will almost never write a sorting algorithm. You will constantly use one — and understanding what your standard library actually runs explains its quirks, its speed, and the one parameter that bites people: stability.

The theory first, because it sets a hard floor: any sort that works by comparing pairs of elements cannot beat O(n log n). That's not an engineering limit, it's a mathematical one — there are n! possible orderings and each comparison gives one bit of information. So the goal of a real-world sort isn't to break that bound; it's to hit it reliably while being fast on the messy, partly-ordered data we actually have.

The three ideas worth knowing

Merge sort

Split in half, sort each, merge. Always O(n log n), stable, but needs O(n) extra space. The basis of Timsort.

Quicksort

Pick a pivot, partition around it, recurse. Fast and in-place, O(n log n) average — but O(n²) on bad pivots.

Modern libraries are hybrids that take the best of each and patch the weaknesses:

  • Timsort — merge sort that detects already-sorted "runs" and merges them. Near-O(n) on real-world, partly-ordered data. Stable.
  • Introsort — quicksort that watches its own recursion depth and flips to heapsort if a bad pivot threatens the O(n²) case. Guarantees O(n log n).
  • pdqsort (pattern-defeating quicksort) — introsort's smarter cousin; detects patterns and exploits existing order. Unstable but very fast.

"Stable" is the word that bites

A stable sort keeps equal elements in their original relative order. Sort users by city, then stably by age, and within each age the city order survives — that's how you build multi-key sorts. An unstable sort may scramble equal elements. It's faster and uses less memory, which is why Go and C++ default to it — but if you rely on prior order, reach for the stable variant.

the part you actually write: the key# sort by length, then alphabetically — a "key" tuple
words.sort(key=lambda w: (len(w), w))

# sort objects by a field, descending
users.sort(key=lambda u: u.score, reverse=True)

This is 95% of real sorting work: not the algorithm, but expressing what order you want via a key or comparator. Get the key right and the library does the rest, optimally.

◎
Where you'll meet it

Anywhere you present data: ranked search results, leaderboards, sorted exports/reports, "newest first" feeds. As a building block: sorting first makes binary search, deduplication, merging time-ranges, and many greedy algorithms (next chapters) possible. Databases sort for ORDER BY, merge-joins, and index builds.

●
Don't sort when you don't have to

Need just the top 10 of a million? Sorting all of it is O(n log n) wasted work — a heap does it in O(n log k) (chapter 10). Need only membership? A hash set is O(n). Sorting is the right tool for order, not for everything.
