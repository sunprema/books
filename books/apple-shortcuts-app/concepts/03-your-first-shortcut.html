Chapter 03 · Hands On

Your First Shortcut, Three Ways

The fastest way to believe in Shortcuts is to ship one in the next five minutes. There are three on-ramps, from zero effort to full control: steal one from the Gallery, remix what you stole, or build from a blank canvas. We'll do all three with the same target — the classic "text my ETA" shortcut.

On-ramp 1: The Gallery

Open Shortcuts and tap Gallery. It's Apple's curated library of pre-built shortcuts — hundreds of them, organized by moment ("Morning Routine", "Work From Home") and by app. Find one close to what you want and tap Add Shortcut (older versions: Set Up Shortcut, which walks you through personalizing it — your home address, your contact, your playlist).

Note

The Gallery is also a reading list. Add anything that looks interesting and open it in the editor to see how it's built. Every Gallery shortcut is fully inspectable — it's the app's own textbook of examples.

On-ramp 2: Remix

Every shortcut in your library can be opened and edited: tap the ⋯ on its tile. Change the recipient, swap the podcast app for Music, add a step. Remixing is how most people actually learn — the structure is already right; you're just re-wiring parameters.

On-ramp 3: Build "Text My ETA" from scratch

Tap + in your library. You get a blank shortcut and a search box. Build this:

Shortcut · "Text My ETA"
⌂
Get travel time Add the Get Travel Time action. Set the destination to your home address, mode: Driving. Its output is a Travel Time variable.
✉
Send Message Add Send Message. In the message body type "On my way — home in about", then insert the Travel Time Magic Variable. Set the recipient to your partner.
➤
Open driving directions Add Show Directions with the same destination, so Maps starts navigating the moment the text is sent.

Tap the name at the top to rename it Text My ETA, pick an icon and color, and hit the play button ▶ to test. That's the whole loop: search action → drop it in → fill its slots → test.

Key Idea

From now on, "Hey Siri, text my ETA" works too — the name of a shortcut is automatically its Siri phrase. Name shortcuts the way you'd naturally say them out loud.

The editor, quickly mastered

  • Search bar (bottom on iPhone, right sidebar on iPad/Mac): type fragments — "travel", "message". Categories and app names group everything.
  • Blue-tinted words inside an action are parameters — tap to edit, or tap the chevron ▸ on the action for its hidden options (e.g. Send Message's "Show When Run" toggle: off = fully silent, on = shows the compose sheet for confirmation).
  • Drag to reorder actions; swipe (or right-click on Mac) to delete.
  • ▶ Run previews the shortcut in place, stepping visually through the actions so you can watch data flow.
  • The ⓘ panel holds the switches that matter later: Show in Share Sheet, Receive input types, Use as Quick Action (Mac), Show on Apple Watch.

When something breaks

Shortcuts fail loudly and fixably. If a step gets content it can't handle, the run stops there and shows which action complained. The debugging ritual:

  • Run with ▶ inside the editor and watch where it halts.
  • Insert Quick Look after a suspect step — it pops up whatever is flowing at that point. It's the print() of Shortcuts.
  • Check the input: tap the variable feeding the failing action and make sure it's the type the action expects (text vs. file vs. image).
Gotcha

Some actions ask permission the first time they touch contacts, photos, location, or another app — a one-time "Allow" per shortcut. If a shortcut mysteriously does nothing when run from Siri or an automation, run it once manually to clear the pending permission prompts.
