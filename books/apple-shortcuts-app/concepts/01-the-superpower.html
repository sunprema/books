Chapter 01 · Why This App Exists

The Superpower Hiding in Plain Sight

There is an app already installed on your iPhone, iPad, Mac, and Apple Watch that can text your partner your arrival time when you leave work, turn your whole house into "movie mode" with one tap, transcribe and summarize a meeting recording, and strip the background out of fifty product photos while you make coffee. Most people have never opened it. It's called Shortcuts, and this book is about turning it from an icon you scroll past into the most useful app you own.

What Shortcuts actually is

A shortcut is a small recipe: a list of steps, called actions, that run in order. Each action does one concrete thing — Get Current Weather, Get Upcoming Events, Send Message — and hands its result to the next step. Chain a few together and you've built a little program, except you built it by dragging colorful blocks, not by writing code.

Key Idea

Shortcuts is programming without the syntax. If you can describe a routine as "get this, do that to it, then send it there," you can build it. The blocks snap together like LEGO, and the hard parts — talking to apps, to the web, even to AI models — are pre-built for you.

Why Apple built it into everything

Shortcuts began life as a beloved third-party app called Workflow. Apple bought it in 2017, shipped it as Shortcuts with iOS 12, and has been promoting it ever since — because it solves a real problem: your phone can do thousands of things, but only through dozens of taps spread across dozens of apps. Shortcuts collapses those taps into one.

Today it runs on iPhone, iPad, Mac, Apple Watch, and Apple Vision Pro, syncs your shortcuts through iCloud, and — as of the OS 26 releases — can call Apple Intelligence models as just another step in a recipe. It went from a power-user toy to a first-class pillar of the platform.

The three levels of the superpower

Everything in this book fits on a ladder with three rungs. You can stop at any rung and still win.

  • Level 1 — Launchers. One tap (or one sentence to Siri) does a multi-step thing: "Directions home, text my ETA, start my podcast." You run it yourself; it just saves the taps.
  • Level 2 — Automations. The shortcut runs itself when something happens: you arrive at the gym, your battery hits 20%, you close a banking app, an NFC sticker is scanned. Your phone starts acting on your behalf.
  • Level 3 — Intelligence. With the OS 26 releases, shortcuts can pass text, images, and files through Apple's AI models mid-recipe — summarize, rewrite, extract, decide — privately, on your device or Apple's Private Cloud Compute.

A taste of what "effective" looks like

Because the user request behind this book was blunt and correct — show me where this is actually useful — here is the honest shortlist. Each of these gets a full treatment in chapters 8 and 9:

  • The commute text: one tap texts your partner your live ETA from Maps and starts navigation. No typing while driving.
  • The morning brief: when your alarm stops, your phone speaks today's weather and first three calendar events, then starts your news podcast.
  • The meeting funnel: a voice memo goes in; a transcript, an AI summary, and a note filed in the right folder come out.
  • The focus wall: opening Instagram after 10pm triggers a gentle "really?" screen — a speed bump you built for yourself.
  • The kitchen tag: an NFC sticker on the microwave starts a 10-minute timer and your cooking playlist when you tap your phone on it.
Note

None of these require jailbreaking, subscriptions, or third-party services. They're built from stock actions in the free, pre-installed app. Where third-party apps do join in (chapter 10), they make Shortcuts stronger, not more fragile.

How to read this book

Chapters 2–4 teach the mental model: what an action is, how data flows, and how to make decisions inside a recipe. Chapters 5–6 cover the many ways to run a shortcut and how to make them run themselves. Chapter 7 is the new AI layer. Chapters 8–9 are pure use cases — daily life, then work. Chapter 10 is the power-user toolkit, and the cheatsheet at the end is the one-page version of everything.

You don't need to memorize anything. You need exactly one idea to carry forward: your devices can already do this; the app is the missing remote control. Let's go build your first button.
