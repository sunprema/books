Chapter 08 · The Cell Cycle & Mitosis

How a Cell Becomes Two

Copy two metres of code perfectly, then split down the middle — on schedule.

You started as one cell. Every other cell in your body — all thirty-seven trillion — came from that one dividing, and dividing, and dividing. Right now, to keep you alive, your body is making a couple of million new cells every second to replace the ones dying: skin flaking off, blood cells aging out, gut lining shed. Each new cell is a copy, and each copy must get a complete, correct set of the DNA. Not almost complete. Complete. Get it wrong and you get a dead cell — or, worse, a cancerous one. So the cell follows a careful, checkpoint-gated routine called the cell cycle.

The one idea

The cell cycle has two big acts. First, a long interphase, where the cell grows and — crucially — copies all its DNA, so every chromosome becomes two identical halves stuck together. Then mitosis, a fast, dramatic act where those halves are lined up dead-center and pulled to opposite ends, so the cell can pinch in two with a full genome on each side. Grow, copy, check, split.

The cycle, as a clock

Mitosis in five beats

  1. Prophase — the copied DNA condenses into visible X-shaped chromosomes (each X is two identical "sister" copies joined at the middle). The nuclear envelope breaks down.
  2. Metaphase — microtubule cables (the "spindle") grab each chromosome and line all of them up precisely along the cell's equator.
  3. Anaphase — the sisters are pulled apart, one of each to opposite poles, by motor proteins reeling in the spindle. This is the moment the genome splits in two.
  4. Telophase — a nuclear envelope reforms around each new set; the chromosomes relax back into loose chromatin.
  5. Cytokinesis — an actin ring cinches the cell's waist like a drawstring until it pinches into two complete daughter cells.
The part I find almost unbearably elegant is the checking. The cell doesn't just barrel ahead. At each checkpoint it stops and asks a yes/no question — "Is my DNA fully copied? Is it damaged? Is every chromosome properly hooked to the spindle?" — and it will not proceed until the answer is yes. If the damage is too bad to fix, the cell chooses to kill itself (a tidy, programmed death called apoptosis) rather than pass on a broken genome. Think about that: a single cell, with no brain, that would rather die than make a bad copy. That discipline is what keeps a trillion-cell body from flying apart.
Why it matters — this is the door to cancer

Those checkpoints are guarded by proteins with names you may have heard: p53, the "guardian of the genome," halts a damaged cell; BRCA genes help repair the DNA. When the guards themselves get mutated, a cell can start dividing without checking — copying its own errors, ignoring the stop signs. That runaway division is exactly what cancer is. The last chapter follows what happens when a cell forgets the rules.

A related trick: making sex cells

Everything above (mitosis) makes two identical cells. But egg and sperm need half a genome each, shuffled for variety. A cousin process, meiosis, does that — two divisions instead of one, with a genetic card-shuffle in between. It's why you're not a clone of either parent, and why every sibling is a fresh hand of cards.

Grow, copy, check, split — done right, billions of times, it builds and maintains a whole you. Done wrong, it's the root of the disease we fear most. Let's finish there: what happens when a cell breaks the rules, and why understanding the cell is quietly rewriting medicine.
