Chapter 04 · Mitochondria

The Powerhouses

Once, they were bacteria. You made a deal with them.

Here's a sentence that should be more famous than it is: a resting adult body makes and spends roughly its own body weight in fuel every single day. The fuel is a molecule called ATP, and you can't store it — there's only ever about 250 grams of it in you at once, so each molecule has to be recharged and reused a thousand times a day. Something has to run that recharging plant nonstop, in every cell, from before you're born until you die. That something is the mitochondrion, and it has a wild origin story: it used to be a free-living bacterium.

The one idea

A mitochondrion takes the food you eat (broken down to a sugar, glucose) plus the oxygen you breathe, and uses them to charge up ATP — the cell's rechargeable battery. Snap ATP's last phosphate off and energy pops out, ready to power anything: a muscle twitch, a pumped ion, a built protein. Mitochondria are where "breathing" and "eating" actually mean something, cell by cell.

Spin one around

A mitochondrion is a bean-shaped bag inside a bag. The outer membrane is smooth; the inner one is crumpled into deep folds called cristae — and that crumpling is the whole point. The folds pack in enormous surface area, and the energy machinery is studded all along them. More folds, more power sockets, more ATP. How the charging actually works — the dam

The mechanism is so clever it feels like cheating. Along the folds, the energy stripped from food is used to pump protons (hydrogen ions) across the inner membrane, piling them up on one side. That's a battery made of nothing but a crowd of ions wanting to get back across — like water held behind a dam. The cell lets them flood back through one specific gate, a molecular turbine called ATP synthase, and the rushing protons literally spin it like a water wheel. Each turn snaps a phosphate onto ADP to make ATP. You are powered by microscopic hydroelectric dams. Billions of them. Right now.

Stop and admire the audacity of the design. The cell didn't evolve a wire or a battery in any form we'd have invented. It evolved a waterfall — it stores energy as a lopsided pile of ions and harvests it with a spinning turbine, the same physics as a hydro plant, at a scale of nanometres. When you find a mechanism this good, ask why it's so common: it's because the proton-gradient trick is ancient, older than the nucleus itself. Nearly everything alive runs on some version of this waterfall.

The tenant that never left

Mitochondria carry their own DNA, make their own inner-membrane proteins, and divide on their own schedule by splitting in two — because they descend from a bacterium that, more than a billion years ago, got engulfed by an ancestor of our cells and simply stayed. Its host got a power plant; it got shelter. This is the endosymbiotic theory, and one of its lovely consequences is that you inherit all your mitochondria from your mother — her cell supplied the ones in the egg.

Why it matters

Your most energy-hungry organs — brain, heart, muscle, liver — are packed with mitochondria, so when mitochondria fail, those organs fail first. Mitochondrial diseases hit exactly there. It's also why "aerobic" exercise works: train hard and your cells build more mitochondria to keep up. You can grow your own power plants.

Power secured. Now the city can afford to build — and what it builds, constantly, is proteins: the workers, walls, tools, and messengers that do nearly every job in biology. Time to visit the assembly line and the shipping department.
