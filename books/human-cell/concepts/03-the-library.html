Chapter 03 · The Nucleus & DNA

The Library

Two metres of thread, folded into a room six microns wide.

Every city in this book — all thirty-seven trillion of them — carries the same book of blueprints. Not a summary. The complete works: how to build every one of the hundred-thousand-odd proteins your body knows how to make. That book is your DNA, and the cell keeps it in a guarded vault at the center called the nucleus. What stuns me isn't that the information is there. It's the packing. Stretch the DNA from a single cell end to end and it's about two metres long. It's folded to fit inside a nucleus about six-millionths of a metre across. That's like stuffing forty kilometres of fishing line into a tennis ball — and still being able to find any sentence you want, on demand.

The one idea

DNA is a four-letter code — A, T, C, G — written twice, once on each strand of a twisted ladder, each letter paired with its partner (A–T, C–G). Because each strand carries the other's mirror image, the cell can unzip the ladder and rebuild both halves perfectly. The code is the backup of itself. That's why life can copy itself at all.

From a thread to a chromosome

You can't just cram two metres of thread into a ball and hope — it would knot into uselessness. So the cell wraps the DNA around protein spools called histones. Each little wrap — 147 letters of DNA looped twice around eight histones — is a nucleosome, and the whole strand of them looks like beads on a string. Those beads coil into a thicker fibre, which loops and coils again, until at its most condensed the thread becomes the fat X-shaped chromosome you've seen in pictures. The folding isn't just about tidiness — it's censorship, and that's the deep part. A skin cell and a brain cell carry the identical book, letter for letter. What makes them different is which pages are coiled shut and which are laid open to be read. The library is the same everywhere; the bookmarks are what make you, you. This is the whole idea behind "epigenetics," and it's why one genome can build hundreds of kinds of cell.

The vault has a controlled door

The nucleus is wrapped in a double membrane pierced by thousands of nuclear pores — protein rings that act as security gates. The precious DNA never leaves. Instead, when a protein is needed, the cell makes a disposable working copy of just that one gene — a molecule called messenger RNA — and that copy is escorted out through a pore to the factory floor. It's exactly how a rare-book library works: you don't get to take the original home; you get a photocopy of the page you asked for.

Also in the vault

Look inside the nucleus and you'll usually spot a dense dark blob — the nucleolus. It's not a separate organelle so much as a busy workshop where the cell mass-produces ribosomes, the machines that will read those RNA copies and build proteins. Keep it in mind; we meet the ribosomes in two chapters.

A blueprint is only worth something if you can afford to build from it. And building — moving atoms against their will, over and over, trillions of times a second — costs energy. Enormous amounts of it. So next we visit the power plants that never, ever switch off.
