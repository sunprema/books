Chapter 05 · Asking Well

Writing a Good Prompt

Here’s where you actually get what you want. A book is only as good as the request behind it, and a great request has just five ingredients: a topic, an optional title, a voice, a look, and a handful of notes that pin down audience and depth. Get those right and the book almost writes itself.

The anatomy of a strong ask

Whether you type it at a terminal or paste it into a request form, the shape is the same. Compare these two asks for the same subject:

Too thin “Make a book about Postgres.”  You’ll get something reasonable — but generic. It can’t tell if you’re a beginner or a DBA, or what you actually care about.
Just right “A book on PostgreSQL indexing for application developers — assume they know SQL but not internals. Focus on B-tree vs. GIN, when an index hurts, and reading EXPLAIN. Voice: the clear mentor. Look: blueprint.”
Rule of thumb: a topic is a scope, not a word. “Rust” is a word; “Rust ownership and borrowing for people coming from Python” is a scope. Scopes make good books.

The levers you can pull

Only the topic is required. Leave the rest blank and BookBank makes sensible choices; fill them in when you have an opinion. Seed concepts are your guarantee — any chapter you list is kept and generated, and the author adds the essential ones around it.

Prompts to steal

Enough theory — here are ready-to-run asks. Each is a real /write-book command you can paste into Claude Code (with the BookBank plugin installed) to build a book locally. Tap Copy, change the subject, and go.

Starter · plain
/write-book the Rust programming language in Feynman’s voice
Scoped · with audience
/write-book HTTP caching headers for web developers who know JavaScript but not the spec. Cover Cache-Control, ETags, and stale-while-revalidate. Voice: the clear mentor. Look: blueprint. Keep every example copy-pasteable.
First-principles · math
/write-book an intuition-first tour of calculus — limits, derivatives, integrals — for a curious teenager. Persona: feynman. Theme: chalkboard. No proofs; lots of pictures and everyday analogies.
Expand one chapter
/write-book expand "async/await" in rust-programming
Your voice, your look
/write-book Redis internals for backend engineers, in the voice of an excited lab partner mid-experiment, with a warm dark-academia look. Emphasize the data structures and the single-threaded event loop.
The empty queue Run /write-book with no arguments and it does the outstanding work in your library — building anything requested, revising anything revising, and filling any single chapter you queued. If the queue is empty, it simply asks you for a topic.

The most under-used field: Notes

If you take one habit from this chapter, take this: use the notes. A single sentence about who the reader is and how deep to go changes the whole book. “For people migrating from X.” “Assume no math background.” “Emphasize the trade-offs over the syntax.” Those steer research and chapter selection.

What makes a great ask

A scoped topic, a voice and look you chose, a few must-have chapters, and one honest sentence about your reader. That’s it. Next we’ll see the friendliest way to make a request when you don’t have a terminal in front of you: opening an issue.

Draft one

  1. Turn a bare topic you care about into a scoped ask with an audience and two seed concepts.
  2. Add a one-line note that would change which chapters get chosen. Notice how much it steers the book.
