Chapter 02 · Foundations

The Twelve Notes

All of Western music — every song you love — is built from just twelve notes, repeating higher and lower forever. Learn those twelve and how they're spaced, and the fretboard stops being random dots.

The musical alphabet

We start with seven letters: A B C D E F G, then back to A. Between most of them sits one extra note — a sharp (♯, one step up) or the same pitch called a flat (♭, one step down). That fills the seven letters out to twelve:

C  C♯  D  D♯  E  F  F♯  G  G♯  A  A♯  B  → C

The two catches There is no note between B–C and no note between E–F. Those pairs are already as close as notes get. That single fact is why the piano has gaps in its black keys — and why the fretboard's pattern isn't perfectly even.

Why a piano makes it obvious

A black key is a sharp/flat. Notice there's no black key between E&F or B&C — the same “missing note” you'll feel on the guitar neck.

Half steps and whole steps

This is the vocabulary theory is built on:

  • A half step (semitone) is the smallest move — one fret on the guitar, one key on the piano.
  • A whole step (tone) is two half steps — two frets.

C to C♯ is a half step. C to D is a whole step (you skip C♯). E to F is a half step — remember, no note between them. Every scale and chord you'll ever build is just a recipe of whole and half steps.

The chromatic wheel — click a note to hear it

Twelve notes, evenly spaced. Clockwise one slice = up a half step. Twelve slices later you're back where you started, one octave higher.

Octaves: the same note, twice as fast

When you go all twelve steps around and land on the same letter, you've reached an octave. Its frequency is exactly double. Your two E strings are two octaves apart: low E at 82.41 Hz, high E at 329.63 Hz — 82.41 × 2 × 2. Same “note name,” different height. That's why they blend so perfectly.

Drill · Speak the twelve

1. Say the chromatic scale up from C using sharps: C C♯ D D♯ E F F♯ G G♯ A A♯ B C.
2. Now come back down using flats: C B B♭ A A♭ G …
3. On your low E string, play fret by fret and name each note out loud. Find the E–F and B–C spots — they're the ones where consecutive frets are different letters with no sharp between.

3 minutes. Slow and correct beats fast and fuzzy.

Why this matters: The fretboard is this twelve-note cycle laid out six times, once per string. Know the cycle cold and reading the neck becomes arithmetic, not memorization.

Sources: standard 12-tone equal temperament; A4 = 440 Hz reference. Octave = 2× frequency.
