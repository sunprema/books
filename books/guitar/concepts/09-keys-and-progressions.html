Chapter 09 · The Grammar

Keys & Progressions

Why do certain chords sound “right” together? Because they come from the same key — one family of notes and the chords built on them. Learn the numbering once and you'll see the same handful of progressions hiding inside thousands of songs.

“Songs aren't random chords. They're a family reunion. Learn who's in the family and you can predict the next chord before it happens.”

A key is a scale plus its chords

Take a major scale — say C major (C D E F G A B) — and build a triad on each of its seven notes, using only notes from the scale. You get a fixed pattern of qualities, which we label with Roman numerals: uppercase = major, lowercase = minor.

DegreeIiiiiiIVVvivii°
Qualitymajminminmajmajmindim
Key of CCDmEmFGAmB°
Key of GGAmBmCDEmF♯°

The three majors — I, IV, V — are the pillars of a key. The minors — ii, iii, vi — add color. The vi chord is the key's “relative minor,” the sad cousin that shares all the same notes.

The progressions behind everything

  • I – IV – V — the engine of blues, rock & roll, and folk. In G: G – C – D.
  • I – V – vi – IV — the “four-chord song” behind an absurd number of pop hits. In G: G – D – Em – C.
  • I – vi – IV – V — the dreamy “50s” progression. In C: C – Am – F – G.
The four-chord song — I–V–vi–IV in G

That's G–D–Em–C looping. If it sounds like half the songs you know, that's the point — this exact progression powers hundreds of them.

The circle of fifths

Keys are related by fifths. Step clockwise and you move up a 5th to the next key, which shares all but one note with the last — that's why neighboring keys sound so natural together. Each key's I, IV, and V sit side by side on the circle.

In the key of G, its neighbors C (IV) and D (V) sit right beside it — the three-chord family for countless songs.

Drill · Hear the numbers

1. Play the four-chord loop above by hand: G–D–Em–C, campfire strum, one chord per bar.
2. Now play I–IV–V in G: G–C–D. Notice all three are neighbors on the circle.
3. Pick a song you love and try to guess its progression in numbers. Nine times in ten it's one of the three above, maybe reordered.

8 minutes. Learn to hear the numbers, not just the chord names.

Why this matters: Thinking in I–IV–V–vi instead of G–C–D–Em lets you transpose to any key, jam with others, and learn songs by ear. This is the doorway from “playing chords” to understanding music.

Where to go next You now have the whole foundation: the notes, the map, intervals, chords, rhythm, scales, and keys. From here it's deliberate practice — short, focused, daily, with a metronome. Learn songs you love, and reverse-engineer them with what you know. Practice like you're debugging code: isolate the one thing that's broken, fix it slowly, then speed up.
Sources: diatonic harmony (I ii iii IV V vi vii°), common progressions (I–IV–V, I–V–vi–IV), and the circle of fifths (standard music theory).
