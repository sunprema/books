Chapter 07 · Playing

Rhythm & Strumming

Here's a hard truth: a simple chord played in time sounds great, and a beautiful chord played out of time sounds broken. Rhythm comes before melody. This is the chapter that makes you sound like a musician.

“Nobody notices your chord voicings. Everybody feels your timing. Fix the timing first.”

Beats, bars, and 4/4

Music moves in a steady pulse of beats. We group them — most pop and folk songs put four beats in a bar, called 4/4 time. You count it out loud: 1 2 3 4, over and over, even and unhurried. That steady count is your metronome, whether or not a device is clicking.

Down-beats and off-beats

Split each beat in half and you can strum twice as often. We count the halves with “and”: 1&2&3&4&. The numbers are down-strokes (↓), the “&”s are up-strokes (↑).

Count1&2&3&4&
Hand↓↑↓↑↓↑↓↑
The pendulum Keep your strumming hand moving down and up the whole time, like a metronome arm — even on beats you don't play. To skip a strum, just let the hand pass the strings without touching them (a “ghost” strum). This is the single biggest secret to steady rhythm.

Start with the pulse

Before any fancy pattern, play four steady down-strokes per bar — one per beat. Boring is good. Boring is in time.

Four-on-the-floor — quarter-note down-strokes on Em

Every lit cell is a click; the ↓ cells strum. Play along, matching the metronome exactly. Then nudge the tempo up.

The one pattern that plays a thousand songs

When the pulse is solid, add up-strokes to get the famous “campfire” pattern:

1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &
↓   ↓ ↑   ↑ ↓ ↑

Down · (skip) · Down-Up · (skip)-Up · Down-Up  =  D - D U - U D U

Campfire strum over a real progression

This strums the pattern while cycling G–Em–C–D, one chord per bar — an actual song loop. Keep your hand pumping through the skipped strums.

Drill · Hand never stops

1. Mute the strings with your fretting hand (rest it lightly across them). Strum the campfire pattern as pure percussion — chk chk-chk — until it's locked to the click.
2. Now fret Em and play the pattern for four bars without stopping.
3. Add the G–Em–C–D loop above. Don't stop the hand to change chords — change during a ghost strum.

6 minutes with the metronome. Timing over everything.

Why this matters: A moving, steady strumming hand is the difference between “playing chords” and “playing a song.” It also frees your brain to think about the next chord while your hand keeps the groove.

Sources: 4/4 time and eighth-note down/up strumming convention; the D-DU-UDU “campfire” pattern taught widely (JustinGuitar, Fender Play beginner method).
