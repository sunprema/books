Chapter 03 · Foundations

The Fretboard is a Map

The neck looks like a grid of anonymous metal bars. It isn't. Each fret is exactly one half step higher than the one behind it. Once you see that, you can find any note anywhere — no memorizing dots, just counting.

“The fretboard is a map, not a maze. Every square has an address. I'll teach you to read the addresses, not memorize the streets.”

One fret = one half step

Press the first fret of your low E string and you get F — the half step above E (remember: no note between E and F). Keep going and you climb the twelve-note cycle:

Fret0123456789101112
Note (E string)EFF♯GG♯AA♯BCC♯DD♯E

At the 12th fret you're back to E — one octave up, the string's pitch exactly doubled. The whole neck above 12 just repeats the pattern. Learn frets 0–12 and you've learned the whole guitar.

The dots are signposts The inlay dots sit at frets 3, 5, 7, 9 and a double dot at 12. They're there so your eye can find position instantly. Use them as landmarks, not decoration.

Top row is the thin high E, bottom row is the thick low E — the way the strings face you when you look down at the guitar. Notice how a note repeats in several places.

The same note lives in many places

Because the six strings overlap in pitch, one note shows up all over the neck. Middle C (C4) is the 1st fret of the B string, the 5th fret of the G string, the 10th fret of the D string, and more — same pitch, different spots. Good players pick the location that keeps their hand relaxed.

The most useful pattern to know is the octave shape: from any note on the low E or A string, the same note (one octave up) sits two strings toward the floor and two frets higher. Find G at the 3rd fret of the low E — its octave is the 5th fret of the D string. One shape, and you can name notes on the middle strings by leaning on the ones you already know.

A bonus: tune the guitar to itself

The map even tells you how to tune without a device. The 5th fret of one string equals the open next string — with one exception at the B string:

Play this=Tune this open string
5th fret, low E=A
5th fret, A=D
5th fret, D=G
4th fret, G=B  ← the exception
5th fret, B=high E

Drill · Hunt the note

1. Using the explorer above (or your guitar), find every C between the nut and the 12th fret. Say “C” each time you land on one.
2. Play the 12th fret of the low E, then the open low E. Hear the octave — same note, one “twice as fast.”
3. Pick any note on the low E string and find its octave with the two-strings-two-frets shape.

4 minutes. Do it slowly enough to name each note, not just point at it.

Why this matters: Chords and scales are just shapes that sit on top of this map. If you know where the notes are, a chord shape becomes “oh, those are the notes C–E–G” instead of “fingers go here, I hope.”

Sources: 12-fret octave doubling (equal temperament); standard 5th-fret relative tuning method.
