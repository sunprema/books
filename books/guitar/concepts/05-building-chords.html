Chapter 05 · The Grammar

How Chords Are Built

A chord is just three or more notes played together. The most important kind — the one behind almost every song — is the triad: three notes stacked in thirds. Learn the recipe once and you'll understand every chord you'll ever play.

“Chords aren't shapes you memorize. They're intervals you stack. The shape is just where those notes happen to land on the neck.”

The recipe: root, third, fifth

Start on any note — the root. Stack a third on top, then a fifth on top of that. Those three notes are a triad. Whether it sounds happy or sad depends entirely on which third you use — the one-fret switch from the last chapter:

TriadRecipe (from root)ExampleSound
Major1  + M3 (4)  + P5 (7)C – E – GBright, happy
Minor1  + m3 (3)  + P5 (7)A – C – EDark, sad

That's the whole secret. A C major chord is the notes C, E, and G — no matter where or how you play it. Move the middle note (the third) down one fret and C major becomes C minor. The fifth just sits there adding strength.

Triad builder — click a root, toggle major/minor
Pick a root note on the wheel. The root glows red, the third gold, the fifth teal — and you'll hear the chord.

Switch between Major and Minor and click the same root twice. Only the middle note (the third) moves — one slice — and the whole mood flips.

Why your open chords contain what they contain

Every open chord you're about to learn is just a triad — the same three note-names repeated across six strings so it rings full. Here's what's actually inside them:

ChordNotes (1–3–5)Type
CC – E – GMajor
GG – B – DMajor
DD – F♯ – AMajor
EE – G♯ – BMajor
AA – C♯ – EMajor
EmE – G – BMinor
AmA – C – EMinor
DmD – F – AMinor

Notice E major and E minor share E and B — only the third differs (G♯ vs G). Same for A/Am and D/Dm. That single note is doing all the emotional work.

Beyond triads Stack one more third on top and you get a seventh chord (like G7 or Am7) — four notes, a richer, more restless flavor used constantly in blues and folk. Same recipe, one more scoop.

Drill · Build it, then find it

1. On the triad builder, make C major (root C, Major). Say its notes: C–E–G.
2. Switch to Minor on the same root: C–E♭–G. Hear the drop.
3. Build A minor (A–C–E). Then flip to A major (A–C♯–E) and hear the third rise.
4. Grab your guitar and play the open C and Am chords — you're now playing the exact notes you just built.

4 minutes. Name the three notes every time — that's what makes it stick.

Why this matters: When you know a chord is “root, third, fifth,” you can spot wrong notes, build chords anywhere on the neck, and understand why some chords belong together in a song (Chapter 09). Shapes are memory; recipes are understanding.

Sources: triad construction (root/3rd/5th), major = 4+3 semitones, minor = 3+4 semitones; open-chord note spellings in standard tuning.
