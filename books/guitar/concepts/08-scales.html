Chapter 08 · The Grammar

Scales — Pathways, Not Boxes

A scale is a chosen set of notes that sound at home together — the palette a song paints from. Chords are built from scales; melodies and solos walk along them. Two scales will carry you a long way: the major scale and the minor pentatonic.

“Don't think of a scale as a box to trap your fingers. Think of it as a path across the neck. Boxes get memorized and forgotten; paths get travelled.”

The major scale: a recipe of steps

The major scale is seven notes built from one fixed pattern of whole (W) and half (H) steps:

W   W   H   W   W   W   H

Start on C and follow it — C (W) D (W) E (H) F (W) G (W) A (W) B (H) C — and you get C–D–E–F–G–A–B, the “do re mi” sound, with no sharps or flats. Those seven notes are numbered 1–7 (the scale degrees), and that numbering is what makes chords and keys click together in the next chapter.

C major across the neck — click notes, or play the scale
Red = the root (C). Teal = the other scale tones. Click any to hear it.

The same seven notes repeat all over the fretboard. A “key” isn't a place on the neck — it's this set of notes, everywhere at once.

The minor pentatonic: five notes that always work

Pentatonic means five notes. The minor pentatonic drops the two trickiest notes of the minor scale, leaving five that sound good over almost anything — which is why it's the scale for your first solos and blues licks. Its recipe from the root:

Root  ·  ♭3rd  ·  4th  ·  5th  ·  ♭7th   (semitones 0–3–5–7–10)

A minor pentatonic = A–C–D–E–G. On the guitar it forms one compact box you can slide anywhere. The classic first shape sits at the 5th fret — look for the block of teal dots between frets 5 and 8 below, with the red A's as your anchors:

A minor pentatonic — the box at the 5th fret
Click the dots between frets 5–8 in order to walk the box. Red dots are the root, A.

Two notes per string, a tidy rectangle. Learn it once here and you can move the whole shape up or down to play in any key — that's the payoff of a pattern.

Slide it to change key Move the same box up so the root lands on a different note and you've changed key with zero new shapes. Root on the 5th-fret low E (A) → A minor pentatonic. Slide it to the 8th fret (root C) → C minor pentatonic. One shape, twelve keys.

Practice scales with a metronome

Scales are where timing and finger control are forged. Play one note per click, slowly, evenly, ascending then descending. Speed is a by-product of clean repetition — never the goal.

Drill · Walk the box

1. Set a slow click (~60 BPM). Play the A minor pentatonic box, one note per beat, low string to high and back.
2. Say “A” out loud every time you hit a root (red dot). Know where home is.
3. When it's clean and even, slide the whole shape up two frets and play it there. Same fingers, new key.

7 minutes. If it's not clean, it's too fast. Slow is smooth; smooth becomes fast.

Why this matters: The pentatonic box connects everything — fretboard knowledge, timing, and your first improvised solos — into one moveable shape. It's the most reused pattern in popular music.

Sources: major scale W-W-H-W-W-W-H; minor pentatonic degrees 1–♭3–4–5–♭7, A-minor-pentatonic pattern 1 at the 5th fret (Guitar Lesson World, JustinGuitar).
