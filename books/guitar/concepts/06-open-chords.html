Chapter 06 · Playing

Your First Open Chords

Now we make music. Open chords use open strings ringing alongside fretted notes — big, warm, and the backbone of countless songs. Seven of them unlock thousands of tunes. Every diagram here is playable: click it to hear it.

How to read a chord box

The legend Vertical lines are strings — low E on the left, high e on the right. Horizontal lines are frets; the thick bar at top is the nut. A dot is a finger, and the number in it is which finger: 1=index, 2=middle, 3=ring, 4=pinky. Above the nut, O = play this string open, × = don't play it.
Your seven starter chords — click any to strum it

Click a diagram to hear it strummed low-to-high. These seven cover a huge share of pop, folk, and rock.

Learn them in this order

  • Em — start here. Two fingers, every string rings, nothing to mute. The easiest full chord on the guitar.
  • Am — same hand shape as E major, moved over one string. Don't play the low E.
  • C — a gentle stretch; ring finger reaches to the 5th string, 3rd fret. Don't play the low E.
  • G — spreads across all six strings; big and bright.
  • D — a compact triangle on the top four strings. Don't play the two thickest strings.
  • E and A — full, ringing majors you'll use constantly, especially for blues and rock.

“Em is the chord I teach first — no muting, no wrong notes lurking. Get one chord to ring perfectly cleanly before you collect ten that all buzz.”

Make every string ring

  • Press with the tips of your fingers, nails short, knuckles arched — a flat finger deadens the string next door.
  • Fret right behind the fret wire, not on top of it and not halfway back. Closer to the fret = less pressure needed, cleaner tone.
  • Press only as hard as you need. Strum, listen, and fix the one string that buzzes — usually a finger that's leaning on it.
Mind the × C, Am, and D don't use all six strings. Strumming a muted string sounds muddy. Aim your strum to start on the chord's lowest played string — the 5th string for C and Am, the 4th for D.

Changing chords: anchors & economy

The hard part isn't holding a chord — it's switching. Two habits fix most of it:

  • Anchor fingers. Moving from C to Am? Your index and middle fingers barely move — keep them planted and just lift the ring finger. Find the fingers two chords share and never lift them.
  • Move as a shape, not one finger at a time. Lift and place all fingers together, like stamping a shape onto the neck.
Chord-change drill — one strum per bar, G → C → D

Press play. It strums once, then gives you a whole bar to form the next chord. Speed up only when every change lands clean and in time. Slow is smooth.

Drill · The one-minute change

1. Set a slow pulse (start at 60 BPM above). Play G, then C, back to G, over and over — one clean strum each.
2. Count how many clean G↔C changes you make in 60 seconds. Write it down.
3. Tomorrow, beat it by one. That's the whole game.

5 minutes. If it sounds messy, you're going too fast. Drop the tempo.

Why this matters: Songs are mostly chord changes, not chords. Smooth, in-time transitions are what separate “learning chords” from “playing a song.” Practice the change, not just the shape.
