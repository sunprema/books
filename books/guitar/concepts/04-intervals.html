Chapter 04 · The Grammar

Intervals

Music isn't about single notes — it's about the distance between them. That distance is called an interval, and it's the reason two notes can sound sweet, sad, or tense. Master a handful of intervals and you can build every chord and scale by ear.

Counting the distance

An interval is measured in half steps — the frets you'd move on one string. Each size has a name. Here are the twelve inside one octave:

Half stepsNameCharacter
1Minor 2nd (m2)Harsh, clashing
2Major 2nd (M2)Stepwise, neutral
3Minor 3rd (m3)Sad / minor
4Major 3rd (M3)Bright / major
5Perfect 4th (P4)Open, strong
6Tritone (TT)Unstable, wants to resolve
7Perfect 5th (P5)Rock-solid (power chord)
8Minor 6th (m6)Wistful
9Major 6th (M6)Sweet, warm
10Minor 7th (m7)Bluesy, restless
11Major 7th (M7)Dreamy, jazzy
12Octave (P8)The same note, higher

The three that carry their weight

You don't need all twelve at your fingertips yet. Three do most of the work:

  • The major 3rd (4 half steps) — the sound of happy. Play C and E together. The bright one.
  • The minor 3rd (3 half steps) — the sound of sad. Play C and E♭. One fret lower, a whole different mood.
  • The perfect 5th (7 half steps) — the sound of power. Play C and G. It's so stable it barely has a mood; it just sounds strong. Root + 5th is the rock power chord.
The one-fret switch Major and minor differ by a single half step in the 3rd. That one fret is the emotional switch of nearly all music — and in the next chapter it's exactly what turns a happy chord into a sad one.

Why some intervals sound “nice”

Consonant intervals — the octave, the 5th, the 3rds — line up as simple frequency ratios (the octave is 2:1, the perfect 5th nearly 3:2). Their waves reinforce instead of fight, so your ear hears them as restful. The tritone and minor 2nd have messy ratios, so they sound tense and “want” to move somewhere restful. Songs work by trading tension for release.

Drill · Name the feeling

1. In the explorer, play the Major 3rd, then the Minor 3rd. Say “happy” / “sad” out loud. Do it until you can predict which is which before it plays.
2. Play the Perfect 5th, then the Tritone right next to it. Feel how one is solid and the other is itchy.
3. On the guitar: root on the low E (3rd fret, G), then reach the 5th two strings over, two frets up (D string, 5th fret) — that's a power chord.

3 minutes of listening > 30 minutes of reading. Trust your ear.

Why this matters: Chords are just stacks of intervals; scales are ladders of them. Recognize a 3rd and a 5th by ear and you can figure out most chords without a chart.

Sources: interval names and semitone counts (standard Western theory); consonant ratios — octave 2:1, perfect fifth ≈ 3:2.
