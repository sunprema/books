Chapter 01 · Foundations

Meet the Guitar

Before notes and theory, you need a friend you can hold and tune. The acoustic guitar is a wooden amplifier: a stretched string vibrates, the body sings it louder. Learn its parts, learn to tune it, and everything else has a place to land.

“You don't play the strings. You play the air the strings move. Learn the machine that moves it.”

The parts that matter

The string runs from a tuner over the nut, down the neck, across the soundhole, to the bridge. Turning a tuner tightens the string and raises its pitch.

How a string becomes a note

A note is just how fast a string vibrates — its frequency, measured in hertz (Hz). Three things set that speed, and you can feel all three on the guitar:

  • Thickness — fatter strings vibrate slower → lower pitch. That's why the low E is the thick one.
  • Tension — turn the tuner tighter → higher pitch. This is tuning.
  • Length — press a fret and you shorten the vibrating part → higher pitch. This is playing.
Reference pitch The whole system is pinned to one agreed note: A = 440 Hz. Every tuner, every app, every orchestra tunes to it. Your open A string is one octave below, at 110 Hz.

Standard tuning: E A D G B E

From the thick 6th string to the thin 1st, the open strings are E A D G B E. Memorize them with a phrase — Eddie Ate Dynamite, Good Bye Eddie. Here are their exact pitches:

Interactive tuner — hear the six open strings

Click a string to hear its target pitch. Match your guitar to it by ear, turning the tuner slowly until the two tones stop “beating” against each other.

Holding it so your hands are free

  • Sit up. Rest the waist of the body on your right thigh (right-handed), guitar roughly vertical, neck tilted slightly up.
  • Your fretting hand should not hold the neck up — the guitar balances on your leg and against your body. The hand only frets.
  • Thumb behind the neck, roughly opposite your middle finger. Fingers curl so the tips press the strings — that's why short nails matter on the fretting hand.

Drill · Tune & name

1. Play the tuner's low E, then tune your 6th string to it. Work up to the high E.
2. With eyes closed, pluck a random string and say its name out loud before you check.

2 minutes, once a day. Clean tuning first — a beautifully played chord on an out-of-tune guitar still sounds wrong.

Why this matters: Every lesson after this assumes standard tuning. Naming strings by ear is the seed of ear-training — the skill that eventually lets you learn songs without tabs.
