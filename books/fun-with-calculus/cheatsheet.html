Calculus in a Nutshell

Everything, compressed. If you remember only this page, you remember the working core of the subject.

The 30-second summary The derivative f′(x) is the slope of the curve — the instantaneous rate of change, born as the limit of a shrinking difference quotient. The integral ∫f dx is the area under the curve — a total built from infinitely thin slices. The Fundamental Theorem says they are inverse operations: ∫ab f = F(b)−F(a), where F′=f.

The two definitions

f′(x) = limh→0 f(x+h)−f(x)h   ·   ∫ab f dx = limΔx→0 Σ f(xi) Δx

Gotchas & reminders

Don't
  • Never set h = 0 before cancelling it — simplify the quotient first, then take the limit.
  • Never drop the + C on an indefinite integral.
  • The slope of a product is not the product of the slopes — use the product rule.
  • A series is only trustworthy inside its radius of convergence.
