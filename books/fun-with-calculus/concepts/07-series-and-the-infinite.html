Chapter 07 · Letting the Infinite Back In

Series & the Infinite

I will confess a personal fondness for this chapter, for infinite series were the workshop in which I first learned to build. A curve like sin x or ex seems to demand a special machine to compute. But there is a deeper and stranger truth: every well-behaved function can be built out of the simplest bricks we own — powers of x — added up forever in just the right amounts.

The phenomenon: a polynomial that pretends to be a curve

Polynomials are the tame functions. We can add, multiply, differentiate and integrate them with our eyes closed. Curves like the sine are wild by comparison. So here is the ambition: approximate a wild function by a tame polynomial — and then make the approximation perfect by using infinitely many terms.

Begin at a point, say x=0. Demand that the polynomial match the function there not only in value, but in slope, and in the slope of its slope, and so on for every derivative. Each new matched derivative pins down one more coefficient, and the polynomial clings to the curve over a wider stretch. The result is the Taylor series (about zero, the Maclaurin series):

f(x) = f(0) + f′(0) x + f″(0)2!x2 + f‴(0)3!x3 + ⋯
Read the recipe Each term carries one derivative of f at the centre, divided by a factorial to keep it honest, times the matching power of x. The factorials appear precisely because differentiating xn n times produces n! — the division cancels it, so the term contributes exactly the right derivative and nothing more.

Sine, rebuilt from dust

Apply the recipe to sin x. Its derivatives at zero cycle 0, 1, 0, −1, 0, 1, …, so only the odd powers survive, with alternating sign:

sin x = x − x33! + x55! − x77! + ⋯

Watch it built term by term. With one term the polynomial is just the line y=x — right near the origin, hopeless further out. Add the x³ term and it bends to follow the first dip. Each term you add lets the red polynomial hug the true sine over a wider arc. This is how a calculator "knows" the sine: it sums a handful of these terms.

Investigate · a curve, grown one term at a time

Green is the true sin x; red is the partial sum of the series. Drag the slider: with every added term the fit spreads outward from the centre. The infinite series is the sine — the partial sums are merely how far we have bothered to build.

The exponential, and a bridge to constants

The same recipe on ex is even simpler, because every derivative of ex at zero is 1:

ex = 1 + x + x22! + x33! + x44! + ⋯

Set x=1 and you get a recipe for the number e itself: 1 + 1 + ½ + ⅛ + … ≈ 2.71828. The infinite tames the irrational.

Investigation — the author's own

The binomial series

As a young man I found that (1+x)r could be expanded as an infinite series even when r is a fraction or negative — extending the schoolboy's binomial theorem past whole powers:

(1+x)r = 1 + rx + r(r−1)2!x2 + ⋯

With r=½ this gives a rapid way to compute square roots by hand; it was one of the first tools that convinced me the infinite could be calculated with, not merely contemplated. Series turned calculus from geometry into arithmetic.

A caution about the infinite Not every series converges. Each has a radius within which the sum is trustworthy and beyond which it flies apart. The geometric series 1 + x + x² + … = 1/(1−x) holds only for |x|<1. Respect the boundary; the infinite rewards care and punishes carelessness.

Reflection

We let the infinite back in — not as the shrinking h of the derivative, but as an endless sum — and it handed us the power to construct the transcendental functions from mere powers of x. This is the quiet engine inside every calculator and every physics simulation: replace a hard function by a few terms of its series and compute. Which brings us, finally, out of the study and into the world — to see where all of this machinery is turning, right now, in 2026.

Sources — Taylor's theorem (Brook Taylor, 1715) and Maclaurin series (Colin Maclaurin, 1742); Newton's generalised binomial series (c. 1665); series expansions of sin x, ex, and the geometric series (standard analysis).
