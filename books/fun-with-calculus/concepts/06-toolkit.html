Chapter 06 · Worked Instruments

A Toolkit of Functions

The theorem told us that to integrate is to run differentiation backwards. So a working knowledge of calculus rests on a short list of functions whose derivatives — and therefore whose integrals — you simply know. Here we meet the essential few, and each earns its place by a reason, not by decree.

The function that is its own rate: ex

Ask a strange question. Is there a function that grows exactly as fast as its own current size — where the rate of change equals the value? Money at continuous interest behaves so; so does an unchecked population; so does a cooling body's approach to room temperature. Such a function must satisfy f′(x) = f(x). There is one, and it defines the number e ≈ 2.71828:

d/dx ex = ex   — unchanged by differentiation. Its own rate. Its own integral.
Why e matters everywhere Any process whose growth is proportional to its amount is governed by ex. Radioactive decay, capacitor discharge, drug clearance from the blood, compound interest — all are e in disguise. It is the natural constant of change.

Its mirror: the natural logarithm

The logarithm ln x is the inverse of ex, and its derivative fills a famous gap. The power rule made the antiderivative of xn equal to xn+1/(n+1) — which fails only when n=−1, for then we divide by zero. What, then, integrates 1/x? The logarithm:

d/dx ln x = 1x ,   so  ∫ 1x dx = ln |x| + C.

The rhythms: sine and cosine

Anything that oscillates — a pendulum, a sound wave, an alternating current, the tides — is written with sine and cosine. Their derivatives chase each other in a four-step cycle, which is exactly why they model things that return to where they began.

Do not forget the +C Every antiderivative carries a "+ C". Because a constant has zero slope, adding any constant to F leaves F′=f unchanged — so the antiderivative is a whole family of parallel curves. Only when we take a definite integral and subtract, F(b)−F(a), does the C cancel and vanish.

The reverse chain rule: substitution

The chain rule was our most useful rule for differentiating. Run it backwards and it becomes our most useful rule for integrating — substitution. If you spot, inside an integral, a function and its own derivative, you can collapse the inner function into a single new variable u and integrate simply.

Investigation

Integrate 2x·cos(x²) dx

The inner function is u = x²; its derivative du = 2x dx is sitting right there in the integrand. Substitute and the tangle unknots:

∫ 2x cos(x2) dx = ∫ cos(u) du = sin(u) + C = sin(x2) + C.

Verify by differentiating the answer with the chain rule — you land back on 2x cos(x²). Integration and differentiation, checking each other's work.

The honest truth about integration Differentiation is mechanical — the rules always terminate. Integration is an art: not every function has an antiderivative in elementary form (e−x², the bell curve, is the famous rebel). When symbols fail, we return to Chapter 4 and sum slices numerically — which is exactly what every computer does. The Riemann sum is not a teaching toy; it is the working method when the art runs out.

Reflection

With six derivatives memorised and two reverse rules — undo-the-chain (substitution) and undo-the-product (integration by parts, its natural partner) — you can handle an enormous range of the calculus that appears in science. But knowing the pieces is not the same as feeling the sweep of the idea. In the next chapter we let the infinite back in, and use it not to define derivatives but to build the very functions in this toolkit out of nothing but powers of x.
