Chapter 05 · The Central Necessity

The Fundamental Theorem

We have built two machines from opposite blueprints. The derivative slices a total into its instantaneous rate. The integral sums infinitely many rates back into a total. This chapter proves the most beautiful fact in all of calculus: these two machines are inverses of one another. Differentiation and integration undo each other. Everything before this was preparation for this single sentence.

Follow one moving quantity: the area so far

Fix a rate curve f. Instead of the area over a fixed interval, let the right edge move. Define a new quantity — the accumulation function —

A(x) = ∫ax f(t) dt   — the area under f collected from a up to x.

As x creeps rightward, A(x) grows by swallowing more area. Here is the question that cracks the subject open: how fast does A(x) grow? That is, what is its derivative A′(x)?

The derivation, in one honest step

Push the right edge from x to x+h. The area gained is a thin sliver under the curve — very nearly a rectangle of width h and height f(x). So the extra area is about f(x)·h, and the rate of accumulation is

A′(x) = limh→0 A(x+h) − A(x) h = limh→0 f(x)·h h = f(x).
The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — Part I The rate at which area accumulates under a curve is exactly the height of the curve: A′(x) = f(x). Differentiating an integral gives you back the thing you integrated. The two operations are inverse.

Watch it happen. In the diagram, the top panel is the rate f with its area shaded up to a moving line; the bottom panel traces the accumulated area A(x). At every instant, the slope of the bottom curve equals the height of the top curve.

The consequence that changes everything

Turn Part I around. If accumulating area is the inverse of differentiating, then to find an area we need only run differentiation backwards — find a function whose derivative is f. Such a function is called an antiderivative, written F (so that F′=f). Then the whole area is just the change in F across the interval:

∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a)
Fundamental Theorem — Part II No infinite sum, no shrinking rectangles at run-time. To find the area under a curve, find its antiderivative and subtract its values at the two ends. A problem that looked like adding up infinitely many things becomes two evaluations and a subtraction.

The area we filled with rectangles, now in three symbols

In Chapter 4 we crept up on the area under f(x)=½x² from 0 to 3 with dozens of rectangles, and it approached 4.5. Now do it exactly. An antiderivative of ½x² is F(x)=x³/6 (check: F′ = 3x²/6 = ½x² ✓). Therefore

Exactly the number the rectangles were straining toward — reached with no limit, no summation, no labour. This is the power the theorem hands you.

Reflection

Two questions born from opposite instincts — the steepness of a curve, the area beneath it — turned out to be one question asked in two directions. The key necessity was the accumulation function: the moment we let the area's edge move and asked for its rate, the curve's own height answered. Differentiation and integration are a matched pair of doors, and this theorem is the hinge. With it, the rest of calculus is technique — knowing the antiderivatives of the common functions, which is exactly what we gather next.

Sources — the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Parts I & II (foreshadowed by Isaac Barrow; established by Newton and Leibniz, 1660s–1680s); the evaluation theorem ∫ab f = F(b)−F(a) (standard calculus).
