Chapter 04 · The Opposite Question

Accumulation — The Integral

So far we have asked, of a changing quantity, "how fast?" Now we ask the reverse. A car's speedometer shows its rate at every instant; from that alone, can we recover the distance it travelled? To go from a rate back to a total is to accumulate — and it will lead us, by a road that looks utterly different, to the same country as the derivative.

The phenomenon: a total built from rates

Suppose a tank fills at a rate that changes minute by minute. Watch only the inflow gauge. How much water is in the tank after ten minutes? If the rate were constant — say 3 litres a minute — the answer is a schoolchild's: rate times time, 3×10 = 30 litres. That product is an area: a rectangle 3 tall and 10 wide.

Hold that thought — a total accumulated from a rate is the area under the rate's graph. It is true when the rate is constant and, as we shall force it to be, true when it is not.

Naive attempt, and the fix

When the rate varies, there is no single rectangle. But there is a cunning escape. Chop the time into many thin slices. Over each thin slice the rate barely changes, so treat it as constant there — one skinny rectangle, rate×(slice width). Add up all the skinny rectangles and you have an approximate total. This is a Riemann sum:

total ≈ Σi f(xi) · Δx   (height × width, summed over all slices)

The thinner the slices, the better the fit — the staircase of rectangles hugs the true curve ever more closely. Drag the slider and see the sum settle on the true area.

Investigate · fill the area with rectangles

The region under f(x)=½x² from 0 to 3. With few rectangles the sum is crude; push n up and it converges on the exact area 4.5. "Midpoint" converges fastest — a hint that how you sample matters.

Emergence of the symbol

Now take the limit we are no longer afraid of: let the slice width shrink to nothing and the number of slices grow without bound. The sum Σ stops being an approximation and becomes the exact area. Leibniz stretched the letter S for summa — a sum — into a tall, elegant stroke, and we write the definite integral:

∫ab f(x) dx = limΔx→0 Σ f(xi) Δx
Read the notation aloud "The integral, from a to b, of f(x), dee-x." The ∫ is the frozen sum; the dx is the ghost of the vanishing slice-width — the same infinitesimal dx that sat under the derivative. That is not a coincidence, and Chapter 5 is about why.

An integral need not be an area on paper

Because "height × width, summed and taken to the limit" is such a general act, integrals measure far more than flat areas:

  • Integrate velocity over time → distance travelled.
  • Integrate power over time → energy delivered.
  • Integrate force over distance → work done.
  • Integrate a cross-sectional area along a length → a volume.

Building a solid from slices

That last one is worth seeing in three dimensions, for it shows the summing-of-slices at its most vivid. Take a curve and spin the region beneath it around an axis. It sweeps out a solid — a vase, a horn, a lens. Each thin vertical slice becomes a thin disc of radius f(x) and thickness dx, so of volume π f(x)² dx. Sum the discs — integrate — and you have the whole volume:

V = ∫ab π f(x)2 dx
The one idea behind every integral Cut the thing into slices so thin that each is simple, measure each simple slice, add them all, and take the slices to zero width. Area, distance, energy, volume, probability, the centre of mass of a bridge — all are this single act with a different quantity in the slice.

Reflection

The derivative took a total and extracted its rate. The integral takes a rate and rebuilds the total — by summing infinitely many infinitesimal contributions. Two questions, seemingly opposite: one about tangents and slopes, the other about areas and sums. It would be astonishing if they were secretly the same operation, run forwards and backwards. Astonishing — and true. Turn the page.

Sources — the Riemann integral as a limit of sums (Bernhard Riemann, 1854); Leibniz's integral sign as an elongated "S" for summa (1675); the disc method for solids of revolution, V=π∫ f(x)² dx (standard integral calculus).
