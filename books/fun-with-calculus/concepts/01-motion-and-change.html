Chapter 01 · The Opening Phenomenon

Motion & Change

Let us begin not with a formula, but with a stone. I hold it at arm's length and open my hand. It falls. In less than a heartbeat it strikes the ground. Something has happened that no still picture can capture: the stone was changing its place, and — this is the mystery — it was changing that place ever faster.

Let us suppose we know nothing. No rules, no symbols. Only a moving thing, and a wish to describe it honestly.

The central question

We can measure where the stone is at any instant. Drop it, and careful observation shows that after a time t it has fallen a distance

s(t) = 12 g t2   (with g ≈ 9.8 m/s2)

That tells us the where. But watch the falling stone and a harder question forces itself upon you — one about the how fast:

The question this whole book answers How fast is the stone moving at one particular instant — not on the average, but right now, at the exact moment it passes my knee?

It sounds simple. It is not. And feeling why it is not is the first real step.

Investigate · a falling stone

The green curve is distance-fallen against time. The dashed red line is the average speed over a small window that shrinks as the stone falls — already it strains to become a single, instantaneous number.

Naive attempts, and where they break

Here is the natural first idea. Speed is distance over time. The stone falls for 2 seconds and covers about 19.6 metres, so its speed is

19.6 m2 s = 9.8 m/s.

True — but only as an average. The stone did not move at 9.8 m/s the whole way. It began at rest and ended much faster. This number is the speed of no single instant. It is like describing a whole day's weather with one temperature.

So we sharpen the idea. To learn the speed near the instant t, do not use the whole fall — use a short interval beginning at t and lasting a little while h. In that interval the stone moves from s(t) to s(t+h), and so

average speed over h = s(t+h) − s(t) h

This ratio — change in distance over change in time — is the quiet hero of the whole subject. We shall meet it again and again. Look at it closely: it is the slope of the straight line joining two points on the distance curve. Now the decisive move.

The dangerous idea: let the interval vanish

If a short interval gives a better speed than the whole fall, a shorter one gives a better still. So we shrink h. We make the window smaller, and smaller, and — here is where the ground trembles — we ask what happens as h approaches zero.

Feel the discomfort We cannot simply set h = 0: that would make the ratio 0/0, which is nonsense. Yet the closer h creeps toward zero, the more the ratio seems to settle on a single, definite number. This tension — never arriving, yet clearly heading somewhere — is a sign we are near something important.

Let us not argue about it. Let us watch it. Below, the ratio is drawn as the slope of the line through two points on a curve. Drag the slider to shrink h, or press Animate, and read what number the slope approaches.

What we are forced to conclude

The ratio does not fly off to infinity, nor collapse to zero. It homes in on a number. At the instant t = 1 second, the slope settles on exactly 1 for this curve; for the true fall, on g t = 9.8 m/s. That limiting number — the value the average speed approaches as the interval vanishes — is what we shall be bold enough to call the instantaneous rate of change.

We have not yet given it a symbol. We have earned it, and that is enough for one chapter. Notice what we did: we took a thing that changes, trapped it between two nearby measurements, and squeezed the gap to nothing. Whatever survived that squeeze is the truth of the instant.

Investigation

The same idea, wearing other clothes

This "rate of change at an instant" is not about stones only. It is the shape of a hundred questions:

  • A population grows. How fast is it growing this year?
  • Water fills a vase. How quickly is the level rising at this height?
  • A hillside climbs. How steep is it right where I stand?

Each is the same act: a small change divided by the small cause of it, with the smallness taken to the limit. Master the stone and you have mastered them all.

Reflection

We began wanting the speed of an instant and discovered we could not have it directly — only as the destination of a shrinking average. The key necessity was this: an instant has no duration, so a rate at an instant can only be defined as a limit. Hold onto that. In the next chapter we give this limiting slope a name and a symbol, and the moment we do, the falling stone will hand us a machine for finding the rate of anything.

Sources — Galileo's law of falling bodies, s = ½gt² (Galileo, Two New Sciences, 1638); Newton's method of first and last ratios, Principia (1687), Book I, Lemma I.
