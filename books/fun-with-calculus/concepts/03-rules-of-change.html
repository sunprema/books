Chapter 03 · First Consequences

The Rules of Change

We have one hard-won rule and a definition that can, in principle, differentiate anything. But grinding every function through the limit by hand would be a tyranny. Let us instead pay the cost of the limit once for each pattern, and pocket the result as a rule. A handful of these will let you differentiate almost any expression on sight.

The power rule — pay once, use forever

We found the slope of x² is 2x. Try x³ the same way — expand (x+h)³, subtract x³, divide by h, and let h vanish. You are left with 3x². A pattern announces itself:

if  f(x) = xn,   then  f′(x) = n xn−1.

Bring the exponent down to the front as a multiplier, then step the exponent down by one. That is the whole of it. It holds not only for whole numbers but for fractions and negatives too — √x = x1/2 differentiates to ½x−1/2.

The easy two: constants and sums

Two rules follow so directly from the definition that they scarcely need proof — but let us see why they must be true.

Constant multiple Stretching a curve vertically by a factor c stretches every slope by the same c. So (c·f)′ = c·f′. The ½g in the falling stone simply rode along — this is why.
Sum rule If you add two changing quantities, their rates of change add too: (f+g)′ = f′ + g′. Change is additive. Differentiate a polynomial term by term and be done.
Investigation

A whole polynomial in one pass

f(x) = 3x4 − 5x2 + 2x − 9

Term by term, using power + constant-multiple + sum:

f′(x) = 12x3 − 10x + 2

The lone −9 contributed nothing — a constant never changes, so its rate is zero. Every polynomial you will ever meet yields this easily.

The product rule — a subtlety worth feeling

You might guess that the slope of a product is the product of the slopes. It is not, and it is worth seeing why in pictures. Think of a rectangle whose width u and height v are both growing. Its area is uv. In a small time the width grows by du and the height by dv, adding two thin strips of area — one of size v du, one of size u dv (the tiny corner du dv is negligibly small). Hence:

(u v)′ = u′v + u v′

The quotient rule

Division is subtler still, but it follows from the product rule with a little algebra. Keep it as a recipe:

( uv )′ = u′v − u v′ v2

Low times d-high, minus high times d-low, over the square of what's below. Sing it once and it stays.

The chain rule — the most useful rule of all

Most real quantities depend on something, which depends on something else. The temperature of an engine depends on the fuel rate, which depends on the throttle. When functions are nested, y = f(g(x)), their rates multiply. If a small step in x nudges g at rate g′, and a small step in g nudges f at rate f′, then the total effect on y is the product:

dydx = dydg · dgdx   — the inner differentials cancel, as fractions ought to.
Why you will meet this rule every day The chain rule is how every neural network learns. "Back-propagation" is nothing but the chain rule applied through many nested layers, multiplying rates from the output back to each weight. We return to this in Chapter 8 — but the rule is right here, and it is elementary.
Investigation

Differentiate (3x² + 1)⁵

Outer function: something5, whose derivative is 5·something4. Inner function: 3x²+1, whose derivative is 6x. Multiply:

d/dx (3x2+1)5 = 5(3x2+1)4 · 6x = 30x(3x2+1)4.

Outer derivative, then inner derivative, then multiply. Never expand the fifth power by hand again.

Reflection

We traded one hard method — the limit — for a purse of quick rules, each bought with a single limit and kept forever. Power, constant, sum, product, quotient, chain: with these six you can differentiate the overwhelming majority of functions in this book without ever writing "lim" again. Next we turn the whole machine around and ask the reverse question — not "how fast is it changing?" but "given the rate, how much has accumulated?"

Sources — standard differentiation rules (power, product, quotient, chain), derivable from the limit definition; the chain rule as the basis of back-propagation (Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams, 1986; NVIDIA, "Gradient Descent and Backpropagation").
