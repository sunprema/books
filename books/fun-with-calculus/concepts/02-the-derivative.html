Chapter 02 · Emergence of Notation

The Derivative

In the last chapter we cornered a number: the value that the average rate of change approaches as the interval shrinks to nothing. We watched it appear on the screen. Now it has earned a name and a symbol — and once named, it becomes a tool we can wield on any curve, not just a falling stone.

Naming the limit

Take any quantity y that depends on x — write it y = f(x). The average rate of change from x to x+h is the ratio we already trust:

f(x+h) − f(x) h

The instantaneous rate is what this becomes as h approaches zero. We give the act of approaching a name — the limit — and we give the result a name: the derivative of f. Here, at last, is the definition on which everything rests:

f′(x) = limh→0 f(x+h) − f(x) h
Two names, one idea I write it f′(x) — "f-prime" — because it is a new function derived from the old. Leibniz, thinking in tiny differences, writes the same thing dy/dx — a ratio of an infinitesimal rise dy to an infinitesimal run dx. In my own work I called the rate a fluxion and wrote ẏ. Three notations, one meaning: the slope of the curve at a point.

The geometry: a line that grazes

Look again at the shrinking chord from Chapter 1. Two points on a curve define a straight line — a secant. As the second point slides toward the first, the secant pivots. In the limit the two points merge, and the line no longer cuts across the curve; it merely touches it. That limiting line is the tangent, and the derivative f′(x) is exactly its slope.

Deriving our first rule, honestly

Let us not simply assert that the slope of x² is 2x. Let us force it out of the definition, so you never have to take my word for it. Put f(x)=x² into the difference quotient:

(x+h)2 − x2 h = x2 + 2xh + h2 − x2 h = 2xh + h2 h = 2x + h.

Now the miracle, and it is an honest one. Before the limit we could not divide by h if it were zero. But the algebra let us cancel one factor of h first — leaving 2x + h, a perfectly tame expression. Now we let h go to zero without any division by zero, and the second term simply vanishes:

f′(x) = limh→0 (2x + h) = 2x.
What just happened This is the whole method of the calculus in miniature: expand, cancel the troublesome h, then let what remains of it fall away. Every differentiation rule in the next chapter is this same manoeuvre, done once and remembered so you never repeat the labour.

The derivative is itself a function

Here is the leap that makes calculus powerful rather than merely clever. We did not find the slope at one point. We found f′(x)=2x — a formula for the slope at every point at once. Feed it any x and it returns the steepness there. The derivative of a function is a new function: the slope-reporting machine of the original.

Green is f; the thin red curve is its derivative f′ — the height of red at each x is the slope of green there. Hover to read both. Where green is flat, red crosses zero; where green rises steeply, red is high.

Reading a derivative like a diagnosis

  • Sign — where f′>0 the quantity is increasing; where f′<0 it is decreasing.
  • Zero — where f′=0 the curve is momentarily level: a hilltop, a valley, or a pause. (We hunt these in Chapter 8.)
  • Size — a large |f′| means rapid change; a small one, gentle change.
Investigation

The velocity of the falling stone, at last

The stone's position was s(t)=½gt². Its velocity is the derivative s′(t). Using the very rule we just derived (and a constant factor ½g that simply rides along):

s′(t) = 12g · 2t = g t.

At t=2 s the speed is 9.8×2 = 19.6 m/s — twice the average over the whole fall, exactly as the moving stone told us it should be. The instant and the formula agree.

Reflection

The necessity that created the derivative was the impossibility of a rate at a durationless instant, resolved by a limit. Its gift is a function that reports the slope everywhere. We have one rule so far, wrung from the definition by hand. In the next chapter we harvest a small orchard of them — so that we may differentiate almost anything on sight, and never again divide by a shrinking h unless we wish to.

Sources — the limit definition of the derivative (standard analysis; Cauchy, 1820s, made the limit rigorous); Newton's fluxions and Leibniz's dy/dx notation, developed independently in the 1670s–1680s.
