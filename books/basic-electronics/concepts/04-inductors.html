Inductors

An inductor is just a coil of wire. Current through a coil builds a magnetic field, and that field pushes back on any change in the current — the exact mirror image of how a capacitor resists any change in voltage.

Faraday's law, in one line

The defining equation VL = L × (di/dt) — the voltage across an inductor is proportional to how fast its current is changing, not to the current itself. Steady DC current through an inductor drops no voltage at all (once settled); only a change in current produces a kick.

That's why unplugging something with a coil in it (a motor, a relay) can produce a spark — the sudden drop in current makes di/dt huge for an instant, and the inductor fights back with a large voltage spike.

Pair it with a capacitor: the LC tank

A capacitor stores energy in an electric field; an inductor stores it in a magnetic field. Wire them together in a loop and energy sloshes back and forth between the two, at a natural resonant frequency:

Resonant frequency f = 1 / (2π√(LC)) — a bigger inductor or a bigger capacitor means a lower resonant frequency.
Live LC oscillation

Worked example

L = 1 mH, C = 100 nF. f = 1 / (2π√(0.001 × 0.0000001)) ≈ 16 kHz.

Why this matters: This is precisely the tuning circuit at the front of every radio (Chapter 08) — a variable capacitor changes C, which changes f, which selects which station's frequency the circuit resonates with (and amplifies) while ignoring the rest.
