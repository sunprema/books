Voltage, Current & the Simple Circuit

Every component in this book does one thing: it shapes the relationship between voltage and current. Get comfortable with those two words and Ohm's law, and every later page is just a variation on the same idea.

Voltage is the push, current is the flow

Voltage (V, volts) is electrical pressure — the difference in potential that pushes charge around a circuit. Current (I, amps) is the actual flow of charge past a point, per second. Think of a water tank on a hill: voltage is the water pressure at the tap, current is how fast the water actually comes out — and that depends on how wide the pipe is.

The one equation Ohm's law: V = I × R. Voltage equals current times resistance. Rearranged, I = V / R — a narrower pipe (bigger R) means less flow (I) for the same push (V).

Series and parallel, in one line each

  • Series (one loop, one path): the same current flows through every component; their resistances simply add.
  • Parallel (branches side by side): the same voltage appears across every branch; current splits between them.

Worked example

A 9 V battery drives a 300 Ω resistor. What's the current?
I = V/R = 9 / 300 = 0.03 A (30 mA).

Why this matters: Every component from here on — resistor, capacitor, inductor, diode — is described by how it bends this V–I relationship. A resistor keeps it a straight, boring line; the rest of this book is what happens once that line stops being straight.

Sources: Ohm's law, V = IR (standard circuit theory).
