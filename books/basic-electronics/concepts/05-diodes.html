Chapter 05 · Semiconductors

The Diode

Every component so far has treated current the same in both directions. A diode doesn't — it's built to pass current one way and block it the other. That asymmetry is the entire idea behind semiconductors.

A junction, not a substance

Fuse a p-type semiconductor (extra positive "holes") against an n-type semiconductor (extra electrons) and, right at the junction, charge diffuses across and leaves a thin depletion region with its own built-in electric field — a barrier.

BiasWhat happens at the junctionResult
Forward (+ to p-side)Barrier collapses once forward voltage is exceededCurrent flows freely
Reverse (+ to n-side)Depletion region widensOnly a tiny leakage current
Typical forward voltage ≈0.7 V for silicon diodes, ≈0.3 V for germanium. Below that, essentially no current flows forward either.
Live bias flip

The classic use: rectification

Feed an AC signal (which swings positive and negative) through a diode and only the half that matches its forward direction gets through — the rest is chopped off. That's a rectifier, and it's also exactly how a radio pulls audio back out of a radio-frequency carrier (Chapter 08).

Not a perfect valve Push reverse voltage far enough and every diode eventually breaks down and conducts hard in reverse. Some diodes (Zeners) are deliberately built to do this at a precise, useful voltage.
