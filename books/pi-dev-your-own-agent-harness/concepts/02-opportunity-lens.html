Chapter 02 · A Reusable Way to See

The Opportunity Lens

Before we tour five domains, here is the instrument we tour them with. A generic chatbot is a commodity; the model providers already own that. The buildable business always lives in the gap between a general model and a specific job — and a reshapeable harness is a machine for closing exactly that gap. The Lens is five questions you point at any vertical. Where a domain answers "yes, and it's painful," there is an opening.

The five questions

Each yes has a Pi answer

The questionWhat it costs a big vendorThe Pi capability
Custom tools? Does the job need to touch a system of record no chatbot knows?A bespoke integration nobody prioritizesAn extension that adds a tool — full system access

Scoring an opening in ten seconds

The strongest openings light up three or more of the five, and sit in a domain where being wrong is costly enough that buyers pay for control. That second half matters: high stakes are usually treated as a reason to avoid AI. Invert it. High stakes are why a generic assistant is unacceptable and a governed, private, domain-shaped one is worth real money.

Here's the opening

The reshapeable harness lets one domain expert assemble tools + private data + memory + guardrails into a product that a foundation-model company will never ship, because it's too specific to be their business. Specificity is your moat. The narrower the vertical, the safer you are from being flattened by the next model release.

Where the risk hides (read this before you get excited)

  • The four tools are powerful. Bash plus full-access extensions means an agent can do real damage. In regulated domains the guardrail is the product, not a footnote — chapter 9 is entirely about this.
  • BYOK is a stance, not a compliance certificate. It keeps the data path yours, but you still own the deployment, the sandbox, and the paperwork.
  • "Reshapeable" cuts both ways. The same flexibility that lets you build fast lets you build something ungoverned. Discipline is the differentiator.

With the Lens in hand, the next five chapters each pick a domain, run the five questions, and name the specific plays — and the specific ways to get burned. We start where the appetite for private, auditable AI is highest and the budgets are deepest: finance.
