Chapter 08 · From Idea to Product

Shipping a Vertical: Packages, SDK & the Moat

Five domains, dozens of plays — but a play is not a business until it ships and defends itself. This chapter is the how: the two ways Pi lets you package and embed a vertical product, the BYOK economics that make the unit math work, and the reason a foundation model can't simply absorb what you built. The short version: your moat is never the model. It's your tools, your data, and your workflow — welded together by a harness.

Pi Packages: the distribution unit

A vertical product on Pi ships as a Pi Package — the four layers from chapter 1 (extensions, skills, prompt templates, themes) bundled and distributed over npm or git. That's the entire go-to-market surface: a customer installs your package and gets your tools, your task recipes, your house voice, and your branded interface, running on their own harness and their own keys. No platform to host, no per-seat SaaS you have to operate — the distribution rails already exist and every developer already trusts them.

SDK & RPC: embedding the loop in your app

If you'd rather ship a conventional application than a package, use the SDK to embed the agent loop directly inside your product, or RPC mode to run the harness as a service your backend drives. Either way you're building on pi-agent-core — the loop, tool calling, and state — instead of reinventing an agent runtime. Your app owns the UX, the accounts, the billing; the harness is the engine under the hood.

PShip a Pi PackageBest when your buyer is technical and runs their own harness. Zero infra to operate; npm/git distribution.
SEmbed via SDK / RPCBest when you want a polished app with your own accounts & billing. The loop lives inside your product.

BYOK economics: whose bill is it anyway?

BYOK quietly fixes the worst problem in AI-product economics. Because the customer brings their own model key, they pay for inference directly — the volatile, usage-scaling cost isn't on your P&L. You're not reselling tokens at a markup and praying the model price holds; you're selling the tools, workflow, and support around the customer's own model relationship. That turns a scary variable-cost business into a cleaner software-margin one, and it's a compliance story too (chapter 3/4): the data path is the customer's, not yours.

Model-agnostic by construction

Because pi-ai is a unified multi-provider API, your product isn't welded to one lab. When a better or cheaper model lands, your customers switch keys and you ship a config change — not a rewrite. You get the upside of every model release without betting the company on any single provider.

The moat, stated plainly

Every few months a new model makes some startup's core feature free. The ones who survive built something the model doesn't contain. On Pi, that something is concrete and defensible:

  • Your tools — integrations into systems of record you did the unglamorous work to reach.
  • Your data — the private corpus your RAG retrieves and your memory accrues, which grows stickier with every customer session.
  • Your workflow — the skills and prompt templates that encode how the job is actually done, tuned by real users.

None of that arrives in the next model release. A smarter base model makes your product better, not obsolete — it's a rising tide under your boat, not a wave over it.

The opening

The reshapeable harness collapses the cost of shipping a vertical AI product to a small team's reach, while BYOK removes the inference-cost risk and hands you a compliance narrative for free. Pick a narrow, painful, high-value job; weld tools + data + workflow around it; ship it as a Package or embed it via the SDK. The narrowness that makes it un-sexy to a foundation lab is exactly what makes it yours.
