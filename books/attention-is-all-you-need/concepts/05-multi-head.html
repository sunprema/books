Chapter 05Multi-Head Attention

“If I have seen further, it is by standing on the shoulders of Giants.” — Isaac Newton

One attention head is a single point of view. Language needs several at once. Multi-head attention is the paper's answer, and it costs almost nothing extra — a genuinely elegant trick.

The problem with one head

A single head produces one set of attention weights per word — one blend. But consider the word "made" in "she made the argument more difficult." To represent it well, the model might want to track the subject ("she"), the object ("argument"), and the completion of the phrase ("more difficult") simultaneously. Forced into a single blend, these competing pulls average together and smear. As the paper puts it: with a single head, "averaging inhibits this."

The Key Idea

Run several attention heads in parallel, each with its own learned view of what to look for. One head can chase syntax, another can resolve pronouns, another can track long-range phrase completion. Then concatenate their answers. The model attends to "different representation subspaces at different positions" all at once.

How it works: project, attend, concatenate

Instead of doing one attention over the full 512-dimensional vectors, we linearly project Q, K, and V down to smaller pieces — h = 8 times, with different learned matrices each time. Each head works in dk = dv = 512 / 8 = 64 dimensions. Each runs the scaled dot-product attention from Chapter 4 independently. Then we concatenate the eight 64-wide outputs back into 512, and pass that through one final projection WO.

MultiHead(Q,K,V) = Concat(head1, …, head8) WO
where  headi = Attention(QWiQ, KWiK, VWiV)

The elegant part: it's nearly free

You might expect eight heads to cost eight times as much. They don't. Each head works in 1/8 the width (64 instead of 512), so the total computation is "similar to that of single-head attention with full dimensionality." You get eight points of view for roughly the price of one. The paper found that too few heads hurt quality — but so did too many; eight was the sweet spot for the base model.

From the Bench

This is a recurring theme in good systems design: instead of one big general-purpose mechanism, use many small specialized ones and combine them. The heads aren't told what to specialize in — training discovers it. When researchers later visualized the heads, some had clearly learned to follow grammatical structure, others to resolve "it"/"its" references. Nobody programmed that; it emerged.

So what did we actually gain?

A richer attention layer that can track several kinds of relationship in parallel — the workhorse block of the entire Transformer. But there's still that hole we flagged in Chapter 2: attention treats the sentence as a bag of words. Scramble the input rows and the math gives the same answer, because nothing in Q, K, or V knows a word's position. For a model with no recurrence, that's a crisis. The next chapter is the fix — and it's made of sine waves.

Challenges

  1. With h = 8 and dmodel = 512, each head gets 64 dimensions. If you doubled the heads to 16 while keeping dmodel fixed, what happens to each head's width — and why might that eventually hurt?
  2. Two heads learn to do almost the same thing. Is that wasteful, or could it be useful? Argue both sides.
