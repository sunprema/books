Chapter 09Training & Results

“In God we trust. All others must bring data.” — W. Edwards Deming

A model is only as convincing as its benchmark. Here is how the Transformer was trained — the data, the optimizer, the three tricks that made it stable — and the exact scores that let it claim its title.

Data and batching

The main task was WMT 2014 English–German: about 4.5 million sentence pairs, tokenized with byte-pair encoding into a shared 37,000-token vocabulary. The larger English–French set had 36 million sentences and a 32,000 word-piece vocabulary. Rather than batching by a fixed number of sentences, they batched by approximate length — roughly 25,000 source and 25,000 target tokens per batch — so each step did a consistent amount of work.

Hardware and schedule

Everything ran on one machine with 8 NVIDIA P100 GPUs.

The optimizer, with a warmup

They used Adam (β₁ = 0.9, β₂ = 0.98, ε = 10⁻⁹) with a hand-shaped learning-rate schedule that has since become iconic:

lrate = dmodel−0.5 · min( step−0.5,   step · warmup−1.5 )

In plain terms: for the first 4,000 steps the learning rate ramps up linearly from near zero (the "warmup"), then decays in proportion to the inverse square root of the step number. The warmup keeps the freshly-initialized, high-variance model from being kicked apart by big early updates; the decay lets it settle as it converges.

Three kinds of regularization

  • Residual dropout (Pdrop = 0.1): dropout on each sub-layer's output before it's added and normalized, and on the sum of embeddings + positional encodings.
  • Label smoothing (εls = 0.1): instead of training the model to predict a target word with 100% confidence, ask for 90% and spread the rest. It hurts perplexity (the model is deliberately less sure) but improves accuracy and BLEU.
  • The big model bumped dropout to 0.3 for EN–DE to fight overfitting.
What is BLEU?

BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) scores a machine translation by how much its word-sequences overlap with human reference translations, 0–100. Higher is better; a couple of points is a big, human-noticeable jump. It's imperfect, but it was the common yardstick, so beating it settled arguments.

The big Transformer set a new state of the art on English–German by more than 2 BLEU — beating even prior ensembles — and a new single-model record of 41.8 on English–French, at less than a quarter of the previous best's training cost. Even the base model, at 3.3·10¹⁸ FLOPs, surpassed everything published before it while costing orders of magnitude less to train.

It generalized, too

To check the architecture wasn't a translation-only trick, they threw it at English constituency parsing — a task with long, rigidly structured outputs where RNNs had historically struggled in small-data settings. With almost no task-specific tuning, a 4-layer Transformer scored 91.3 F1 (WSJ-only) and 92.7 semi-supervised, beating most prior models. The mechanism was general.

The Key Idea

Better scores and a fraction of the cost and generalization to a second task. This is why "Attention Is All You Need" didn't just win a benchmark — it reset the field's default architecture.

So what did we actually gain?

Proof. The paper's dare paid off in hard numbers. And yet the numbers above are almost the least interesting part of the story now — because what happened after 2017 dwarfs the translation results. The final content chapter follows the thread from this paper to the models you use every day.

Challenges
