Chapter 10From Transformer to Modern LLMs

“The best way to predict the future is to invent it.” — Alan Kay

You opened this book already knowing what a large language model does. This chapter closes the loop: how the machine we just built became GPT, BERT, and every model you've typed a prompt into. The paper solved translation; the field noticed it had accidentally built something far more general.

The pivot: pretraining on all the text there is

The Transformer's first life was supervised translation: sentence-pairs in, translations out. The insight that lit the fuse was different — you can train the same architecture on a self-supervised objective that needs no labels at all: just predict missing or next words in ordinary text. The internet is an infinite, free training set for that game. Train a big Transformer to play it, and the representations it learns transfer to almost any language task. That recipe — pretrain, then adapt — is the whole modern paradigm.

Three ways to cut the architecture

Chapter 7 gave us two towers. The families of models that followed each kept a different part:

Encoder-only — BERT and the art of understanding

Keep only the encoder. Because it reads the whole sentence at once, it sees context from both directions. BERT (2018) trained it by masking random words and asking the model to fill them in — a "cloze test." The result is a powerhouse for understanding tasks: classification, sentiment, search ranking, and the sentence embeddings that power semantic search. It doesn't generate fluent text; that's not its job.

Decoder-only — GPT and the art of generation

Keep only the decoder, with its causal mask (Chapter 7's "no peeking ahead"). Train it on one relentless objective — predict the next token — over a large corpus. That is the GPT family, and it is the shape of nearly every chat model you use: GPT, Llama, Mistral, Gemini, and the Claude model reading this to you. The mask that started as a correctness detail for translation became the defining feature of generative AI.

The Key Idea

"LLM" almost always means a decoder-only Transformer trained to predict the next token, then aligned to follow instructions. Every layer inside it is the multi-head self-attention + feed-forward block from Chapter 7, stacked dozens of times and scaled to billions of parameters. You already understand the atom; an LLM is just a very large crystal of it.

Why scale changed everything

The 2017 base model had 65 million parameters. Modern frontier models have hundreds of billions. Researchers found scaling laws: make the model, data, and compute bigger together, and quality improves smoothly and predictably. Past certain scales, abilities the model was never explicitly trained for — arithmetic, translation between unseen language pairs, step-by-step reasoning — began to emerge. The Transformer turned out to be the rare architecture that keeps paying off as you pour more into it, which is exactly why the whole industry poured.

What changed, what didn't

Modern LLMs refine the details: learned or rotary positions (RoPE) instead of sinusoids, normalization moved before each sub-layer ("pre-norm"), GELU/SwiGLU activations instead of ReLU, grouped-query attention and FlashAttention to tame that O(n²) cost, and alignment stages (instruction tuning, RLHF) after pretraining. But the beating heart — multi-head self-attention — is unchanged from the equation you took apart in Chapter 4.

So what did we actually gain?

The whole arc, end to end. We started with the sequential bottleneck, built attention from a library analogy, assembled it into a translator, and watched that translator's core become the foundation of an entire field. When you next watch an LLM answer, you'll see through the magic: tokens becoming vectors, positions added in, and layer upon layer of every-word-looking-at-every-word. Attention really was all they needed. One chapter remains — a dictionary for every acronym and term we've used, so nothing stays a black box.
