Chapter 06Giving Words a Place

“Time is what keeps everything from happening at once.” — Ray Cummings

Attention is order-blind. It sees a set of words, not a sequence. But "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" are very different news stories. Somehow we must smuggle position back in — without recurrence, and without breaking the parallelism we fought so hard to win.

The problem: attention is a bag of words

Look again at the attention formula. It sums over values weighted by query–key similarity. Addition doesn't care about order: shuffle the rows of the input and you shuffle the rows of the output identically — the relationships computed are unchanged. An RNN got order for free, because it literally walked the sentence left to right. We threw the walk away. So we must hand the model position on a plate.

The Key Idea

Don't change the architecture to reintroduce order — change the input. Add a positional encoding: a vector, unique to each position, summed directly into that word's embedding. Now the very first thing every attention layer sees already carries "I am word 5" baked into its numbers.

The idea: a signature made of sine waves

We need a vector PEpos of width 512 (so it can be added to the embedding) that is distinct for every position and, ideally, makes relative distances easy to compute. The authors' answer is a bank of sine and cosine waves at geometrically spaced frequencies:

PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 100002i/dmodel)
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 100002i/dmodel)

Read it as: dimension 2i of the vector is a sine wave; the next dimension is the matching cosine; and as i climbs from 0 toward 256, the wavelength stretches from short (~2π) to very long (~10000·2π). Each position gets a unique fingerprint across these 512 channels — like a binary counter, but smooth.

Why sinusoids, of all things?

Two reasons the authors give. First, relative position becomes linear: for any fixed offset k, the encoding of pos + k can be written as a fixed linear function (a rotation) of the encoding of pos. That means a head can learn "attend to the word three positions back" as a simple, position-independent operation. Second, it may extrapolate: because the waves are defined for any real input, the scheme extends smoothly to sequences longer than any seen in training.

Fixed vs. learned

The authors also tried learned positional embeddings — a trainable vector per position — and found "nearly identical results." They kept the sinusoids for the extrapolation property. Many later models (including GPT and BERT) went with learned positions anyway, and newer schemes like RoPE and ALiBi refine the idea further. But the principle set here — inject position by addition, up front — is universal.

One practical detail: because the embeddings were scaled up by √dmodel in Chapter 2, they don't get drowned out when the positional signal is added. The two live at comparable magnitudes, and dropout is applied to their sum before the first layer.

So what did we actually gain?

Order, restored — without a single recurrent step. Every token vector now answers two questions at once: "what word am I?" (embedding) and "where do I sit?" (positional encoding). That combined vector is the true input to the Transformer. We now hold every raw ingredient: embeddings, position, scaled dot-product attention, and multi-head attention. Time to assemble them into the actual machine.

Challenges

  1. Positional encodings are added to embeddings, not concatenated. What does the model save by adding, and what capacity might it give up?
  2. The wavelengths span 2π up to about 10000·2π. Why might a mix of very short and very long waves be more useful than making them all the same length?
