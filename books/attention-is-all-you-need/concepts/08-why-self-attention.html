Chapter 08Why Self-Attention Wins

“In theory there is no difference between theory and practice. In practice there is.” — Yogi Berra (probably)

A new mechanism deserves a fair trial. The authors compare self-attention against recurrent and convolutional layers on three yardsticks, and the numbers are the reason the whole field changed direction.

Three things we care about

To map a sequence of n vectors (each of width d) to another sequence of the same shape, the paper asks three questions:

  • Computational complexity per layer. How much total arithmetic?
  • Parallelism — the minimum number of sequential operations. This is the assembly-line cost from Chapter 1: fewer is better, because a GPU does the rest at once.
  • Maximum path length between any two positions. How many hops must a signal travel to link two words? Short paths make long-range dependencies easy to learn.

The table that made the case

Layer type Complexity / layer Sequential ops Max path length
Self-Attention O(n²·d) O(1) O(1)
Recurrent O(n·d²) O(n) O(n)
Convolutional O(k·n·d²) O(1) O(logk n)
Self-Attention (restricted) O(r·n·d) O(1) O(n/r)

Read the two right-hand columns first, because that's where self-attention shines. Its sequential ops are O(1) — a constant, independent of sentence length. And its maximum path length is O(1) too: any word reaches any other in a single attention step. Compare the recurrent row, O(n) on both — every extra word adds another sequential step and lengthens every path. That single contrast is the entire Chapter-1 promise, now proven on paper.

The one honest catch: that n²

Self-attention isn't free. Its per-layer cost is O(n²·d): the n × n score grid means work grows with the square of sentence length. The saving grace in 2017 was that for typical sentences n is smaller than d (512), so n²·d is actually cheaper than the recurrent n·d². The authors even floated restricted attention — each word attending only to a neighborhood of size r — for very long inputs.

This footnote launched a thousand papers

That quadratic cost is the reason "long context" is hard and expensive in modern LLMs, and why a decade of research — sparse attention, FlashAttention, linear attention, sliding windows — chips away at it. The trade-off the table states is still the central tension in the field today.

A bonus: interpretability

Self-attention hands you something recurrence never did: a readable n × n map of what attended to what. When the authors inspected these maps, individual heads had visibly specialized — some following long-distance grammatical dependencies (completing "making … more difficult" across many words), others resolving which noun a pronoun like "its" refers to. The model's reasoning became, in part, something you could look at.

The Key Idea

Self-attention trades a quadratic compute cost for two prizes recurrence couldn't offer: constant sequential depth (full parallelism) and constant path length (easy long-range learning) — plus inspectable attention maps as a bonus. For sentence-length sequences on GPU hardware, that trade was overwhelmingly worth it.

So what did we actually gain?

A principled reason to believe the architecture, not just a nice diagram. But an argument on paper is a hypothesis; the paper earns its title with real numbers on real benchmarks. Next: how they trained it, and exactly how well it did.
