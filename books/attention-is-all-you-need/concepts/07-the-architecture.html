Chapter 07Assembling the Machine

“Architecture is the thoughtful making of space.” — Louis Kahn

We have the parts. Now we build the Transformer itself: two towers, an encoder and a decoder, each a stack of six identical layers. Every piece here you've already met — this chapter is where they click together.

The overall shape: encoder → decoder

The Transformer keeps the classic encoder–decoder frame used by translation models. The encoder reads the whole source sentence and turns it into a stack of context-rich vectors. The decoder then generates the output one token at a time, each step allowed to look at (a) what it has produced so far and (b) the encoder's representation of the source. Generating one token at a time, feeding it back in, is called being auto-regressive.

The encoder layer: two sub-layers

Each of the encoder's N = 6 identical layers has exactly two sub-layers: a multi-head self-attention (every word attends to every word in the source), and a small position-wise feed-forward network. Nothing you haven't seen. What binds them is a pattern repeated everywhere in the model.

Residual connections + layer normalization

Around each sub-layer the model wraps a residual connection: it adds the sub-layer's input back to its output, then normalizes. In symbols, every sub-layer computes

LayerNorm( x + Sublayer(x) )

The residual "+ x" gives gradients a clean highway straight down the stack — the same trick that let ResNets go very deep in vision. Layer normalization then re-centers and re-scales each vector so activations stay well-behaved layer after layer. To make the "+ x" addition even possible, everything in the model — embeddings and all sub-layer outputs — is kept at width dmodel = 512.

The position-wise feed-forward network

After attention mixes information across words, the FFN processes each word on its own. It's two linear layers with a ReLU between:

FFN(x) = max(0, x W1 + b1) W2 + b2

It briefly expands each vector from 512 up to dff = 2048, applies the non-linearity, and projects back to 512. Think of attention as "gather what's relevant" and the FFN as "now think hard about what you gathered." The same FFN weights apply at every position (identical across positions, different across layers).

The decoder layer: one extra sub-layer, and a mask

Each decoder layer has the encoder's two sub-layers plus a third: an encoder–decoder attention (also called cross-attention). This is where the queries come from the decoder, but the keys and values come from the encoder's output — the mechanism by which the translation actually "looks at" the source sentence.

The mask: no peeking ahead

When generating word i, the decoder must not see words i+1 onward — they don't exist yet at inference time. So the decoder's self-attention is masked: illegal future positions have their pre-softmax scores set to −∞, which softmax turns into exactly zero weight. Combined with shifting the outputs right by one, this guarantees prediction for position i depends only on positions before it. Break this and the model "cheats" by reading the answer.

So what did we actually gain?

A complete, trainable translator built entirely from attention, addition, and small feed-forward layers — no recurrence anywhere. Every arrow in that diagram is something you can now explain. The natural question is: is this really better, or just different? The next chapter answers it with the complexity table that made the case.

Challenges

  1. Cross-attention takes its keys and values from the encoder but its queries from the decoder. In the library analogy, who is asking and who is being searched?
  2. The FFN expands to 2048 then back to 512. Why bother widening in the middle instead of two 512-to-512 layers?
  3. Explain why setting masked scores to −∞ (not 0) before the softmax is the correct way to forbid attending to future tokens.
