Concept 01 · The Why

Hardware as Software

Ash made business logic declarative. AshDevices asks a cheeky question with a serious answer: what if a robot could be declarative too?

Let me start where I always like to start — with something you already know. When you build an ordinary Ash application, you don't sit down and write SQL, and controllers, and validation, and a little state machine, all by hand. You describe a thing. You say "there is a Task; it has a title; you may complete it; only its owner may delete it." Then the framework — and this is the magic — turns that description into a running system. You stated what is true, and the machine figured out what to do.

Now walk across the hall into the world of physical devices, and watch the magic evaporate. To make a door lock, somebody writes GPIO.write(17, 1). To read a temperature, somebody opens a serial port and parses bytes. To tell a robot to move, somebody publishes an MQTT packet to a topic whose name is taped to the side of a monitor. Every device is its own little snowflake of wires and protocols, and the moment you swap a vendor, you rewrite everything that touched it.

Here is the whole idea of AshDevices in one sentence, and everything in this book is just detail hung on it:

🔑
The one sentence

Developers should build physical systems using the same declarative, composable, and testable principles they already use to build modern Ash applications. When in doubt, prefer the solution that makes hardware feel more like software.

The trick is the same trick

Why would the same idea work twice — once for database rows and once for steel? Because the hard, repetitive parts are regular. A robot, a camera, an incubator, a door: they all have state, they all accept commands, they all change over time, they all want to tell you what just happened. That regularity is exactly the thing a declarative framework eats for breakfast.

So in AshDevices you don't reach for a pin. You describe a device and ask it to do something a human would recognize:

elixir · the shape of the idea# Not this — a wire, a number, a prayer:
GPIO.write(17, 1)

# This — an intent a scientist could read aloud:
Door.unlock(door)
Robot.move(robot, :loading_station)
Microscope.capture(scope)

Read those the way you'd read any Ash call: they're descriptions of intent, not procedures. "Unlock the door." How that becomes a GPIO toggle, a Modbus write, or — and this is the part that will matter most — a perfectly faithful simulation, is the framework's problem. Exactly as turning a resource into SQL is the framework's problem.

🧩
Is this real yet?

AshDevices as a single shipped package is a proposal — an engineering manifesto. But the ground under it is very real: Ash and Spark give you the declarative resource and the DSL toolkit; AshStateMachine gives you lifecycles; Nerves and Circuits put the BEAM on real hardware. Throughout, we'll point at what exists versus what you'd still build.

Where we're headed

Next, the mindset shift that makes all of this click — learning to stop thinking in wires. Once you stop naming things after their cables, the whole architecture falls into place almost on its own.
