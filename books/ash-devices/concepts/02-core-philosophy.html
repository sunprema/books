Stop Thinking in Wires

AshDevices reaches Ash out into the physical world. But the first move isn't code — it's a change of vocabulary. Get the nouns right and the architecture practically writes itself.

Here's a funny thing about how engineers name things. Walk into a lab and somebody will point and say "go reset the GPIO-17 thing." Not "the door." The GPIO-17 thing. We name the object after the cable sticking out the back of it. And I always want to ask: what happens to that name the day you change vendors and the door is now on pin 22, over Modbus, behind a relay board? The name rots. It described the plumbing, and the plumbing moved.

So let me give you the analogy I keep coming back to. You don't call your kitchen "the 14-gauge-copper-circuit room." You call it the kitchen. There is copper in the walls — of course there is — but it lives behind the wall, and you never think about it while you're making breakfast. The wiring is an implementation detail. The kitchen is the thing that's real to you. That is exactly the instinct we want for hardware.

🔑
The punchline

Hardware is an implementation detail. Resources are the product. A name tied to a wire dies the day you swap a vendor; a name tied to the real-world concept — the door, the robot, the incubator — survives every cable underneath it.

So before any DSL, before a single defmodule, AshDevices asks you to retrain your tongue. There are two piles of nouns, and you want to live entirely in the second one.

🔌
Never name things after these

The plumbing nouns: GPIO MQTT Modbus Registers USB Vendor SDK. These are real and they matter — but they're the copper in the wall, not the kitchen.

🧭
Think in these instead

The domain nouns: Resource Capability Action Relationship State Machine Workflow Digital Twin. Every one of these is something a human can reason about without a datasheet open in the other hand.

Two vocabularies, side by side

The cleanest way to feel the difference is to read the same four jobs written both ways. On the left, you're describing wires. On the right, you're describing intent — and notice you can read the right column out loud to a scientist who has never seen a register in their life.

Plumbing vocabulary (avoid)Domain vocabulary (prefer)
GPIO.write(17, 1)Door.unlock()
publish to mqtt topicRobot.move(:bay_3)
write_register(0x12)Incubator.set_temperature(37)
vendor SDK callExperiment.start()
 
elixir · same intent, no wires# You wrote a fact about the world, not a recipe:
Incubator.set_temperature(incubator, 37)
Robot.move(robot, :bay_3)
Experiment.start(experiment)

Why the domain side wins

  • Durability. Door.unlock() outlives every vendor swap. The pin number changes; the door does not.
  • Testability. You can Simulator an intent far more honestly than a wire. "Unlock the door" has a meaning to simulate; "set pin 17 high" only has a voltage.
  • Readability. A scientist, a manager, a future you at 2am — all of them can read the domain column. None of them want to decode 0x12.
  • It forces the plumbing down. Once the surface speaks in domain nouns, every protocol detail has nowhere to live but an adapter behind the resource — the copper goes back into the wall. (We build that layer in Chapter 5.)
Left: the app fused to a wire — change the wire, break the app. Right: domain nouns on top, the protocol quarantined in an adapter.

Where we're headed

If hardware is just an implementation detail, then the unit you actually program — the thing that is the product — is the Resource. Which raises the obvious next question, and it's the whole of Chapter 3: what, exactly, IS a resource?
