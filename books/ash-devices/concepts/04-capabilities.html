Concept 04 · Composition

Composition Over Inheritance

There's a rule in AshDevices, and it's blunt: never grow a deep inheritance tree. Build behavior by snapping together capabilities instead.

Let me show you the wrong way first, because it feels so reasonable. You've got a robot. So you write a Robot. Then a fancier one with servos, so naturally ServoRobot extends Robot. Then an industrial cell, IndustrialRobot extends that. Then you buy an ABB arm, so ABBRobot extends that. Each step felt like progress. Each subclass quietly froze an assumption into the floor.

And then the day comes — it always comes — when you get a robot that is industrial but emphatically not ABB. Or a camera that moves. Or a door that, weirdly, has a motor. Now the tree fights you. The thing you need doesn't live on any single branch, and there is no honest place to put it.

✗ Inheritance tree

elixir · brittleRobot
 └── ServoRobot
      └── IndustrialRobot
           └── ABBRobot

Every subclass freezes an assumption. The "industrial but not ABB" device has nowhere to go.

✓ Composition

elixir · assembledRobot
 ├── Motor     # motion
 ├── Position  # where it is
 └── Camera    # capture

A Robot has motion. A Door has a lock. A Camera has capture. Plug it in; don't inherit it.

· 1

Here's the first-principles bit, the reason inheritance betrays you. A tree says "an ABBRobot is-a IndustrialRobot is-a Robot." That single spine has to carry every trait at once, in one fixed order. But the real traits of a device — it moves, it sees, it locks — have no business being ranked. They're independent. Inheritance couples things that were never coupled in reality.

🧱
The analogy

Inheritance is a family tree: you're stuck with whatever flows down the bloodline, in one rigid line of descent. Composition is a box of LEGO: a flat library of bricks you snap on as needed. You did not inherit the ability to take a photograph from your great-grandfather — you attached a camera. Capabilities work the same way: a device gains "capture" by bolting on the part, not by being born under it.

Capabilities provide behavior

So what is one of these bolt-on parts? In AshDevices it's a capability, and a single capability owns a whole vertical slice of behavior:

  • Actions — the verbs (move/1, capture/0)
  • Events — what it announces (Moved, Captured)
  • Telemetry — the signals it emits
  • Simulator logic — how it behaves with no hardware attached
  • Adapter contracts — what a real driver must implement
  • State-machine fragments — the lifecycle pieces it contributes
🔑
The payoff

Write :motion once. Every moving device just opts in — no copy-pasting an action, an event, and a sim model across a dozen resources. Capabilities are reusable by design; that is the entire point.

What's really under the hood

Now let me be honest about the machinery, because the pretty capability :motion line is the proposed AshDevices surface — but it doesn't float in air. It rests on something that already exists and is battle-tested: Spark.

Spark is the DSL toolkit that Ash itself is built on. An extension in Spark defines DSL sections and entities (Spark.Dsl.Section, Spark.Dsl.Entity) and can inject real behavior through transformers that rewrite the resource at compile time. A resource opts into an extension the ordinary way:

elixir · real spark surfaceuse Ash.Resource, extensions: [AshDevices.Resource]
🧩
Proposal vs. bedrock

A capability is, concretely, a packaged Spark extension a resource includes. The capability :motion block below is AshDevices-proposed sugar; the thing it expands into — DSL entities plus transformers that inject actions, events, and state — is Spark's real, shipping extension machinery.

So the aspirational shape and the solid ground meet here. The DSL on the next page is illustrative — clearly the proposed form — but every move it makes is one Spark already knows how to perform.

· 3

The shape you'd write

elixir · proposed dsldefmodule Lab.Devices.Robot do
  use Ash.Resource,
    domain: Lab.Devices,
    extensions: [AshDevices.Resource]

  device do
    capability :motion   # move/1 + Moved event + sim model
    capability :vision   # capture/0 + Captured event
  end
end

Because :motion is reusable, an AGV, a gantry, and a microscope stage all just say capability :motion — same action, same event, same simulator, zero copy-paste.

⬡
Why it scales

This is why "everything is a resource" doesn't collapse. You never get a combinatorial explosion of ServoIndustrialABB… subclasses. You get a small library of capabilities you mix — a handful of bricks, endless devices.

Where we're headed

A capability declares the intent: "move," "capture." But intent alone won't spin a motor — something has to actually talk to the hardware. That something is the Adapter, and it's why the next rule insists on domain before protocol.
