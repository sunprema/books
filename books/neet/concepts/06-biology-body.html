Chapter VI · Biology

The Body, Heredity & the Living World Biology

The rest of biology ranges from the beat of a single heart to the diversity of all life. These questions look like pure memory, but each rests on a small, rememberable why. Anchor the fact to its reason and it stops slipping away.

1What the ECG's big spike means

An electrocardiogram is a picture of the heart's electrical events. Each heartbeat draws three landmarks: a small P wave as the atria depolarise, a tall QRS complex as the much larger ventricles depolarise and contract, and a T wave as the ventricles recover (repolarise). The QRS is tall precisely because the ventricles are the big pumping chambers.

Q69Body fluids & circulation

The QRS complex in a standard ECG represents:

  • (1) Depolarisation of auricles
  • (2) Depolarisation of ventricles
  • (3) Repolarisation of ventricles
  • (4) Repolarisation of auricles
Reasoning

QRS = ventricular depolarisation (which triggers their contraction). Auricular (atrial) depolarisation is the small P wave; ventricular repolarisation is the T wave. The size of the spike is your mnemonic — big chambers, big deflection.

Answer (2) · Depolarisation of ventricles

2Which stage of the malaria parasite infects us

The malarial parasite Plasmodium has a life cycle split between human and mosquito. The stage injected into our blood by the mosquito's bite — the infective stage — is the sporozoite. The other named stages appear later or belong to the mosquito's gut.

Q65Human health & disease

The infectious stage of Plasmodium that enters the human body is:

  • (1) Sporozoites
  • (2) Female gametocytes
  • (3) Male gametocytes
  • (4) Trophozoites
Reasoning

Sporozoites travel in the mosquito's saliva and enter us at the bite. Gametocytes go the other way — they are what a mosquito picks up from an infected human. Trophozoites are a later feeding stage inside our cells.

Answer (1) · Sporozoites

3Sickle-cell: one letter, one chromosome

Genetic disorders on this exam are usually testing whether you can pair a disease with its exact mode of inheritance. Sickle-cell anaemia is an autosomal recessive disorder — it needs two copies of the mutant allele — and the gene sits on chromosome 11. (Haemophilia, by contrast, is X-linked recessive, not Y-linked.)

Q110Principles of inheritance

Select the correct match.

  • (1) Phenylketonuria — Autosomal dominant trait
  • (2) Sickle-cell anaemia — Autosomal recessive, chromosome 11
  • (3) Thalassemia — X linked
  • (4) Haemophilia — Y linked
Reasoning

Only (2) is right on both counts. Phenylketonuria is autosomal recessive, not dominant; thalassemia is autosomal, not X-linked; haemophilia is X-linked, not Y-linked. Eliminating the three that misname the mode leaves (2) standing.

Answer (2) · Sickle-cell anaemia — autosomal recessive, chromosome 11

4How many species share the planet?

A number worth carrying: although a little over 1.5 million species have been described and named, the ecologist Robert May put the likely global total at about 7 million. The exam wants May's estimate, not the described count.

Q68Biodiversity

According to Robert May, the global species diversity is about:

  • (1) 20 million
  • (2) 50 million
  • (3) 7 million
  • (4) 1.5 million
Reasoning

May's estimate is ≈7 million. The 1.5 million figure (option 4) is the trap — that's how many have actually been described, not the estimated total.

Answer (3) · 7 million

5Same tool, different toolmakers

A deep idea in evolution: unrelated animals facing the same problem often arrive at the same solution from different starting points. A penguin is a bird and a dolphin is a mammal, yet both evolved flippers for swimming. Structures with different ancestries but the same function are analogous, and the process that produces them is convergent evolution.

Q85Evolution

Flippers of penguins and dolphins are examples of:

  • (1) Convergent evolution
  • (2) Industrial melanism
  • (3) Natural selection
  • (4) Adaptive radiation
Reasoning

Different ancestors, same functional structure = analogous organs = convergent evolution. Adaptive radiation (option 4) is the opposite pattern — one ancestor diverging into many forms, like Darwin's finches.

Answer (1) · Convergent evolution

The habit that wins biology

For every fact, store the reason beside it — QRS is tall because ventricles are big; sporozoites infect because they ride the saliva; flippers converge because the sea sets the same problem. Reasons are far stickier than isolated facts, and they defend you against the planted traps.
