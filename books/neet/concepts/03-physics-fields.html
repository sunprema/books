Chapter III · Physics

Fields, Waves & the Quantum Physics

The second half of physics leaves solid objects behind and asks about the invisible: the field around a charge, the ripple of light, the strange bookkeeping of the atom. The equations look more forbidding, but the method is unchanged — find the one law that governs the situation, and let the numbers follow.

1The field around a charged sphere

A curious and useful fact: from outside, a uniformly charged sphere behaves exactly as if all its charge sat at its centre. So the field at a point beyond the surface is just Coulomb's point-charge field, with r measured from the centre — not from the surface:

E = 14πε₀ · Qr² = kQr²,   k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²

Q9Electrostatics

A spherical conductor of radius 10 cm has a charge of 3.2 × 10⁻⁷ C distributed uniformly. What is the magnitude of the electric field at a point 15 cm from the centre?

  • (1) 1.28 × 10⁵ N/C
  • (2) 1.28 × 10⁶ N/C
  • (3) 1.28 × 10⁷ N/C
  • (4) 1.28 × 10⁴ N/C
Reasoning

The point at 15 cm is outside the 10 cm sphere, so use r = 0.15 m:

E = (9×10⁹)(3.2×10⁻⁷)(0.15)² = 28800.0225 = 1.28 × 10⁵ N/C.

Had they asked for a point inside the conductor, the answer would be zero — a conductor holds no field within it. Reading “from the centre” carefully is the whole battle.

Answer (1) · 1.28 × 10⁵ N/C

2Reading a resistor's stripes

A resistor wears its value in coloured bands. The first two are digits, the third is a power-of-ten multiplier, the fourth is the tolerance. The digit order is fixed — Black, Brown, Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Violet, Grey, White for 0 through 9. Decode it yourself below, then meet the exam's version.

Q7Current electricity

The colour code of a resistor is Yellow, Violet, Brown, Gold. The values of resistance and tolerance, respectively, are:

  • (1) 47 kΩ, 10%
  • (2) 4.7 kΩ, 5%
  • (3) 470 Ω, 5%
  • (4) 470 kΩ, 5%
Reasoning

Yellow = 4, Violet = 7, Brown = ×10¹, Gold = ±5%. So the value is 47 × 10 = 470 Ω with 5% tolerance. Set the decoder above to yellow / violet / brown / gold to confirm. The trap is Brown: as a multiplier it means ×10, not ×1000.

Answer (3) · 470 Ω, 5%

3Light interfering with itself

Send light through two narrow slits and it lands not as two bright lines but as a row of evenly spaced fringes — proof that light is a wave. The spacing between fringes, the fringe width β, obeys a beautifully simple relation:

β = λ Dd

Read it like a sentence: fringes spread out if the wavelength λ grows or the screen distance D grows, and they crowd together if the slit separation d grows. Test each of those claims on the pattern below.

Q18Wave optics

In Young's double-slit experiment, if the separation between coherent sources is halved and the distance of the screen from the sources is doubled, then the fringe width becomes:

  • (1) half
  • (2) four times
  • (3) one-fourth
  • (4) double
Reasoning

From β = λD/d: halving d doubles β, and doubling D doubles it again. Two doublings compound to ×4. Drag the sliders to d = 0.5 and D = 2.0 and watch the fringes spread fourfold.

Answer (2) · four times

4The atom's bookkeeping

Nuclear reactions look intimidating until you notice they obey two plain conservation rules: the total mass number (top) and the total atomic number (bottom) must each balance across the arrow. Everything else is subtraction.

Q8Nuclear physics

When a uranium isotope ²³⁵₉₂U is bombarded with a neutron, it generates ⁸⁹₃₆Kr, three neutrons, and:

  • (1) ⁹¹₄₀Zr
  • (2) ¹⁰¹₃₆Kr
  • (3) ¹⁰³₃₆Kr
  • (4) ¹⁴⁴₅₆Ba
Reasoning

Balance the two ledgers. Mass numbers: left side 235 + 1 = 236; right side so far 89 + 3(1) = 92, so the missing nucleus has mass 236 − 92 = 144. Atomic numbers: left 92 + 0 = 92; right so far 36 (neutrons carry none), so the missing charge is 92 − 36 = 56 — the element barium.

→ ¹⁴⁴₅₆Ba

Answer (4) · ¹⁴⁴₅₆Ba

5A particle with a wavelength

De Broglie's astonishing idea: matter has a wavelength too. For an electron accelerated from rest through a potential difference V, the working shortcut (with λ in nanometres) is

λ = 1.227√V nm

Q42Dual nature of matter

An electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of V volt. If the de Broglie wavelength of the electron is 1.227 × 10⁻² nm, the potential difference is:

  • (1) 10² V
  • (2) 10³ V
  • (3) 10⁴ V
  • (4) 10 V
Reasoning

Rearrange to √V = 1.227 / λ with λ in nm:

√V = 1.2271.227×10⁻² = 10²  →  V = 10⁴ V.

The numbers were chosen so the digits cancel cleanly — a hint from the examiner that the shortcut formula is the intended path.

Answer (3) · 10⁴ V

Constants: k = 1/4πε₀ = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C². The de Broglie shortcut λ = 1.227/√V nm follows from λ = h/√(2meV). Questions from the source paper's Physics section.
