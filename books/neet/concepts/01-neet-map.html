Chapter I

The NEET Map

Before we solve a single question, let us look at the terrain. A great many students meet this exam as a wall of one hundred and eighty problems and try to climb it by memorising footholds. That is exhausting, and it fails. Let us instead ask a simpler question — what is this exam actually made of? — because once its shape is clear, the strategy almost writes itself.

1The shape of the paper

One booklet. Three hours. Three sciences. The marks are not scattered evenly — biology is half the paper. That single fact should reorganise how you spend your months.

2Why negative marking changes everything

Suppose you cannot decide and you guess at random among four options. You are right one time in four and wrong three times in four. Over four such guesses your expected score is

E = 14(+4) + 34(−1) = 1 − 34 = +14 per guess.

Barely positive — and only if the four options are truly equal. The moment you can rule out even one option, the arithmetic swings sharply in your favour: a one-in-three guess is worth (⅓)(4) + (⅔)(−1) = +⅔. This is why elimination is not a fallback; it is the whole game. Every question in this book ends by asking which options we could have crossed out on sight.

The key idea

You are not being asked to know 180 answers. You are being asked to reconstruct them from a much smaller set of principles. Learn the principles; the answers fall out.

3The three temperaments

Each subject rewards a different habit of mind. Meet them on their own terms.

SubjectWhat it really testsWhere marks are won
PhysicsTurning a sentence into an equation, then dimensions and limitsA handful of formulas used fluently; unit discipline
ChemistryThree faces: countable (moles), structural (bonds & shape), and reactive (mechanisms)Physical chemistry is the most learnable; organic patterns repeat

4How to use the chapters that follow

We will not march through all 180 questions — that would be a table, not a book. Instead each chapter takes a small, representative cluster and follows it down to bedrock. A dozen questions understood completely will teach you more than a hundred skimmed.

  • Chapters II–III — Physics, with two live figures you can play with: an Atwood pulley, a falling ball, a double-slit pattern, and a resistor whose colours you decode yourself.
  • Chapter IV — Chemistry, including a crystal you can rotate in 3D to see why the atomic radius formula is what it is.
  • Chapters V–VI — Biology, the biggest half, organised so the matched-pair and “odd-one-out” questions stop feeling like a memory lottery.
  • The Cheatsheet — every formula, constant, and fact used here, on one spread.
A note on the voice

Throughout, we prefer “let us see why” to “remember that.” When a formula appears, it will have been earned a line or two earlier. If a step feels uncomfortable, that discomfort is usually the sign that we are standing right next to the important idea.
