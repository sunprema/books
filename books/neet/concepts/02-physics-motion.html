Chapter II · Physics

Motion, Forces & Falling Bodies Physics

Mechanics is the friendliest part of the paper, because almost every problem is the same problem in disguise: something has a mass, something pushes or pulls it, and we are asked what happens. The whole art is turning the story in the question into the one line F = ma — and being honest about which forces act.

1Two weights on a string

Here is a machine two and a half centuries old and still on every exam: two masses hang from a string over a frictionless pulley. Let us not memorise its answer. Let us build it. Two bodies share one string, so they share one acceleration a (the string does not stretch). The heavier mass m₂ falls; the lighter m₁ rises. Write F=ma for each:

m₂g − T = m₂a   ·   T − m₁g = m₁a

Add the two lines and the unknown tension T cancels — that is the trick worth remembering. We are left with (m₂−m₁)g = (m₁+m₂)a, so

a = (m₂ − m₁) gm₁ + m₂

Q4Newton's laws · pulley

Two bodies of mass 4 kg and 6 kg are tied to the ends of a massless string over a frictionless pulley. The acceleration of the system, in terms of g, is:

  • (1) g/2
  • (2) g/5
  • (3) g/10
  • (4) g
Reasoning

Straight from what we built: a = (6−4)g/(6+4) = 2g/10 = g/5. Set the sliders above to 4 and 6 to watch it. Notice the sanity check built into the formula — if the masses were equal the top would vanish and a=0; if one side were massless you would recover free fall, a=g. Both extremes are right, so we trust the middle.

Answer (2) · g/5

2A ball thrown downward

Now drop the pulley and keep only gravity. A ball is thrown down from a tower — so it already has speed when it leaves the hand — and we know how fast it is going when it lands. We are asked how tall the tower is. We do not need time here, only speeds and distance, and there is exactly one kinematic equation with no time in it:

v² = u² + 2gh

Q6Kinematics · 1-D motion

A ball is thrown vertically downward with a velocity of 20 m/s from the top of a tower. It hits the ground with a velocity of 80 m/s. The height of the tower is (g = 10 m/s²):

  • (1) 340 m
  • (2) 320 m
  • (3) 300 m
  • (4) 360 m
Reasoning

Put the numbers in and solve for h:

80² = 20² + 2(10)h  →  6400 − 400 = 20h  →  h = 300 m.

Because the ball was thrown down, both u and g point the same way, so both are positive — the commonest slip here is a stray minus sign.

Answer (3) · 300 m

3Weight is not a constant

One idea trips up more students than any other: your weight is not built into you — it is the Earth's pull on you, and that pull weakens with distance. Newton's law of gravitation says the force falls off as the inverse square of the distance from the Earth's centre:

W = GMmr², so at the surface W₀ ∝ 1/R², and at height h, W ∝ 1/(R+h)².

Take the ratio and the messy constants GMm disappear:

WW₀ = R²(R + h)²

Q34Gravitation

A body weighs 72 N on the surface of the Earth. What is the gravitational force on it at a height equal to half the radius of the Earth?

  • (1) 32 N
  • (2) 30 N
  • (3) 24 N
  • (4) 48 N
Reasoning

Here h = R/2, so R+h = 3R/2. Then

W = 72 · R²(3R/2)² = 72 · 19/4 = 72 · 49 = 32 N.

A quick gut-check: you have moved out to 1.5 Earth-radii, so weight should drop to (1/1.5)² = 4/9 of its value — a little under half. 32 N is a little under half of 72 N. Good.

Answer (1) · 32 N

4How much a wire stretches

One more, to show how a definition alone can be the whole solution. A wire of natural length L and cross-section A stretches to L₁ when a mass M hangs from it. Young's modulus is simply stress divided by strain — the material's stiffness:

Y = stressstrain = F/AΔL/L = F · LA · ΔL

Q1Elasticity

A wire of length L, area of cross-section A, hangs from a fixed support. Its length becomes L₁ when a mass M is suspended from its free end. The expression for Young's modulus is:

  • (1) Mg(L₁−L)/AL
  • (2) MgL/AL₁
  • (3) MgL / A(L₁−L)
  • (4) MgL₁/AL
Reasoning

Put F=Mg and ΔL = L₁−L into Y = FL/(A·ΔL) to get Y = MgL / A(L₁−L). The extension L₁−L — not the new length L₁ — belongs in the denominator, which is exactly what options (2) and (4) get wrong.

Answer (3) · MgL / A(L₁−L)
Carry this forward

Every problem in this chapter was solved by (i) naming the forces, (ii) writing F=ma or a definition, and (iii) checking the answer against an extreme case. That third step is free insurance — use it on every question.

Constant used: g = 10 m/s² where the question specifies it, 9.8 m/s² otherwise. All four questions are from the source paper's Physics section.
