Chapter IV · Chemistry

Moles, Crystals & Carbon Chemistry

Chemistry shows three faces on this paper. The physical face counts things — moles, pressures, energies — and rewards arithmetic. The inorganic face is about structure and periodicity — how atoms stack and how electrons fill. The organic face is about reactions that repeat like grammar. We take one bite of each.

1The density of a gas

The ideal gas law, PV = nRT, hides a density inside it. Since the number of moles is mass over molar mass, n = m/M, we can rearrange to put density ρ = m/V on one side:

ρ = PMRT

Q2States of matter

A cylinder contains hydrogen gas at a pressure of 249 kPa and temperature 27 °C. Its density is (R = 8.3 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹):

  • (1) 0.2 kg/m³
  • (2) 0.1 kg/m³
  • (3) 0.02 kg/m³
  • (4) 0.5 kg/m³
Reasoning

Work in SI. P = 249,000 Pa, M = 2 g = 0.002 kg/mol, T = 300 K:

ρ = (249000)(0.002)(8.3)(300) = 4982490 = 0.2 kg/m³.

The single most common error is leaving M in grams — the units then betray you by a factor of a thousand.

Answer (1) · 0.2 kg/m³

2Sharing the pressure

In a mixture of gases that do not react, each gas pushes on the walls as if it were alone. Its share of the total pressure — its partial pressure — is just its share of the molecules, i.e. its mole fraction:

Pgas = Ptotal · ngasntotal

Q165Dalton's law

A mixture of N₂ and Ar in a cylinder contains 7 g of N₂ and 8 g of Ar. If the total pressure is 27 bar, the partial pressure of N₂ is (N = 14, Ar = 40 g mol⁻¹):

  • (1) 12 bar
  • (2) 15 bar
  • (3) 18 bar
  • (4) 9 bar
Reasoning

Count moles. n(N₂) = 7/28 = 0.25; n(Ar) = 8/40 = 0.20; total 0.45.

P(N₂) = 27 · 0.250.45 = 27 · 59 = 15 bar.

Note N₂ has molar mass 28 (two nitrogen atoms), not 14 — forgetting the diatomic pairing is the built-in trap.

Answer (2) · 15 bar

3Why the crystal radius formula is what it is

Now a question you should not memorise but see. In a body-centred cubic (bcc) crystal, identical atoms sit at the eight corners of a cube and one more sits dead centre. The atoms touch along the body diagonal — the line from one corner, through the centre atom, to the opposite corner. Rotate the cell below (drag it) and follow that green diagonal.

Along that diagonal the atoms touch, so the diagonal's length equals four radii: one radius from each end corner atom and a full diameter (two radii) across the centre atom, totalling 4r. And by Pythagoras in three dimensions the body diagonal of a cube of edge a is √3·a. Setting them equal:

4r = √3·a  →  r = √34 a

Q159The solid state

An element has a body-centred cubic (bcc) structure with a cell edge of 288 pm. The atomic radius is:

  • (1) (√2/4) × 288 pm
  • (2) (4/√3) × 288 pm
  • (3) (4/√2) × 288 pm
  • (4) (√3/4) × 288 pm
Reasoning

Exactly the relation we just built: r = (√3/4)a = (√3/4) × 288 pm ≈ 124.7 pm. The √2 options are for the face-centred cubic, where atoms touch along a face diagonal instead. Seeing the cell in 3D is the surest way never to confuse the two.

Answer (4) · (√3/4) × 288 pm

4Counting unpaired electrons

A transition-metal ion's magnetism comes from its unpaired electrons. The “spin-only” magnetic moment depends on just their number n:

μ = √n(n+2) BM

Q136d-block elements

The calculated spin-only magnetic moment of the Cr²⁺ ion is:

  • (1) 4.90 BM
  • (2) 5.92 BM
  • (3) 2.84 BM
  • (4) 3.87 BM
Reasoning

Chromium is element 24: [Ar]3d⁵4s¹. Removing two electrons for Cr²⁺ (the 4s electron first, then one 3d) leaves 3d⁴ — four electrons in five d-orbitals, all unpaired by Hund's rule, so n = 4:

μ = √4(6) = √24 = 4.90 BM.

Answer (1) · 4.90 BM

5Where chlorine attacks toluene

Organic chemistry is about conditions. The same toluene gives different products depending on how you chlorinate it. With light (Cl₂/hν) the reaction is a free-radical attack on the side chain — the CH₃ group — replacing its hydrogens one by one: CH₃ → CH₂Cl → CHCl₂ → CCl₃. (With a Lewis-acid catalyst instead, chlorine would attack the ring.) Hydrolysing the two-chlorine stage, benzal chloride (CHCl₂), gives an aldehyde.

Q170Haloarenes · aldehydes

Identify compound X: toluene —Cl₂/hν→ X —H₂O/373 K→ benzaldehyde.

  • (1) benzyl chloride, C₆H₅CH₂Cl
  • (2) benzal chloride, C₆H₅CHCl₂
  • (3) C₆H₅CCl₃
  • (4) chlorobenzene, C₆H₅Cl
Reasoning

To end at an aldehyde (one C–H, one C=O on that carbon), the carbon must have carried exactly two chlorines before hydrolysis — the two OH groups that would form collapse, losing water, to a C=O. That is benzal chloride, CHCl₂. One chlorine (benzyl chloride) would hydrolyse to an alcohol; three (option 3) to a carboxylic acid.

Answer (2) · benzal chloride, C₆H₅CHCl₂
