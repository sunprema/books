Chapter V · Biology

The Cell & Its Code Biology

Biology is half the paper, and its heart is the cell — a factory that stores its instructions as a chemical text and reads them with astonishing precision. Where physics rewards derivation, biology rewards exactness: the right enzyme, the right number of bonds, the right sequence. But even here, understanding the mechanism beats blind recall.

1The pairing rule that runs on hydrogen bonds

DNA's two strands are held together by pairs of bases, and the pairing is not arbitrary — it is dictated by how many hydrogen bonds each pair can form. Adenine pairs with thymine through two hydrogen bonds; guanine pairs with cytosine through three. That extra bond is why G–C-rich DNA melts at a higher temperature.

Q100Molecular basis of inheritance

Which of the following statements is correct?

  • (1) Adenine pairs with thymine through one H-bond.
  • (2) Adenine pairs with thymine through three H-bonds.
  • (3) Adenine does not pair with thymine.
  • (4) Adenine pairs with thymine through two H-bonds.
Reasoning

Straight from the ledger above: A=T is two hydrogen bonds. The “three” in option (2) is the G≡C count, planted to catch a half-remembered fact.

Answer (4) · two H-bonds

2How long is the thread in one cell?

The genome is a text, but it is also a physical thread, and we can measure it. Consecutive base pairs sit a fixed distance apart along the helix — 0.34 nm — so the total length is simply that spacing times the number of base pairs:

length = (number of bp) × (0.34 × 10⁻⁹ m)

Q82DNA packaging

If the distance between two consecutive base pairs is 0.34 nm and the total number of base pairs in a typical mammalian cell is 6.6 × 10⁹, then the length of the DNA is approximately:

  • (1) 2.5 metres
  • (2) 2.2 metres
  • (3) 2.7 metres
  • (4) 2.0 metres
Reasoning

(6.6 × 10⁹)(0.34 × 10⁻⁹ m) = 2.244 m ≈ 2.2 m.

Two metres of thread folded into a nucleus a few millionths of a metre across — that is the real marvel the number is pointing at.

Answer (2) · 2.2 metres

3The enzyme that unzips for reading

To copy DNA (replication) the cell uses a dedicated enzyme, DNA helicase, to prise the strands apart. But to read a gene into RNA (transcription), no separate unwinder is needed — the enzyme RNA polymerase does both jobs at once: it opens the helix and stitches the RNA. The exam turns on knowing which process is named.

Don't confuse the two

Replication → DNA helicase opens the helix. Transcription → RNA polymerase itself opens the helix as it reads. The word “transcription” is the tell.

Q56Transcription

Name the enzyme that facilitates opening of the DNA helix during transcription.

  • (1) DNA helicase
  • (2) DNA polymerase
  • (3) RNA polymerase
  • (4) DNA ligase
Reasoning

Because the process is transcription (DNA → RNA), the answer is RNA polymerase, which unwinds the helix as part of reading it. DNA helicase (option 1) belongs to replication; ligase seals nicks; DNA polymerase copies DNA to DNA.

Answer (3) · RNA polymerase

4Molecular scissors that read backwards the same

Restriction enzymes cut DNA only at specific short sequences — and those sequences are palindromes: read 5′→3′ on one strand, they say the same thing as 5′→3′ on the other. EcoRI recognises GAATTC. Watch the symmetry: the top strand read left-to-right and the bottom strand read left-to-right are identical.

Q109Biotechnology · tools

The specific palindromic sequence recognised by EcoRI is:

  • (1) 5′-GGAACC-3′ / 3′-CCTTGG-5′
  • (2) 5′-CTTAAG-3′ / 3′-GAATTC-5′
  • (3) 5′-GGATCC-3′ / 3′-CCTAGG-5′
  • (4) 5′-GAATTC-3′ / 3′-CTTAAG-5′
Reasoning

EcoRI reads GAATTC. Test the palindrome: top strand 5′→3′ is G-A-A-T-T-C; bottom strand read 5′→3′ (right to left of the drawing) is also G-A-A-T-T-C. Option (3), GGATCC, is BamHI's site — a common distractor.

Answer (4) · 5′-GAATTC-3′ / 3′-CTTAAG-5′

5Where sugars get attached to proteins

A last, quieter fact of cell logistics. Proteins destined for the membrane or for secretion are decorated with sugar chains — made into glycoproteins and glycolipids. The first assembly of these happens in the folded-membrane network that threads the cell: the endoplasmic reticulum (with the Golgi apparatus doing later modification).

Q117Cell organelles

Which is the important site of formation of glycoproteins and glycolipids in eukaryotic cells?

  • (1) Peroxisomes
  • (2) Golgi bodies
  • (3) Polysomes
  • (4) Endoplasmic reticulum
Reasoning

The endoplasmic reticulum is the primary site of this glycosylation in the NCERT framing the exam follows. The Golgi (option 2) is tempting because it further processes and sorts these molecules, but formation begins in the ER.

Answer (4) · Endoplasmic reticulum
