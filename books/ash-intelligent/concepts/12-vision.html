Concept 12 · Vision

The Long-Term Vision & Roadmap

Ash turned business logic into something you declare. The horizon is to do the same for intelligence — and then build a whole family on top of it.

We end where we began, on a single through-line. Declarative domain modeling worked. Developers stopped hand-writing controllers, queries, and validations and started describing resources, actions, and policies — and the framework generated the rest. That bet paid off so completely that Ash became, in practice, the operating system for business logic. The next layer is the obvious one: declarative intelligence.

The pattern is the same. Instead of manually orchestrating prompts, context, memory, and workflows for every AI feature you ship, you declare the intelligence once and let the framework generate the infrastructure underneath it. The scheduled job, the context snapshot, the structured call, the stored decision — all of it becomes the framework's problem, exactly as SQL generation already is.

Carry that idea far enough and a destination comes into focus. Just as Ash became the operating system for business logic, AshIntelligence aims to become the operating system for AI-native applications — the layer every intelligent feature is built on, uniform and inspectable, rather than a pile of bespoke glue.

🔑
The closing principle

Business logic should be declarative. Intelligence should be declarative too. Everything in this book — and everything on the roadmap that follows — is detail hung on that one sentence.

The roadmap, in three honest bands

A vision is only useful if you can see where it touches the ground. Here is the same horizon as a timeline: what ships today, what the AshIntelligence core would unify near-term, and the companion family that comes later.

From things that ship today, up through a unifying core, out to a companion family.

Notice the direction of travel: the bottom band isn't science fiction, it's a consequence. Once intelligence is declared in one place, packages that record decisions, plan goals, or run multi-agent workflows become natural extensions of the same data — not seven separate inventions.

A family of companion packages

The "later" band deserves names. Each of these is a single, focused extension that leans on the declarative core — together they sketch a full stack for AI-native applications.

Read the list as a sentence: remember what matters, build agents over it, connect it into a graph, record the decisions, snapshot the context, plan the goals, and execute the work under explicit approval. That is what an operating system for intelligence is actually made of.

🧩
Why "operating system" is the right frame

An OS isn't impressive because it does any one thing — it's impressive because every program gets scheduling, memory, and isolation for free. Ash gave business logic that kind of leverage: every resource inherited policies, tenancy, and tooling without re-implementation. Applied to intelligence, the same leverage means every AI feature inherits memory, context, and approval policies by default. That shared substrate, not any single clever prompt, is the whole point.

· 3

An honest word before we close

Let's keep the same candor we've kept all book. AshIntelligence as a single named package is a proposal — a north star, not a release on Hex. Several of the companion packages above are aspirational names for capabilities that don't yet exist as such.

But the gap is shrinking, and quickly. The ecosystem is already moving in exactly this direction: ash_ai brings LLM tooling and structured outputs into Ash, ash_oban schedules the background work, ash_state_machine models workflow, and Spark makes the whole DSL pattern possible. The pieces of the "today" band are real, and they keep accreting toward the unifying core. The vision isn't a leap — it's the next step on a path the community is already walking.

So here is the grounded version of the dream. You will, before long, declare what your application understands and decides the same way you already declare what it stores and protects. The plumbing will be the framework's problem. And the through-line that opened this book will close it, unchanged.

🔑
The book's last line

Business logic should be declarative. Intelligence should be declarative too.
