Concept 01 · The Why

The Big Idea: Declarative Intelligence

Ash made business logic declarative. AshIntelligence asks a simple, ambitious question: what if intelligence could be declarative too?

Here is the whole proposal in one sentence. You should be able to declare what your application understands and decides — its context, memory, priorities, and actions — the same way you already declare its resources and policies, and let the framework generate the rest. Everything in this book is detail hung on that sentence.

Think about how you build an Ash app today. You don't write controllers and SQL and validation by hand; you describe a Task resource, its actions, and who's allowed to do what. The framework turns that description into a running system. It's declarative: you state intent, the machine handles orchestration.

Now think about how you add AI to that same app today. You drop down out of the declarative world entirely. You hand-write a prompt, hand-roll a background job to call an LLM, hand-stitch the database context into the request, hand-parse the reply, and hand-wire the result back into your domain. Every feature reinvents that plumbing. AshIntelligence's bet is that this plumbing is regular enough to declare.

🔑
The guiding principle

Business logic should be declarative. Intelligence should be declarative too. That one line is the north star for everything that follows.

What "declarative intelligence" buys you

When intelligence is a declaration rather than a pile of scripts, four good things happen — the same four that made Ash itself worth using:

  • It's uniform. Every intelligent feature is built the same way, so you learn the pattern once.
  • It's inspectable. The declaration is data. Tools, dashboards — and LLMs — can read your app's own structure.
  • It's safe by construction. Because it runs through Ash, it inherits your policies and tenancy instead of bypassing them.
  • It's swappable. The provider (OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, a local model) is a config detail, not a rewrite.

Concretely, the proposal is an Ash extension. You add it to a domain and describe an intelligence block. A first taste — we'll earn every line of this over the next chapters:

elixir · the shape of the ideadefmodule MyApp.Intelligence do
  use Ash.Domain,
    extensions: [AshIntelligence]

  intelligence do
    provider :anthropic
    memory :persistent

    brief :daily do
      include :priorities
      include :risks
      include :recommendations
    end
  end
end

Read it the way you'd read any Ash DSL: it's a description, not a procedure. "Use Anthropic. Keep memory. Every day, produce a brief of what's important, what's at risk, and what I should do." How that becomes a scheduled job, a context snapshot, a structured LLM call, and a stored result is the framework's problem — exactly as turning a resource into SQL is the framework's problem.

🧩
Is this real yet?

AshIntelligence as a single named package is a proposal. But — and this is the encouraging part — most of its machinery already ships today in ash_ai, ash_oban, ash_state_machine, and the Elixir LLM clients. We'll keep pointing at what exists versus what you'd still build.

Where we're headed

Next we'll make the problem sharp: modern apps model their data beautifully and understand its meaning not at all. That gap is the whole reason this layer wants to exist.
