Foundations: Ash, Spark & the Declarative Mind

Before we can add intelligence to a framework, we need to meet the two real technologies that make declaring it possible.

To make intelligence declarative, you need a framework that's already declarative — Ash — and a tool for inventing new declarations — Spark. Everything AshIntelligence proposes is built on these two, so a few pages spent here will make every later chapter land. They are not hand-waving; they ship today, they're battle-tested, and they're the reason "declarative intelligence" is an engineering plan rather than a wish.

Ash: describe the domain, generate the plumbing

Ash Framework is a declarative, resource-oriented backend framework for Elixir, created by Zach Daniel. The central move is simple: instead of hand-writing controllers, context modules, schemas, and SQL, you describe the shape of your domain — its resources, actions, relationships, and policies — and Ash generates the plumbing, lifecycle logic, and persistence for you.

A small, idiomatic resource reads like a specification of what's true, not a script of what to do:

elixir · a support ticket, declareddefmodule MyApp.Support.Ticket do
  use Ash.Resource,
    domain: MyApp.Support,
    data_layer: AshPostgres.DataLayer

  attributes do
    uuid_primary_key :id
    attribute :subject, :string, allow_nil?: false
    attribute :status, :atom,
      constraints: [one_of: [:open, :closed]],
      default: :open
  end

  actions do
    defaults [:read, :destroy]
    create :open, accept: [:subject]
    update :close, change: set_attribute(:status, :closed)
  end

  relationships do
    belongs_to :reporter, MyApp.Accounts.User
  end

  policies do
    policy action_type(:read) do
      authorize_if relates_to_actor_via(:reporter)
    end
  end
end
· 1

Nothing in that file says how to read a ticket from Postgres, check the policy, or run the migration. You declared the shape; Ash supplies the behavior. That inversion — intent over orchestration — is the habit of mind this whole book builds on.

What Ash already models

Out of the box, Ash gives you a vocabulary for the parts of an application that are regular enough to declare:

  • Resources — the nouns of your domain.
  • Actions — the verbs: create, read, update, destroy, and custom ones.
  • Relationships — how the nouns connect.
  • Policies — who may do what, enforced uniformly.
  • State Machines — legal transitions over a lifecycle.

Hold that list. The entire pitch of AshIntelligence is that it adds new members to it — and the mechanism for adding them is the second technology.

Spark: the toolkit for inventing DSLs

Spark is the DSL-building toolkit that powers every DSL in Ash. It's battle-tested, handling thousands of DSL definitions across the ecosystem. When you write attributes do … end above, you're using a Spark DSL without ever thinking about Spark — which is exactly the point.

You build a Spark DSL from three pieces:

  • An Entity — a single constructor, the "word" you get to use (like attribute).
  • A Section — a named block, the "clause" that groups entities (like attributes do … end).
  • An Extension — the package that bundles sections together and hooks into a host like Ash.
elixir · a Spark extension, skeletaldefmodule MyApp.Thing.Dsl do
  use Spark.Dsl.Extension,
    sections: [@thing_section],
    transformers: [MyApp.Thing.Transformers.Derive],
    verifiers: [MyApp.Thing.Verifiers.Validate]
end

Two of those lines deserve a closer look, because they're the moving parts AshIntelligence will lean on hardest.

⚙️
Transformer vs. Verifier

A transformer runs at compile time, in dependency order, and can read and modify the DSL data structure — injecting extra entities or deriving values. A verifier also runs at compile time but is read-only: it inspects the final DSL and returns :ok, {:error, term}, or {:warn, warning} to validate it.

· 2

The seam, drawn

Put it together. An Extension bundles sections + transformers + verifiers via use Spark.Dsl.Extension. That is precisely the seam AshIntelligence plugs into — it is, mechanically, "just" an Ash/Spark extension. On the left is what Ash already models. On the right is what this book adds on top.

Same machinery, new vocabulary: an extension adds the right-hand concerns on top of the left.
· 3

That right-hand column — Context, Memory, Priorities, Reasoning, Recommendations, Agents, Execution — is the table of contents for the rest of this book. Each one becomes an entity or section in an intelligence DSL, validated by verifiers and assembled by transformers, exactly the way attribute and policy already are.

🔑
The whole seam

An Ash extension is just sections + transformers + verifiers. That's the whole seam — AshIntelligence is an extension that adds an intelligence section and generates the rest at compile time.

Why does this matter so much? Because it means AshIntelligence inherits everything that makes Ash trustworthy. The intelligence layer compiles into the same data structure, runs through the same policies and tenancy, and is just as inspectable as the rest of your domain. There's no parallel universe of hand-rolled AI plumbing sitting beside your app — it's inside the app, declared in the same language.

🧩
Why build on Spark at all

Because the hard parts — compile-time validation, derivation, introspectable structure — already exist and are proven across thousands of DSLs. AshIntelligence doesn't need to invent a config format or a plugin system. It writes a few entities and verifiers, and Spark does the rest.

Where we're headed

We now have the two pillars: a framework that already declares its domain, and a toolkit for declaring new things on top of it. What we don't yet have is a set of principles for how this new intelligence layer should behave — when it should defer to policies, how it should treat memory, what "safe by construction" really demands. Those design principles are next.
