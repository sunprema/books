Core Philosophy & Design Principles

Before the architecture, the convictions. Six principles decide what AshIntelligence is allowed to be — and everything in later chapters is just these principles made concrete.

Ash earned its keep by being declarative: you describe what your business is and does — resources, actions, relationships, policies, state machines — and the framework figures out how to make it run. AshIntelligence inherits that ethos wholesale. It refuses to bolt a pile of imperative AI scripts onto a declarative core. Instead it asks: if business logic deserves a vocabulary, doesn't understanding deserve one too?

So AshIntelligence adds a second vocabulary alongside Ash's first. Where Ash gives you nouns for structure, AshIntelligence gives you nouns for cognition: Context, Memory, Priorities, Reasoning, Recommendations, Agents, Execution. The split is clean and worth memorizing:

  • Ash describes what the business is and does.
  • AshIntelligence describes what the application understands and decides.
Two vocabularies, one stack. The intelligence layer rests on the model — it never replaces it, and never reaches around it.

The six design principles

Every API, every DSL section, every default in AshIntelligence is answerable to these six commitments. Read them as constraints the framework imposes on itself so that you don't have to police it by hand.

PrincipleWhat it meansWhy it matters
DeclarativeDescribe intent; the framework handles orchestration.You state what to understand, not how to call an LLM. Less plumbing, one pattern to learn.
Event-DrivenEverything reacts to domain events.A change happens, intelligence responds — no polling, no glue jobs. The model and its meaning stay in lockstep.
ExplainableEvery recommendation carries its reasoning and a confidence score.No black-box "do this." You can audit, trust, and override what the system suggests.
Permission-AwareRespects Ash Policies and Authorizers.Intelligence can't see or do what the actor can't. Safety is inherited, not re-implemented.
Provider-AgnosticOpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, Ollama, local models — provider is config.Swap models without rewrites. No lock-in to one vendor's roadmap or pricing.
ExtensibleImplemented through Spark DSLs.Teams add their own sections and entities. The framework is a foundation, not a cage.
🔑
Why Explainable is the keystone

An AI layer is only worth wiring into your domain if you can trust it enough to act. That trust comes from one rule: every recommendation arrives with its reasoning and a confidence score attached. You get a "why" and a "how sure," not a verdict. That is the difference between a tool you supervise and an oracle you obey.

🧩
Why Permission-Aware keeps it safe

A layer that can read your whole domain and take actions is exactly as dangerous as it is powerful — unless it's bound by the same gates as a human actor. Because intelligence runs through Ash, it inherits your Policies and Authorizers automatically: it sees only what the current actor may see, and acts only where the actor may act. We make this concrete in chapter 10.

🔌
Provider-Agnostic, in practice

The provider is a single config line. Move from a hosted Anthropic model to a local Ollama instance — for cost, privacy, or an outage — and your business logic never changes. The vocabulary above is provider-shaped; the wire underneath is swappable.

From principles to parts

These six aren't decoration — they're a spec. Declarative demands a DSL, Event-Driven demands a trigger system, Explainable demands a reasoning record, Permission-Aware demands an authorizer hook, Provider-Agnostic demands an adapter boundary, and Extensible demands Spark underneath all of it. Next we assemble exactly those parts into a concrete architecture.
