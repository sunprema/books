Java & the JVM — Tracing GC at Scale

Java made a bet in 1995 that defined a generation: take memory away from the programmer entirely. You new an object and never, ever free it. A collector watches the whole heap and reclaims what you've stopped using. Thirty years of relentless engineering have made that collector astonishingly good.

The JVM is the purest expression of the tracing, generational ideas from Concept 05. Its heap is split by age, exactly as the generational hypothesis predicts:

New objects land in Eden. Survivors bounce through the survivor spaces, then graduate to Old.

A "minor GC" cleans just the young generation — quick, because almost everything there is already dead. The few survivors copy into a survivor space; repeat survivors get promoted to the old generation, which a "major GC" visits far less often. Effort follows the garbage.

▣
Stack vs heap in Java

Primitives (int, double) and object references live on the stack, in frames, just like everywhere. But every new object's body lives on the GC-managed heap. The stack holds the arrow; the heap holds the thing.

Pick your collector (this is Java's superpower)

The JVM ships several collectors and lets you choose by tuning flag, trading throughput against pause time:

CollectorCharacter
G1 defaultregion-based, balanced; pauses in the tens–hundreds of ms. The sensible default.
ZGCfully generational; sub-millisecond pauses at any heap size. Costs ~15–30% more memory.
Shenandoahalso concurrent, low-pause; a Red Hat alternative to ZGC.
Parallelmax throughput, longer pauses; great for batch jobs.
★
The pause, and the war on it

Older collectors did marking with the world stopped — a freeze you could feel. The modern ones (ZGC, Shenandoah) do almost all their work concurrently, while your threads run, using read/write barriers and colored pointers to stay correct. The stop-the-world window shrank from seconds to under a millisecond.

▣
As of 2026 (JDK 25 LTS)

ZGC is now generational by default — the old non-generational mode was removed in JDK 24, so if you're on ZGC at all, you're on the generational variant. G1 stays the default and picked up real pause and memory improvements in JDK 25.

What you gain

Zero memory-safety bugs, huge productivity, and a collector you can tune for your workload — from batch throughput to trading-floor latency.

What you pay

A heavier runtime, more memory headroom, GC CPU overhead, and timing that's less predictable than Rust's deterministic drop.

Java collects the whole graph by tracing, so cycles are a non-issue — A↔B is collected together once nothing outside reaches them. Hold that thought, because the next language took the opposite road and had to bolt a cycle-catcher on afterward. Meet Python.
