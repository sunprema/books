Concept 03 · Core

The Heap — Flexible & Yours to Manage

The stack was a cafeteria stack of plates: rigid, automatic, instant. The heap is a giant warehouse. You can ask for a space of any size, at any time, and keep it for as long as you like. Freedom — and freedom always sends a bill.

When you need memory whose size you only learn at runtime (a file you just read, a list that grows, an image the user picked) or that must outlive the function creating it, you go to the heap. You ask the allocator — a librarian who manages the warehouse — "I need 4,000 bytes." It finds a free shelf big enough, hands you back its address, and writes down that the shelf is now taken.

C — ask, use, returnint* a = malloc(n * sizeof(int)); // reserve a shelf
if (!a) handle_oom();                 // warehouse can say "full"
a[0] = 7;                            // use it (lives past this scope)
free(a);                              // give the shelf back
a = NULL;                            // don't aim at it again

Notice what's different. On the stack, the pointer a lives in your current frame, but the four thousand bytes it points to live out on the heap, untouched by returns. The frame can pop; the shelf stays reserved. That's exactly the power we wanted — and exactly why nobody automatically cleans it up. The warehouse has no idea when you're truly finished.

◆
Why isn't it automatic like the stack?

The stack frees in perfect reverse order — last in, first out — so a single pointer says it all. Heap objects die in any order, at unpredictable times, often pointed at from many places. There's no simple rule the hardware can follow. Someone has to decide when each one is dead.

The pointer is small and lives on the stack; the data it names lives on the heap.

Freedom isn't free — three hidden costs

1 · It's slow(er)

The allocator must search for a fitting free block, split it, and record it. Versus the stack's single instruction, a malloc is hundreds of times more work.

2 · It fragments

Allocate and free in a jumble and the warehouse fills with small gaps. You may have 1 GB free total yet no single 100 MB shelf left.

●
3 · You must remember to free it

This is the big one. The warehouse trusts you completely. Forget to return a shelf and it's lost until the program exits — a leak. Return it twice, or use it after returning, and you get the corruption bugs of the next chapter. The cost of heap freedom is responsibility.

Every language in this book gives you a stack for free and the same warehouse underneath. The entire difference between Rust, Zig, Java, Python, and Go is the answer to one question: who decides when a heap shelf is dead, and how? Let's watch what happens when the answer is "you, by hand."
