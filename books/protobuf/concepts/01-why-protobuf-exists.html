Why Protobuf Exists

Before we look at a single line of a .proto file, let's ask the question every format has to answer for its keep: what problem was so annoying that someone built a whole compiler to avoid it? Once you see the shape of that problem, the syntax in the next chapter stops looking arbitrary — it starts looking inevitable.

A thousand services, one conversation

Picture Google, early 2001. Not the search box you know — a fast-growing thicket of internal services, each one calling several others, all of them exchanging structured data over the network hundreds of times a second. Every one of those calls needs an answer to a deceptively small question: how do two programs, quite possibly written in two different languages by two different teams, agree on what a message means?

Google's engineers were already leaning on a general-purpose internal RPC system — Stubby, which dates from roughly the same period and would eventually inspire the open-source gRPC — and it needed a wire format underneath it. The obvious 2001-era answer was XML: it's self-describing, human-readable, and every language already had a parser for it. The trouble is that "self-describing" is expensive. Repeating every field's name, as text, on every single message, millions of times a second, is a tax you pay forever just so a human could eyeball the bytes — a human who, in practice, almost never does.

A sense of scale Figures on exactly how many internal calls Google's RPC layer handles per second are widely repeated in secondary write-ups but were never an official published statistic — treat any specific number you see quoted (ten billion per second is one that circulates) as "this is the kind of scale we're talking about," not a fact to cite.

So a small internal format — nicknamed Proto1, after a class that literally buffered the bytes of one RPC call — grew up around that pain. It didn't start as a grand design. It grew the way most useful internal tools grow: a feature bolted on here, a convention added there, whenever a team needed it and was willing to do the work. By the time Google decided to open it to the world in July 2008, the code was rewritten almost from scratch — what shipped kept Proto1's ideas but shed its internal-only dependencies. That rewrite is the Protocol Buffers you'll write in this book.

What XML and JSON actually cost you

Skip ahead twenty-five years and the same tradeoff still shows up, just with JSON standing in for XML. JSON fixed XML's worst ergonomic sins — it's lighter, friendlier to write by hand — but it inherited the same structural gap: no schema travels with the data. A JSON object doesn't know whether "count" is supposed to be an integer, whether it's allowed to be missing, or whether a client three versions old even understands what "count" means. All of that lives in documentation, in code comments, in the tribal knowledge of whoever wrote the client — which means a mismatch surfaces as a runtime crash, not a compiler error.

Google's own comparison at the time put it bluntly: protobuf was "like XML, but smaller, faster, and simpler." The commonly repeated figures — something like three-to-ten times smaller and twenty-to-a-hundred times faster to parse than equivalent XML — come from Google's own historical framing rather than an independent audit, so treat them as "this is the right order of magnitude," not a benchmark you could reproduce on demand.

Don't trust a single number The size/speed gap against JSON specifically depends heavily on your data's shape. A message that's mostly small integers, enums, and booleans benefits enormously — those encode as one to a few bytes each with no field-name text at all. A message that's mostly long strings barely benefits, because strings are stored almost identically in both formats. Whatever multiplier a blog post quotes you, ask what kind of payload it measured.

The real reason it stuck around

Here's the trap: if you walk away thinking protobuf's whole pitch is "smaller and faster than JSON," you'll wonder why anyone bothers with the extra build step for a service that handles ten requests a second. For a lot of software, that request would be entirely reasonable. The speed and size wins are real, but they're not why teams still reach for protobuf a decade into a project.

The durable reason is that a .proto file is a contract, and the compiler enforces it. Because the wire format tags each field with a number instead of a name, you can rename a field in the schema and every already-deployed binary keeps working — the wire never saw the name to begin with. An old client reading a message with new fields simply skips bytes it doesn't recognize instead of choking on them. A new client reading an old message falls back to sensible defaults for fields that never arrived. None of that is a JSON convention you have to remember to follow by hand; it's a property of the format, checked by generated code, every time.

That's the argument you'll see engineering teams make explicitly: the win isn't the bytes on the wire, it's that dozens of teams, writing in different languages, can evolve a shared schema for years without a synchronized "everyone upgrade on Tuesday" event. We'll spend most of this book on exactly that discipline — how to design a schema that can keep changing safely, and how tooling can catch a breaking change before it ships instead of after it pages someone at 2 a.m.

Key idea Protobuf isn't primarily a compression trick. It's a way to let a schema — not a person, not a wiki page — be the source of truth that many teams, many languages, and many years of change all agree on.

A format with siblings, not a monopoly

Protobuf wasn't the only answer to this problem, and it's worth knowing its neighbors so you can recognize when one of them might fit better.

FormatOriginReach for it when…
Apache Thrift Facebook, 2007 You want schema, codegen, and an RPC framework bundled together out of the box
Apache Avro Hadoop ecosystem, ~2009 You're in a streaming/batch pipeline where schema-with-the-data, not a compile step, fits better
Cap'n Proto Kenton Varda, 2013 The decode step itself, not just the payload size, is your bottleneck — it reads wire bytes with zero copying
FlatBuffers Google, 2014 You're in a game, on mobile, or anywhere allocation at 60fps is the enemy
MessagePack 2008 You want JSON's schema-free convenience, just smaller and faster, with no schema at all

Notice the pattern in that middle column: nobody on this list wins on every axis. Protobuf's particular bet is schema-required (unlike Avro or MessagePack) but still fully decoded into normal in-memory objects (unlike the zero-copy formats). That's a deliberate middle ground, and it's paired with the largest, most mature multi-language ecosystem of the group — which is its own kind of advantage once you have twelve services in six languages all needing to talk to each other.

It's also fair to say not everyone is convinced. A well-known critical essay, "Protobuffers Are Wrong," argues the design carries ad hoc rough edges and mostly solves a scale problem that few organizations outside Google actually have. It's worth reading once you've used the format for a while — not because it's the final word, but because knowing the strongest counter-argument sharpens your own judgment about when protobuf is the right tool and when it's reflexive habit.

So what did we actually gain?

We traded a format anyone can read in a text editor for one that needs a compiler step. In exchange, we got a schema that's checked instead of hoped-for, a wire format that doesn't care which of a dozen languages is on the other end, and compatibility rules that are a property of the encoding itself rather than a discipline every engineer has to remember. That trade doesn't make sense for a five-line config file. It makes enormous sense for a message shape that fifteen services, three teams, and four years of feature work are all going to depend on.

Keep that framing in mind as we go. Every chapter from here on — writing your first schema, seeing how a message actually becomes bytes, and especially how you evolve a schema without breaking anyone — is really just filling in the mechanics behind this one idea: the schema is the contract, and the tooling exists to keep everyone honest to it.

Before you turn the page
  1. Think of a bug you've hit (or heard about) caused by two systems silently disagreeing about a JSON field's type or presence. Would a compile-time schema have caught it?
  2. List two properties of your own project's data that would benefit least from protobuf's tradeoffs — small payloads, human-authored config, a one-off script. Knowing where not to reach for a tool is half of knowing the tool.
