Chapter 01 · Foundations

What Is Gravity, Really?

Everybody knows what gravity does: things fall. Drop your keys, they hit the floor. But what is it? Newton gave us a fabulous formula for how strong the pull is — and then, being honest, admitted he had no idea what the pull actually was. Two centuries later a patent clerk in Bern had a thought so simple it sounds like a trick. It wasn’t. It rebuilt the universe.

“The most incomprehensible thing about the world is that it is comprehensible. Let’s take the most everyday thing there is — falling — and refuse to stop asking why until it surprises us.”

Everything falls the same

Here is the first surprise, and it’s old — Galileo, around 1600. Take a heavy cannonball and a light musket ball, drop them together. Which lands first? Your gut says the heavy one. Your gut is wrong. They land together. Drop a hammer and a feather where there’s no air to cheat — an astronaut did exactly this on the Moon in 1971 — and they hit the dust at the same instant.

Sit with how weird that is. Gravity pulls harder on the heavy ball (more mass, more weight) — yet the heavy ball is also harder to move (more mass, more stubbornness). The two effects cancel exactly, for every object, always. The strength of the pull and the resistance to being pulled are secretly the same number. Newton wrote it down and moved on. Einstein stared at it.

The clue That two different-looking things — “gravitational mass” (how hard gravity grabs you) and “inertial mass” (how hard you are to shove) — are always identical is called the equivalence principle. It is the seed of all of general relativity. Nature is hinting that gravity isn’t really a force at all.

The happiest thought

In 1907 Einstein had what he later called “the happiest thought of my life.” It goes like this. Imagine you’re in a windowless elevator and the cable snaps. You, and your keys, and everything in the box, fall together. Let go of the keys and they don’t drop — they hang there beside you. Inside the falling box, gravity has completely vanished. You are weightless. This is not a metaphor: it is exactly why astronauts float. The Space Station isn’t “above gravity” — it’s falling, endlessly, and everything inside falls with it.

Now the flip side. Put the same box in deep space, far from anything, and tie a rocket to it pulling steadily. The floor rushes up to meet you; you feel planted, heavy. Drop your keys and they “fall.” Standing in that accelerating box feels exactly like standing on Earth. Einstein’s leap: it doesn’t just feel the same — it is the same. Gravity is not a mysterious tugging force reaching across space. Gravity is what you feel when something stops you from falling freely.

Think about it

Right now you feel your weight. Newton says: gravity is pulling you down. Einstein says the opposite is closer to the truth — the floor is pushing you up, shoving you off the natural, free-falling path you’d otherwise take. The only reason you feel “heavy” is that the ground won’t let you fall. Remove it, and the feeling of gravity disappears instantly.

Why this matters: If free-fall is the “natural” state and feels like nothing at all, then falling objects aren’t being pushed by a force. They’re coasting in a straight line — through a spacetime that’s been bent. That’s the whole book, and we’ll build it piece by piece.

So what’s actually doing the bending?

If gravity isn’t a force, and falling is really coasting in a straight line, then the “straight lines” themselves must be curved near a planet. Curved through what? Through spacetime — a single fabric woven from the three directions of space and the one direction of time. Before we can bend it, we have to meet it. Turn the page.
