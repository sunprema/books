Chapter 04 · The Strangest Consequence

Time Dilation

Time is not a universal drumbeat everyone marches to. It runs at different rates for different people — faster or slower depending on how they move and where they sit in gravity. This isn’t a clock malfunction or an illusion. Time itself flows at different speeds. And we measure it, every day, to nanoseconds.

The clock that must run slow

Build the simplest possible clock: two mirrors, a photon bouncing between them. Each round trip is one “tick.” Since light always travels at c, the tick rate is fixed — a perfect clock. Now watch such a clock fly past you. From your seat, the photon can’t just go straight up and down; the clock has moved sideways meanwhile, so the photon travels a longer, diagonal zig-zag. Longer path, same speed c → more time per tick. The moving clock ticks slower. Watch it happen:

A moving light clock ticks slow

Push the flyer toward c. Its photon has farther to travel each bounce, so it logs fewer ticks than your stationary clock. The factor is γ (gamma) — and it blows up as you approach light speed.

The gamma factor γ = 1 / √1 − v²/c²
At everyday speeds v is a whisper compared to c, so γ ≈ 1 and nobody notices. At 87% of c, γ = 2: moving clocks run at half rate. At 99.5%, γ = 10. The cost of speed is time.

The twin “paradox”

Send one twin on a near-light-speed round trip to a star while the other stays home. When the traveller returns, she is genuinely younger than her stay-at-home sibling — fewer ticks logged on her ship’s clocks, fewer heartbeats, fewer birthdays. It sounds like a contradiction (couldn’t each say the other was moving?), but it isn’t: only the traveller turns around, and that turnaround breaks the symmetry. One of them accelerated; one didn’t. The younger one is the one who felt the engines.

“People hear ‘paradox’ and think something’s broken. Nothing’s broken. The universe is just telling you, plainly, that time is personal. Your clock is yours. Move differently, and you keep different time. That’s not a bug — that’s the deepest thing we know.”

Gravity slows time too

Speed isn’t the only thing that stretches time. So does gravity. The deeper you sit in a gravitational well, the slower your clock runs compared to someone higher up. Your feet, closer to Earth’s center, age very slightly slower than your head. In 2010, physicists at NIST measured exactly this with a pair of atomic clocks raised just 33 centimetres apart — and saw the higher one tick faster. A third of a metre is enough. Slide a clock down toward a black hole and the effect becomes enormous:

A clock deep in a gravity well

Lower the deep clock toward the horizon. Its hand crawls relative to the far observer’s. Right at the horizon the rate hits zero — from far away, the falling clock appears to freeze forever (much more on that in Chapter 7).

Proof in your pocket: GPS

This is not exotic. The GPS in your phone depends on both effects at once, and it would be useless without correcting for them. Each satellite carries an atomic clock, and relativity pulls on it two ways:

EffectDirectionPer day
Gravity (satellite is higher, weaker gravity)clock runs faster+45 µs
Speed (satellite orbits at ~14,000 km/h)clock runs slower−7 µs
Netahead of ground clocks+38 µs
Why 38 microseconds matters Thirty-eight millionths of a second sounds like nothing. But GPS finds your position by timing light-speed signals, and in 38 µs light travels about 11 kilometres. Ignore relativity and your “you are here” dot would drift off by roughly 10 km every day — worthless within minutes. Engineers pre-tune each satellite’s clock before launch to cancel the shift. Every turn you take by GPS is Einstein, quietly getting it right.

Moving clocks, deep clocks — time bends exactly as the geometry demands. Now we can finally explain the thing that looked like magic in Chapter 1: why an orbit is nothing more than falling, done properly.
