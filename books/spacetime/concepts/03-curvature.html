Chapter 03 · The Big Idea

Mass Bends the Fabric

Here is all of general relativity in one sentence, from the physicist John Wheeler: spacetime tells matter how to move; matter tells spacetime how to curve. Two-way conversation. A planet dents the fabric around it; things nearby roll along the dent. That rolling is what we’ve been calling gravity.

“Don’t picture a rope pulling the Earth toward the Sun. Picture a valley. The Earth isn’t pulled — it’s just going as straight as it can, on ground that happens to be curved. No force required. Only geometry.”

The straightest possible line

On a flat table, the straightest line between two points is obvious. But on a curved surface — the skin of the Earth — “straight” means something subtler: the path that never turns left or right, only follows the ground. Fly “straight” from New York and you arc up over Greenland to reach Paris. That great-circle route is straight; the surface is curved. Such a shortest, straightest path is called a geodesic.

General relativity says every freely-falling object — a dropped apple, the Moon, a ray of starlight — travels a geodesic through spacetime. It’s going perfectly straight. The path only looks curved because the spacetime it’s crossing is curved by nearby mass. Orbits, arcs, falls: all just straight lines on a warped stage.

Honesty about the rubber sheet The stretched-sheet picture (and the grid above) is a useful lie. It shows space bending in a way we can see — but it secretly uses real gravity to make the marble roll “downhill,” and it leaves out the most important part: the bending of time. For everyday gravity — you, the Earth, a thrown ball — it’s almost entirely the time curvature that matters, not the space curvature. Keep the picture; just don’t trust it too far.

The part the sheet hides: curved time

Why does a ball you toss come back down, if nothing is pulling it? Because near the Earth, time itself runs a hair slower lower down than higher up (we’ll measure this next chapter). An object always ages as much as it possibly can along its path — and to do that, its straightest route through spacetime bends back toward where time ticks slower: toward the ground. What you see as “falling” is really your body seeking the path of most elapsed time. Gravity is time’s slope.

Even light has to bend

If mass curves the stage, then everything crossing that stage follows the curve — including light, which has no mass at all. A star’s beam grazing the Sun gets deflected. Einstein predicted the exact angle, and in 1919 an eclipse expedition led by Arthur Eddington measured starlight bending around the darkened Sun by just the amount he’d calculated. Overnight, Einstein was world-famous. Play with it:

The equation, without the fear

Einstein’s field equations are often written Gμν = 8πG/c⁴ · Tμν You don’t need the indices to get the poetry. The left side, G, measures how spacetime is curved. The right side, T, measures the mass and energy sitting there. The equals sign is Wheeler’s sentence made exact: matter on the right, geometry on the left, forever set equal.

Why this matters: Everything else in this book — time dilation, orbits, black holes, gravitational waves — is just this one equation, solved in different situations. Learn to read it as “stuff curves space,” and you’ve got the gist Einstein chased for ten years.

We keep promising that time bends. It’s time to prove it — with clocks you can watch tick at different rates, and a real technology in your pocket that would fail within minutes if we got it wrong.
