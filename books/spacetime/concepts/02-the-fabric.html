Chapter 02 · The Stage

The Fabric of Spacetime

We casually say “space” and “time” as if they were separate — a stage, and a clock ticking beside it. Einstein’s 1905 relativity welded them into one four-dimensional fabric: spacetime. To see why they had to merge, we start with the strangest fact in physics — a fact about light.

The speed everyone agrees on

Light travels at c = 299,792,458 metres per second — about 300,000 km/s, seven and a half times around the Earth in the time it takes to say “light.” That number is famous. Here is the part that broke physics: everyone measures the same c, no matter how they’re moving. Chase a light beam at 99% of c and it still races away from you at the full c. Fly toward it and — still c. The beam refuses to let you gain or lose on it.

“It’s not that the speed of light is fast. It’s that it’s stubborn. And when a speed refuses to change no matter how you chase it, something else has to give — and the things that give are space and time themselves.”

Think about what “speed” is: distance divided by time. If everyone gets the same answer for light’s speed but they’re moving differently, then they cannot be agreeing on distance and time separately. One person’s “one metre” and “one second” are another person’s slightly different mix. Space and time trade into each other, like two currencies pegged by a single exchange rate — and that rate is c.

Drawing spacetime

Physicists picture this with a spacetime diagram: time runs up the page, space runs across it. A thing that just sits still still moves upward — because it’s travelling through time. The path an object traces is its worldline. Because light sets the top speed, its worldlines are fixed 45° diagonals, forming a cone. That cone divides everything you could ever affect (inside) from everything forever out of reach (outside).

What everyone does agree on Different observers disagree about lengths and durations — but they always agree on one combined quantity, the spacetime interval, which mixes space and time with a telltale minus sign: s² = (c·Δt)² − Δx² That minus sign is the whole difference between time and space. It’s why you can revisit a place but never a moment.

The block universe

Here’s a dizzying way to hold it. If space and time are one fabric, then the past and future are as “out there” as distant places — the whole of history is a fixed four-dimensional block, and what we call now is just the particular slice we’re passing through. Whether you take that literally or as a useful picture, the math doesn’t care: it treats when and where on the same footing.

So far the fabric is flat and featureless — the empty stage of special relativity, where light rules and clocks are honest. The magic starts when we let something heavy sit on it. Mass, it turns out, bends the fabric. That bending is gravity, and it’s next.

Sources: speed of light, exact SI value 299,792,458 m/s (BIPM definition of the metre). Special relativity and the invariant interval per Einstein (1905); Minkowski’s spacetime formulation (1908). Light-cone treatment: Hartle, Gravity.
