Chapter 08 · The View From Here

The Universe This Opens

One idea — that mass curves spacetime — has carried us from a falling elevator to the edge of a black hole. But the same idea keeps paying out. It predicts ripples in the fabric, a cosmos that grows, and a frontier where our best theories fail. Here’s where the story is still being written — maybe by you.

Ripples in spacetime

If mass curves the fabric, then mass that moves — two black holes whirling around each other — should send ripples racing outward at light speed, like waves from a struck drum. Einstein predicted these gravitational waves in 1916, then doubted anyone could ever detect something so faint. He was almost right.

On 14 September 2015, the twin LIGO detectors caught it: a wave from two black holes, 36 and 29 times the Sun’s mass, that had spiralled together and merged 1.3 billion light-years away. In the final fraction of a second, they radiated the energy of three entire Suns as gravitational waves — for that instant, outshining every star in the visible universe combined, not in light but in pure ripples of spacetime. By the time the wave reached Earth it stretched LIGO’s 4-km arms by less than one ten-thousandth the width of a proton. We measured it anyway.

A new sense For all of history we studied the sky with light. Gravitational waves are an entirely new sense — a way to hear the universe through a channel light can’t carry. We’ve since heard dozens of black-hole mergers, and in 2017 a neutron-star collision seen in both waves and light at once, confirming where the gold in your ring was forged. LIGO’s founders won the 2017 Nobel Prize.

Spacetime on the largest scale

Feed the whole universe into Einstein’s equations and something wonderful falls out: spacetime doesn’t have to sit still. It can stretch. Distant galaxies really are rushing apart — not flying through space, but carried along as the space between them expands. Run the film backward and everything was once unimaginably hot and dense: the Big Bang, 13.8 billion years ago. The same geometry that bends a ray of starlight governs the expansion of everything there is.

And it’s not slowing down as gravity should demand — it’s accelerating, driven by something we’ve named dark energy without yet knowing what it is. That’s roughly 68% of the universe. Another 27% is dark matter, invisible mass we detect only by the way it curves spacetime around galaxies. Everything you have ever seen or touched — all the stars, all the atoms — is the leftover 5%.

Where the map ends

This is the honest, thrilling edge. General relativity describes the very large — stars, galaxies, spacetime itself — with flawless precision. Quantum mechanics describes the very small — atoms, particles — equally flawlessly. But push both to the same place at once, the center of a black hole or the first instant of the Big Bang, and they contradict each other. No one yet has a working theory of quantum gravity. Strings, loops, and stranger ideas are all still contenders.

“I was born not knowing, and have had only a little time to change that here and there. The fact that we don’t understand what’s at the center of a black hole isn’t a failure — it’s the most exciting sentence in physics. It means there’s a door, and nobody’s walked through it yet.”

What to carry away

Gravity is not a force but the shape of spacetime. Mass and energy bend that shape; everything else — falling apples, planetary orbits, bending starlight, slowing clocks, black holes, and ripples across the cosmos — is just things moving as straight as they can across a curved stage. One idea. The whole sky.
