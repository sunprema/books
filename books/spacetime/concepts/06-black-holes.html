Chapter 06 · The Deep End

Black Holes

A black hole is what happens when the ideas in this book are pushed to their limit: so much mass, packed so tight, that spacetime folds completely over on itself and seals shut. Once the strangest theoretical curiosity in physics, black holes have now been photographed. They are real, they are common, and one sits at the heart of our own galaxy.

Escape velocity, taken to the extreme

Throw a ball up and it comes back. Throw it faster and it goes higher. Throw it at 11.2 km/s — Earth’s escape velocity — and it never comes back at all; it coasts away forever. Escape velocity depends on how much mass you’re fighting and how close you start:

Escape velocity vesc = √2GM / r More mass M, or a smaller radius r to launch from, means a higher wall to climb. Now ask the dangerous question: what if we shrink r until the wall reaches the one speed nothing can exceed — the speed of light?
Squeeze a star until light can’t escape

Take the Sun’s entire mass and compress it. As the radius shrinks, escape velocity climbs. Squeeze the Sun inside about 3 km and escape velocity reaches c — light itself can no longer leave. You’ve made a black hole.

The event horizon & the Schwarzschild radius

The radius at which escape velocity equals c is the event horizon — the point of no return. Karl Schwarzschild found it in Einstein’s equations in 1916, while serving on the WWI front, and its size bears his name:

Schwarzschild radius rs = 2GM / c² It scales simply with mass. Squeeze the Sun inside 2.95 km, or the whole Earth inside 8.87 mm — a marble — and it becomes a black hole. The horizon isn’t a surface you could touch; it’s a one-way membrane in spacetime. Cross it, and every path leads inward.

The anatomy of a black hole

  • Event horizon — the one-way boundary at rs. From outside you see only blackness, because no light escapes it. It is the “surface” we photograph.
  • Photon sphere — at 1.5 × rs, gravity bends light so hard that photons orbit in circles. Its glow forms the bright ring in the pictures.
  • Accretion disk — gas spiralling in, compressed and heated until it outshines whole galaxies. This is how we spot black holes: by the fury of what they eat.
  • Singularity — the center, where our equations predict infinite density and simply break down. Nobody knows what really sits there (Chapter 8).

We have the photographs

On 10 April 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope — eight radio dishes across the planet, linked into one Earth-sized eye — released the first-ever image of a black hole: the six-and-a-half-billion-solar-mass giant in galaxy M87, a glowing orange ring around a dark shadow, 55 million light-years away. On 12 May 2022 they unveiled the one at our own galaxy’s heart, Sagittarius A* — 4 million Suns, 27,000 light-years away, sitting quietly the whole time. The rings matched Einstein’s predictions to the pixel.

Why this matters: A theory born from a thought about a falling elevator now predicts, correctly, the shape of a shadow cast by a monster across the galaxy. That is what it looks like when an idea is true.

We’ve stood safely outside and looked. But the real adventure — and the real strangeness — is to ask what happens if you fall in. Take a breath.
