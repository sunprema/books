Chapter 05 · Motion on a Curved Stage

Falling Forever

The Moon has been falling toward the Earth for four and a half billion years and has never once hit it. That’s not a riddle — it’s the literal truth, and once it clicks, orbits stop being mysterious and start being obvious. An orbit is just falling with enough sideways speed to keep missing the ground.

Newton’s cannon on a mountain

Newton drew the picture in the 1680s. Put a cannon on an impossibly tall mountain, above the air, and fire it horizontally. A weak shot arcs down and lands a mile away. A stronger shot lands farther — the ground curving away beneath it the whole time. Fire hard enough and the ground curves away just as fast as the ball falls. Now the ball never lands. It has fallen around the world. That’s an orbit. Try it:

Newton’s cannonball

Set the muzzle speed and hit Fire. Too slow and it crashes back down; hit the sweet spot and it settles into a closed orbit; push harder and it swings out into an ellipse, then escapes altogether. Same fall, different sideways speed.

Why astronauts float Astronauts on the Space Station aren’t weightless because they’ve “escaped gravity” — gravity up there is still about 90% as strong as on the ground. They float because they, and their station, are in continuous free fall, exactly the elevator of Chapter 1. They’re falling around the Earth at 28,000 km/h, and everything falls together, so nothing presses on anything. Free fall is weightlessness.

The relativity upgrade

Newton got the answer right and the story wrong. He imagined a force reaching invisibly from the Earth to grip the ball. Einstein removes the grip entirely. The orbiting ball feels no force — ask the astronaut, weightless the whole way. It is simply coasting along a geodesic, the straightest available path through the spacetime the Earth has curved. The ellipse you see is a straight line, drawn on a bent page.

“Newton said: a force holds the Moon in its path. Einstein said: there is no rope, there is no pull — the Moon is going as straight as it knows how, and the straight line just happens to close into a loop because the Sun has bent the neighbourhood. Same Moon. Deeper truth.”

The clue Newton couldn’t explain

For most orbits the two theories agree to spectacular precision. But Mercury, whipping around close to the Sun’s deep curvature, gave Newton trouble: the long axis of its ellipse slowly rotates — precesses — and a tiny sliver of that rotation, 43 arcseconds per century, refused to be explained by the tug of the other planets. Astronomers even invented a hidden planet, “Vulcan,” to account for it. It doesn’t exist.

When Einstein plugged Mercury into his new equations in 1915, out fell 43 arcseconds per century — not approximately, exactly. He wrote that he had heart palpitations for days. His geometry hadn’t just matched Newton; it had fixed him.

Everything so far has been gravity being gentle. But the cannonball hinted at something violent: fire hard enough and you escape. So turn the question around. What if an object’s gravity were so ferocious that nothing — not even light — could ever fire hard enough to leave? Then you’ve made a black hole.

Sources: Newton’s cannonball, A Treatise of the System of the World (1728). ISS orbital speed ~7.66 km/s and ~90% surface gravity (NASA). Mercury perihelion advance 43″/century explained by GR (Einstein, 1915).
