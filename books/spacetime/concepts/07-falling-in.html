Chapter 07 · The Plunge

Falling Into a Black Hole

Let’s do the experiment we can only do in imagination: fall in. Two of us — you leap, I hover at a safe distance and watch. Here is the part that catches everyone off guard: we will not agree on what happens to you. Not because one of us is fooled, but because near a black hole, time bends so hard that our two stories are both completely true.

Drag to orbit · scroll to zoom. The dark shadow is the horizon; the thin bright ring is light circling on the photon sphere; the warm band is the accretion disk of doomed gas.

What I see: you freeze and redden

From my safe perch, I watch you fall — and fall slower. Remember gravitational time dilation from Chapter 4: the deeper you go, the slower your clock runs by my reckoning. As you approach the horizon that slowing runs away to infinity. You appear to freeze, hovering just above the horizon, moving ever more sluggishly and never quite arriving. Meanwhile the light coming off you is stretched to longer and longer wavelengths — gravitational redshift — sliding from white to red to infrared to invisible. You fade to black. I never actually see you cross.

“To me you hang there forever, dimming like an ember. It’s tempting to say ‘but that’s just what it looks like.’ No — that is your story in my time. The trouble is that you have a time of your own, and it tells a very different tale.”

What you feel: nothing special, then everything

In your own frame, no clock stutters, no ember freezes. You sail across the horizon at a definite moment, feeling nothing — for a big enough black hole there’s no wall, no flash, no barrier to mark the most important boundary in the universe. You’ve just crossed the point of no return without a bump. But now every possible path, every direction you could point a rocket, leads to the center. Falling inward is no longer a choice; it’s the future, as unavoidable as next Tuesday.

Spaghettification Gravity pulls harder on whatever’s nearer. Fall in feet-first and your feet are tugged harder than your head — the difference is a tidal force, and as you near the center it grows without limit. It stretches you head-to-toe while squeezing you side-to-side, drawing you into a thread. Hawking called it spaghettification. For a stellar-mass black hole this shreds you long before the center; for a supermassive one the tides at the horizon are gentle, and you’d cross intact — only to meet the same fate deeper in.

The singularity: where physics runs out

At the center, general relativity predicts a singularity — a point of infinite density and infinite curvature where the equations return nonsense. That infinity is not an answer; it’s the theory raising its hand to say “I don’t know.” To describe something so small and so heavy at once, we’d need to marry general relativity to quantum mechanics — and those two theories, each flawless in its own domain, flatly refuse to shake hands. What’s really at the center is one of the great open questions in physics.

Black holes aren’t quite black

In 1974 Stephen Hawking dropped a bombshell. Combine quantum theory with the horizon and black holes must glow, ever so faintly, with Hawking radiation. The empty space at the horizon churns with quantum fluctuations, and the black hole slowly leaks energy — which means it slowly loses mass. Black holes evaporate.

A patience beyond the universe

Don’t hold your breath. A black hole the mass of the Sun has a Hawking temperature of about 60 billionths of a kelvin — far colder than empty space — and would take on the order of 10⁶⁷ years to evaporate. That’s a 1 followed by 67 zeros, against a universe merely 1.4×10⁶⁰ years old. Smaller holes evaporate faster and end in a final flash; giants like M87* are still growing. But the principle rewrote physics: black holes have a temperature, an entropy, and a lifespan.

Why this matters: Hawking radiation ties gravity, quantum mechanics, and thermodynamics into a single knot — and raises the information paradox: what happens to everything that fell in when the hole finally winks out? Physicists are still arguing. That argument is where the next revolution may hide.

You fell in; I watched you freeze; you felt nothing, then everything. Both true. If a single idea about falling can lead here — to frozen light, threads of matter, and glowing holes that evaporate over 10⁶⁷ years — imagine what else it predicts. In the last chapter, we listen to the universe ring.

Sources: gravitational time dilation & redshift near horizons (Schwarzschild solution). Tidal “spaghettification” (Hawking, A Brief History of Time). Hawking radiation and solar-mass evaporation time ~10⁶⁷ yr, T~6×10⁻⁸ K (Hawking, 1974–75).
