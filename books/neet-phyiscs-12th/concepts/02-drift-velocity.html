Chapter 02 · Current & Drift

The Electrons Crawl,
the Light Is Instant

Here is a puzzle that should bother you. Flip the switch on your wall and the lamp across the room lights up at once. Obvious, right? Now here is the twist: the electrons carrying that current are so slow you could out-walk them. In a copper wire they drift forward at about a millimetre per second. So how does the lamp know, instantly, that you flipped the switch?

Just how slow is “drift”?

Inside copper the outer electrons roam free, a gas of charge sloshing through a lattice of fixed positive ions. Even with no battery they are anything but still — thermal energy hurls them around at random at roughly 105 metres per second, in every direction, so the average motion is zero. No net flow, no current.

Connect a battery and you tip the whole space with an electric field. On top of that furious random jitter, each electron now gains a tiny, steady nudge backward toward the + terminal. That gentle sideways bias is the drift velocity. NCERT works it out for an ordinary copper wire carrying a few amperes:

vd = I / (n·A·e) ≈ 1.1 × 10−3 m/s = 1.1 mm/s

One millimetre per second. An electron leaving the switch would take hours to physically reach the lamp. And yet the lamp lit instantly. Watch the paradox play out:

Flip the switch, watch two speeds at once

Blue dots are electrons; the amber one is tagged so you can follow it. Notice they jitter wildly even with the switch off. Hit Flip the switch: the dashed “field front” races down the wire and the lamp lights immediately — while your tagged electron has barely budged from its dashed starting line.

Resolving the paradox

The trick is that the lamp doesn’t wait for these particular electrons to arrive. When you close the switch, an electric field is set up along the entire wire almost instantly — at nearly the speed of light. That field tells every electron in the circuit, including the ones already sitting in the lamp’s filament, to start drifting at the same moment. It’s like a pipe already full of water: open the tap and water leaves the far end at once, even though any single drop is barely crawling.

Three wildly different speeds — all true at once
WhatSpeedWhy it matters
Thermal jitter~105 m/sRandom; averages to zero, no current
Drift of electrons~10−3 m/sThe actual net flow = the current
The signal / field~3×108 m/sWhy the lamp lights instantly

“People think electricity is electrons whooshing down a wire like water down a hose. It isn’t. The electrons barely move — it’s the field that flies. What travels at nearly light-speed is the news to start pushing, not the pushers.”

And this is where Ohm’s law comes from

Between collisions with the lattice, an electron accelerates in the field; each collision randomises it again. Average over that stop-start dance and the drift speed comes out proportional to the field — which is exactly Ohm’s law, V = IR, seen from the inside. Resistance is just how often the drifting electrons bump the lattice. Heat a wire and the ions jiggle more, the bumps come faster, resistance rises — the everyday fact that hot wires resist more, explained from first principles.

Exam trap Current direction is defined as the flow of positive charge — opposite to the electrons’ actual drift. The electrons go one way; “conventional current” points the other. Both are correct; they’re the same fact stated with opposite sign.
Grounded in NCERT Physics, Class 12, Ch. 3 (Current Electricity): drift of electrons, Example 3.1 (vd ≈ 1.1 mm/s for a copper conductor), and the origin of Ohm’s law from the drift picture. Signal speed ~ speed of light along the conductor.
