Chapter 04 · Electromagnetic Induction

Move a Magnet,
Make Electricity

Nearly every watt of electricity you have ever used — from the grid, from a dam, a coal plant, a wind turbine, a nuclear station — was made in exactly one way: a magnet was moved near a coil of wire. That’s it. That’s the whole secret of the power industry, and Michael Faraday found it in 1831 with a bar magnet, a loop of wire, and a needle that twitched.

The idea of flux

First we need a word for “how much magnetic field is threading through a loop.” Picture the field lines as rain and the loop as a ring held out in it: the magnetic flux ΦB is how many lines pass through the ring’s opening. Hold the ring face-on to the field and flux is large; turn it edge-on and the lines slip past without threading — flux drops to zero.

Faraday’s insight was that a steady flux, however large, does nothing. What makes electricity is flux that changes. The faster it changes, the bigger the push:

ε = − dΦB / dt

The induced voltage (“emf”) equals the rate at which flux changes. Move a magnet twice as fast and you get twice the voltage. Wind the coil with a hundred turns instead of one and every turn adds its share. Try it — and notice what happens when the magnet stops:

Faraday’s experiment, in your hands

Drag the bar magnet toward and away from the coil. The galvanometer needle swings only while the magnet is moving — hold it still and the needle falls to zero. Reverse the motion and the needle flips the other way. Or press Auto-swing to turn it into a generator making alternating current.

Lenz’s law: nature pushes back

That minus sign in Faraday’s law is not decoration. It’s Lenz’s law, and it says: the induced current always flows so as to oppose the very change that created it. Push a magnet’s north pole toward the coil and the coil answers by presenting its own north pole back, shoving against you. Pull the magnet away and the coil reaches out to hold it. The coil always resists.

Why it has to be this way — energy conservation Suppose the coil helped your motion instead of resisting it. Then the magnet would speed up on its own, making more current, speeding up more — free energy forever. The universe doesn’t allow that. The push-back you feel is exactly the mechanical work that gets converted into electrical energy. A generator is hard to turn precisely because it’s making power. Turn off all the lights in a city and its turbines literally spin easier.

“Faraday couldn’t write the equations — Maxwell did that later. What Faraday had was something rarer: he could see the invisible lines, thick in his mind’s eye, and he asked what happens when you cut across them. The answer turned a magnet and a coil into civilisation’s power supply.”

Run it backwards Push current into a coil in a magnetic field and it’s forced to turn — that’s a motor. A generator and a motor are the same machine read in opposite directions: motion → electricity, or electricity → motion. The next chapter asks what happens when you drive one of these coils with electricity that’s already a sine wave.
