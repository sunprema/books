Chapter 05 · Alternating Current

Tuning the Invisible

Right now, hundreds of radio stations are pouring through the room you’re in — every one of them, all at once, all overlapping in the same air. Yet you turn a single knob and hear exactly one, clean and alone. How does a circuit pluck one voice out of a shouting crowd? The answer is resonance, the same trick that lets a swing build from tiny pushes and a wine glass shatter at one pure note.

Electricity that changes its mind

The current from the wall doesn’t flow one way — it reverses fifty times a second (a sine wave, straight from the spinning generator of the last chapter). Feed alternating current to different components and they behave in wonderfully opposite ways:

  • A capacitor hates slow change and loves fast change — it passes high frequencies, blocks low ones. Its opposition falls as frequency rises.
  • An inductor (a coil) is the opposite — by Lenz’s law it fights any change in current, so it passes low frequencies and chokes high ones.

Put both in a circuit with a resistor and you have an LCR circuit — two stubborn opposites in the same loop. At most frequencies they disagree and the current is feeble. But there is exactly one special frequency where the capacitor’s opposition and the inductor’s opposition cancel perfectly. There, they get out of each other’s way, only the resistor is left, and the current surges. That is resonance:

Tune a radio by hand

The curve is how much current flows at each drive frequency. Slide Tune toward the dashed line f₀ and the current spikes — a station locks in and the signal bars light up green. Off resonance, all you get is static. Now change C: watch f₀ slide onto a different station. That knob on an old radio was a variable capacitor.

Sharpness — the quality factor

A good radio must do more than peak at the right frequency; it must peak narrowly, so the station next door doesn’t bleed in. How sharp the resonance is — how choosy the circuit — is measured by the quality factor Q. A high-Q circuit has a tall, thin spike: exquisitely selective. A low-Q circuit has a broad, lazy hump that lets neighbours through. Less resistance means higher Q means a fussier, cleaner radio.

“Resonance is nature’s way of listening. Push a swing at just its own rhythm and tiny shoves pile into a huge arc; push at any other rhythm and you fight yourself. The LCR circuit has a natural rhythm too — and it answers, loudly, only to the frequency that matches it. Tuning a radio is teaching a circuit which rhythm to fall in love with.”

The double edge Resonance is powerful precisely because small inputs build to large outputs — wonderful in a radio, dangerous in a bridge. The same mathematics that tunes your phone once helped shake the Tacoma Narrows bridge apart in the wind. Amplification doesn’t care whether you wanted it.
