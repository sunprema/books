Chapter 06 · Electromagnetic Waves

Light Is Just This

Everything in this book has been building to one sentence, maybe the most beautiful in all of physics. We’ve seen charges make electric fields, and moving charges make magnetic fields, and changing magnetic fields make electric fields. James Clerk Maxwell asked one more question — and the answer was light itself. Not light explained by electromagnetism. Light equals electromagnetism.

Maxwell’s missing piece

Faraday had shown that a changing magnetic field creates an electric field (that’s Chapter 4). Maxwell, staring at the equations, felt a gap where symmetry demanded one: shouldn’t a changing electric field, in turn, create a magnetic field? He added exactly that term — the displacement current — and completed the set of four equations that bear his name. Now the fields could feed each other:

The self-making wave A changing electric field creates a magnetic field. That magnetic field is also changing, so it creates an electric field. Which is changing, so it creates a magnetic field… Each one’s death is the other’s birth. The pair doesn’t need charges or wires — it lifts off into empty space and propagates on its own, forever leapfrogging.

When Maxwell computed how fast this self-sustaining ripple must travel, the speed fell out of two constants he could measure on a lab bench — the electric constant ε0 and the magnetic constant μ0:

c = 1 / √(μ0 ε0) ≈ 3 × 108 m/s

Three hundred million metres per second — the measured speed of light. Maxwell had not been studying light at all; he’d been studying sparks and coils and capacitors. The speed of his imaginary electromagnetic ripple came out equal to light’s, and he understood in a flash: light is the ripple. Two threads of physics that had run separately since antiquity — optics and electricity — were one thread all along.

The whole spectrum is one wave

The red curve is the electric field E, the blue is the magnetic field B — perpendicular, in step, sailing right at speed c. Slide λ to stretch or squeeze the wave and watch the marker travel the spectrum: radio, microwave, infrared, the thin band of visible light, ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma. Same wave. Same speed. Only the wavelength changes.

The rainbow you can’t see

Here is the humbling part. Visible light — every colour your eyes have ever known — is a sliver of the spectrum, one narrow octave between about 400 and 700 nanometres. On either side stretches an enormous, invisible world made of the exact same stuff. The warmth of the sun (infrared), the signal to your phone (microwave & radio), a dentist’s image (X-ray), the tan on your skin (ultraviolet) — all identical electromagnetic waves, differing only in how tightly the fields wave up and down.

“From a long view of the history of the world — seen from, say, ten thousand years from now — there can be little doubt that the most significant event of the 19th century will be judged as Maxwell’s discovery of the laws of electromagnetism. Wars will shrink to insignificance beside it. Light is a wave in the fields we’ve been playing with all along.”

The whole book in one breath Charges make fields (Ch. 1). Fields push charges into currents (Ch. 2). Moving charges and magnets are two views of one force (Ch. 3). Change one and you make the other (Ch. 4–5). And their endless mutual creation, cut loose into space, is light (Ch. 6). One invisible machine, running the world — and now you can see it.
