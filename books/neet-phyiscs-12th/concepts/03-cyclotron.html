Chapter 03 · Moving Charges & Magnetism

Caught in a Circle

A magnet does nothing to a stationary charge. Absolutely nothing. But let that charge move, and the magnet grabs it — not to speed it up or slow it down, but to shove it sideways. Keep shoving something sideways and it goes in a circle. That single, strange sideways push is behind the picture tube, the mass spectrometer, the particle accelerator, and the aurora glowing over the poles.

The strangest force in the book

The magnetic force on a charge q moving with velocity v through a field B is the Lorentz force:

F = q · v × B   → magnitude  F = qvB sinθ

Read the cross-product carefully, because it’s where the weirdness lives. The force is perpendicular to the velocity — always at right angles to wherever the charge is heading. A force that’s always sideways can never do work; it never changes the particle’s speed, only its direction. And a constant-magnitude force forever perpendicular to the motion is the exact recipe for uniform circular motion.

Balancing the books Set the magnetic push equal to the centripetal force a circle needs, mv²/r = qvB, and solve for the radius:
r = m v / q B
Faster particle → bigger circle. Stronger field → tighter circle. Heavier particle → bigger circle. That last one is how a mass spectrometer weighs atoms: fire ions through a known field and measure the radius of their arc.
A charge trapped in a magnetic field

The little crosses mean B points into the page. Green arrow = velocity, red arrow = the sideways magnetic force, always aimed at the centre. Turn up v and the circle grows; turn up B and it tightens. Watch the readout: the time for one loop never changes with speed.

The miracle that makes a cyclotron work

Here is the beautiful part. A faster particle travels a bigger circle — but it also has farther to go, and it goes proportionally faster. The two effects cancel exactly. The time to complete one loop, and therefore the frequency of going round, depends only on the field and the particle, never on its speed:

fc = q B / 2πm   (the cyclotron frequency)

That constancy is the whole trick of E. O. Lawrence’s cyclotron. Put the charge in a magnetic field so it circles, and give it a little electric kick twice per loop, always perfectly in time because the rhythm never drifts. Each kick adds energy; the circle spirals outward; the particle emerges at enormous speed. A metronome you can never fall out of step with, set by nothing but q, B, and m.

“Isn’t it wonderful? The particle speeds up, the circle gets bigger, but it keeps perfect time — so you don’t have to chase it with your electric field. Nature handed us a free clock, and Lawrence noticed. That’s what a good physicist does: find the thing that stays the same while everything else changes.”

One more wrinkle If the velocity isn’t purely across the field — if part of it runs along B — that part feels no force and just coasts. Circle + coast = a helix. It’s why charged particles from the Sun spiral down Earth’s field lines into the poles and light up the aurora.
Grounded in NCERT Physics, Class 12, Ch. 4 (Moving Charges and Magnetism): the Lorentz force F = q v×B, the circular-motion radius r = mv/qB, the cyclotron frequency f = qB/2πm (independent of speed), and the cyclotron. Helical motion & the aurora.
