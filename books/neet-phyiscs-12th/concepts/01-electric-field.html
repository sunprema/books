Chapter 01 · Charges & Fields

The Field Nobody Can See

Run a plastic comb through your hair on a dry day and hold it over torn bits of paper. The paper jumps up to meet it — across a gap, through nothing but air. No string, no glue, no touch. Something reaches out from the comb and grabs the paper. Physics has a name for that “something,” and learning to see it is the first step into the whole electromagnetic story.

First, the force between two charges

Rub two objects and electrons hop from one to the other; one is left with a shortage (positive), the other with a surplus (negative). Put two charges a distance r apart and the force between them is Coulomb’s law — and notice how exactly it echoes Newton’s gravity, just with charge in place of mass:

F = k · q1q2 / r2   ,   k = 1 / 4πε0 ≈ 9 × 109 N·m2/C2

Like signs push apart; opposite signs pull together; and the strength dies off as the square of the distance. Double the gap, quarter the force. That’s the whole law. But Coulomb’s law has a puzzle hiding in it that bothered physicists for two centuries: how does one charge know the other is there? There’s empty space between them. What carries the message?

Faraday’s answer: the field

Michael Faraday — who could barely do the algebra — had the deepest idea. Don’t think of charge tugging charge across a void. Think of each charge filling the space around it with a condition, an invisible tension in every direction. Call it the electric field. A second charge doesn’t feel the first one directly — it only feels the field right where it sits. The field is the messenger.

We make this precise by asking: if I placed a tiny +1 coulomb test charge at a point, what force would it feel? That force-per-charge is the field there:

E = F / q0   (units: newtons per coulomb)
Paint the field with your hands

Every arrow shows which way a +1 test charge would be shoved there, and how hard (longer, darker = stronger). Drag either charge around. Flip a sign and watch the whole pattern reorganise. Hit Release a test charge to send little green explorers surfing down the field lines — that path is a field line.

Superposition — the quiet superpower With many charges around, you don’t need new physics. Work out the field from each charge alone, then add the arrows tip-to-tail. That’s it. The whole tangled pattern in the figure is just two simple fields added at every point. Nature adds vectors.

Reading the lines

Field lines are Faraday’s bookkeeping made visible. They stream out of positive charge and into negative charge; where they crowd together the field is strong; they never cross (the field can’t point two ways at once). A single rule about lines replaces a page of vector arithmetic.

“You might say the field is just a way of talking — bookkeeping for forces. But wait until Chapter 6, when we shake a magnet and a field appears out where there’s no charge at all, carrying real energy. The field isn’t bookkeeping. It’s as real as the chair you’re sitting on.”

The one idea to carry forward A charge doesn’t act on distant things directly. It fills space with a field, and the field does the pushing, locally, right where the next charge is. Hold onto that — everything else in this book is what happens when fields start to move.
