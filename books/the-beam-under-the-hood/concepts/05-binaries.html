Chapter 05 · The Sharp Edge

Binaries & the Large-Binary Trap

The one thing we share — and the tidy little disaster it can cause.

Everything so far has been about not sharing. Private heaps, copied messages, isolated deaths. Now meet the deliberate exception. Binaries — the blobs of bytes behind your strings, your JSON payloads, your file contents — are big and get passed around a lot, and copying a megabyte on every send would be silly. So the BEAM shares them. That sharing is a wonderful optimisation, and it is also the source of the single most famous "why won't my memory come down?" mystery in all of Erlang. Let's understand both halves.

Two kinds of binary

The BEAM splits binaries at a threshold: 64 bytes. A binary of 64 bytes or fewer is a heap binary — it lives right on the process heap and is copied like any ordinary term. Small, cheap, unremarkable. A binary larger than 64 bytes is a refc binary (reference-counted): its bytes live off in a separate, shared, global binary area, and on the process heap sits only a small structure called a ProcBin that points at the blob and counts as one reference to it.

The trap, told as a story

Now watch how the good idea turns into the famous bug. That off-heap blob is freed only when its reference count hits zero — that is, only when every process holding a ProcBin to it has garbage-collected and dropped its pointer. And here's the collision: a ProcBin is tiny. From the garbage collector's point of view, a process holding a hundred ProcBins looks nearly empty — a hundred little pointers, a few kilobytes of heap. The GC sees no pressure, so it doesn't run. But each of those "tiny" pointers may be pinning a multi-megabyte blob alive off-heap.

So picture a process that reads big payloads, keeps a reference to each, and is otherwise quiet — it rarely allocates, so it rarely collects, so it rarely drops its ProcBins, so the blobs never get freed. Your :observer shows the process using a few KB of heap while the machine's total memory climbs and climbs into the gigabytes. That gap — small heap, huge real footprint — is the tell-tale fingerprint of a binary leak.

The classic leak

Off-heap binary memory is invisible to a process's ordinary heap size, and it will not come down until the holding process garbage-collects. A busy system with a few idle-but-reference-holding processes can bleed memory for hours with nothing in the "process heap" numbers to explain it. Every seasoned Erlang operator has been bitten by this exactly once, and never forgotten it.

The sub-binary twist

It gets one turn more subtle. When you match a piece out of a binary — a header, a field, a single line — the BEAM often doesn't copy those bytes. It hands you a sub-binary: a tiny reference that says "bytes 40 through 60 of that big blob." Efficient! But that little slice still holds a reference to the whole original binary. Parse a 10 MB file, keep one 20-byte field from it, and you may be keeping all 10 MB alive to hold that one field. The small thing pins the big thing.

How to break the leak

Once you can see it, the cures are direct:

iex — detach a slice, and force a stubborn holder to let go# A tiny slice you want to KEEP but that pins a huge parent:
field = :binary.copy(:binary.part(big, 40, 20))
# copy/1 makes a fresh, standalone binary → the 10 MB parent can now be freed.

# A quiet process pinning blobs: make it hibernate between jobs.
# hibernate does a full sweep and shrinks the heap, dropping stale ProcBins:
{:noreply, state, :hibernate}   # a GenServer callback can return this

# Or force the issue when you must:
:erlang.garbage_collect(pid)
Find it with recon

The tool built for exactly this is recon. :recon.bin_leak(10) forces GC across processes and reports the ten that freed the most binary memory — i.e. the ten that were hoarding it. It's the fastest way to turn "memory is climbing and I don't know why" into "these three processes, right here."

So what did shared binaries buy us? The freedom to pass big blobs around a huge system almost for free — no megabyte copies on every send, exactly what a web server shuffling payloads needs. The bill is subtle and it's the honest kind: memory that hides off-heap, freed on a schedule the garbage collector — not you — decides. Know the 64-byte line, know that a small reference can pin a large blob, and the scariest memory graph in Erlang becomes a five-minute fix.

Try it yourself

  1. Build a 1 MB binary and check Process.info(self(), :binary) — the list of refc binaries this process holds. Watch it grow as you keep references.
  2. Match a 10-byte sub-binary out of a big one, drop the big one, and confirm memory doesn't fall. Now wrap the slice in :binary.copy/1 and confirm it does.
  3. Add :recon to a project and run :recon.bin_leak(5) under load. Meet your hoarders.
