Chapter 08 · It All Adds Up

Why LiveView Scales

Seven chapters of machinery, assembled into one answer.

You ship LiveView. Every connected user holds an open websocket and a little server-side process that remembers their state, and somehow one ordinary machine can hold an enormous crowd of them at once. This isn't luck, and it isn't a framework trick painted over a slow runtime. It's every idea in this book, stacked. Let's put them together and watch the whole thing stand up — and then, because I promised honesty, let's name the ways it falls down, because those are your production pages at 2 a.m.

One process per user, and why that's affordable

A LiveView connection is a stateful BEAM process — one per connected client. Say that to someone from a thread-per-connection world and they wince, imagining a thread and its megabyte of stack per user. But you know better now. A BEAM process starts at about 233 words (Chapter 03), a couple of kilobytes. Fifty thousand of them is a hundred-odd megabytes of process memory — pocket change. The reason "a process per user" is sane is the reason from Chapter 01: these aren't OS threads, they're tiny cheap isolated things, and we stopped rationing them long ago.

The stack, top to bottom

Cheap processes (Ch 1) mean one per user is fine. Reduction-counted scheduling (Ch 2) means no one user's work can freeze the others — everyone gets a fair, frequent slice. Private heaps and per-process GC (Ch 3) mean fifty thousand users means fifty thousand tiny independent collections, never one giant stop-the-world freeze. And isolation (Ch 1) means one user's crash takes down one user's process — a supervisor restarts it, the other 49,999 never notice. Scale isn't one feature. It's all of them at once.

The famous number, told straight

You've probably heard "Phoenix did 2 million connections on one box." It's true, and it's worth getting right. In 2015 the Phoenix team (Gary Rennie and friends) pushed Phoenix Channels to 2 million simultaneous websocket connections on a single 40-core, 128 GB machine — and what finally capped them wasn't the BEAM running out of processes but the operating system's limits on file descriptors and ports. Note the two honest footnotes: that was Channels, a few years before LiveView existed, and LiveView is heavier per connection because each process holds real UI state, not just a pipe. So LiveView won't hit 2 million on the same box. But it stands on the identical foundation, and that foundation is why "hundreds of thousands of live, stateful connections per node" is a normal Tuesday rather than a heroic feat.

The three tools to keep close

:observer for the live picture — schedulers, memory, the busiest processes. :recon for the surgical questions — recon:bin_leak/1 for hoarded binaries, recon:proc_count/2 for who's using the most of anything. And telemetry to make the metrics that matter visible before the pager rings. Together they turn every symptom in that table from a mystery into a measurement.

Know the real ceiling

What actually limits connections-per-node is, in order: the OS (file descriptors, ports — raise your ulimit), then memory, and only much later the BEAM itself. And remember fan-out isn't free: broadcasting to 100,000 LiveView processes copies the message 100,000 times (Chapter 04). Scaling out to more nodes brings in distribution's costs (Chapter 07). The BEAM makes the process cheap; it can't make physics free.

So here's the whole thing, in one breath. LiveView scales because the BEAM made a process cheap enough to spend one per user, scheduling fair enough that none can starve the rest, garbage collection private enough that a crowd never freezes together, and failure local enough that one broken session is just one restart. None of it is magic. It's a handful of stubborn, honest design choices — each with a price you can now name — stacked until they hold up something that looks, from the outside, like magic. That's the machine. Now you can read it.

Try it yourself

  1. Open :observer on a running Phoenix app, sort processes by memory and by message queue length, and put names to the busiest few. You now understand every column.
  2. Raise a dev machine's ulimit -n, then load-test websockets upward until something gives. Notice what breaks first — it won't be the process table.
  3. Pick one symptom from the table above, reproduce it deliberately in a scratch app, then fix it with the tool from its chapter. Do it once in calm and you'll do it in your sleep at 2 a.m.
