Chapter 03 · Memory

Per-Process Heaps & Garbage Collection

A great many tiny gardens, each weeded on its own.

Most runtimes you've met have one big heap, and when it fills up, everything stops while the collector tidies it — the dreaded stop-the-world pause. The BEAM does almost the opposite, and the difference is the reason your Elixir stays responsive under load. Instead of one enormous shared garden that must be weeded all at once, every process gets its own tiny private plot. When one plot needs weeding, only that one process pauses — for a fraction of a millisecond — and everybody else keeps growing.

What a process's memory actually looks like

When you spawn a process, the BEAM hands it a small contiguous block of memory. At one end sits the stack; at the other end sits the heap; and they grow toward each other, like two people filling a shelf from opposite ends. When they meet in the middle — when the block is full — it's time to collect. A fresh process starts astonishingly small: about 233 words (a word is 8 bytes on a 64-bit machine, so we're talking a couple of kilobytes). That tininess is exactly why you can afford a hundred thousand of them.

Generational, copying, and quick

The collector itself is a generational copying collector — Cheney's algorithm, if you want the textbook name. Two ideas do the heavy lifting. First, copying: instead of hunting for dead objects, the collector copies the live ones to fresh space and then throws the old space away whole. If most of your data is garbage — and in a busy process it usually is — you copy a little and discard a lot, which is fast. Second, generational: the observation that most values die young. So the heap is split into a young generation and an old one. Minor collections sweep just the young part, cheaply and often. Data that survives a few minor collections is assumed to be long-lived and gets promoted to the old generation, where it's checked far less often.

The one idea

Because a heap is private and small, collecting it is a local, brief event. There is no global lock, no whole-VM freeze. One process stops, its little plot is weeded in microseconds, and it resumes — while a hundred thousand siblings never even paused. That property — one process's GC never pauses another — is the quiet engine under every low-latency Erlang system you've admired.

The knobs, and when to touch them

You will almost never tune GC, and that's the correct default. But two spawn options exist for the rare day you must. min_heap_size sets a floor on a process's heap, so a process you know will handle big bursts doesn't waste time collecting tiny heaps over and over on its way up. fullsweep_after sets how many minor collections happen before the collector does a full sweep of both generations (the global default is 65535 — effectively "rarely"). Lowering it makes a process give memory back sooner, at the cost of more work; you'd do that for a process holding onto something big you want released promptly.

iex — tuning a process that you've measured# Give a known-bursty worker a bigger starting heap and eager full sweeps.
pid = spawn_opt(fn -> Worker.loop() end,
  [{:min_heap_size, 4_000},       # words — fewer early collections
   {:fullsweep_after, 10}])    # sweep both gens often → release memory sooner

Process.info(pid, :garbage_collection)   # inspect the current settings
:erlang.garbage_collect(pid)             # force a collection right now
Measure first, always

These knobs are for problems you have observed, not problems you imagine. Raising min_heap_size trades memory for speed on every such process, and you may have a lot of them. The honest rule: leave GC alone until :observer or :recon shows you a real cost, then change one thing and measure again.

The one thing that isn't on the heap

There's a deliberate exception to "each process weeds its own plot," and it's important enough to get its own chapter. Large binaries do not live on the process heap. They sit in a separate shared area, and the process heap holds only a small pointer to them. This keeps per-process collections short no matter how much binary data you're shuffling — you never copy a megabyte during a GC. But it also means a process's real memory footprint can be much larger than its heap suggests, and it sets up the single most famous memory surprise in all of Erlang. We'll walk right into it in Chapter 05.

So what did private heaps buy us? The end of the stop-the-world pause. By refusing to share memory, the BEAM turned garbage collection from a global event the whole system dreads into a private chore each process does quietly on its own. The cost is that data must be copied when it moves between processes — which is the very next chapter — and that big binaries need a home of their own, which is the chapter after. Every gain has its bill; here the bill is cheap and the gain is enormous.

Try it yourself

  1. In iex, build a big list in a process and call Process.info(self(), :memory) before and after. Then :erlang.garbage_collect() and measure again.
  2. Read Process.info(self(), :garbage_collection) and find minor_gcs. Do some work and watch it climb.
  3. Spawn a process with {:min_heap_size, 1_000_000} and compare its starting memory to a default one. Feel the trade in your hands.
