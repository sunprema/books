Chapter 04 · Talking

Message Passing & Mailboxes

If nobody shares memory, how does anyone say anything?

We've built a world of hermits — millions of processes, each in its own room, unable to touch anyone else's things. A charming world, but a useless one unless they can talk. They talk by posting letters. One process sends a message; it lands in the other's mailbox and waits to be read. Simple. But how the letter travels — that's where the interesting decisions and the honest costs live, and it's what this chapter is about.

Copy, don't share

Here's the rule, and it follows straight from private heaps: when you send a message, the BEAM copies it. The whole term — your tuple, your map, your list — is deep-copied out of the sender's heap and onto the receiver's heap. Nothing is shared. The sender can change its own copy, or die, or garbage-collect, and the receiver's message is entirely unaffected, because it was never the same bytes.

The one idea

Copying looks wasteful until you see what it buys: the garbage collector never has to trace a pointer from one process into another. Each heap is a closed world. That's what keeps GC private and brief (Chapter 03) and what lets a process die without dangling anyone else's data (Chapter 01). Copying isn't a compromise the BEAM tolerates — it's the price that makes isolation cheap, paid on purpose.

The one exception, foreshadowed

There is exactly one common thing the BEAM does not copy: a large binary — more than 64 bytes. Those live in the shared off-heap area we met at the end of Chapter 03, so sending one just copies a small reference and bumps a counter. Send a 5 MB binary to a thousand processes and you do not copy 5 GB; you share one blob and hand out a thousand little pointers. It's a lovely optimisation with a sting in its tail, which is why it gets its own chapter next.

The mailbox, and the cost of a crowded one

Every process has a mailbox — a queue of messages in arrival order. receive walks that queue from the front and takes the first message that matches one of its patterns, leaving the rest exactly where they are. This is selective receive, and it's genuinely useful: you can wait for one specific reply while unrelated messages pile up harmlessly behind it.

But look closely at "walks the queue from the front." If your mailbox has ten thousand messages and the one you want is a rare pattern, receive may scan past thousands of non-matching letters every single time. Selective receive is O(n) in the mailbox size. A process that falls behind — receiving faster than it handles — doesn't just leak memory as the queue grows; it gets slower, because every receive has more junk to wade through. This is the symptom people call mailbox bloat, and now you can see it's two problems wearing one coat: growing memory and degrading speed.

Why GenServer.call stays fast anyway

You might worry that GenServer.call — which waits for a reply — would suffer from this. It doesn't, thanks to a neat trick. call tags its request with a freshly made reference, and the BEAM optimises a receive that matches on a brand-new reference so it skips every message older than that reference — they can't possibly be the reply. So the scan starts from the right place instead of the front. Selective receive is O(n) in general, but this one extremely common case is made cheap on purpose.

Back-pressure: the cure for bloat

If a mailbox grows without bound, the deep cause is almost always that a sender is outrunning a receiver. The fix isn't a bigger mailbox — there's no such setting, and there shouldn't be. The fix is back-pressure: make the sender wait. GenServer.cast is fire-and-forget, so a hot loop of casts can bury a slow server. GenServer.call blocks the caller until the server answers, which naturally throttles the sender to the server's pace. When in doubt, prefer call, precisely because its waiting is the flow control.

iex — watch a mailbox, and apply back-pressure# How many letters are waiting for a process right now?
Process.info(pid, :message_queue_len)
#=> {:message_queue_len, 24_817}   # uh oh — this server is drowning

# cast floods; the caller never waits:
for _ <- 1..1_000_000, do: GenServer.cast(srv, :work)

# call throttles; the caller waits for each reply, so it can't outrun the server:
for _ <- 1..1_000_000, do: GenServer.call(srv, :work)
A tuning knob you'll rarely need

For a process that genuinely must hold a huge mailbox, the message_queue_data: :off_heap flag stores messages off the process heap, so a giant queue doesn't drag its garbage collections down. It's a real tool for a real edge case — but reach for back-pressure first. A huge mailbox is usually a design telling you something, not a thing to accommodate.

So what did copying buy us? A world where talking is safe — where a message, once posted, belongs wholly to the receiver, and no amount of chaos in the sender can reach across to spoil it. The bill comes in two parts: big terms cost real time to copy, and a mailbox you let grow will both swell and slow. Both are visible, measurable, and fixable — which is the whole promise of understanding the machine instead of fearing it.

Try it yourself

  1. Spawn a slow server and flood it with cast. Watch :message_queue_len climb. Now switch to call and watch it stay near zero.
  2. Send a large map to a process and use :erts_debug.size/1 (words) to see how much got copied. Then send a 1 MB binary and compare — the binary barely moves.
  3. Write a receive that only matches :rare, pre-load the mailbox with 100 000 :common messages, and time how long grabbing the rare one takes. Feel the O(n).
