The Process Model

Everything else in this book is a consequence of one stubborn idea.

Here is the whole machine in a sentence. Instead of a few heavy threads sharing one big lump of memory and fighting over locks, the BEAM gives you a great many tiny things — call them processes — that share nothing at all and talk only by sending each other messages. That is the idea. If you hold on to it, nothing else in this book will surprise you.

I want to be plain about the word "process," because it is a borrowed word and borrowed words cause trouble. A BEAM process is not an operating-system process. It is not a thread either. It is a much smaller thing — a little bookkeeping structure inside one OS process (the virtual machine), with its own private scrap of memory and its own letterbox. It costs a few hundred bytes to make and a few microseconds to start. So you make a lot of them. Not dozens. Hundreds of thousands, quite happily, on an ordinary machine.

The one idea

A process has private state and a mailbox. No other process can reach in and touch its memory. The only way to affect it is to post it a message, which lands in the mailbox and waits. No shared memory means no data races, no mutexes, no half-written values. That is the actor model, and it is the ground floor everything else stands on.

Why share nothing? Because sharing is where the pain lives

Think about how we usually get two threads to cooperate. They share a variable, and then we stand guard over it with a lock so they don't both scribble on it at once. It works, roughly, until the day it doesn't — a lock forgotten, a lock taken in the wrong order, a value read half-updated — and those bugs are horrible because they don't happen on your machine, they happen on the customer's machine, on a Tuesday, once.

The BEAM's answer is almost rude in its simplicity: don't share the variable. If two processes never touch the same memory, there is nothing to lock and nothing to corrupt. Each one keeps its own copy of whatever it needs. When they must cooperate, one posts a message and the other reads it — in its own time, one at a time. You have traded the whole family of shared-memory bugs for the modest cost of copying a message now and then. On the BEAM that is a wonderful trade, and most of this book is really about the machinery that makes the trade cheap.

Isolation buys you more than safety

The share-nothing rule does a second job, and it is the one that made Erlang famous. Because a process owns all of its own memory and nothing else's, a process can die without hurting anybody. Its little heap is simply thrown away. Nobody else was pointing into it, so nobody else notices except the friends who asked to be told. That is why we say, cheerfully, "let it crash" — a crash is just one small isolated thing failing, cleanly, in a box. Contrast that with a shared-memory program, where one thread scribbling on a bad pointer can quietly poison data the others are still using. Isolation is what makes failure local, and local failure is what makes a supervisor able to just restart the broken bit and carry on.

A homely picture

Think of an office where everyone has their own desk and their own papers, and they send each other memos. If one person faints, you don't lose the whole office — you help that one person up, hand them a fresh in-tray, and the memos keep flowing. Now picture an office where everyone writes on a single enormous shared sheet of paper. One spilled coffee and the whole company is guessing. The BEAM is the first office.

The three verbs underneath everything

All the grand behaviours — GenServers, supervisors, LiveView — are built from three primitive functions. spawn makes a new process and hands you its pid (its address). send drops a message into a pid's mailbox and returns immediately. receive takes the next matching message out of your own mailbox, waiting if there isn't one yet.

iex — the whole primitive layer# spawn returns a pid at once; the function runs concurrently.
pid = spawn(fn ->
  receive do
    {:ping, from} -> send(from, :pong)
  end
end)

send(pid, {:ping, self()})   # self() is our own address, so it can reply

receive do
  :pong -> IO.puts("it answered")
after
  1_000 -> IO.puts("gave up")   # waiting can time out
end

Notice we posted our own self() inside the message so the other fellow knows where to write back. That "who do I reply to?" bookkeeping is so common that GenServer does it for you — but underneath, it is exactly this. There is no hidden cleverness. There is spawn, send, receive, and a great many small isolated things doing them.

So what have we bought with this one stubborn rule? A unit of concurrency so cheap we stop rationing it. We no longer ask "can I afford another thread?" We model each connection, each job, each user session as its own process — isolated, crash-contained, and, as the next chapter shows, scheduled fairly for us without our lifting a finger. Everything else the BEAM does is in service of keeping this cheap and keeping it fair.
The bill, honestly

Share-nothing isn't free. Because processes don't share memory, sending data means copying it (Chapter 04) — cheap for small messages, not free for big ones. And a process that receives more messages than it can handle grows an unbounded mailbox, which is a slow-burning memory leak (Chapter 04 again). The model is a wonderful default. Like every real engineering choice, it has a price, and knowing the price is the whole point of this book.

Try it yourself

  1. Spawn 500 000 tiny processes in a loop and time it with :timer.tc/1. Notice you can. Then imagine trying that with OS threads.
  2. Run Process.info(self(), :memory) in iex. See how few words a fresh process starts with — we'll pick that number apart in Chapter 03.
  3. Spawn a process, don't link to it, and make it raise. Confirm your shell survives. Now spawn_link the same crash and watch the difference — isolation, and the choice to couple, are both yours.
