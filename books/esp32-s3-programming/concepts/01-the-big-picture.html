The Big Picture

You asked three questions at once: how do I program an ESP32, what is it good for, and how do I connect Rust firmware on it to an Elixir backend? Let's answer all three in one breath, then spend the rest of the book earning each answer honestly.

Here is the whole story before we slow down. An ESP32-S3 is a tiny computer with a radio built in. You write a program — its firmware — on your laptop, cross-compile it, and squirt it down a USB cable into the chip's flash memory, where it runs the instant power arrives and forever after. That program reads sensors, blinks lights, listens to microphones; and because the chip has Wi-Fi, it can also phone home. Home, in your case, is a server written in Elixir.

Why these three pieces fit

Think of it as a nervous system. The ESP32-S3 is a nerve ending — cheap, plentiful, out in the world touching reality. Rust is the language of the nerve — fast and careful, so the firmware doesn't crash a device you can't reach. And Elixir is the brain — one server that calmly holds tens of thousands of these nerve endings at once, because that is precisely what the BEAM virtual machine was born to do.

🔑
The one-sentence answer

Write Rust firmware for the ESP32-S3, have it publish little messages over MQTT across Wi-Fi, and let an Elixir/Phoenix app subscribe to those messages and react — dashboards, storage, commands back to the device.

The shape of the system

Readings flow up; commands flow back. The broker in the middle decouples thousands of devices from one backend.

How to read this book

We go in the order a builder actually thinks:

  • The board (Ch. 2–4): what the silicon is, what it's for, and which language to write it in.
  • The firmware (Ch. 5–6): Rust on the chip, then getting it onto Wi-Fi and onto MQTT.
  • The brain (Ch. 7–8): the Elixir backend, and finally the whole rope braided end-to-end.
🧰
One honest caveat up front

Elixir does not run on the ESP32 — it runs on your server. (You may have heard of Nerves; that's Elixir for Linux-class boards like a Raspberry Pi, not for a microcontroller like this.) The ESP32 speaks to Elixir over the network. We'll make that boundary crisp in Chapter 7.
