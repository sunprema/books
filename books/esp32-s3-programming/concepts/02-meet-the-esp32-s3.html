Concept 02 · The Chip

Meet the ESP32-S3

Before you can program a thing, you have to know what it is. So let's open the lid and look at the actual machine — not the marketing, the silicon.

Hold an ESP32-S3 module in your hand and you're holding a complete computer the size of a postage stamp. Inside that little metal can are two processors, a pile of memory, a two-way radio, and dozens of wires waiting to touch the world. The wonderful thing is that none of it is magic — every piece does an ordinary, understandable job. Let's name them.

Two brains, not one

At the heart sit two Xtensa LX7 cores, each able to run at 240 MHz. Two cores matters more than it sounds: one core can babysit the radio — Wi-Fi is fussy and time-sensitive — while the other runs your program without stutter. It's the difference between a cook who must answer the phone mid-omelette and a kitchen with a dedicated phone person.

🔑
The "S3" in the name

Espressif makes a family. The original ESP32 and the -S3 use Xtensa cores; the -C3/-C6 use RISC-V. The S3's party trick is vector (SIMD) instructions for neural-network and signal-processing math — that's the "AI" in AIoT.

The memory, from fast to far

Memory on a microcontroller comes in layers, like a desk versus a filing cabinet versus a warehouse. The closer and faster, the smaller:

MemorySizeWhat it's for
Internal SRAM512 KBLive variables, stack, the data your code touches constantly.
Internal ROM384 KBBurned-in boot & core routines you can't change.
RTC SRAM16 KBSurvives light-sleep — keeps a few bytes alive while the chip naps.
External flashup to 16 MBYour firmware lives here permanently (the "filing cabinet").
External PSRAMup to 8 MBOptional extra RAM for big buffers — camera frames, audio.
🧠
Why the numbers feel small

512 KB sounds tiny next to a laptop's gigabytes — but this chip does one job, not a thousand. The whole discipline of embedded programming is making a job fit. That constraint is exactly why we'll reach for Rust.

7 ·

The radio and the senses

What makes the ESP32-S3 special isn't the CPU — plenty of chips compute. It's that the same $5 part also connects and perceives:

  • Wi-Fi 4 (802.11 b/g/n, 2.4 GHz) — talks to your router, and through it, to your Elixir server anywhere on Earth.
  • Bluetooth 5 LE — short-range, low-power; great for phones, beacons, mesh.
  • 45 GPIO pins — each can read a button, drive an LED, or speak I2C/SPI/UART to a sensor.
  • USB-OTG — show up as a keyboard, a serial port, or a flash drive.
  • Camera & LCD interfaces, ADC for analog signals, I2S for microphones & audio.
🍎
The big idea

A computer that can sense the world, think a little, and phone home over a radio — for the price of a coffee. Stack a thousand of them and you have a nervous system. That is what we're learning to build.
