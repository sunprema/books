Chapter 13

Avoiding Scams and Bad Advice

Here's the part nobody warns new investors about: the moment you start putting real money to work, you become a target. Not because you did anything wrong — because you're exactly who a con artist is looking for. The good news? Every investment scam ever run, from a guy with a briefcase in 1920 to a slick app promising you can “10x your money in crypto by Friday,” leans on the same handful of tricks. Learn the tricks once, and you'll spot them for the rest of your investing life. That's what this chapter hands you — plus a glossary and a resource list to keep in your back pocket. Let's get you scam-proof.

The Oldest Trick in the Book: “Guaranteed” Returns

Say this sentence out loud a few times until it's automatic: every real investment carries risk. Not “most.” Not “except this one special opportunity.” Every single one — stocks, bonds, real estate, all of it. The one and only exception is an FDIC-insured deposit like a savings account or CD, and even that is only guaranteed up to the insurance limit. So when someone promises you a guaranteed high return with little or no risk, they haven't found a loophole in how money works. They're lying to you. Investor.gov, the SEC's own investor-education site, puts “guaranteed high returns with little or no risk” at the very top of its official red-flags checklist — and here's the rest of that list, dressed up in plain English:

  • Overly consistent returns. Real markets bounce around — up some months, down others. A strategy that supposedly goes up steadily, every single month, no matter what the market is doing, isn't skill. It's a spreadsheet someone made up.
  • Pressure to act right now. “Only 3 spots left,” “this closes tonight,” “don't tell anyone else about this.” Urgency is a tool for shutting off your judgment before you can use it.
  • Unlicensed sellers and puffed-up credentials. Anyone can claim to be a “wealth strategist.” Real advisors and brokers are registered — and you can check that in five minutes (more on exactly how, below).
  • Secrecy and exclusivity. If the pitch involves keeping it quiet, avoiding “putting anything in writing,” or feeling flattered to be let in on a secret, that secrecy is doing work for the scammer, not for you.
  • Affinity fraud. Fraudsters deliberately target tight-knit groups — a church, an immigrant community, a military unit, an online forum — because trust inside the group does their convincing for them. The SEC has an entire investor alert dedicated to exactly this pattern.
⚠️
The One-Sentence Version

If it sounds too good to be true, it isn't a hidden gem you got lucky enough to find — it's too good to be true.

Anatomy of a Ponzi Scheme

The single most common structure behind a “guaranteed returns” scam has a name: a Ponzi scheme. The SEC defines it precisely — “an investment fraud that involves the payment of purported returns to existing investors from funds contributed by new investors,” rather than from any actual profit earned. There is no real underlying investment doing the work. There's just a pile of cash, and a very convincing story about where it's going.

This is exactly why “overly consistent returns” is such a reliable tell: a scheme operator is writing the account statement, not the market. And it's why the collapse always looks sudden from the outside — the fraud was never sustainable, it just hadn't run out of new recruits yet.

How to Spot a Trustworthy Financial Advisor

Most bad advice doesn't come from cartoonish villains — it comes from someone who's legally allowed to put their own paycheck ahead of your goals, and just doesn't mention that part. The single most important question you can ask anyone managing your money is: what standard are you legally held to?

StandardWho it applies toWhat it actually means
Fiduciary duty Registered Investment Advisers (RIAs) Legally required to act in your best interest at all times — ahead of their own compensation
Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI) Broker-dealers Must not put their own interest ahead of yours when recommending a specific product — a narrower, more recent SEC standard, not the older fiduciary duty
💡
Fee-Only vs. Commission-Based

A fee-only advisor is paid only by you — a flat rate, an hourly rate, or a percentage of the assets they manage — with no commissions from the products they recommend. A commission-based advisor earns a cut every time they sell you a particular fund, insurance policy, or annuity, which is exactly the kind of incentive a real fiduciary standard exists to police. Neither label alone guarantees good advice, but it's always fair — and normal — to simply ask “how do you get paid?” before you sign anything.

Run the Background Check: FINRA BrokerCheck & SEC IAPD

Here's the part most people skip, and it takes less time than ordering coffee. Every legitimately registered broker and investment adviser in the United States is on the public record, and you can search that record yourself, for free, right now:

  • FINRA BrokerCheck (brokercheck.finra.org) — look up any broker or brokerage firm's registration status, licenses, employment history, and disciplinary or regulatory actions.
  • SEC Investment Adviser Public Disclosure, “IAPD” (adviserinfo.sec.gov) — covers SEC- and state-registered investment advisers, and gives you their full Form ADV — the filing that spells out exactly how they're paid, what they charge, and any disciplinary history.

Your Investing Glossary

You've picked up a lot of vocabulary across this book. Here are ten terms worth having in one place, in plain English, no jargon left un-translated:

TermPlain-English meaning
DividendA slice of a company's profit paid straight to shareholders, usually every quarter.
CompoundingEarning returns on your past returns, not just your original money — growth that feeds itself.
DiversificationSpreading your money across many different investments so no single one can sink the whole portfolio.
VolatilityHow much and how fast a price bounces around; a jumpy stock is “more volatile” than a steady bond.
Expense ratioThe yearly fee a fund charges, as a percentage of your investment, taken automatically before you ever see your return.
Index fundA fund that simply buys everything in a market benchmark, instead of trying to pick winners.
Dollar-cost averagingInvesting a fixed amount on a regular schedule, so you buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when they're high.
Capital gainThe profit you make when you sell an investment for more than you paid for it.
FiduciaryAn advisor who's legally required to put your interests ahead of their own paycheck.
Ponzi schemeA fraud that pays “returns” to earlier investors using cash from newer investors — not real profit.
