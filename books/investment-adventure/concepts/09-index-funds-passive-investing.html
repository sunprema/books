Chapter 9

Index Funds and Passive Investing

Here's the chapter where I get to tell you my favorite secret in all of investing: the single most effective tool for building wealth isn't a hot tip, a clever chart pattern, or a friend-of-a-friend stock story. It's a fund so simple it barely does anything — it just buys the whole market and holds it. You met the idea briefly back in Chapter 5. Now let's see exactly why it works, why the single greatest investor alive keeps telling ordinary people to use it instead of listening to him, and how you can own one for less than the price of a sandwich.

What exactly is an index fund?

A market index is just a scoreboard — a defined list of securities (say, the roughly 500 large U.S. companies in the S&P 500) tracked together as one number. You can't buy an index directly; it's a measurement, not a product. An index fund is what closes that gap: a mutual fund or ETF built to hold those same securities, in the same proportions, so its performance tracks the index as closely as possible — before fees. That's the entire strategy. There's no manager trying to guess which companies will do best; the fund simply owns all of them and lets the market's overall growth do the work.

That makes an index fund the purest form of passive investing — the opposite bet from the active management you saw in Chapter 5. An active fund pays a manager to research, trade, and try to beat the index. A passive index fund pays almost nobody, does almost no trading, and simply tries to match it. Less activity sounds like it should mean less impressive returns. As you're about to see, it's usually exactly the opposite.

💡
The One-Sentence Version

Instead of trying to find the one company that will outperform, an index fund buys a little piece of every company on the list — so you automatically own tomorrow's winners and losers alike, at rock-bottom cost, with zero guessing required.

The track record: professionals lose this bet too

“Just buy everything” sounds almost too lazy to work. So here's the part that should really grab your attention: it isn't beginners who struggle to beat a plain index fund — it's the professionals whose entire job is stock-picking. Every year, S&P Dow Jones Indices runs the numbers in its SPIVA Scorecard, comparing actively managed funds against their benchmark indexes.

92.89% of actively managed U.S. large-cap funds underperformed the S&P 500 over the 15 years ending 2025

Widen the lens and it gets worse for the pros, not better: over that same 15-year window, 93.15% of all domestic U.S. stock funds trailed a broad market index, and over 20 years the large-cap figure climbs to 92.89% and the all-domestic figure to 95.01%. Even more striking — SPIVA's companion Persistence Scorecard found that over the 15 years ending 2024, not one of 22 U.S. equity fund categories had a majority of managers beat their benchmark. Not one. This isn't a story about a few unlucky years; it's the pattern, decade after decade.

⚠️
Why It's So Hard, Even For Experts

It's not that professional managers are bad at their jobs — many are brilliant. The problem is structural. Every fund's gains are measured after its fees, trading costs, and taxes are subtracted, and stock markets are famously efficient at pricing in public information fast, so a genuine, repeatable edge is rare and hard to identify in advance. Add it up across thousands of managers and thousands of trades, and the honest math says most of them will lag a benchmark that charges almost nothing and never has an off year of judgment.

Why a “tiny” fee difference is actually enormous

Here's the part beginners underrate most: you don't even need the active fund to underperform for the index fund to win — the fee gap alone does most of the damage. Chapter 5 showed you that broad index funds now often charge 0.03–0.10% a year, while the typical actively managed U.S. stock fund charges closer to 1%. That 1% doesn't sound dramatic. Compounded over decades, it is.

*Illustrative only — assumes a constant 10% average annual return before fees, deducted every year, with no withdrawals. Real markets don't move in a straight line and past returns never guarantee future ones. The point isn't the exact dollar figure; it's that a roughly 1-point fee gap alone costs this example over $40,000 — before either fund manager has to be smarter or dumber than the market at all.

Why Warren Buffett keeps recommending index funds — to everyone but himself

Warren Buffett built one of history's greatest fortunes by picking individual stocks and whole businesses through Berkshire Hathaway. And yet, for decades, his standing advice to ordinary people has been the opposite of what made him rich: buy the index, not the stock.

🏆
From Buffett's 2013 Shareholder Letter

Describing the instructions he left for the trustee managing money for his wife's future: “Put 10% of the cash in short-term government bonds and 90% in a very low-cost S&P 500 index fund. (I suggest Vanguard's.) I believe the trust's long-term results from this policy will be superior to those attained by most investors — whether pension funds, institutions, or individuals — who employ high-fee managers.”

He's repeated the theme in his own words to shareholders more than once: “My regular recommendation has been a low-cost S&P 500 index fund,” and more bluntly still, “Both large and small investors should stick with low-cost index funds.” His reasoning isn't modesty — it's the same math you just saw in the SPIVA numbers and the fee table above: high-fee active management has to clear a real hurdle before it even breaks even with a fund that barely charges anything, and most of the time it doesn't.

Buffett didn't just say this — he put a million dollars on it. In 2007 he bet hedge fund manager Ted Seides $1 million that a plain, unmanaged S&P 500 index fund would outperform a hand-picked basket of hedge funds — funds run by smart, expensive, professional managers — over ten full years, fees and all. Seides picked five funds-of-funds for the challenge. The bet ran from January 1, 2008 through the end of 2017.

7.1% annualized — the index fund's result over the decade

The hand-picked hedge fund basket managed roughly 2.2% annualized over the same ten years, net of fees — not even a third of the index fund's return, despite employing some of the most highly paid stock-pickers on Wall Street. Buffett won comfortably and donated the winnings to Girls Inc. of Omaha, Nebraska. It's the same lesson from a different angle: even professionals whose entire job is beating the market usually don't — so why would picking your own stocks, part-time, after work, be any easier?

💡
This Isn't “Nobody Can Ever Beat the Market”

Buffett himself is proof that skilled, disciplined stock-picking can work over a lifetime — and a handful of managers do beat their benchmark in any given stretch. His point isn't that it's impossible; it's that reliably identifying who will do it, in advance, is far harder than it looks — hard enough that his advice for “most people,” including his own family's inheritance, is still: don't try. Buy the whole market instead.

How to actually buy one — even with $20

Here's the best part: you don't need to save up for a “real” investment amount before you start. Remember fractional shares from Chapter 4? They apply just as well to index funds and index ETFs as to individual stocks. Most major brokers — Fidelity, Charles Schwab, and Robinhood among them — now let you buy a slice of a broad-market index ETF for as little as $1, no round number of shares required.

  1. Open (or use) a brokerage account. You already covered this in Chapter 4 — nothing new to set up.
  2. Search for a broad index fund or ETF — something tracking the total U.S. stock market or the S&P 500, from any major fund family.
  3. Check its expense ratio. Aim for well under 0.20%; many of the largest total-market and S&P 500 index funds now run 0.03–0.10%.
  4. Enter a dollar amount, not a share count — “buy $20 of it” instead of trying to afford a whole share.
  5. Place the order. That's it — you now own a tiny slice of hundreds or thousands of companies at once.

What to Do Next

You don't need $600, a finance degree, or a hot tip — you need $1, a broker that supports fractional shares, and one broad, low-cost index fund. Today, just find that fund and write down its expense ratio and today's price; you don't have to buy anything yet. You now know what an index fund actually is, why even professional stock-pickers mostly lose to one, and why Warren Buffett — of all people — keeps telling everyone else to skip stock-picking entirely.
