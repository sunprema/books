Chapter 12

Taxes and Investing

Last chapter you learned about the special tax-favored boxes — IRAs and 401(k)s — that shield your investments from the IRS. Great news: not everything has to live in one of those boxes. But money you invest in an ordinary taxable brokerage account is still fair game for the tax collector, and here's the exciting part — a handful of simple, completely legal choices about when you sell and where you hold things can save you real money, year after year, for the rest of your investing life. This isn't about dodging taxes. It's about not paying more than you have to. Let's dig in.

The single most valuable number in this chapter: 366

When you sell an investment for more than you paid, that profit is a capital gain — and the IRS taxes it differently depending on one thing: how long you owned it. Hold an investment one year or less before selling, and any profit is a short-term capital gain, taxed at your regular income tax rate — the same 10%–37% brackets that tax your paycheck. Hold it more than one year, and it becomes a long-term capital gain, taxed at a special, almost always lower, 0%, 15%, or 20% rate instead.

1 Year the IRS line between short-term (taxed like a paycheck) and long-term (taxed at a discount) capital gains

The One-Sentence Version

Selling one day too early can tax the exact same profit at a much higher rate — patience isn't just a virtue here, it's a discount.

For 2026, the IRS's long-term capital gains brackets are wider than most people expect — plenty of everyday investors pay 0% on long-term gains:

Head-of-household filers get their own thresholds in between. Long-term gains “stack” on top of your other income — your job's paycheck fills the lower brackets first, and your investment gains sit on top, landing wherever the stack runs out. Short-term gains get no such grace; they're simply added to your ordinary income and taxed at whatever bracket that lands in.

⚠️
Higher Earners: Watch for the NIIT

Once your modified adjusted gross income tops $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly), an additional Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) of 3.8% applies on top of whatever capital gains rate you'd otherwise owe. Stack the top 20% long-term rate with the 3.8% NIIT and the effective top federal rate on investment gains reaches 23.8%. These thresholds aren't adjusted for inflation, so more investors cross them every year as incomes rise.

23.8% the highest realistic federal rate on long-term gains once the 20% bracket and the 3.8% NIIT both apply

One more discount worth knowing: most stock and fund dividends you receive are qualified dividends, and the IRS taxes those at the very same 0/15/20% long-term rates — not your ordinary rate — as long as you've held the underlying shares for a minimum period around the dividend date. A smaller slice of dividends (from REITs, for instance) don't qualify and are taxed as ordinary income instead.

Tax-efficient fund placement: the right investment in the right box

You now have two kinds of accounts in your toolkit: tax-advantaged wrappers (401(k)s, IRAs) from last chapter, and ordinary taxable brokerage accounts. When you have money in both, a strategy called asset location asks a smart question: given that some investments are naturally more tax-hungry than others, which account should each one live in?

Broad index funds and ETFs — the kind you met back in Chapters 6 and 9 — are wonderfully tax-efficient on their own: low turnover means few taxable events, and most of their dividends are qualified. That makes them a great fit for an ordinary taxable account. Bond funds, REITs, and actively managed funds that trade frequently are the opposite — they tend to throw off taxable interest, ordinary dividends, or surprise year-end capital gains distributions even if you never sold a share yourself. Those belong inside a 401(k) or IRA, where none of that annual noise is taxed.

Why ETFs Often Beat Mutual Funds on Taxes

ETFs have a structural tax edge over traditional mutual funds. When large institutional traders create or redeem ETF shares, it happens through an in-kind exchange of the fund's underlying stocks — not a cash sale — so the fund itself rarely has to sell holdings and realize a taxable gain. Mutual funds don't have that mechanism, so they're more likely to pass a surprise capital gains distribution on to every shareholder in a given year, whether you sold anything or not.

One rule to know before you go bargain-hunting for tax breaks: the wash sale rule. If you sell an investment at a loss to claim the tax benefit, then buy the same or a “substantially identical” investment within 30 days before or after that sale, the IRS disallows the loss. The rule applies across all your accounts — including your spouse's, and even a repurchase inside an IRA — so it's easy to trip without realizing it.

When to bring in a tax professional

This chapter gives you the map, not a substitute for a guide. A CPA or enrolled agent earns their fee back many times over once your situation gets genuinely complicated. A few common trip-wires worth a real conversation with a professional:

  • Your income is creeping toward — or past — the $200,000 / $250,000 NIIT thresholds above.
  • You received stock compensation: RSUs, stock options, or an employee stock purchase plan (ESPP).
  • You bought, sold, or earned cryptocurrency — the tax rules are still evolving and easy to get wrong.
  • You're weighing tax-loss harvesting or a big rebalance and want to avoid an accidental wash sale.
  • You turned a home into a rental, started a side business, or had any other major life or income change.

This Chapter Is Education, Not Advice

When real money and real deadlines are on the line, a qualified tax professional is worth the conversation.

What to Do Next

Before your next sale, check the purchase date — if you're within a few weeks of the one-year mark, waiting could genuinely be worth it. Then take five minutes to glance at what's sitting in your taxable account versus your 401(k)/IRA, and ask whether a bond fund or actively managed fund would be happier on the tax-advantaged side. You don't need to fix everything today — you just need to know the levers exist.
