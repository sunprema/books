Chapter 4

How to Buy and Sell Stocks

You now know what a share is, where it comes from, and who stands between you and the exchange. Time for the fun part — actually doing it. Opening the account, funding it, and clicking “Buy” for the first time is far less intimidating than it sounds, and once you've done it once, it's genuinely just a habit, like setting up direct deposit. Let's walk through every step, in order, so your first trade is a confident one instead of a nervous one.

Step 1: Open a brokerage account

A brokerage account is your personal gateway to the exchanges — the account that actually holds your cash and your shares. Opening one online takes about ten minutes and looks a lot like opening a bank account:

  1. Pick a broker. Any well-known, SIPC-member firm works — Fidelity, Schwab, and Vanguard are long-standing full-service options; Robinhood, Webull, Fidelity Youth, and others are popular mobile-first apps. Compare fees, the tools you'll actually use, and how the app feels in your hand — the “best” broker is the one you'll actually open every week.
  2. Verify your identity. You'll enter your legal name, address, date of birth, and Social Security number, and usually upload a photo ID. This is a federally required step (an anti–money-laundering rule called “Know Your Customer”) — every legitimate broker asks for it.
  3. Answer a few questions about you. Employment status, income range, and investing experience. This isn't a test you can fail — it just helps the broker show you appropriate features and satisfy its own regulatory obligations.
  4. Link a bank account. You'll enter your bank's routing and account number (or connect it instantly through your bank's login) so money can move between the two.
  5. Submit and wait for approval. Most online brokerage applications are approved within one to two business days — sometimes instantly.
🛡️
Safety Net

Look for the letters SIPC (Securities Investor Protection Corporation) on any broker you're considering. If the brokerage firm itself fails, SIPC coverage protects your cash and securities up to $500,000, including a $250,000 limit for cash. Important nuance: SIPC protects you from your broker going under — it does not protect you from your investments losing value. Normal market ups and downs are a risk you're taking on knowingly, not one any insurance covers.

Step 2: Fund the account

An approved account starts empty — a shopping cart with no wallet attached yet. Link your bank and transfer money in, usually through an ACH transfer, which typically takes one to three business days to fully settle (some brokers give you limited instant access to a deposit while it clears). And here's the beginner-friendly part: you almost never need a round number of shares. Most major brokers now let you buy fractional shares for as little as $1–$5, so a $600 share of a company you believe in doesn't have to be out of reach — you can simply buy 0.05 of one.

💡
Fast Fact

Trading stocks and ETFs used to cost a commission every time — often $5–$10 a trade. Starting in October 2019, nearly every major U.S. online broker (Schwab, Fidelity, TD Ameritrade, E*TRADE, and the mobile-first apps that followed) dropped commissions on online stock and ETF trades to $0. That's a real structural change in investors' favor — but remember, “commission-free” doesn't mean “free of all cost”: funds still carry their own ongoing expense ratios, covered in the next couple of chapters.

Step 3: Choose your order type

Chapter 3 introduced market and limit orders in passing — here's the full toolkit, plainly explained, that every beginner should know before placing a trade.

Order typeWhat it doesTrade-off
Market order Buy or sell right now, at whatever the best available price is Guarantees the trade happens — not the exact price
Limit order Buy or sell only at your chosen price, or better Guarantees the price — not that the trade happens at all
Stop order (a.k.a. stop-loss) Sits quietly until the stock hits your “stop” price, then instantly becomes a market order Triggers reliably — but once triggered, behaves like a market order (see below)
Stop-limit order Same trigger as a stop order, but once triggered it becomes a limit order instead of a market order Caps your price — but in a fast-moving market it can trigger and still not fill

The stop-loss order is the one every new investor should learn early: it's an automatic safety cord. Say you buy a stock at $50 and set a stop-loss at $45 — if the price ever falls to $45, your stop order wakes up and immediately tries to sell, capping how much you can lose without you having to babysit the screen all day.

⚠️
Watch Out

A triggered stop order becomes a plain market order — it does not guarantee your $45. In a fast, gappy market the stock can blow straight through your stop price and actually fill lower, say at $43. A stop-limit order fixes the price problem by adding a floor — but that floor means it can also simply fail to fill if the price keeps falling past your limit. Neither order type is a force field; both are tools, with a trade-off attached.

Reading a stock quote before you click “Buy”

Before you place any order, pull up the stock's quote page. It looks like a wall of numbers at first, but every field is answering one specific question about the stock right now:

A high P/E isn't automatically “bad” and a low one isn't automatically a bargain — it usually just means the market expects faster (or slower) future growth. It's most useful as a comparison tool: how does this company's P/E stack up against others in its own industry, and against its own history?

Placing the order: your ticket, step by step

Every broker's “Buy” screen — the order ticket — asks the same handful of questions, just arranged differently:

  1. Search the ticker symbol and confirm it's the right company (a lot of similarly-named companies exist — double-check before you commit).
  2. Choose Buy or Sell.
  3. Enter shares or a dollar amount. “Buy $100 of AAPL” and “buy 0.47 shares of AAPL” land you in the same place if fractional shares are supported.
  4. Pick the order type — market, limit, stop, or stop-limit, from the table above.
  5. Set the time-in-force. Day means the order expires automatically if it doesn't fill by market close; Good-'til-Canceled (GTC) keeps it open for weeks until it fills or you cancel it.
  6. Review the ticket — symbol, side, quantity, order type, and estimated cost — then submit.
  7. Get your confirmation. A market order in a liquid stock typically fills in well under a second; official settlement (the paperwork catching up) follows one business day later, on the T+1 cycle from Chapter 3.
