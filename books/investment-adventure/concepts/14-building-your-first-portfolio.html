Chapter 14

Building Your First Portfolio

Thirteen chapters in, and you're not a beginner anymore — you're just someone who hasn't clicked “Buy” for real yet. You know what stocks, ETFs, and bonds actually are. You know why diversification matters, why index funds quietly beat most stock-pickers, why a boring recurring contribution beats a clever one-time bet, and which account wrapper hands you a permanent tax break. This chapter's whole job is to stop teaching you new ingredients and instead hand you the recipe: a real, step-by-step path from “I have some savings and a lot of vocabulary” to “I own an actual, diversified portfolio built on purpose.” Let's build it.

From zero to your first investment: the full path

Here's every step, in order, pulling together the mechanics from earlier chapters into one single walk:

  1. Pick the right account wrapper first. Before you pick a single fund, decide where the money lives. Following Chapter 11's order of operations: capture your full 401(k) match if you have one, then open (or use) a Roth or Traditional IRA, then a plain taxable brokerage account for anything beyond those limits. The wrapper doesn't change what you can own — it changes how much of your gains the IRS gets to keep.
  2. Open and fund the account. This is Chapter 4's ten-minute process: pick an SIPC-member broker, verify your identity, link your bank, and move money in via ACH transfer. If it's a 401(k), this step is usually just raising a percentage in your employer's portal.
  3. Name your goal and your time horizon. “Retirement in 35 years,” “a house down payment in 5 years,” and “extra long-term wealth with no deadline” are three very different goals, and the honest answer to “when do I need this money?” is the single biggest input into everything that follows.
  4. Choose your risk bucket. Conservative, moderate, or aggressive — matched to that time horizon and to how you'd actually react to a bad year, not just how brave you feel today. The next section walks through exactly how to pick.
  5. Fill your allocation with real funds. Translate your target percentages into specific index funds or ETFs from Chapters 5, 6, and 9 — a total U.S. stock market fund, an international stock fund, a total bond market fund, sized to your mix.
  6. Place your first order. You don't have to buy every slice on day one. Many first-timers start with a single broad, low-cost fund (a total U.S. stock market ETF is a common first purchase) and layer in the rest of the allocation over the next few contributions — there's no rule that says the whole portfolio has to exist by Friday.
  7. Automate the next contribution before you close the tab. Set up a recurring transfer and a recurring buy order, the dollar-cost-averaging habit from Chapter 10, so the portfolio keeps building itself on schedule whether or not you feel like logging in that week.
  8. Track it, and adjust on a schedule — not a mood. The last section of this chapter covers exactly how, and the worksheet below gives you a place to write it all down.

The One-Sentence Version

A portfolio isn't one clever pick — it's a small, deliberate set of broad funds, sized by percentage to match your time horizon and risk tolerance, that you keep feeding on a schedule and checking on a schedule, not a whim.

Know your risk bucket before you know your funds

Watch Out

Time horizon and risk tolerance aren't the same thing, and both matter. Devon's 25-year runway lets him afford to ride out a crash; if Devon also happens to panic-sell every time the market dips, aggressive isn't actually the right bucket for him — the “best” portfolio is the one you'll actually stay in through a bad year, not the one with the highest number on a chart.

Three sample starter portfolios

Financial firms publish model portfolios in roughly this same shape — Schwab's own risk-profile guidance and Vanguard's model-portfolio education both describe conservative mixes leaning heavily toward bonds and cash, moderate mixes splitting close to 60/35/5, and aggressive mixes running 80–90% stocks. Here are three illustrative starting points, each one built entirely from ingredients you already know from earlier chapters — no single stock-picking required, though a small “satellite” slice of individual stocks you've researched (Chapter 3–4 style) can sit on top of any of these without breaking the core idea:

Tracking and adjusting your portfolio over time

A portfolio isn't a “set it and never look again” object, but it's also not something to refresh daily — that's a habit that produces anxiety, not better returns. Here's the actual, boring, effective cadence:

  • Check quarterly, not daily. Once every few months is plenty to catch meaningful drift without turning investing into a stressful hobby.
  • Rebalance on a threshold, not a calendar. The common professional guideline — the one Vanguard's own advisory service uses — is to rebalance when any slice drifts more than 5 percentage points from its target. If your 60% stock target has drifted to 67% after a strong year, that's your signal, not an arbitrary date on the calendar.
  • Rebalance for free where you can. Inside a tax-advantaged account (Chapter 11), you can sell winners and buy the lagging slice with zero tax consequence. In a taxable account, prefer directing new contributions toward whichever slice has fallen behind, rather than selling winners and triggering the capital gains tax from Chapter 12.
  • Let your bucket drift on purpose as your time horizon shortens. Many investors gradually shift from aggressive toward moderate, and moderate toward conservative, as a big goal (retirement, a house) gets closer — the same logic behind “target-date” funds, just done by hand.
  • Increase contributions before you increase risk. Got a raise? Raising your recurring contribution from Chapter 10 usually does more for your long-term number than chasing a hotter allocation.

Capstone: build your own portfolio, with your own numbers
