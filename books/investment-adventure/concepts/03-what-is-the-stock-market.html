Chapter 3

What Is the Stock Market?

Here's the first thing to unlearn: there is no giant vault somewhere labeled “the stock market.” There's no building you could visit and point at and say “there it is.” What actually exists is a vast, humming network — millions of buyers and sellers, a handful of regulated exchanges, and the brokers and platforms that connect the two — all agreeing, moment to moment, on what tiny slices of the world's companies are worth. Once you can see the machine instead of the mystery, the whole thing stops feeling like Wall Street magic and starts feeling like exactly what it is: a marketplace, with rules, that you're already allowed to walk into.

Your first share: what you actually own

A stock (also called a share or an equity) is a unit of ownership in a corporation. Buy one share of a company and you genuinely own a sliver of that business — a claim on its assets and its future earnings, proportional to how many shares exist. Own 1 share out of 1 billion outstanding, and you own one-billionth of the company. That's not a metaphor; it's a legal fact recorded in the company's books. Depending on the company, that slice can carry real perks: a vote at the shareholder meeting on things like the board of directors, and sometimes a small quarterly cash payment called a dividend, paid out of profits.

Every publicly traded company gets a short nickname called a ticker symbol — Apple is AAPL, Microsoft is MSFT, Coca-Cola is KO. That's the handle you'll search for on any trading app, and it's how the whole market keeps track of who's trading what, in real time, all day long.

Where shares come from: the IPO vs. everyday trading

A company's shares are born exactly once, in an event called an Initial Public Offering, or IPO. That's the primary market: the company sells brand-new shares directly to investors and pockets the money to fund the business. After that day, though, those shares just keep changing hands between investors, forever — that ongoing trading is the secondary market, and it's the one you'll actually use.

💡
Common Mix-Up

When you buy 10 shares of a company on your trading app next Tuesday, your money does not go to that company. It goes to whoever sold you those 10 shares — another investor, just like you. The company already cashed its check at the IPO. What you're doing every day after that is trading ownership with other people, not funding the business directly. (You're still betting on the business, though — your share's value rises and falls with how it performs.)

Meet the exchanges: NYSE and Nasdaq

An exchange is a regulated marketplace that exists to do one job well: match buyers with sellers at a fair, transparent price, and keep a public record of every trade. In the U.S., the two giants are the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), founded in 1792, and the Nasdaq, the world's first electronic exchange, launched in 1971. They run on two different models. The NYSE is an auction market: each listed stock has a single Designated Market Maker (DMM) — the modern name for what used to be called a “specialist” — whose job is to keep trading in that one stock fair and orderly. Nasdaq is a dealer market: instead of one official market maker per stock, dozens of competing market-making firms quote prices for the same stock at once, and the best price wins.

Both exchanges are open the same hours — 9:30 am to 4:00 pm Eastern Time, Monday through Friday, with optional extended-hours trading roughly 4:00 am to 8:00 pm for investors who want it (thinner trading, wider spreads — more on spreads in a moment). Together they list well over five thousand companies worth tens of trillions of dollars — genuinely the plumbing of the global economy, not a side game.

Buyers, sellers, and the price you see

That one number on your screen — “AAPL $214.32” — isn't a fixed price tag. It's just the price of the most recent trade, and behind it are always two other numbers: the bid, the highest price any buyer is currently offering, and the ask (or “offer”), the lowest price any seller will accept. The gap between them is the bid–ask spread — and it's how the market makers who keep quotes flowing all day get paid for being ready to trade with you instantly, whether you're buying or selling.

Brokers and trading platforms: your gateway in

Here's a detail most beginners never hear: you cannot personally walk up to the NYSE or Nasdaq and place a trade. Access to the exchanges is restricted to licensed broker-dealers — firms like Fidelity, Schwab, or any of the trading apps on your phone. When you tap “Buy,” you're not talking to the exchange; you're handing an instruction to your broker, and your broker is legally required to seek best execution — the most favorable price and speed reasonably available — on your behalf. From there, your broker chooses where to send the order: straight to an exchange, to a market maker who fills it from their own inventory, to an Electronic Communication Network (ECN) that matches buyers and sellers directly, or occasionally it fills the order internally, out of the firm's own book. (Some market makers pay brokers a small fee for routed orders, called payment for order flow — it's legal and SEC-regulated, but it's exactly why that best-execution duty matters.)

Once your broker knows where to send the order, you decide how. The two order types every beginner should know cold:

📅
Fast Fact

Your trade fills in a blink — but the official change of ownership on the books, called settlement, happens one business day later. The SEC shortened the U.S. standard settlement cycle to “T+1” (trade date plus one day) in May 2024, specifically to shrink the window of risk between a trade and its final bookkeeping.

What to Do Next

Open any trading app or a free quote site and look up one real company's ticker symbol. Watch the bid and ask for sixty seconds — you'll actually see the spread move as new orders arrive. Then check that your own broker is a real, registered firm using FINRA's free BrokerCheck tool. You now know what you'd be buying and who's standing between you and the exchange.
