Chapter 6

ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

Now for my favorite shortcut in all of investing. An exchange-traded fund — ETF for short — takes everything you just learned about mutual funds (pool your money, buy a diversified basket in one shot) and bolts on a superpower: it trades all day long on a stock exchange, just like a share of Apple or Coca-Cola. Cheap, flexible, and famously tax-friendly — it's no wonder ETFs have become the default starting point for millions of beginner investors. Let's find out exactly why.

What exactly is an ETF?

Like a mutual fund, an ETF is an SEC-registered fund that pools money from many investors and holds a basket of stocks, bonds, or other assets on their behalf. When you buy a share of an ETF, you own a slice of everything inside that basket — sometimes just a handful of holdings, sometimes thousands.

The difference is how you buy it. A mutual fund is priced once a day and you buy it directly from the fund company. An ETF is listed on an exchange — the NYSE, Nasdaq, and so on — and trades continuously throughout the day at a live market price, the same way an ordinary stock does. You can place a market order, a limit order, even buy on margin, at 10:07 a.m. or 3:52 p.m., and the price you get reflects that exact moment, not an end-of-day snapshot.

🔁
How New ETF Shares Get Made

You and I never create ETF shares directly — that job belongs to big institutional traders called Authorized Participants (APs). An AP assembles a basket of the underlying stocks or bonds and swaps it with the fund in kind for a big block of new ETF shares (a “creation unit,” often 25,000–100,000 shares), then sells those shares to investors like us on the open exchange. Redemptions work the same way in reverse. This constant arbitrage is what keeps an ETF's market price trading close to its underlying Net Asset Value — and, as you'll see next, it's also the secret behind the ETF's tax advantage.

ETF vs. mutual fund: the real differences

For a broad, low-cost index strategy, an ETF and an equivalent index mutual fund often end up owning nearly the same holdings. The differences that actually matter live in how you trade them, what they cost, and how they're taxed.

💰
The Tax Edge, in One Number

Because in-kind redemptions rarely force a sale of appreciated holdings, ETFs distribute far fewer taxable capital gains than mutual funds. Across the U.S. fund industry, a small single-digit percentage of ETFs paid out a capital gains distribution in a typical recent year, versus a large share of mutual funds. In a taxable brokerage account, that gap can meaningfully add up over decades — though inside a tax-advantaged account like a 401(k) or IRA, it barely matters, since neither fund type triggers a tax bill there anyway.

⚠️
Watch Out

Not every ETF trades heavily. A thinly traded ETF can have a wide bid-ask spread — the gap between the price you'd pay to buy and the price you'd get to sell — which quietly eats into your returns on every trade. Before you buy, check an ETF's average daily trading volume and its typical bid-ask spread; for a first ETF, stick to large, heavily traded funds with billions of dollars in assets, where spreads are usually just a penny or two.

Popular beginner ETF categories

You don't need dozens of ETFs to build a serious portfolio — a handful of broad, low-cost categories cover almost everything a beginner needs. This is a map of the territory, not a shopping list: research any fund's own prospectus, holdings, and fees before you buy.

CategoryWhat it holdsWhy beginners like it
Total U.S. stock market Nearly every publicly traded U.S. company, large to small One purchase, instant exposure to the entire U.S. economy
S&P 500 The ~500 largest U.S. companies The most-quoted U.S. benchmark; extremely low cost, extremely liquid
Total international stock Thousands of companies outside the U.S., developed and emerging markets Diversifies you beyond a single country's economy
Total U.S. bond market A broad mix of U.S. government and investment-grade corporate bonds Ballast — smooths out the ride when stocks fall
Sector & thematic One slice of the market — tech, energy, clean power, robotics, and so on A way to lean into a theme you believe in — but concentrated, so higher risk
💡
Not a Recommendation

This chapter deliberately names categories, not specific tickers to buy. Within each category, several fund families (Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab, iShares, and others) offer near-identical, ultra-low-cost options tracking the same index — the differences between them are usually a fraction of a percent in fees. Compare the actual expense ratios and holdings yourself before choosing one.

Building a simple ETF portfolio

Professional portfolios can get complicated. A beginner's doesn't need to. A classic, widely used starting point is the three-fund portfolio: one broad U.S. stock ETF, one international stock ETF, and one bond ETF, mixed in proportions that match how long you can leave the money invested and how much short-term ups and downs you can stomach.

  1. Pick your stock/bond split first. A common rule of thumb: the further away your goal, the higher your stock percentage can be, since you have time to ride out drops. Investors decades from retirement often lean heavily toward stocks; investors near a goal shift more toward bonds for stability.
  2. Split your stock allocation between U.S. and international. A frequent starting mix is roughly 60–80% U.S., 20–40% international — enough to diversify beyond one country without overcomplicating things.
  3. Add a bond ETF for ballast. Even a modest bond allocation smooths out the portfolio's swings, which matters most when markets get scary and you're tempted to sell everything (remember Chapter 2!).
  4. Rebalance occasionally, not constantly. Once or twice a year, check whether your mix has drifted from your target and nudge new contributions toward whichever piece has fallen behind.
