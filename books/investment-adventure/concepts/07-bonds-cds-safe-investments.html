Chapter 7

Bonds, CDs, and Other “Safe” Investments

Every portfolio needs a seatbelt. Stocks and stock ETFs are your growth engine — but growth engines lurch, and you don't want to be forced to sell one in a downturn just because you needed cash that week. That's the job of this chapter's cast: bonds, CDs, and money market funds. None of them will make you rich. All of them will let you sleep at night, and used well, they're what let you stay invested in the exciting stuff through the scary years. Let's get precise about what “safe” actually means here — because it's relative, not absolute.

What exactly is a bond?

A bond is an IOU. When you buy one, you're lending money to whoever issued it — the U.S. government, a city, a corporation — for a fixed period of time. In exchange, the issuer promises two things: regular interest payments (called the coupon) while you hold the bond, usually every six months, and your full principal back when the bond matures. Buy a $1,000 bond with a 5% coupon and a 10-year maturity, and — barring default — you collect $50 a year for ten years, then get your $1,000 back at the end.

That's the whole mechanism. Everything else in this chapter is just who's borrowing (the government, a city, a company), how long (days to 30 years), and how confident you can be you'll actually get paid back.

📈
The One Bond-Math Gotcha to Internalize

Bond prices and bond yields move in opposite directions. If interest rates rise after you buy a 3% bond, new bonds start offering 5% — so your old 3% bond is worth less to anyone else, because they could get a better deal elsewhere. Its price falls until its effective yield roughly matches the market. The reverse happens if rates fall. This isn't a flaw; it's just how a fixed coupon gets re-priced against a moving market — and it's the single most common thing that surprises new bond investors.

Treasuries Come in Several Shapes

Treasury bills (T-bills) run 4 to 52 weeks, are sold at a discount to face value, and pay no separate coupon — the “interest” is just the gap between what you pay and the face value you get back at maturity. Treasury notes run 2, 3, 5, 7, or 10 years and pay a fixed coupon every six months. Treasury bonds run 20 or 30 years, same fixed semiannual coupon, just a much longer commitment. There's also TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities), whose principal adjusts with inflation, and non-marketable savings bonds (Series I and EE), bought only through TreasuryDirect and built for individual savers rather than trading. All of it is auctioned and tracked at TreasuryDirect.gov, the U.S. Treasury's own retail platform.

⚠️
Watch Out: “Safe” Isn't “Risk-Free”

Every bond carries some mix of interest rate risk (its price falls if rates rise before maturity — and the longer the maturity, the bigger the swing), credit risk (the issuer might not pay you back — real for corporates and some munis, essentially nonexistent for Treasuries), and inflation risk (a fixed coupon buys less if prices rise faster than expected). “Safe” in this chapter means safer than stocks and far more predictable — not zero risk. If you hold an individual bond to maturity and the issuer doesn't default, you get exactly what was promised; sell early, and the price you get depends on where rates have moved since.

CDs: locking in a rate with a bank

A certificate of deposit (CD) is a savings account with a handshake attached: you agree to leave a set amount of money with a bank or credit union for a fixed term — commonly three months to five years — and the bank agrees to pay you a fixed interest rate for the whole term, usually higher than an ordinary savings account. Take the money out early and you'll typically owe an early withdrawal penalty, often a few months' worth of interest, so a CD is best for money you're genuinely willing to set aside untouched.

🛡️
The FDIC Safety Net

CDs (and ordinary bank deposits) at an FDIC-insured bank are insured up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category — if the bank fails, the FDIC makes you whole up to that limit. Credit unions carry the equivalent protection through the NCUA. That's a real, government-backed guarantee that individual bonds simply don't carry — it's what makes a CD the single most predictable, most insured instrument in this whole chapter.

Money market funds vs. money market accounts

These two share a name and confuse almost everyone at first — but they're regulated completely differently, which matters more than it sounds like it should.

What it is A mutual fund that holds very short-term debt — T-bills, commercial paper, repurchase agreements A bank deposit account, close cousin of a savings account, sometimes with check-writing
Regulator SEC (an investment product) FDIC/NCUA (a bank product)

In practice, both aim to be extremely stable, liquid places to park cash you'll need soon — an emergency fund, a house down payment you're saving for next year, the cash sitting in a brokerage account between trades. The fund version has historically paid a bit more when rates are elevated; the account version trades a little yield for that explicit FDIC guarantee.

📊
The Rate Environment Changes — Check It Yourself

As of mid-2026 the Federal Reserve's target federal funds rate sits around 3.50%–3.75%, and the most competitive CDs and money market funds are paying somewhere near 4% — well above the roughly 2% national average for an ordinary 1-year CD, which is why shopping around matters. These numbers move with every Fed meeting, so treat them as a snapshot, not a promise. Before you commit money, check the current numbers yourself: the Fed's own rate is published on the Federal Reserve's H.15 release, Treasury rates and auction results live at TreasuryDirect, and the FDIC publishes a national deposit rate you can compare your bank's CD offer against.

So when do these actually belong in your portfolio?

Three situations, and they're not mutually exclusive:

  1. As ballast for a stock-heavy portfolio. Remember the three-fund portfolio from Chapter 6? The bond ETF slice exists to smooth out the ride — bonds typically fall less than stocks in a downturn, and sometimes rise while stocks fall, which is exactly the cushion you want when you're tempted to panic-sell (Chapter 2 says hello).
  2. For money you'll need soon. If you'll need the cash within the next few years — a down payment, tuition, an emergency fund — it generally shouldn't be in stocks at all. A short Treasury bill, a CD timed to when you'll need the money, or a money market fund is the more appropriate home: you're trading upside for certainty, on purpose.
  3. As you get closer to a goal. The same glide-path logic from the ETF chapter applies here: the closer you get to retirement (or any big goal), the more your mix typically shifts from stocks toward bonds and cash, because you have less time to recover from a bad stretch in the market.
