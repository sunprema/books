Chapter 5

Mutual Funds Explained

Picking a single stock — even a good one — means betting on one company's future. A mutual fund lets you skip that bet entirely: pool your money with thousands of other investors and buy a professionally assembled basket of dozens, hundreds, even thousands of companies in one single order. It's the single biggest shortcut to instant diversification that beginner investors have — but not every mutual fund deserves your money equally. This chapter shows you exactly how to tell a great one from an expensive dud.

What exactly is a mutual fund?

Legally, a mutual fund is an SEC-registered investment company that pools money from many investors and invests it — in stocks, bonds, short-term cash instruments, or some mix — on their collective behalf. When you buy in, you're not buying a piece of any one company; you're buying shares of the fund itself, and the fund's professional manager (or managers) decides what the pooled money actually holds.

Unlike a stock, a mutual fund doesn't trade minute-to-minute during the day. It's priced once, every business day, after the major exchanges close — that price is called the Net Asset Value, or NAV: the fund's total assets minus its liabilities, divided by the number of shares outstanding. Every order placed that day — whether at 9:35 a.m. or 3:55 p.m. — fills at that same end-of-day NAV.

🕑
One Price a Day

A stock's price ticks constantly while the market is open. A mutual fund's price — its NAV — is calculated just once, after the close. Buy or sell a mutual fund at 10 a.m. or 3 p.m., and you get the exact same price: whatever the NAV works out to be once the trading day ends.

Active vs. passive: two very different promises

Every mutual fund is managed one of two fundamentally different ways, and the difference matters enormously to your long-term returns.

An actively managed fund employs a manager (or a team) who researches companies, picks what to buy and sell, and tries to beat a benchmark — say, the S&P 500. A passively managed fund, usually called an index fund, makes no such attempt: it simply buys every company in a chosen index, in proportion, and aims to match the benchmark, not outsmart it.

📊
The Uncomfortable Stat

Beating the market is far harder than it sounds — even for professionals. In S&P Dow Jones Indices' year-end 2025 SPIVA Scorecard, 79% of actively managed U.S. large-cap funds underperformed the S&P 500 over the year, the fourth-worst showing in the scorecard's 25-year history. Skilled stock-picking exists — but reliably identifying it in advance, year after year, is the hard part.

Fees that quietly eat your returns

A mutual fund never sends you a bill. Its costs are deducted automatically from the fund's assets, a little at a time, which is exactly why they're so easy to ignore — and so important to check before you buy.

FeeWhat it isWhen it hits you
Expense ratio An annual percentage of your investment, covering management and operating costs Every year, automatically, whether the fund gains or loses money
Front-end load A sales charge taken off the top when you buy (common on “Class A” shares) The moment you invest — less of your money actually gets put to work
Back-end load (CDSC) A charge for selling too soon, that typically shrinks toward zero the longer you hold Only if you sell within the fund's holding-period window
12b-1 fee An ongoing marketing/distribution fee Baked into the expense ratio every year, not billed separately
⚠️
Watch Out

“No-load” means the fund doesn't charge a sales commission — it does not mean the fund is free. Every mutual fund, load or no-load, still carries an ongoing expense ratio. Always check the fund's prospectus fee table for the actual number before you buy.

How to choose your first fund

You don't need to become a fund analyst. For a first fund, a short checklist gets you almost all the way there:

  1. Start with the expense ratio. For a broad index fund, look for something well under 0.20% — many of the largest total-market and S&P 500 index funds now charge closer to 0.03–0.10%.
  2. Skip the loads. Choose a no-load fund so 100% of your money goes to work immediately, not to a sales commission.
  3. Favor broad diversification. A total U.S. stock market fund or an S&P 500 index fund spreads you across hundreds or thousands of companies in one purchase — exactly the diversification a beginner needs most.
  4. Check the minimum investment. Many major fund families now offer $0-minimum share classes; a few still require $1,000–$3,000 to start.
  5. Read the fee table in the prospectus. It's a short, standardized table near the front of every fund's prospectus — it lists every fee above in one place, no digging required.
