Chapter 15

Staying the Course

Remember the little seed from Chapter 1? Here's the part nobody puts on the seed packet: real trees live through storms. Hail flattens the leaves. Wind bends the trunk sideways. Some years a drought turns everything brown and you'd swear the thing is dying. And yet — if the roots are deep and nobody panics and digs it up to “check if it's okay,” the tree is still standing, still growing, years later. Your portfolio is that tree. Every skill in this book — the accounts, the funds, the diversification, the automation — exists to build deep roots. This last chapter teaches the one skill that actually protects them: staying planted when the sky goes dark.

Every crash in history has, so far, had a comeback

“The market crashed” is a headline designed to make your pulse spike. Here's the same event with the ending included — something the panicked headline never has room for. Three of the scariest U.S. stock market drops in recent memory, and what actually happened next:

CrashPeak → troughDeclineTime to a full recovery
2007–2009 financial crisis Oct 2007 → Mar 2009 −57% ~5.5 years (new highs by early 2013)
2020 COVID crash Feb 2020 → Mar 2020 −34% ~5 months — the fastest full recovery in over 150 years
2022 bear market Jan 2022 → Oct 2022 −25% ~2 years (new highs by January 2024)
💡
The One-Sentence Version

A crash is a temporary drop in price; a loss only becomes permanent the moment you sell — every major U.S. downturn on record has, eventually, been followed by a full recovery and new highs for investors who simply stayed invested.

Your brain again: why the worst moment to sell always feels like the smartest one

Back in Chapter 2 you met loss aversion — the wiring that makes losses feel roughly twice as intense as equivalent gains. A crash is loss aversion's biggest stage: the number is falling every single day, the news is using words like “plunge” and “bloodbath,” and every instinct you have is screaming make it stop. Selling feels like relief. The data says it's usually the single most expensive decision an investor can make.

$32,871 vs. $71,750 — what $10,000 invested in the S&P 500 from 2005–2024 grew to if you missed just the 10 single best trading days of those 20 years, versus staying fully invested the whole time (J.P. Morgan Asset Management)

Here's the cruelest twist: the market's best days cluster right next to its worst ones — often within days of each other, in the exact chaos of a crash. J.P. Morgan modeled an investor who sold at the depths of the 2008 crisis and again near the bottom of the 2020 COVID crash, re-buying only after each recovery was already underway; that investor's $10,000 grew to less than $30,000 (about 5.3%/year) over a 20-year span where simply staying invested turned the same $10,000 into roughly $71,750 (about 10.4%/year). The dip didn't cost that investor money — the exit and re-entry did.

This isn't a hypothetical about strangers. Fidelity studied 7.1 million real 401(k) accounts through the 2008–2009 crash: participants who dumped all their stock holdings between October 2008 and March 2009 and never got back in saw their balances grow just 2% by mid-2011. Participants who kept some money in stocks through the whole storm saw their balances grow 50% over that same stretch — twenty-five times better, for doing less: nothing at all.

⚠️
Watch Out

This is the exact “behavior gap” from Chapter 2's Morningstar research, showing up again in a different dataset: DALBAR's long-running investor-behavior study finds the average equity-fund investor trailed the S&P 500 by roughly 1 percentage point a year over the trailing 20 years — not because they picked bad funds, but because they bought and sold at the wrong moments, usually clustered around exactly the kind of volatility this chapter is about.

The Storm Simulator: watch a bad year get absorbed by good decades

This is the same growth engine from Chapters 1 and 10 and 14, one last time. Try this: set Annual return deep into the red — say −15% or −20% — and Years invested to just 1 or 2, and watch how grim that looks in isolation. Now slide Years invested up toward 20 or 30 and Annual return back to a realistic long-run 7–9%. That single ugly year doesn't vanish from history — but it stops being able to sink the whole voyage. (Real markets zig-zag year to year rather than holding one constant rate, exactly like the chart above — this game trades that realism for a clear, controllable lesson: time is what turns a storm into a footnote.)

Rebalancing during a storm: your only “action,” on a schedule

Chapter 14 already gave you the mechanical rule: check quarterly, rebalance only when a slice has drifted 5 percentage points or more from its target — the same threshold Vanguard's own advisory service uses. What that rule actually means during a crash is worth spelling out, because it's the opposite of what panic tells you to do:

  • A stock crash mechanically makes rebalancing mean “buy stocks while they're cheap.” If stocks fall and bonds don't, your portfolio's stock percentage drops below target — so a disciplined rebalance sells a little of the bonds that held up and buys more of the stocks that are on sale. You don't have to feel brave to do this; the threshold makes the decision for you, in advance, before the fear shows up.
  • Don't rebalance more often just because it's a scary week. The 5-point threshold exists precisely so a routine 8% wobble doesn't trigger action — only a real, meaningful drift does.
  • Keep the automatic contribution running. The recurring buy from Chapter 10 is quietly doing the same job as a rebalance every single payday — buying more shares for the same dollar when prices are down.
  • Never stop contributions to “wait it out.” That's the exact move Fidelity's 401(k) data punished hardest — missing new contributions during the recovery, not just holding through the drop, is what widens the gap.
🌲
The Crash-Day Playbook

Next time a headline says the market “plunged,” do this instead of checking your account for the fifth time today: (1) close the app. (2) Re-read the “why” sentence you wrote in Chapter 2. (3) Check — on your quarterly schedule, not today — whether any slice has actually drifted 5+ points; if not, there is nothing to do. (4) Let your automatic contribution run. (5) If you really want to act, act on step 3, never on the headline.

You planted the seed. Now let it grow.

Fifteen chapters ago you learned that a single $1,000 seed, left alone at a steady 7% for 30 years, becomes roughly $7,610 — not from anything clever, just from time and math working quietly in the background. Everything between here and there — the stock market's mechanics, mutual funds and ETFs, bonds, diversification, index funds, dollar-cost averaging, the right account wrapper, taxes, dodging scams, and building a real first portfolio — was never about outsmarting the market. It was about building roots deep enough, and a plan boring enough, that a storm like 2008 or 2020 or 2022 becomes a chapter in the story instead of the end of it. That's not a lack of ambition. That's the whole strategy that has actually worked, decade after decade, for ordinary people who simply didn't sell.
