Chapter 11

Retirement Accounts:
IRAs, 401(k)s, and More

Everything you've learned so far — stocks, funds, ETFs, dollar-cost averaging — answers the question “what should I buy?” This chapter answers a different, sneakier question: what container should I buy it inside? Because here's the part nobody tells beginners loudly enough — the United States government will happily hand you a permanent tax discount just for saving toward retirement in the right kind of account. Not a coupon. Not a limited-time offer. A standing, legal, use-it-every-year discount. Skipping it isn't playing it safe; it's leaving free money on the table. Let's go collect it.

A retirement account is a wrapper, not an investment

Here's the single biggest “aha” beginners have about retirement accounts: an IRA or a 401(k) isn't a thing you invest in — it's a special, tax-favored box you put your existing investments inside. The stocks, index funds, and ETFs from earlier chapters all still apply; you're simply choosing to hold them inside a wrapper the IRS agrees to tax differently, instead of an ordinary taxable brokerage account.

A 401(k) is a workplace retirement wrapper your employer sets up and often contributes to alongside you. An IRA (Individual Retirement Account) is a wrapper you open yourself at any brokerage — no employer required. Both come in two tax flavors, Traditional and Roth, and the choice between them is really just a choice about when you'd rather pay the tax bill.

💡
The One-Sentence Version

Retirement accounts don't change what you invest in — they change how it's taxed, and the government rewards you for using them with a break you don't get in an ordinary brokerage account.

Traditional vs. Roth IRA: pay taxes now, or later?

An IRA is the account you can open yourself, at almost any brokerage, in about ten minutes — no employer needed. Per the IRS, for 2026 you can contribute up to $7,500 across all your IRAs combined if you're under 50, or $8,600 (including a $1,100 catch-up) if you're 50 or older.

The Roth's Hidden Superpower

Because you already paid tax on the money you put into a Roth IRA, the IRS lets you withdraw your contributions — not the earnings, just what you put in — at any age, for any reason, with zero tax and zero penalty. It's the closest thing to a retirement account you can also lean on in a genuine emergency, though the real win comes from leaving it alone for decades.

One catch worth knowing up front: Roth IRAs phase out at higher incomes. For 2026, the ability to contribute directly to a Roth IRA phases out between $153,000–$168,000 of modified adjusted gross income for single filers, and $242,000–$252,000 for married couples filing jointly. Traditional IRA deductibility has its own, separate phase-out if you (or your spouse) are covered by a workplace plan — for 2026 that's roughly $81,000–$91,000 single, $129,000–$149,000 married filing jointly. Miss the income window for a deductible Traditional IRA or a direct Roth contribution, and you can still contribute — you just won't get that particular tax perk, so it's worth checking the current numbers on IRS.gov before you file.

The 401(k): investing before your paycheck even lands

A 401(k) is a retirement wrapper your employer sponsors, and it works a little differently from an IRA: your contribution is deducted straight from your paycheck, before you ever see the money — which is exactly what makes it one of the easiest habits to keep. Most 401(k) plans offer both a Traditional (pre-tax) and a Roth (after-tax) option, so the same “pay now vs. pay later” choice from the IRA section above usually applies here too.

$24,500 2026 employee 401(k) contribution limit — plus up to $11,250 more in catch-up if you're 60–63

The IRS sets the 401(k) employee limit at $24,500 for 2026. If you're 50 or older, you can add a $8,000 catch-up contribution (bringing your total to $32,500) — and there's a special super catch-up of $11,250 just for employees aged 60, 61, 62, or 63, for a total of $35,750. Counting your employer's contributions too, the combined employee-plus-employer ceiling is $72,000 (under 50), rising to $80,000 or $83,250 with the catch-ups above.

⚠️
New for 2026: High Earners Must Catch Up in Roth

A SECURE 2.0 Act rule takes full effect this year: if you're 50+ and your prior year's FICA wages were $150,000 or more, any catch-up contributions you make to a 401(k) must go into the Roth side of the plan — after-tax dollars, no upfront deduction. If your employer's plan doesn't offer a Roth 401(k) option, high earners simply can't make catch-up contributions at all until it does. Worth a quick check with your plan administrator if this is your first year crossing that threshold.

Employer matching: the closest thing to free money you'll ever find

Many employers sweeten their 401(k) by adding money of their own whenever you contribute — called a match. A common formula: dollar-for-dollar on the first 3% of your salary you contribute, then 50 cents on the dollar for the next 2%. Contribute 5% of a $60,000 salary ($3,000) under that formula, and your employer kicks in roughly $2,400 more — money that simply appears in your account because you showed up and contributed.

💡
Do the Math on “Free”

A 50% match is an instant, guaranteed 50% return on that portion of your money, the moment it lands — before the market has moved a single cent. A 100% match is an instant 100% return. No stock, bond, or index fund in this entire book can promise that. Leaving a match unclaimed isn't “playing it safe” — it's turning down a raise.

One nuance: employer match money is often subject to vesting — a waiting period before it's fully, permanently yours if you leave the job. Your own contributions are always 100% yours immediately, no matter what.

How to maximize your retirement savings: the order of operations

With a limited amount of money to invest, seasoned savers tend to fill these buckets roughly in this order — each step captures a benefit the next step can't:

  1. Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match. That instant, guaranteed return beats every other step on this list — grab it first, every time.
  2. Max out an IRA — Roth if your income qualifies, Traditional otherwise. IRAs often unlock lower fees and a far wider menu of investments than a workplace plan.
  3. Go back and raise your 401(k) contribution toward the annual limit, if you can still afford to.
  4. Automate all of it — the same “set it and forget it” habit from Chapter 10, just aimed at accounts that also hand you a tax break.

What to Do Next

Open your 401(k) portal (or your pay stub) today and confirm two things: your contribution percentage, and your employer's match formula. If you're not capturing the full match, raise your contribution right now — it's the single highest-return move in this entire book. Don't have a 401(k)?
