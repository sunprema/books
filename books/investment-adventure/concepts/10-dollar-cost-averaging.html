Chapter 10

Dollar-Cost Averaging
and Long-Term Thinking

Quick confession: nobody — not your uncle with the hot stock tip, not the loudest voice on financial TV, not even the professionals running billion-dollar funds — can reliably tell you when the market is about to go up or down next. The good news? You don't need that superpower. There's a boring, unglamorous habit that beats trying to be clever, and it fits in one sentence: put in the same amount, on the same schedule, no matter what the market is doing. That's dollar-cost averaging, and it's about to become your favorite autopilot button.

What is dollar-cost averaging, exactly?

The SEC's Investor.gov puts it plainly: dollar-cost averaging (DCA) means investing your money in equal portions, at regular intervals, regardless of what the market is doing that day. Pay yourself $100 every payday and buy shares with it — rain or shine, boom or crash — and you're dollar-cost averaging. No spreadsheet of predictions required.

Here's the quietly brilliant part: because you're investing a fixed dollar amount rather than a fixed number of shares, you automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer shares when prices are high — without ever having to decide which is which. Your $100 simply stretches further on a discount day and less far on an expensive day. Do that for years and your average cost per share tends to land somewhere comfortably in the middle of the ride, instead of at whatever price happened to be showing the one day you nervously decided to click “buy.”

💡
The One-Sentence Version

Instead of trying to guess the perfect moment to invest, you invest on a fixed schedule for a fixed amount — turning “when should I buy?” from a stressful guessing game into a question you never have to answer again.

Why timing the market is a losing game

It's tempting to think a sharp investor could do better by waiting for dips and selling before drops. In practice, this is one of the most consistently punishing bets in finance — because the market's best days and worst days tend to cluster close together, often inside the same volatile stretch, which makes them almost impossible to separate in advance.

8.4% → 2.1% annualized S&P 500 return over the past 30 years if you stayed fully invested, versus if you missed just the market's 30 best days

That's not a typo. Looking at the S&P 500 from mid-1995 through mid-2025, an investor who simply stayed put the entire time earned about 8.4% a year. Miss only the 10 best days out of those roughly 7,500 trading days and your return is cut roughly in half. Miss the 30 best days and it falls all the way to 2.1%. Miss 50, and the three decades would have actually lost you money on average. The catch: those best days almost never announce themselves — they routinely show up in the middle of the scariest stretches, which is exactly when a nervous investor is most tempted to step out.

⚠️
The Behavior Gap Is Real Money

DALBAR's long-running Quantitative Analysis of Investor Behavior tracks what actual fund investors earn versus what the market itself returned. Over the 20 years ending December 2024, the average U.S. equity fund investor earned about 9.24% a year — while the S&P 500 itself returned 10.35% a year. That one-point gap, compounded over two decades, is the cost of buying after good news and selling after bad news instead of just staying the course. It's not a knowledge problem. It's a behavior problem — and a fixed, automatic schedule is the fix, because it removes the decision entirely.

One nuance worth getting right

There's a version of this question research has actually tested head-to-head: if you already have a lump sum sitting in cash — a bonus, an inheritance, a settled account — is it better to invest it all today, or spread it in gradually over the next several months? Vanguard's research, examining U.S., U.K., and Australian markets back to 1926, found that investing the lump sum immediately beat spreading it out in about two out of every three rolling periods studied.

~68% of historical periods where investing a lump sum immediately outperformed spreading it in gradually — Vanguard Research

The reason is simple math, not a trick: markets rise more years than they fall, so cash you leave sitting on the sidelines while you “ease in” is, on average, cash missing out on growth. Vanguard's own conclusion was blunt — they titled the paper “Dollar-Cost Averaging Just Means Taking Risk Later.”

🌱
Don't Let That Confuse You

That study is about a windfall you're already holding — the question is whether to delay investing money you already have. This chapter is about something different: money you don't have yet, arriving a paycheck at a time. When you invest each paycheck the moment it lands instead of letting it pile up in a checking account, you're not “delaying” anything — you're investing every dollar as soon as it exists. That's not a compromise strategy. For ongoing income, it's simply the fastest path in, and it happens to look exactly like dollar-cost averaging from the outside.

The “set it and forget it” strategy

This is where dollar-cost averaging turns from a concept into a habit that runs without you. Set up an automatic, recurring transfer — from your paycheck into a 401(k), or from your checking account into a brokerage — on a schedule you'll never have to think about again. No morning-news anxiety, no watching red or green candles, no willpower required. The transfer happens whether the market is celebrating or panicking, and that's exactly the point: your best investing decisions are the ones you only have to make once.

What to Do Next

Open your paycheck, 401(k), or brokerage app today and turn on (or increase) one recurring, automatic investment — even a small one. Then go back to the game above, set the monthly contribution to a number that actually matches your real budget, and watch what thirty years of not-stopping looks like. You now know the two ingredients that beat almost every clever market-timing trick: a fixed schedule, and the patience to leave it alone.
