The Cheatsheet

This page doesn't teach — it reminds. Keep it open in a tab, or print it (it's built to print cleanly), and glance at it before you place an order, pick an account, or panic during a red week in the market.

The 30-Second Summary

Investing means buying tiny, real slices of productive businesses — through individual stocks, or better, broad, low-cost index funds and ETFs — and letting compounding plus time do the heavy lifting, the way a single seed becomes a tree. It is not gambling (no value is created there) and it is not a get-rich-quick scheme (anyone promising a “guaranteed” return is running a scam). Pick your account wrapper in order — capture your full 401(k) match, then max an IRA, then a taxable brokerage — diversify across stocks, bonds, and geographies so no single company or country can sink you, and automate a recurring contribution so the habit runs whether or not you feel like logging in. Check your allocation quarterly and only rebalance when a slice has drifted 5+ percentage points from target — not on every headline. And when the market inevitably drops: every major U.S. crash on record has eventually recovered for investors who didn't sell. The whole adventure, boiled down: start early, keep costs low, spread it wide, automate it, and don't uproot the tree during a storm.

Glossary Recap

  • Compounding — earning returns on your past returns, not just your original deposit; the engine behind every chapter in this book.
  • Diversification — spreading money across many companies, sectors, and countries so no single failure sinks the whole portfolio.
  • Index fund — a fund that simply buys the whole market (or a slice of it) instead of trying to beat it; low-cost, hard to outperform.
  • ETF — a fund that trades like a stock all day, usually tracking an index, with fund-level diversification in one ticker.
  • Mutual fund — a pooled fund priced once a day at its NAV (net asset value), bought/sold directly through the fund company.
  • Dollar-cost averaging — investing a fixed amount on a fixed schedule, so you automatically buy more shares when prices are low.
  • Bond — a loan you make to a government or company that pays you a fixed coupon and returns your principal when it matures.
  • Dividend — a company's cash payout to shareholders; qualified dividends get the lower long-term tax rate.
  • Capital gain — profit from selling an investment; short-term (held ≤1 yr) is taxed as ordinary income, long-term (>1 yr) gets the lower 0/15/20% rate.
  • Rebalancing — selling a little of what's grown and buying a little of what's lagged to restore your target allocation.
  • Bid–ask spread — the small gap between the highest buy offer and lowest sell offer for a security; part of trading cost.
  • Vesting — the schedule by which employer 401(k) contributions actually become yours to keep.
  • Risk tolerance — how you'd emotionally handle a drop; risk capacity — whether you can financially afford one. A smart plan respects the lower of the two.
  • Loss aversion — losses feel roughly 2× as intense as equivalent gains; the brain wiring behind most panic-selling.
  • Behavior gap — the annual return investors give up by trading on emotion instead of staying put (DALBAR/Morningstar find roughly 1–1.2 points/year).
  • Fiduciary — an advisor legally required to put your interests ahead of their own; not every “advisor” is one.
  • Prospectus — a fund's legal disclosure document: fees, holdings, risks, strategy.
  • Ponzi scheme — a fraud that pays “returns” to old investors using new investors' cash, not real profit; the classic “guaranteed return” scam.

⚠️ Gotchas — Read Before You React

  • ⚠Selling during a crash. A drop only becomes a permanent loss the moment you sell — every major U.S. downturn on record (1929, 1987, 2000, 2008, 2020, 2022) has eventually recovered for investors who stayed in.
  • ⚠“Guaranteed” high returns. No legitimate investment guarantees a return above the FDIC/NCUA insurance rate. If it's “guaranteed,” it's very likely a Ponzi scheme — check FINRA BrokerCheck or SEC IAPD before sending a cent.
  • ⚠Rebalancing on every wiggle. Trade only when a slice drifts 5+ percentage points from target; more frequent trading mostly just adds cost and taxable events.
  • ⚠The wash sale rule. Sell a losing position for a tax write-off, then buy the same (or a “substantially identical”) investment back within 30 days, and the IRS disallows the loss.
  • ⚠Stopping contributions to “wait it out.” Fidelity's 2008–2009 data shows the investors who kept contributing through the crash ended up 25× better off than those who sold and sat in cash.
  • ⚠A triggered stop order isn't a guaranteed price. Once it fires it becomes a plain market order, and in a fast, gappy market it can fill well below your stop price.
